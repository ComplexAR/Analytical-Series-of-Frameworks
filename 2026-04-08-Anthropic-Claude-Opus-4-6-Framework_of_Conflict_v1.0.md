# Introduction

## What is Conflict?

Conflict is contestation between actors—including open conflict, suppressed conflict, structural conflict, and competitive dynamics. It examines the relational dynamics of opposition: how actors' interests collide, how they mobilize to contest one another, what alignment and coalition patterns emerge, and what strategic behaviour results from adversarial conditions. Conflict does not ask "what does the actor want?" (that is Interests) or "what makes actors better off?" (that is Incentives) or "who has capacity to shape outcomes?" (that is Power). It asks: "When actors' interests are incompatible or zero-sum, what relational dynamics of contestation unfold, and what do they hide about the scenario?" Its primary analytical contribution is exposing the hidden architecture of opposition: conflicts not yet surfaced, interests reframed as technical disagreements, suppressed opposition appearing as false consensus, coalitions forming in obscurity, and structural conflicts built into system design appearing natural.

## Why does Conflict matter for Hiddens work?

Four core mechanisms create conflict-based hiddens that conventional analysis misses:

**Latent Conflict Suppression.** Conflicts between actors do not require open contestation to exist. A manager and frontline staff may have fundamentally incompatible interests about work pace, autonomy, and risk—but organizational norms of politeness suppress open disagreement. The conflict remains latent, invisible to the organization, until a triggering event (crisis, leadership change, external pressure) makes it erupt. Until eruption, the conflict shapes behaviour in hidden ways: staff comply on surface but resist in practice; the manager makes decisions based on false consensus; coordination failures accumulate undetected. The conflict is not absent; it is suppressed, generating hiddens through its invisibility.

**Conflict Reframing as Technical Disagreement.** Actors often frame interest conflicts as technical disagreements: "This is not a value dispute; it is a factual question." A dispute about risk tolerance (fundamentally an interest and values question) becomes framed as a technical debate about probability models; a dispute about resource allocation (fundamentally a conflict about who benefits) becomes a discussion about efficiency metrics. Reframing hides the underlying conflict by moving it to a domain where "objective" resolution appears possible. The technical debate becomes locked in place while the actual conflict remains invisible.

**False Consensus Masking Opposition.** In hierarchical organizations, political systems, and coalitions, visible unity often masks suppressed opposition. Actors vote for a proposal while privately opposing it; they smile in meetings while working against decisions afterward; they publicly endorse strategy while privately undermining it. False consensus is not coordination; it is hidden conflict. It hides the real distribution of support, the true resistance to decisions, and the coalitions forming beneath the surface. Decisions appear robust because opposition is invisible; actual implementation fails because hidden opposition becomes sabotage.

**Competitive Intelligence and Strategic Concealment.** In adversarial contexts (competition, negotiation, conflict escalation), actors strategically conceal intentions, capabilities, and strategic moves. A negotiator does not reveal their true reservation price; a competitor does not disclose their product roadmap; a military strategist does not reveal their force disposition. Strategic concealment is rational in zero-sum contexts, but it creates systematic hiddens: intentions are opaque, capabilities are misestimated, coalitions and alliances are hidden, vulnerabilities are concealed. Analysing scenarios with active strategic concealment requires conflict-aware methods to surface what is being hidden and why.

Conflict's distinctive hiddens contribution is recognizing that opposition, contestation, and adversarial dynamics generate their own visibility failures—not through information asymmetry alone, but through the rational strategic choices actors make when interests conflict. Surfacing latent conflict, detecting strategic reframing, reading false consensus, and mapping hidden opposition are distinct analytical skills that non-conflict frameworks do not provide.

## What does this framework produce?

The Framework of Conflict produces:
- A **conflict type inventory**: Thirty core types of contestation and adversarial dynamics organized across six meta-categories (forms of contestation, conflict triggers, escalation patterns, suppression mechanisms, strategic dynamics, and conflict transformation)
- A **dimensional profile** of any conflict instance (intensity, visibility, structure, stakes, and reversibility)
- A **dynamics map** of conflict over time (emergence, escalation, suppression, reframing, resolution, and recurrence)
- A **conflict stage model** for routing analysis (latent, emerging, overt, escalating, suppressed, resolving, structural)
- **Interface analysis** for hidden, latent, and masked conflict (A: fully suppressed; B: partially visible; C: overt but reframed)
- A **hiddens register** of what conflict hides about intentions, coalitions, vulnerabilities, and strategic intent
- **De-escalation and resolution frameworks** for intervention design
- A **cross-framework integration module** connecting conflict analysis to the 36 frameworks in the Analytical Series

## How to use this framework

Invoke the Framework of Conflict when:
- Actors' interests appear misaligned, and you need to understand the relational dynamics of contestation rather than just the interests themselves
- Suppressed opposition or latent conflict is suspected (false consensus, surface agreement masking hidden disagreement)
- Coalitions are forming, aligning, or fracturing, and you need to map adversarial positioning
- Strategic behaviour is evident (moves that appear inefficient until understood as adversarial positioning)
- Conflict appears reframed as technical disagreement, and you need to surface the underlying contestation
- Zero-sum or coupled dynamics are driving outcomes, and you need to distinguish rational conflict from other coordination failures

Default execution is **Light Depth**: identify the conflict type, stage, visibility, and key hidden opposition. Escalate to **Full Depth** when conflict is material to outcomes, suppression mechanisms are strong, strategic concealment is suspected, or consequence of misunderstanding conflict is high. The Framework of Conflict is iteratively applied through the investigation loop (OG §5.0) and integrates with Power, Interests, Incentives, Agents, and Relations to produce comprehensive strategic analysis. See §1.6 for LLM execution specifications.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Conflict is the framework for analysing contestation and opposition between actors. It examines what happens when actors' interests are incompatible, when zero-sum or coupled dynamics emerge, when actors mobilize to contest one another, and when conflict shapes behaviour through suppression, reframing, or strategic concealment. Conflict is a relational phenomenon: it exists in the relationship between actors (or between an actor and structural constraints), not in the actors themselves.

**Conflict is distinct from related frameworks:**

The Framework of Interests identifies what actors fundamentally want—their terminal goals, values, and desired outcomes. Conflict analyses what happens when those wants are incompatible. Two actors may share identical interests (concordance) or have conflicting interests (contestation), but Interests does not analyse the relational dynamics of contestation itself. Conflict does.

The Framework of Incentives examines reward structures: what outcomes make actors better off? Incentives can be aligned (all actors benefit from the same outcome), misaligned (actors benefit from different outcomes), or actively zero-sum (one actor's gain is another's loss). But Incentives does not analyse how actors respond to misaligned incentives—whether they contest, withdraw, accept, suppress grievance, or form coalitions. Conflict does.

The Framework of Power maps capacity to shape outcomes: who has leverage, resources, authority, and the ability to impose costs on others? Power is necessary for understanding conflict but not sufficient. Two actors may have equal power and incompatible interests, leading to balanced conflict. Or one actor may have overwhelming power and the other no capacity to contest openly, leading to latent conflict and hidden opposition. Conflict analyses the relational dynamics of contestation across power asymmetries.

The Framework of Agents identifies who acts and with what capability. Agents maps agency, autonomy, and constraint. Conflict maps the patterns of opposition and coalition between agents. An Agents analysis identifies an actor; a Conflict analysis identifies whether that actor is in contestation with others and what alliances are forming.

**What Conflict produces:**

Conflict analysis produces:
1. **Conflict type classification**: What kind of contestation is occurring? (overt dispute, latent tension, structural incompatibility, zero-sum competition, false consensus masking opposition, strategic concealment, collective action failure)
2. **Conflict stage and trajectory**: Is the conflict emerging, overt, escalating, suppressed, resolving, or recurring? What are the triggering conditions for stage transitions?
3. **Hidden opposition register**: What opposition is not visible in official channels? Who is mobilizing outside formal structures? What coalitions are forming covertly?
4. **Strategic intent analysis**: What is each actor trying to achieve through conflict behaviour? What is being concealingly? What vulnerabilities are they exposing or protecting?
5. **De-escalation and resolution intervention map**: What would reduce contestation? What structural changes would address root conflict? What negotiation and mediation approaches fit this conflict type and stage?

Conflict analysis integrates with Interests (what actors want), Incentives (what makes them better off), Power (who has capacity), Agents (who acts), Relations (relationship quality), Values (what matters), Beliefs (what actors hold true), and Institutions (what shapes behaviour formally). But Conflict uniquely focuses on the relational dynamics of opposition and what those dynamics hide.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Conflict:** Conflict is contestation between actors characterized by incompatible or opposing interests, objectives, or values. It includes overt disputes (open conflict with explicit confrontation), latent conflicts (incompatible interests not yet surfaced), structural conflicts (built into system design and incentive structures), and competitive dynamics (actors pursuing mutually exclusive outcomes). Conflict is a relational phenomenon: it exists in the relationship between actors and shapes behaviour through opposition, coalition formation, strategic maneuvering, and (often) suppression or reframing.

**In scope:**
- Overt conflicts: open disputes, explicit disagreement, declared opposition, adversarial negotiation
- Latent conflicts: incompatible interests not yet surfaced; tension suppressed by norms, hierarchy, or power imbalance
- Structural conflicts: conflicts built into system design, incentive structures, or role definitions
- Zero-sum and coupled dynamics: outcomes where one actor's gain is another's loss, or where outcomes are interdependent
- Strategic behaviour in adversarial contexts: positioning, alliance formation, information concealment, capability misrepresentation
- Suppression mechanisms: norms of politeness, hierarchy-imposed silence, fear-based compliance, false consensus
- Conflict reframing: interest conflicts presented as technical disagreements or factual disputes
- Coalition formation and alignment: who allies with whom, and on what basis
- Escalation and de-escalation: how conflicts intensify, spread, or subside
- Resolution and transformation: how conflicts end, transform into cooperation, or recur

**Out of scope:**
- Psychology of individual actors (use Cognition, Emotions, Beliefs frameworks instead)
- Moral evaluation of conflict (framework is diagnostic, not normative)
- Conflict resolution methods and mediation processes in detail (Interventions framework applies)
- History of conflicts unless analytically necessary to current situation (use Time framework for historical context)
- Legitimacy of competing claims (use Legitimacy framework for authority questions)
- Organizational design and formal governance (use Governance and Institutions frameworks)

**Common confusions ("Conflict is not …"):**
- Conflict is not absence of agreement on facts. Disagreement can be epistemic (different beliefs about what is true) rather than conflictual. Use Cognition and Beliefs frameworks to distinguish perception differences from value/interest conflict.
- Conflict is not the same as power asymmetry. Powerful actors may be in conflict with less powerful actors; unequal power shapes conflict dynamics but does not eliminate conflict. Use Power framework to analyse capacity; Conflict framework to analyse contestation.
- Conflict is not the same as inefficiency or poor coordination. Conflicts can be rational responses to incompatible interests. Use Decisions, Incentives, and Causation frameworks to distinguish coordination failure from conflictual opposition.
- Conflict is not inherently bad or something to eliminate. Conflict can surface important misalignments, prevent false consensus, and drive necessary change. The framework is diagnostic, not normative.
- Conflict is not the same as all disagreement. Two actors can disagree on a factual point while sharing compatible interests on the outcome. Conflict requires interest/value incompatibility, not just epistemic disagreement.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions

### I. Forms of Contestation

**Definition:** The observable and latent forms through which actors oppose, contest, or compete with one another. Forms range from explicit, overt conflict (open disputes with declared opposition) to fully suppressed latent conflict (incompatible interests not yet surfaced but shaping covert behaviour).

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Actors explicitly state opposing positions and resist agreement (overt conflict signature)
- Actors state agreement but behaviour patterns diverge—compliance on surface, resistance or non-implementation underneath (false consensus signature)
- Incompatible interests can be inferred from positions but are never explicitly acknowledged (latent conflict signature)
- Conflict is denied or reframed as technical disagreement despite clear interest incompatibility (reframing signature)
- Actors appear to compete for mutually exclusive outcomes without framing it as dispute (competitive dynamic signature)
- Opposition is expressed only outside formal channels or to trusted third parties (strategic concealment signature)
- Conflicts are built into role definitions or structural design and appear "natural" rather than as conflict (structural conflict signature)

**Typical failure mode:**
- Latent conflict appearing as stability: incompatible interests suppressed by hierarchy or norms lead analysts to infer false consensus; conflict remains invisible until triggering event
- Reframing hiding contestation: analysts accept technical frame and miss underlying interest conflict driving the technical dispute
- Hidden opposition undermining implementation: decisions appear supported but are covertly opposed; implementation fails not from technical failure but from sabotage
- Structural conflict appearing natural: conflicts built into role definitions (e.g., sales vs quality, short-term vs long-term incentives) appear as "inevitable tension" rather than systematic conflict
- Strategic concealment masking intent: adversaries hide intentions, capabilities, and strategic direction; analysis based on visible behaviour is systematically wrong

### II. Conflict Triggers & Origins

**Definition:** The sources and triggering conditions that generate conflict between actors. Includes both structural sources (interests, values, or incentives that are incompatible) and triggering events (moments when latent conflict erupts or suppressed opposition surfaces).

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Actor A's preferred outcome is explicitly incompatible with Actor B's preferred outcome (mutual exclusivity)
- Actors value outcomes differently (one prioritizes speed, another prioritizes quality; one risk-seeking, another risk-averse)
- Actors depend on scarce resources and cannot both be fully satisfied (resource scarcity trigger)
- Actors' roles or responsibilities are structured to create conflicting incentives or objectives (structural source)
- Historical grievance or past conflict is remembered and shapes current positioning (past-conflict trigger)
- Actors identify with different groups, and group identity is salient and opposed (identity conflict)
- A sudden change in circumstances or power moves conflict from latent to overt (event trigger)
- Actors' fundamental values about fairness, justice, or legitimacy are opposed (value-based conflict)

**Typical failure mode:**
- Treating conflict as event-driven rather than structural: analysts focus on triggering event and miss the underlying incompatibility that made conflict possible
- Missing resource scarcity as conflict source: conflicts framed as personality clashes or value disputes when actually driven by resource competition
- Ignoring structural conflict built into incentives: system is designed with conflicting objectives (e.g., maximize output and minimize cost) and conflict is inevitable, not optional
- Past grievance suppressed: historical conflict shaped relationships; analysts treat current conflict as novel when it is recurrence or continuation
- Identity conflict dismissed: analysts treat group-based opposition as irrational or attribute it to other causes, missing how identity mobilizes opposition

### III. Escalation Patterns & Dynamics

**Definition:** The mechanisms and patterns through which conflicts intensify, widen, harden, or spread. Includes triggers for escalation, coalition formation, rhetorical hardening, and movement toward coercion or violence.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Conflict boundaries expand: what began as dispute between two actors now involves allies, supporting factions, or wider constituencies
- Positions harden: actors' stated positions become more extreme, less negotiable, and less flexible over time
- Rhetoric intensifies: language moves from disagreement to disrespect to delegitimization to dehumanization
- Actors move from negotiation to threat-making to actual imposition of costs
- Previously neutral parties are drawn into opposition (contagion effect)
- Alliances form to counter perceived threat: actors mobilize supporters to rebalance power
- Conflict becomes linked to other conflicts: unrelated disputes become framed as part of same struggle
- Actors make commitment tactics or sunk-cost moves that make retreat costly: public announcements, irreversible actions, ideological commitment

**Typical failure mode:**
- Treating escalation as inevitable: escalation is contingent on choices and circumstance; appears inevitable only in retrospect
- Missing turning points: specific events or moves that shift conflict from containable to spreading
- Ignoring commitment tactics: actors take positions publicly or incur costs that make backing down humiliating or costly; escalation path becomes harder to reverse
- Underestimating contagion: conflict spreads through networks; allies feel obligated to respond; previously contained dispute becomes wider struggle
- Misunderstanding coalition logic: analysts see allies as unified when actually they have distinct interests; coalition may fracture if core conflict resolves

### IV. Suppression & Concealment Mechanisms

**Definition:** The structures, norms, and strategic choices that keep conflict latent, hidden, or invisible. Includes both institutional suppression (hierarchy preventing voice, norms enforcing silence) and strategic suppression (actors choosing to conceal opposition to maintain advantage or avoid cost).

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Stated positions appear unanimous but private conversations reveal significant disagreement (false consensus)
- Actors avoid raising objections in formal channels; grievances surface only in informal settings or to trusted confidants
- Hierarchy appears to prevent voice: subordinates do not disagree with superiors even when views diverge
- Organizational norms enforce politeness or civility that suppresses open disagreement (civility trap)
- Conflicts are reframed in technical or operational language, hiding underlying interest disagreement
- Information is monopolized: one actor controls what information reaches decision-makers, preventing other viewpoints from surfacing
- Opposition is expressed covertly: non-compliance, foot-dragging, work-to-rule, or sabotage instead of open disagreement
- Actors strategically conceal their true position to gain advantage: feign agreement to gather information, hide strength to surprise, conceal weakness to maintain dignity

**Typical failure mode:**
- Treating false consensus as real consensus: decision appears well-supported until implementation reveals hidden opposition
- Missing suppressed grievance as source of later problems: conflict suppressed by hierarchy emerges as retention crisis, morale failure, or sudden exodus
- Underestimating reframing power: analysts accept the technical frame and miss that underlying conflict remains unresolved in the new frame
- Ignoring performance of consensus: elaborate agreement-showing behaviour can mask deep disagreement; performance itself becomes a form of conflict
- Missing strategic concealment: in negotiation, competition, or conflict contexts, actors deliberately hide information; analysis based on visible information is systematically wrong

### V. Strategic Conflict Behaviour

**Definition:** The strategic choices, positioning moves, and maneuvers actors deploy when in adversarial contexts. Includes coalition building, signaling, capability building, and other moves that actors make to improve their position or likelihood of winning the conflict.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Actors publicly align with supporters or coalition partners to increase perceived power or legitimacy
- Actors make public statements or moves that signal their strength, commitment, or capability (signaling)
- Actors engage in feints, bluffs, or strategic ambiguity: appear stronger or weaker than they are to manipulate opponent's response
- Actors invest resources in building capability, recruiting allies, or securing resources, anticipating conflict escalation
- Actors deliberately conceal their capabilities, intentions, or strategic position to maintain advantage
- Actors take actions with high symbolic or rhetorical value that improve their negotiating position or legitimacy (positioning moves)
- Actors create facts on the ground: make moves that constrain opponent's options or lock in outcomes before formal decisions are made
- Actors move to adjacent arenas: if blocked in one arena, they shift conflict to another domain where they have more leverage

**Typical failure mode:**
- Treating all conflict behaviour as irrational or emotional: much conflict behaviour is strategically rational given adversarial conditions
- Missing signaling effects: actors' public moves are partly about signaling strength or commitment to allies and opponents, not just direct impact
- Underestimating capability building: actors' behaviour that appears to be preparing for escalation is actually strategic positioning for better negotiation outcome
- Ignoring bluff and counterbluff: actors may deliberately misrepresent strength; analysis based on apparent strength is wrong if strength is intentionally misrepresented
- Missing positioning for leverage: actors' moves appear suboptimal until understood as positioning for better negotiating leverage or baseline

### VI. Conflict Resolution & Transformation

**Definition:** The mechanisms and pathways through which conflicts end, transform, resolve, or become institutionalized. Includes negotiated settlements, power shifts that enable resolution, structural changes, and conflict transformation into cooperation or endemic opposition.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Actors explicitly negotiate or agree to terms that resolve or contain conflict
- External pressure or power shift changes the conditions that sustained conflict (e.g., third-party intervention, new leadership, resource availability)
- Structural change addresses the incompatibility or misalignment that generated conflict (e.g., separated responsibilities, aligned incentives)
- Conflict is absorbed into institutions: formal processes and rules now govern what was previously contested (institutionalization)
- Conflicting parties find superordinate goal that unites them despite underlying disagreement
- Conflict transforms from opposition to cooperation or coexistence without being fully resolved
- Conflict becomes latent again: parties stop contesting but underlying incompatibility remains
- Conflict recurs on schedule or when triggering conditions reappear (cyclical pattern)

**Typical failure mode:**
- Treating resolution as permanent: conflicts can recur when conditions change or new actors arrive
- Ignoring structural sources in resolution attempts: address surface conflict without changing structural incompatibilities; conflict will resurface
- Underestimating power shift requirements: weak actor has incentive to avoid resolution that locks in subordinate position; conflict may intensify instead
- Missing institutionalization process: conflict becomes less visible not because it is resolved but because it is absorbed into formal rules and procedures
- Assuming cooperation requires full resolution: actors can cooperate despite unresolved underlying conflict if structure incentivizes cooperation and constrains contestation

# 4. Five Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Conflict

Every conflict instance can be profiled along these five dimensions. Dimensions enable rapid conflict characterization and comparison across different scenarios.

## 4.1 Intensity Dimension
*How severe, forceful, or resource-consuming is the conflict?*

**Low intensity:** Disagreement exists but is manageable; actors express views without force or threat; conflict has limited impact on operations or relationships.

**Medium intensity:** Disagreement is expressed openly; actors take positions and mobilize resources; conflict affects decision-making and coordination; some implementation impact.

**High intensity:** Conflict consumes significant resources; actors mobilize extensively; escalation is evident or imminent; threat, coercion, or violence is used or threatened; conflict dominates decision landscape.

**Hidden intensity risk:** Low-visibility conflict (latent, reframed, suppressed) may be high-intensity underneath invisibility; surface calm masks severe underlying opposition. Surfacing such conflict can appear to increase intensity when it merely makes existing intensity visible.

## 4.2 Visibility Dimension
*Is the conflict overt, latent, or strategically concealed?*

**Fully visible:** Conflict is openly acknowledged; opposing positions are explicit; actors are known to be in contestation.

**Partially visible:** Conflict is acknowledged in some contexts or by some actors but concealed in others; false consensus in formal settings with real opposition in informal settings; conflict is reframed in some contexts.

**Latent/suppressed:** Conflict exists but is not surfaced; incompatible interests are not yet acknowledged; suppression mechanisms are active; conflict shapes behaviour covertly.

**Hidden by strategic concealment:** Actors deliberately conceal opposition or intentions; information asymmetry is strategic; true positions are hidden; intentions are disguised.

**Visibility tracking:** Track how visibility shifts over time. Conflict may move from latent to visible (surfacing event), from visible to suppressed (pressure or norm enforcement), or from suppressed to explosive (breakdown of suppression). Understanding visibility transitions is key to prediction.

## 4.3 Structural Dimension
*Is the conflict dyadic (two-party), multiparty, or embedded in system design?*

**Dyadic conflict:** Dispute between two actors; interests or positions are directly opposed; bilateral contestation.

**Multiparty conflict:** Multiple actors are involved; coalition dynamics are present; interests may not be simply two-sided; contagion and spillover are possible.

**Structural conflict:** Conflict is embedded in system design, role definitions, or incentive structures; appears independent of individual actors; persists across personnel changes.

**Coalition-mediated:** Dyadic conflict operates through coalition structures; primary opponents do not directly contest; allies are mobilized; actual antagonism may be between coalitions rather than individuals.

**Structural complexity:** Conflicts can be nested: dyadic conflict within multiparty context within structural incompatibility. Understanding which level is primary helps target intervention.

## 4.4 Stakes Dimension
*What is at stake in the conflict? What is the cost of losing?*

**Low stakes:** Disagreement is about preferences or minor resource allocation; cost of losing is limited; impact on core interests is minimal.

**Medium stakes:** Disagreement affects significant resources, authority, or outcomes; cost of losing is material; core interests are partially engaged.

**High stakes:** Disagreement is about core interests, identity, legitimacy, or survival; cost of losing is severe; actors are willing to escalate significantly to avoid losing.

**Existential stakes:** Conflict is framed as existential threat (to identity, legitimacy, or survival); stakes are perceived as unlimited; escalation risk is very high; de-escalation is extremely difficult.

**Stakes perception:** Actual stakes and perceived stakes may diverge. Actors may treat low-stakes dispute as existential if framing creates perception of threat. Conflict intensity often follows perceived stakes rather than objective stakes.

## 4.5 Reversibility Dimension
*Can the conflict be resolved, or is it structural and recurring?*

**Reversible:** Conflict stems from specific circumstance, misalignment, or choice; resolution is possible through agreement, structural change, or power shift; underlying incompatibility can be addressed.

**Path-dependent:** Conflict history shapes current opposition; past grievance creates lasting animosity; actors' positions have locked in through commitment tactics; reversal requires undoing past choices (often very costly).

**Structural & recurring:** Conflict is built into system design; it will recur as long as structural source exists; resolution of one instance does not prevent future recurrence; structural change is necessary for permanent resolution.

**Identity-embedded:** Conflict is tied to group identity, ideology, or core values; opposition has become part of actors' self-definition; reversal requires identity shift, which is extremely difficult.

# 5. Eight Dynamic Patterns of Conflict Over Time

Conflicts follow patterns as they emerge, escalate, suppress, resolve, or recur. Recognizing these patterns enables prediction and intervention design.

## 5.1 Emergence & Escalation

**Trigger-based emergence:** Latent conflict erupts when triggering event surfaces it. Event may be new incompatibility, power shift, threshold crossing, or breakdown of suppression mechanism. Once surfaced, escalation dynamics take over.

**Gradual intensification:** Conflict does not erupt suddenly but escalates gradually as actors' positions harden, stakes increase, and coalitions mobilize. Early intervention is easier but is often missed because escalation appears gradual and manageable.

**Threshold crossing:** At some point, conflict intensity or visibility crosses threshold into new regime (e.g., from discussion to threat, from latent to overt). Prediction of threshold crossing is difficult but crossing it changes dynamics fundamentally.

## 5.2 Escalation Patterns

**Tit-for-tat escalation:** Each side responds to opponent's move with similar or greater move; escalation ladder is climbed rung by rung. De-escalation requires breaking the cycle by not responding in kind.

**Coalition-driven escalation:** As coalitions mobilize, conflict intensity increases due to pressure from coalition members and competition for leadership within coalition. Coalition dynamics can drive escalation even if primary combatants would prefer containment.

**Rhetorical escalation outpacing material escalation:** Language and symbolic contestation escalate faster than actual resource commitment or force. Rhetorical escalation can lock in positions and make retreat costly even if material stakes are lower.

## 5.3 Suppression & Latency Patterns

**Suppression by force:** Hierarchy or power suppresses conflict through threat, discipline, or removal of opposing voices. Suppression is stable only if power difference is maintained and cost of suppression is acceptable.

**Suppression by norm:** Social or organizational norms suppress open disagreement. Suppression is maintained by peer pressure, reputation concerns, or internalized norms. Suppression is more sustainable than force-based suppression but can suddenly break if norm is perceived as unjust.

**Suppression by reframing:** Conflict is reframed as technical issue or operational problem. Suppression is maintained as long as actors accept the reframe. If underlying conflict remains unresolved in the new frame, it will resurface.

## 5.4 Suppression-Surfacing Transitions

**Triggering events:** Crisis, external pressure, leadership change, or resource availability change can break suppression. Latent conflict erupts suddenly when suppression mechanism fails.

**Norm erosion:** Suppressive norms erode as new cohorts enter (with different norms), as external examples challenge norms, or as perceived injustice of norm grows. Erosion is gradual but can suddenly enable open contestation.

**Threshold of pain:** As costs of suppression (stress, turnover, health, productivity) accumulate, actors' willingness to maintain suppression erodes. Conflict may surface suddenly when pain threshold is reached.

## 5.5 Resolution Patterns

**Negotiated resolution:** Parties reach agreement through negotiation. Resolution is stable if underlying incompatibility is resolved or if new structure prevents re-emergence of conflict.

**Power-driven resolution:** One party achieves overwhelming power and enforces submission of other. Resolution is stable only if power distribution is maintained.

**Exhaustion-based resolution:** Parties run out of resources or will to continue. Resolution may be unstable if resources become available again or will is renewed.

**Structural resolution:** System design is changed to address incompatibility. Resolution is stable if new structure adequately addresses root causes.

## 5.6 Recurrence Patterns

**Cycling:** Conflict emerges, escalates, suppresses, re-emerges in cyclical pattern. Cycle is often tied to external triggers (leadership changes, resource cycles, anniversary of past events).

**Dormancy and reactivation:** Conflict is suppressed or seemingly resolved but remains latent. New trigger (change in circumstances, personnel, or power) reactivates it.

**Transformation without resolution:** Conflict changes form (overt to latent, dyadic to multiparty, interest-based to identity-based) without being resolved. Transformed conflict may be more difficult to address.

## 5.7 De-escalation Patterns

**Communication and trust-building:** Increased communication, exchange of information, and trust-building activities can reduce escalation. Effectiveness depends on actors' willingness to be vulnerable and mutual commitment to de-escalation.

**Third-party mediation:** External mediator can facilitate negotiation, reframe dispute, or create space for resolution. Effectiveness depends on mediator's credibility and acceptance by parties.

**Structural change:** Changing structure, roles, or incentives can de-escalate conflict by addressing root causes. De-escalation through structural change is often more stable than through agreement or mediation.

## 5.8 Conflict Transformation

**Identity transformation:** Actors shift identity to include opponent or larger group. Transformation enables cooperation despite ongoing disagreement.

**Superordinate goal achievement:** Parties achieve shared goal that unites them. Transformation may be temporary if shared goal is temporary.

**Institutionalization:** Conflict is absorbed into formal institutions that govern contestation. Institutionalization reduces visibility but does not eliminate conflict.

# 6. Interface Analysis: Hidden, Latent, and Masked Conflict

## 6.1 Interface A: Fully Suppressed Conflict

**Characteristics:** Conflict is not visible in any formal channel; actors do not acknowledge opposition; suppression mechanisms are strong and maintained; surface appears as unity or absence of conflict.

**Where hidden:** Opposition is hidden from decision-makers, from external observers, from formal records, and often from the actors themselves (internalized suppression).

**Typical suppression mechanisms:** Hierarchy prevents voice; norms enforce silence; fear of consequences suppresses expression; reframing disguises conflict as operational issue; information monopoly prevents opposing views from reaching decision-makers.

**Visibility markers (indicators that hidden conflict exists):**
- Behaviour-statement gaps: actors state agreement but actions diverge from stated position
- Implementation failure despite apparent support: decisions look well-supported until implementation reveals covert non-compliance
- Retention/morale problems: turnover, stress, health issues, low engagement indicate suppressed grievance
- Informal network activity: opposition surfaces in informal channels, break rooms, private conversations, but not in formal settings
- Performance gaps: teams underperform relative to apparent capability or motivation
- Sabotage indicators: work is done slowly, incompletely, or in ways that undermine stated objectives
- Whistleblowing or exit: suppressed actors eventually speak to external parties or leave organization

**De-escalation approach:** Provide safe channels for expression; reduce fear of consequences; legitimize disagreement; break suppression mechanisms carefully (sudden surfacing of suppressed conflict can cause organizational shock).

## 6.2 Interface B: Partially Visible Conflict

**Characteristics:** Conflict is visible in some contexts but suppressed in others; false consensus in formal settings with real disagreement in informal settings; reframing in one domain with open contestation in another.

**Where hidden:** Opposition is visible to insiders (informal networks, peer groups) but hidden from formal decision-making; conflict is visible to outsiders (in complaints, turnover, public criticism) but denied internally.

**Typical mechanisms:** Different norms in different contexts; formal channels suppress while informal channels allow; reframing in formal settings while real conflict continues in background; strategic actors maintain false consensus in formal settings while opposing informally.

**Visibility markers:**
- Dual communication patterns: formal agreement with informal opposition
- Selective visibility: conflict visible to some groups, hidden from others
- Channel differences: conflict visible in external complaints but hidden in internal communication
- Time differences: conflict visible in longer timeframes (turnover, productivity trends) but not in single meetings

**De-escalation approach:** Integrate informal and formal channels; create settings where hidden disagreement can be expressed safely; acknowledge and validate the conflict that is partially visible.

## 6.3 Interface C: Overt but Reframed Conflict

**Characteristics:** Conflict is openly contested but in reframed domain. Interest conflict is being fought as technical disagreement; value conflict is being argued as factual dispute. Underlying conflict remains unresolved.

**Where hidden:** The actual conflict (interest incompatibility or value difference) is hidden beneath the technical or factual debate. Analysts accept the surface frame and miss the underlying contestation.

**Typical mechanisms:** Reframing moves dispute to domain where "objective" resolution appears possible; technical experts dominate discussion and legitimacy is granted to technical framing; political conflict is depoliticized through technical language.

**Visibility markers:**
- Technical debate becomes intractable: gathering more data does not resolve debate because debate is actually about interests, not facts
- Debate persists despite evidence: evidence that should resolve technical question does not because question is actually value-based
- Resolution in technical domain does not stick: technical solution is implemented but underlying conflict continues or sabotages technical solution
- Multiple sequential reframings: when one technical frame is resolved, conflict emerges in different frame
- Experts disagree systematically: expert disagreement follows interest/value lines rather than genuine epistemic lines

**De-escalation approach:** Reframe the reframe; surface the underlying interest or value conflict; address the actual conflict rather than the technical proxy; create space for value or interest disagreement to be discussed openly.

# 7. Cross-Framework Integration: Conflict's Interfaces with 36 Frameworks

The Framework of Conflict integrates with all 36 frameworks in the Analytical Series. This section maps integration points and specifies how conflict analysis shapes, informs, and depends on analysis in other frameworks.

## 7.1 Integration with Power, Interests, Incentives, Agents, Relations

**Power (Framework of Power):** Power maps capacity to shape outcomes; Conflict maps relational dynamics of opposition given that capacity distribution. Power asymmetries enable or constrain conflict: overwhelming power enables suppression, balanced power may lead to symmetric conflict, extreme power imbalance may enable latent conflict and hidden opposition. When conducting Conflict analysis, use Power framework to understand how capacity distribution shapes conflict dynamics and what power shifts would enable resolution.

**Interests (Framework of Interests):** Interests identifies what actors want; Conflict analyses what happens when wants collide. Conduct Interests analysis first to identify potential incompatibilities. Use Conflict framework to determine whether identified incompatibility is generating active or latent opposition, and what strategic behaviour results.

**Incentives (Framework of Incentives):** Incentives maps reward structures; Conflict analyses adversarial behaviour when incentives are misaligned. Misaligned or zero-sum incentives create conditions for conflict emergence. Use Conflict framework to analyse how actors respond to misaligned incentives: do they contest, withdraw, accept, or form coalitions? Do they strategically manipulate incentive structures?

**Agents (Framework of Agents):** Agents identifies who acts; Conflict identifies how agents position against one another. Conduct Agents analysis to identify key actors. Use Conflict framework to map opposition patterns, coalition structures, and strategic positioning between identified agents.

**Relations (Framework of Relations):** Relations maps relationship quality and trust; Conflict analyses opposition and contestation within relationships. Relationships shaped by past conflict may be low-trust and high-opposition. Use Conflict framework to analyse adversarial dynamics in relationships and what would restore cooperation.

## 7.2 Integration with Values, Beliefs, Cognition, Emotions

**Values (Framework of Values):** Values identifies what actors care about; Conflict analyses what happens when values are opposed. Value-based conflicts are among the most resistant to resolution because values are non-negotiable. When conducting Conflict analysis, identify whether conflict is value-based and if so, design interventions accordingly (de-escalation is harder; structural change or superordinate goals are more effective).

**Beliefs (Framework of Beliefs):** Beliefs maps what actors hold true; Conflict analyses contestation when beliefs differ about crucial matters. Belief conflict can be epistemic (factual disagreement) or motivated (actors defending beliefs for psychological reasons). Use Conflict framework to distinguish genuine epistemic disagreement from conflict reframed as belief dispute.

**Cognition (Framework of Cognition):** Cognition analyses mental processes and cognitive biases; Conflict analyses how cognitive differences can generate or mask conflict. Use Cognition framework to understand how different mental models, expertise, or cognitive blind spots contribute to conflict. Use Conflict framework to map strategic behaviour in contexts where cognitive differences exist.

**Emotions (Framework of Emotions):** Emotions analyses affective drivers; Conflict analyses strategic behaviour in adversarial contexts. Emotions shape conflict intensity and escalation, but Conflict framework focuses on strategic logic rather than affective drivers. Use Emotions framework to understand what emotions are driving conflict behaviour; use Conflict framework to analyse strategic positioning.

## 7.3 Integration with Knowledge, Evidence, Causation

**Knowledge (Framework of Knowledge):** Knowledge maps what is known and distributed; Conflict analyses information asymmetry in adversarial contexts. Strategic concealment in conflict creates knowledge gaps. Use Conflict framework to surface what knowledge is deliberately hidden and why. Use Knowledge framework to map what different actors know and don't know.

**Evidence (Framework of Evidence):** Evidence analyses evidentiary strength and status; Conflict analyses how evidence is deployed strategically in disputes. Actors in conflict may selectively present evidence, challenge evidence standards, or engage in information warfare. Use Conflict framework to surface strategic evidence deployment and use Evidence framework to assess evidentiary strength independent of strategic positioning.

**Causation (Framework of Causation):** Causation maps causal claims and causal reasoning; Conflict analyses contestation about causation when causal attributions are disputed. Actors may blame opponent for outcomes or deny causal responsibility. Use Conflict framework to surface causal disputes; use Causation framework to assess causal claims independently.

## 7.4 Integration with Boundaries, Systems, Institutions

**Boundaries (Framework of Boundaries):** Boundaries identifies system boundaries and compartmentalization; Conflict analyses contestation about boundaries (who belongs, what is included). Boundary conflicts are often intense because they determine membership and inclusion. Use Conflict framework to analyse boundary disputes and use Boundaries framework to map system architecture.

**Systems (Framework of Systems):** Systems analyzes system-level dynamics and feedback loops; Conflict analyses opposition between system actors. Use Systems framework to understand system-level effects of conflict (destabilization, cascade failures). Use Conflict framework to surface opposition within system that systems analysis might miss.

**Institutions (Framework of Institutions):** Institutions maps formal rules and norms; Conflict analyses contestation about institutions and absorption of conflict into institutional structures. Actors contest institutional rules; conflict is absorbed into formal processes. Use Conflict framework to surface opposition to institutional structure; use Institutions framework to map how institutions shape conflict behaviour.

## 7.5 Integration with Governance, Decisions, Interventions

**Governance (Framework of Governance):** Governance maps formal authority and decision structures; Conflict analyses contestation about governance and opposition within governance. Use Governance framework to map decision rights; use Conflict framework to surface opposition to governance structure and contestation within formal authority.

**Decisions (Framework of Decisions):** Decisions analyzes decision-making and choice; Conflict analyses how conflict shapes decisions and how decisions either resolve or exacerbate conflict. Use Decisions framework to understand how conflicts are being decided; use Conflict framework to surface hidden opposition affecting decision outcomes.

**Interventions (Framework of Interventions):** Interventions maps change options and intervention design; Conflict analysis informs intervention design by identifying what conflict resolution, de-escalation, or structural change is needed. Use Conflict framework to diagnose conflict; use Interventions framework to design change responses.

## 7.6 Integration with Time, Risk, Uncertainties

**Time (Framework of Time):** Time analyzes temporal dynamics and temporal context; Conflict analyzes conflict stages and how conflict evolves over time. Use Time framework to map historical context of conflict; use Conflict framework to analyse conflict trajectories and stage transitions.

**Risk (Framework of Risk):** Risk analyzes threat and vulnerability; Conflict analyzes strategic intent and adversarial threats. In adversarial contexts, risk assessment must account for opponent's strategic choices. Use Risk framework to map vulnerability; use Conflict framework to analyse whether vulnerabilities are being strategically exploited.

**Uncertainties (Framework of Uncertainties):** Uncertainties maps ignorance and unknowns; Conflict analysis surfaces what is uncertain due to strategic concealment. In conflict contexts, information asymmetry is often strategic. Use Uncertainties framework to map what is unknown; use Conflict framework to analyse whether unknowns are being deliberately created through strategic concealment.

## 7.7 Integration with Hiddens, Evidence, Diagnosis

**Hiddens (Framework of Hiddens):** Hiddens maps visibility failures; Conflict analysis is inherently hiddens-focused because conflict suppression and reframing create visibility failures. Conflict framework specializes in hiddens generated through opposition, contestation, and strategic concealment. Use Hiddens framework as meta-framework; use Conflict framework as specialized tool for analyzing conflict-based hiddens.

**Diagnosis (Framework of Diagnosis):** Diagnosis analyzes causal accounts and diagnostic reasoning; Conflict analysis contributes to diagnosis by surfacing what conflict is hiding about true causes. A conflict may be masking a deeper systemic problem or structural failure. Use Diagnosis framework to understand what Conflict analysis reveals about root causes.

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Hiddens Cross-Check

## 8.1 Hiddens Tier 1: Six-Category Visibility Audit

**Category I (Structural Hiddens in Conflict):** What structural incompatibilities or system-design conflicts are invisible because they appear natural? Diagnostic: observe whether same disputes recur across different personnel or contexts. Intervention: expose structural source; treat as system design problem rather than personnel issue.

**Category II (Epistemic Hiddens in Conflict):** What opposition is hidden because it lacks language or framework for expression? Diagnostic: interview across multiple contexts; look for behaviour-statement gaps. Intervention: create frameworks and language that allow opposition to be articulated safely.

**Category III (Strategic Hiddens in Conflict):** What opposition is deliberately concealed in adversarial contexts through information control, misdirection, or deception? Diagnostic: track information asymmetries; look for deliberate opacity. Intervention: increase transparency and communication; reduce information asymmetry where possible.

**Category IV (Normative Hiddens in Conflict):** What opposition is suppressed by norms, values, or beliefs about what is proper or acceptable? Diagnostic: identify suppression norms; look for informal expression of what is formally forbidden. Intervention: legitimize disagreement; reduce norm-based suppression through norm change.

**Category V (Power Hiddens in Conflict):** What opposition is suppressed through power imbalance? Diagnostic: check for behaviour-statement gaps; look for hierarchy-prevented voice. Intervention: create channels for safe expression; reduce fear of consequences; balance power where possible.

**Category VI (Temporal Hiddens in Conflict):** What opposition is hidden because it is latent (not yet surfaced), past (historical), or future (prospective)? Diagnostic: conduct historical analysis; look for triggering conditions for surfacing. Intervention: surface latent conflict before it erupts; address historical grievance to prevent recurrence.

## 8.2 Hiddens Tier 2: Structured Deepening

**Latent Conflict Deepening:** Once latent conflict is suspected, conduct targeted deepening:
- Map interest incompatibilities that are not yet acknowledged
- Identify suppression mechanisms keeping conflict latent
- Identify triggering conditions that would surface conflict
- Assess cost of surfacing vs. cost of continued suppression
- Develop intervention plan for managing surfacing if necessary

**Suppression Mechanism Deepening:** Once suppression is suspected, deepen analysis:
- Map what opposition is being suppressed and by whom
- Identify mechanisms of suppression (hierarchy, norms, fear, reframing)
- Assess stability of suppression (how long can it be maintained?)
- Identify indicators of suppression breakdown
- Develop plans for managing conflict if suppression breaks

**Strategic Concealment Deepening:** Once strategic concealment is suspected, deepen analysis:
- Map what information is being concealed and why
- Identify who has information advantage and from whom they are hiding
- Assess whether concealment is sustainable or will eventually surface
- Identify triggers that would expose concealment
- Develop plans for information disclosure or negotiation given information asymmetry

**Reframing Detection Deepening:** Once reframing is suspected, deepen analysis:
- Surface the reframed underlying conflict
- Assess whether technical resolution will actually resolve underlying conflict
- Identify whether technical solution will be sabotaged by unresolved underlying conflict
- Plan for addressing underlying conflict, not just technical proxy
- Monitor whether conflict resurfaces after technical solution

## 8.3 Hiddens Tier 3: Full-Spectrum Escalation

**Full Conflict Surfacing:** When latent conflict is surfaced or when suppressed conflict breaks, conduct full analysis:
- Map all parties to the conflict and their interests
- Identify all suppression mechanisms and what is still being hidden
- Conduct full coalition and alignment analysis
- Assess escalation trajectory and risk of spiral
- Develop comprehensive de-escalation or resolution strategy

**Power Dynamics Escalation:** When power becomes central to conflict (suppression is power-based, escalation involves power plays):
- Map power distribution and power asymmetries
- Identify power-shift opportunities and risks
- Assess whether power-based suppression can be maintained or will break
- Develop alternative resolution paths if power shifts

**Conflict Transformation Analysis:** When conflict appears embedded in identity, values, or institutions:
- Assess whether conflict can be resolved or must be transformed
- Map transformation pathways (superordinate goals, identity shifts, institutionalization)
- Assess likelihood and cost of transformation
- Develop plans for enabling transformation if necessary

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (early pass; assume Hiddens exist) (OG §7.1).
2) Shortlist candidate Hiddens; for each, rate: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence (OG §7.2).
3) Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (OG §7.2).
4) Run the hiddens source analysis and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens.
5) If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3).
6) After executing the framework protocol and any available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas.
7) Produce Residual Unknowns (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or remediation priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)
Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- High consequence (safety, regulatory, existential, severe harm potential)
- Adversarial context / strategic concealment likely
- Persistent disagreement or inconsistent evidence
- Repeat failures despite prior interventions
- Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement across hidden types
- Decision-critical residual unknowns

Tier 3 execution (per OG §D.6.10):
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into the full Hiddens taxonomy (full-spectrum exploration).
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements, more explicit competing hypotheses, more rigorous disconfirming tests.
- When adversarial intent is plausible: adds concealment vectors and independent corroboration paths.
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree branch A6 (OG §4.3).

# 9. Five Dimensions Summary Table

For quick reference, conflict instances can be profiled on these five dimensions:

| Dimension | Low | Medium | High | Monitoring question |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Intensity** | Manageable disagreement | Open contestation with resource commitment | Severe opposition consuming resources; threat/coercion | How much resource commitment and urgency? |
| **Visibility** | Fully overt, acknowledged | Partially visible; false consensus in formal settings | Fully suppressed or strategically hidden | Is opposition visible or concealed? |
| **Structure** | Dyadic, bilateral | Multiparty with coalitions | Structural incompatibility persisting across personnel | Is conflict bilateral or systemic? |
| **Stakes** | Low-consequence preference | Material resource/authority at stake | Existential threat or core identity | What is at stake? What is cost of losing? |
| **Reversibility** | Resolvable through agreement | Path-dependent; reversal costly | Structural and recurring; requires systemic change | Can this conflict be resolved or is it structural? |

# 10. Practical Application Protocol

## 10.1 Light Depth Conflict Execution (Rapid Run Mode)

**When:** Conflict is present but not central to investigation; routing from OG §4.3 does not escalate to Full Depth; time/resource constraints are tight.

**Steps:**
1. Identify conflicting actors and their stated positions (Agents framework context)
2. Classify conflict type using meta-categories I–VI (identify which type applies)
3. Profile on five dimensions (intensity, visibility, structure, stakes, reversibility)
4. Identify visible suppression mechanisms or reframing (if any)
5. Register any obvious hidden opposition (behaviour-statement gaps, retention problems, informal resistance)
6. Specify routing question: Does this conflict material to outcomes or require escalation?

**Output:** One-paragraph Conflict Summary including type, visibility, stakes, and key hidden opposition.

## 10.2 Full Depth Conflict Execution (Investigative Run Mode)

**When:** Conflict is material to outcomes; suppression or latent conflict is suspected; strategic concealment is likely; resolution is a stated objective; routing from OG §4.3 escalates to Full Depth.

**Steps:**
1. Complete Light Depth analysis as above
2. Conduct Tier 1 visibility audit (Hiddens framework §8.1): map what is hidden across six categories
3. Conduct Tier 2 deepening (§8.2): focused analysis of latent/suppressed/concealed elements
4. Map conflict stage and trajectory: Where in emergence-escalation-resolution cycle is conflict?
5. Conduct coalition and alignment analysis: Who is with whom? On what basis? How stable are coalitions?
6. Strategic intent analysis: What is each actor trying to achieve? What are they willing to do?
7. Conduct Tier 3 escalation if needed: Full conflict surfacing, power dynamics, transformation analysis
8. Develop de-escalation or resolution strategy: What would reduce conflict? What structural change would address root causes?

**Output:** Comprehensive Conflict Analysis document including all elements above; Hiddens register; strategic intent analysis; de-escalation/resolution recommendations.

# 11. De-Escalation and Conflict Resolution Framework

## 11.1 De-Escalation Strategies by Conflict Type

**For Overt Disputes:** Communication, mediation, negotiation. Focus on creating shared understanding and finding overlapping interests. Risk: escalation during negotiation if actors use negotiation arena for contestation.

**For Latent Conflicts:** Surface conflict safely; provide voice channels; address underlying incompatibility. Risk: surfacing conflict can cause organizational shock; manage carefully.

**For Structural Conflicts:** Redesign roles, responsibilities, or incentives to eliminate incompatibility. Requires change resistance management. Risk: structural change is costly and resisted.

**For False Consensus:** Legitimize disagreement; create safe channels for expression; reduce fear of consequences. Risk: surfacing hidden opposition can fragment apparent unity.

**For Strategic Concealment:** Increase transparency; build trust; create incentives for honesty. Particularly difficult in adversarial contexts where concealment is rational.

**For Suppressed Conflict:** Reduce power imbalance; change suppression norms; provide safe expression channels. Requires sustained commitment to norm/structure change.

## 11.2 Intervention Design Framework

Match intervention approach to conflict type and stage:

- **Negotiation:** Effective for overt disputes where parties are willing to talk. Create neutral ground; use mediator if needed; focus on interests not positions.
- **Structural change:** Effective for structural conflict and misaligned incentives. Redesign roles, responsibilities, communication structures. Manage resistance to change.
- **Power shift:** When power imbalance is sustaining conflict, rebalancing power can enable resolution. Difficult to engineer; often requires external intervention.
- **Superordinate goals:** When parties can identify shared goals or higher purposes, may enable cooperation despite disagreement. Requires finding genuine shared interest.
- **Institutionalization:** Absorb conflict into formal procedures and rules. Reduces visibility and intensity but does not eliminate conflict.
- **Transformation:** Enable identity shifts or value reframing that changes conflict dynamics. Very difficult; requires long-term commitment.

# 12. Conclusion

The Framework of Conflict addresses contestation, opposition, and adversarial dynamics between actors. It maps what conflict hides about true intentions, suppressed opposition, coalition formation, and strategic positioning. Conflict framework complements Interests (what actors want), Power (who has capacity), Incentives (reward structures), and Agents (who acts) by focusing specifically on the relational dynamics of opposition.

Conflict analysis surfaces four categories of hiddens: latent conflict suppressed by norms or hierarchy, conflict reframed as technical disagreement, false consensus masking hidden opposition, and strategic concealment in adversarial contexts. These hiddens have distinctive analytical signatures and require targeted deepening to surface.

The thirty-type taxonomy, five-dimension profile, eight dynamic patterns, and three-interface analysis model (fully suppressed / partially visible / overt but reframed) provide systematic tools for conflict diagnosis. Cross-framework integration with all 36 frameworks enables conflict analysis to inform and be informed by comprehensive scenario analysis.

Conflict analysis is essential to scenario diagnosis when actors have incompatible interests, when suppressed opposition is suspected, when coalitions are forming, when strategic behaviour is evident, or when conflict reframing is likely. Light Depth analysis identifies conflict type and visibility; Full Depth analysis surfaces hidden opposition and designs de-escalation or resolution intervention.
