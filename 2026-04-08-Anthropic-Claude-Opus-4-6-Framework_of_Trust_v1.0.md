# Introduction

## What is Trust?

Trust is the extension of confidence to other actors, institutions, or information sources, coupled with willingness to be vulnerable to their actions or knowledge claims. Trust is not blind faith; it is grounded in assessments of competence, integrity, reliability, and shared interest. When A trusts B, A depends on B's actions, judgement, or information despite lacking complete visibility or control over B's choices. Trust creates capacity for delegation, cooperation, and information sharing that would otherwise be impossible.

Trust operates at three distinct registers: interpersonal (between individuals), institutional (toward organisations, systems, and rules), and epistemic (toward knowledge sources and expertise). Interpersonal trust depends on direct relationships, repeated interaction, and signals of competence and intent. Institutional trust depends on structural safeguards, transparency, consistency, and institutional reputation. Epistemic trust depends on source credibility, domain expertise, track record, and independence from conflicts of interest. These registers interact: weak institutional trust makes interpersonal trust more reliant on individual relationships; weak epistemic trust fragments communities into information silos; absent interpersonal trust can doom institutional initiatives even when structure is sound.

Trust is analytically distinct from, but operationally intertwined with, legitimacy, power, and incentives. An authority can be legitimate (recognised as rightfully holding power) without being trusted (confidence in its actions being competent or aligned with my interests). Trust does not require formal authority; informal trust networks often exceed institutional reach. Trust can persist when incentives would not support cooperation; trust can be destroyed by making incentive structures too explicit (shifting from "we cooperate because we trust each other" to "you benefit if you cooperate"—the shift signals distrust and can dissolve the relationship).

## Why does Trust matter for Hiddens work?

Trust asymmetries create invisible structural dependencies that shape information flow, decision-making authority, and delegation patterns. When A trusts B but B does not trust A, A shares information that B filters or withholds, creating hidden influence and distorted communication. Upward filtering in hierarchies—where subordinates withhold bad news to protect the trust of superiors—hides accumulating problems until crisis breaks the system.

Misplaced trust conceals actual risks. An organisation trusts a vendor, supplier, or leader despite insufficient evidence of competence, and the gap between apparent and actual trustworthiness remains hidden until failure. Trust halo effects (reputation for one competence domain generalises to unrelated domains) hide overreach and incompetence. Epistemic trust chains—where information is trusted because its source is trusted, without independent evaluation of reliability—hide the question of whether that source is actually reliable. Information spreads credibly but false; the hidden mechanism is trust transference.

Trust deficits suppress information sharing. Where distrust exists between parties who would both benefit from sharing, the distrust becomes the hiding mechanism. Community members do not report safety concerns to institutions they distrust; institutional knowledge of danger remains hidden. Trust breakdown triggers information fragmentation where multiple incompatible narratives emerge because no shared source is trusted; reality becomes contested.

Institutional trust substitution hides performance gaps behind reputational halo. An institution trusted for past performance is assumed trustworthy in new domains; the assumption remains unexamined. Historical trust erodes slowly; institutions operate in a grace period where trust exceeds earned competence. Crisis reveals the gap, often lethally.

This framework integrates trust discovery directly into visibility analysis through tiered scans (Tier 1 across all six Hiddens categories, Tier 2 deepening of top candidates, Tier 3 escalation for high-stakes asymmetries) and targeted scans for trust-specific hiding mechanisms (trust asymmetry chains, epistemic cascades, trust substitution, betrayal amnesia). Early warning signals are often visible in Hiddens signals: asymmetric information access, selective disclosure, escalating secrecy, unexpected defection, and communication breakdown.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework produces a multipart trust map. First, a **types taxonomy** across six meta-categories (Interpersonal Trust Foundations, Institutional Competence Trust, Epistemic Trust Mechanisms, Behavioral Trust Signals, Vulnerability & Asymmetry Dynamics, Trust Crisis & Recovery) with thirty operationally distinct types, enabling practitioners to diagnose which trust bases are relied upon, which are fragile, and where breakdown is imminent. Second, a **five-dimensional profile** (trust basis mix, relationship asymmetry, epistemic access, temporal character, recovery potential) that summarises trust's structural properties in any case. Third, a **dynamics and interface audit** that identifies patterns of accumulation, erosion, betrayal, repair, and cascading breakdown; maps trust failures to three key interfaces where visibility and intervention concentrate (formal institution, direct relationship, information environment); and assigns remediation actions.

Fourth, the framework produces a **tiered Hiddens cross-check** (Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens categories with detection checks; Tier 2 mapping of top 10–15 Hiddens candidates to mechanisms, detectability, agency, consequence, and remediation interfaces; Tier 3 escalation rule for high-stakes asymmetries). Fifth, it produces a **trust intervention portfolio** addressing trust gaps directly, upgrading transparency and competence visibility, rebuilding after betrayal, managing asymmetries, and detecting early breakdown signals. Finally, it produces a **monitoring dashboard** with leading indicators (information asymmetry, communication patterns, defection signals, competence feedback, relationship sentiment) and escalation triggers, plus a **closure note** with residual unknowns, acceptability rationale, ownership, monitoring plan, and learning hook.

## How to use this framework

This framework is invoked when trust is fragile or asymmetric, when delegation is failing due to confidence gaps, when relationships are strained, when institutions are suffering trust deficits, or when investigations need to understand why cooperation or information sharing broke down. It is best suited to Investigative Run Mode for complex or high-stakes analysis; Rapid Run Mode for triage and early-stage diagnosis. The framework is routinely paired with Legitimacy (G4), Relationships (G1), Power (G3), Incentives (G3), Hiddens (G5), and Communications (G4).

Default execution depth is Light Depth Framework Execution (run the meta-category scan, dimension profile, and a short Tier 1 Hiddens check). Full Depth Framework Execution is triggered when routing decision logic at OG §4.3 indicates high consequence, weak/contested evidence, asymmetric trust, or cascade potential. The framework sits upstream of Governance, Decisions, and Risk (which depend on trust assessments), alongside Culture & Norms and Narratives (which shape what can be trusted), and downstream of Institutions and Systems (which provide the structures through which trust operates).

Within the unified iteration loop (OG §5.0: Initialise → Generate → Route → Execute → Test → Re-scan → Decide/Close), this framework contributes to the Route phase (determining if trust is in scope), to the Execute phase (conducting the types/dimensions/dynamics/Hiddens analysis), to the Test phase (running trust stress tests and vulnerability probes), and to the Decide/Close phase (crafting trust interventions and setting monitoring thresholds). Tier 1 Hiddens scans must run at least twice: early (before main framework execution) and pre-closure (after tests and table population); if symptoms point to specific hiding mechanisms, invoke Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scans from OG §7.5.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise

Trust concerns the extension of confidence to other actors, institutions, and information sources under conditions of vulnerability—dependence on others despite incomplete visibility or control. Trust is conferred through assessments of competence (can you do what you say?), integrity (will you do it?), and alignment (do your interests align with mine?). Because trust shapes delegation, cooperation, information sharing, and the willingness to be influenced, it often determines whether relationships, institutions, and systems succeed, fail, or trigger cascading breakdown. This framework provides a structured way to map trust bases, asymmetries, and dynamics so that trust risks can be detected early and addressed with appropriate interventions.

Trust operates as a portfolio of bases: personal reliability, institutional competence, professional expertise, value alignment, historical track record, and relationship mutuality. No single base sustains trust in complex environments; interdependence among bases is common. Tension between bases is generative of contestation: an institution can be competent but misaligned with stakeholder values; an expert can be technically skilled but lack integrity; a person can be well-intentioned but unreliable. Trust asymmetry is structural: one party may depend on another while that dependence is unreciprocated, creating hidden influence and information filtering. Durable trust requires explicit recognition of asymmetry, regular reality-testing (does actual behaviour match trust assumptions?), and portfolio design that prevents any single trust base from becoming too critical.

Trust differs analytically from legitimacy, power, and incentives, though they interact operationally. Legitimacy concerns whether authority is recognised as rightful; trust concerns whether someone will be honest and competent in exercising it. Power concerns structural capacity to act; trust concerns willingness to depend on another's actions. Incentives concern reward and punishment structures that motivate behaviour; trust concerns cooperation that persists even when incentive structures would not support it. When explicit incentive structures are introduced into a trust relationship, the shift can paradoxically dissolve the relationship by signalling distrust.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

- **Trust (operational definition):** The extension of confidence to another actor, institution, or information source, coupled with willingness to be vulnerable to their actions or knowledge claims, grounded in assessments of competence, integrity, and alignment.
- **In scope:**
  - Six trust meta-categories and thirty trust types
  - Five dimensions for profiling trust in any case (basis mix, relationship asymmetry, epistemic access, temporal character, recovery potential)
  - Trust dynamics (accumulation, erosion, betrayal, repair, cascade)
  - Key interfaces where trust failures concentrate (Type A: mandate/institution; Type B: relationship/behaviour; Type C: information/narrative)
  - Hiddens sources plus tiered Hiddens cross-check (Tier 1 six-category scan, Tier 2 deepening, Tier 3 escalation)
  - Application protocol and standard deliverables (trust structure statement, basis assessment, dimension profile, dynamics audit, intervention plan, monitoring dashboard)
  - Closure discipline with residual unknowns, acceptability rationale, monitoring plan, ownership, and learning hook
- **Out of scope:**
  - Full psychological assessment of trustworthiness (use psychology frameworks; treat personality assessment here as contextual input only)
  - Full institutional audit (use Governance and Systems frameworks jointly; trust assessment is one lens, not comprehensive institutional assessment)
  - Full epistemic evaluation (use Evidence and Epistemology frameworks jointly; this framework focuses on trust mechanisms, not independent reliability assessment)
  - Detailed Hiddens taxonomy (reference Framework of Hiddens for full 30-type taxonomy; this framework focuses on trust-specific generators and mechanisms)
- **Common confusions:**
  - Trust is not obedience: one can follow orders without trusting the authority giving them.
  - Trust is not alignment: one can trust someone's competence while disagreeing with their values or goals.
  - Trust is not incentive-dependence: trust supports cooperation that persists absent explicit incentive reinforcement.
  - Trust is not legitimacy: an authority can be legitimate without being trusted, or trusted without being legitimate.
  - Trust is not transparency: transparency can support trust-building, but information alone does not create trust in someone's good intent or competence.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions
### I. Interpersonal Trust Foundations
- **Definition:** Trust between individuals grounded in direct relationship, character assessment, reliability history, and relational depth. Personal trust is built through repeated interaction, shared experience, and signals of competence and good intent.
- **Diagnostic cues:** What is the history of interaction? Are there consistent patterns of reliability? Do parties feel psychologically safe with each other? Is there evidence of reciprocal care and commitment?
- **Typical failure mode:** Trust assumed from proximity (family, long tenure) without testing current reliability; historical trust applied to new contexts; personal trust extended to people in authority despite competence gaps; betrayal amnesia where past breaches are forgotten when choosing new dependence.

### II. Institutional Competence Trust
- **Definition:** Confidence that an institution has the capability, safeguards, and consistency to do what it claims. Institutional trust depends on structural design, demonstrated competence, transparency, and accountability mechanisms.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Does the institution have documented procedures and quality controls? Are outcomes consistent and predictable? Are there mechanisms for feedback and remedy? Is there evidence of learning and adaptation?
- **Typical failure mode:** Trust halo effects where reputation in one domain generalises to unrelated domains; assumption that credential or mandate guarantees competence; institutional trust substitution where historical performance is assumed to apply to new challenges; performance gaps hidden behind process compliance.

### III. Epistemic Trust Mechanisms
- **Definition:** Confidence in knowledge sources, experts, and information systems to provide reliable, independent, and accurate information. Epistemic trust depends on source credentials, domain expertise, methodological rigor, and independence from conflicts of interest.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Does the source have relevant expertise? Is the source independent from interested parties? Is the methodology transparent and sound? Is there a track record of accuracy? Are uncertainty and limitations acknowledged?
- **Typical failure mode:** Source credibility assessed without independent evaluation of reliability; information trusted because its source is trusted despite evidence quality issues; epistemic trust chains where false information travels far because it's trusted; expertise extrapolated beyond domain; conflicts of interest not disclosed or minimised.

### IV. Behavioral Trust Signals
- **Definition:** Observable patterns of action, communication, responsiveness, and consistency that indicate trustworthiness or untrustworthiness. Trust signals are actions that demonstrate competence, integrity, and alignment over time.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Does behaviour match what was promised? Are commitments honoured? How does the person respond to feedback and setbacks? What happens when no one is watching? Are there patterns of transparency and responsiveness?
- **Typical failure mode:** Trust signals absent or misleading (words vs actions diverge); hidden behaviour when not under observation; selective responsiveness that signals favouritism; consistency maintained only for valued observers; repair attempts that are performative rather than substantive.

### V. Vulnerability & Asymmetry Dynamics
- **Definition:** Patterns where one party depends on another while that dependence is not reciprocated, creating hidden influence, information filtering, and unbalanced power over decisions and information. Asymmetric trust structures create structural risks.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Who withholds information from whom and why? Who can make decisions that affect others without consent? Who benefits from the asymmetry? Are there mechanisms to make asymmetries visible? How would the less-dependent party know if they were being filtered or manipulated?
- **Typical failure mode:** Asymmetries remain invisible until crisis; upward filtering suppresses information about problems; dependent parties lose voice in decisions affecting them; hidden influence chains where A defers to B who defers to C; information becomes unreliable due to filtering at each stage.

### VI. Trust Crisis & Recovery
- **Definition:** Breach events that shatter trust, mechanisms for detection and acknowledgment, and the capacity to rebuild trust after betrayal through accountability, systemic change, and demonstrated behaviour change. Recovery is distinct from mere passage of time.
- **Diagnostic cues:** When did trust break? What caused it? Was the breach acknowledged? What remedy or change followed? Is there evidence of learning? Have systems changed to prevent recurrence? How long has behaviour change persisted?
- **Typical failure mode:** Betrayal denied or minimised rather than acknowledged; remedies offered without systemic change; blamed on circumstances rather than actor choice; insufficient time for behaviour change to accumulate; learned distrust that is then applied inappropriately to new relationships; amnesia where new leadership faces old problems.

How do they handle tasks beyond their capability? | Competence asserted without evidence; overreach into domains where capability is unproven; failures attributed to external factors; competence in one domain assumed to apply to others; skill gaps hidden until crisis. |
| 4 | Relational-Reciprocity Trust | Trust grounded in mutual care, genuine interest in the other's wellbeing, and reciprocal vulnerability. Relational trust is built through demonstrated care for the other's interests alongside self-interest, and willingness to be vulnerable to the other. | Do both parties show genuine interest in the other's wellbeing? Is vulnerability reciprocal, or does one party depend while the other maintains distance? Is there evidence of care independent of utility? | Care that is conditional on utility; vulnerability by only one party while the other protects position; care that disappears when the relationship is no longer useful; absence of genuine interest in the other's perspective. |
| 5 | Shared-Purpose & Values Alignment Trust | Trust grounded in genuine alignment on core values, goals, and purpose, such that the parties believe their interests and wellbeing are genuinely connected. Alignment trust is built through clear communication of values and observed consistency between values and choices. | Do both parties understand and endorse the shared purpose? Are values explicitly discussed or only assumed? What happens when values are tested? Are there deep misalignments being papered over? | Values assumed to be aligned without testing; values that diverge become apparent only under stress; hidden value disagreements that create betrayal when exposed; performative alignment without genuine shared purpose. |

## 3.2 Types (Category II: Institutional Competence Trust)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 6 | Procedural & Process Integrity Trust | Trust grounded in confidence that the institution follows its own documented procedures, applies rules consistently, and maintains safeguards against corruption and capture. Procedure trust is built through transparency and consistency of application. | Are procedures documented? Are they followed consistently? Are there independent audits? What happens when rules are broken? Are there remedies for procedure violations? | Documented procedures not followed; selective enforcement of rules; procedures modified without transparency; compliance theatre where forms are filled but substance is absent; procedures that exist on paper only. |
| 7 | Performance & Delivery Reliability Trust | Trust grounded in consistent delivery of promised services, outcomes, or capabilities over time and across contexts. Performance trust is built through track record of meeting commitments and handling failures with accountability. | What is the track record of delivery? How consistent is performance across different conditions and time periods? How are failures handled? Is performance improving or degrading? | Short-term performance wins masking structural fragility; performance metrics that diverge from actual outcomes; failure handling that blames external factors rather than improving systems; performance that depends on exceptional effort unsustainably maintained. |
| 8 | Institutional Safeguards & Redundancy Trust | Trust grounded in structural design choices (checks and balances, separation of duties, redundancy, transparency mechanisms) that make corruption difficult and enable detection. Safeguard trust is built through design that works whether people are trustworthy or not. | Are there multiple checkpoints and approvals required? Can one person cause major harm? Are there transparency mechanisms? Are there independent audits? Is there redundancy if key personnel leave? | Safeguards that only work if people follow them; critical functions dependent on individual key persons; safeguards that are easily circumvented; absence of independent oversight; "good people" culture substituting for structural safeguards. |
| 9 | Reputation & Social-Proof Trust | Trust grounded in the institution's reputation and recognition by peers, stakeholders, and external observers. Reputation trust is built through consistent performance and is fragile because external perception can shift suddenly. | What do independent observers say about this institution? What is the trajectory of reputation? Are there gaps between internal and external perception? How quickly does reputation change? | Reputation for one capability generalised to unrelated domains; external reputation maintained through PR while internal performance is poor; reputation halo effect where past performance assumed to continue; reputational crisis revealing hidden performance gaps. |
| 10 | Institutional Responsiveness & Learning Trust | Trust grounded in demonstrated capacity to learn from feedback, adjust systems based on evidence, and improve over time. Learning trust is built through visible correction of errors and adaptation to changed conditions. | When problems are pointed out, does the institution respond? Does it improve? Are improvements visible? How fast is the learning cycle? Are the same mistakes repeated? | Defensiveness rather than learning; change is cosmetic or performative; improvements don't persist; same problems recur under different names; learning is slow or requires catastrophe; leadership transition resets institutional knowledge. |

## 3.3 Types (Category III: Epistemic Trust Mechanisms)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | Source Expertise & Domain Knowledge Trust | Trust grounded in demonstrated expertise, education, and track record of competence in a specific domain. Expertise trust is built through credentials, peer recognition, and consistent production of credible work. | What are the credentials? What is the peer recognition? What is the track record in this domain? What is the breadth of expertise vs depth in one area? Are uncertainty and limits acknowledged? | Credentials without demonstrated competence; expertise in one domain applied to others; overconfidence about what can be known; unwillingness to acknowledge limits; credentials that are outdated or gained through non-rigorous means. |
| 12 | Source Independence & Conflict-of-Interest Trust | Trust grounded in independence from interested parties and absence of financial, professional, or ideological conflicts that would bias judgment. Independence trust is built through transparency about funding sources, affiliations, and incentives. | Who pays for this work? What are the organisational affiliations? What financial incentives exist? Are conflicts disclosed? Is there pressure to reach particular conclusions? What would an independent party say? | Conflicts of interest undisclosed; financial incentives hidden; ideological alignment with interested parties unacknowledged; appearance of independence without substance; pressure to reach conclusions that benefit funders; institutional conflicts that distort judgment. |
| 13 | Methodological Transparency & Rigor Trust | Trust grounded in transparent methodology, appropriate use of evidence and logic, and capacity for independent verification. Method trust is built through documentation of process and openness to scrutiny. | Is the methodology documented? Is evidence presented? Are assumptions stated? Can someone reproduce the work? Are limitations acknowledged? Are alternative interpretations considered? | Methodology hidden or opaque; evidence cherry-picked to support predetermined conclusions; assumptions unstated or unreasonable; results cannot be replicated; limitations hidden; alternative explanations dismissed without engaging. |
| 14 | Track Record of Accuracy & Prediction Trust | Trust grounded in demonstrated accuracy of past claims and reliability of predictions, particularly when tested against evidence. Accuracy trust is built through consistent alignment between claims and outcomes. | What does the track record say about accuracy? When predictions were made, did they come true? How has the source handled disconfirming evidence? Are there patterns of overconfidence or underconfidence? | Track record not examined; accuracy claimed without testing; predictions that fail are reinterpreted rather than acknowledged; disconfirming evidence ignored or explained away; overconfidence about what can be known; claims that sound confident but have little basis. |
| 15 | Collaborative & Peer-Reviewed Knowledge Trust | Trust grounded in work that has been subject to peer review, critique, and collaborative building of knowledge across credible sources. Collaborative trust is built through integration with broader epistemic communities and willingness to engage with critique. | Is the work peer-reviewed? Are there external critics with credibility? How does the source engage with criticism? Is the work integrated with other credible sources or isolated? Are there transparent uncertainty estimates and disagreements with peers? | Work that bypasses peer review; isolation from epistemic communities; dismissal of criticism without engagement; claims of unique insight without acknowledging peer disagreement; inability to explain disagreements with other credible sources; echo chambers masquerading as credibility. |

## 3.4 Types (Category IV: Behavioral Trust Signals)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 16 | Promise-Keeping & Follow-Through Signals | Trust signals based on observable pattern of honouring commitments, delivering on time, and not overcommitting beyond capacity. Follow-through signals are verifiable through outcomes. | What do people say about this person's follow-through? Are there documented cases of promises kept? How are missed commitments handled? Is the person careful about what they commit to? | Promises made casually without real intent; commitments expanded or reinterpreted after the fact; failures explained without accountability; inconsistency between public promises and private behaviour; pattern of overpromising and underdelivering. |
| 17 | Responsiveness & Attentiveness Signals | Trust signals based on observable pattern of timely response to questions, requests, and concerns, and evidence of genuine attention to the other's needs. Responsiveness signals include follow-up, asking clarifying questions, and acting on feedback. | How quickly and thoroughly does this person respond? Do they ask clarifying questions? Do they follow up? Do they remember prior conversations? Is attention consistent or selective by status? | Selective responsiveness based on status or utility; slow or minimal responses that signal low priority; failure to follow up; lack of attention to detail; responsiveness that disappears when power dynamics shift. |
| 18 | Consistency & Predictability Signals | Trust signals based on observable pattern of consistent behaviour, values, and treatment across different contexts and observers. Consistency signals include alignment between public and private behaviour and stable responses to similar situations. | Is behaviour consistent across contexts? Are values enacted consistently? Does the person behave differently when observed? Is treatment consistent regardless of status? How does the person respond to similar situations across time? | Behaviour that varies dramatically by observer; values enacted selectively; hypocrisy between public and private; inconsistent treatment based on status; behaviour that shifts when power dynamics change; instability that makes prediction impossible. |
| 19 | Transparency & Honesty Signals | Trust signals based on willingness to be transparent about thinking, uncertainties, limitations, and conflicts of interest, and pattern of honesty even when deception would be advantageous. Transparency signals include explaining rationales and acknowledging what is not known. | Does this person explain their thinking? Do they acknowledge uncertainties? Do they disclose conflicts of interest? Are they honest when honesty is costly? How do they handle questions about sensitive topics? | Selective honesty; information withheld strategically; explanations that hide true reasoning; conflicts of interest not disclosed; deception when advantageous; opacity presented as discretion or wisdom. |
| 20 | Accountability & Repair Signals | Trust signals based on willingness to acknowledge mistakes, take responsibility, and take corrective action. Accountability signals include acceptance of consequences and genuine repair attempts, not just apology. | When mistakes happen, are they acknowledged? Does the person take responsibility or blame external factors? What repair attempts are made? How do people who were harmed experience the response? Is there evidence of learning and change? | Mistakes denied or minimised; blame shifted to others; repair efforts that are performative; apology without change; insufficient time for trust to be rebuilt; repair that benefits the wrongdoer more than the wronged. |

## 3.5 Types (Category V: Vulnerability & Asymmetry Dynamics)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 21 | Upward-Filtering & Gatekeeping Dynamics | Asymmetric information flow where subordinates or less-powerful parties filter information going upward to protect their relationships with more-powerful parties, hiding problems and risks from decision-makers. Upward filtering is driven by power asymmetry and fear of consequences for bearer of bad news. | What information goes upward versus what is known at ground level? Are there documented cases of information withheld from leadership? Do leaders know about problems before they become crises? Is there psychological safety for delivering bad news? | Patterns of bad news suppressed; leaders surprised by crises; discrepancy between internal knowledge and external visibility; punished messengers; cultural norm that bad news is unwelcome; "we didn't know" explanations after fact. |
| 22 | Epistemic Cascade & False Consensus Dynamics | Information trust chains where information is trusted because its source is trusted, without independent verification, leading to false information propagating widely and creating appearance of consensus. Cascades hide the fact that information has not been independently verified. | Is information traced to original sources or accepted based on who conveyed it? Are there multiple independent sources for critical claims? How would disagreement be visible? Is there appearance of consensus based on shared sources? | Information travels far based on source trust rather than evidence; false consensus based on cited agreement that is actually coordinated messaging; critical claims not independently verified; disagreement invisible because information travels in trusted circles; cascade collapse when original source is questioned. |
| 23 | Selective-Access & Information-Hoarding Dynamics | Asymmetric access to information where some parties control information availability to others, creating hidden influence and power over decisions and perception. Information control enables the controlling party to shape narratives and limit options visible to others. | Who controls information access? Are there information gates? Who knows what, and who decides? What happens if someone tries to access restricted information? Are alternative information sources available? | Key information restricted to privileged few; decisions based on incomplete information for most parties; hoarders have hidden influence; information sharing blocked without explanation; no alternative sources available; dependence on gatekeepers for reality. |
| 24 | Hidden-Delegation & Accountability-Dodging Dynamics | Formal authority assigned to one person while real decisions are made by another, creating confusion about who is actually accountable. Hidden delegation creates liability for those nominally responsible while actual decision-makers escape scrutiny. | Who is formally in charge versus who makes decisions? Does nominal authority know what is being done in their name? Are there discrepancies between decision-making and accountability? Are there patterns of scapegoating the nominal authority? | Nominal leaders unaware of actual decisions; accountability for decisions they didn't make; lack of clarity about who actually decided; patterns where someone else's signature appears on decisions; actual decision-maker protected from consequences; confusion about whom to approach to change decisions. |
| 25 | Competence-Halo & Unwarranted-Generalization Dynamics | Trust extended to someone based on proven competence in one domain is applied to unrelated domains without testing capability in the new domain. Halo effects hide the question of whether competence actually extends. Organisational and reputational halo effects generalise trust beyond evidence. | What domain was this person competent in? Is competence in a new domain assumption or demonstrated? What happens when competence assumptions are tested? Are failures in new domains attributed to circumstances rather than lack of actual competence? | Expertise in one domain assumed to apply to others; failures blamed on bad luck rather than lack of competence; new-domain incompetence excused by past success; formal credentials generalised beyond what they support; overreach into areas beyond actual capability. |

## 3.6 Types (Category VI: Trust Crisis & Recovery)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 26 | Breach-Detection & Violation-Acknowledgment | Whether and how quickly trust breaches are detected, whether the breach is acknowledged or denied, and whether the violation is treated seriously or minimised. Detection and acknowledgment are preconditions for recovery. | Was the breach noticed? When? By whom? Was it acknowledged or denied? How seriously was it treated? Was the breacher defensive or accepting? | Breach hidden or discovered only by outsiders; denial or minimisation when discovered; defiance; blame-shifting; insufficient acknowledgment of harm; time-buying rather than accountability; mixed signals about seriousness. |
| 27 | Repair-Capacity & Corrective-Action Signals | Whether and how the party who violated trust responds: acknowledgment of harm, expression of remorse, corrective action, and evidence of systemic change to prevent recurrence. Repair must address the root cause, not just the symptom. | What actions did the violator take? Were systems changed to prevent recurrence? How quickly and thoroughly? Is repair visible to those harmed? How long has behaviour change persisted? | Apology without change; change that is cosmetic or performative; root cause not addressed; insufficient systemic improvement; behaviour change that is time-limited; repair efforts that benefit the violator more than those harmed. |
| 28 | Behavioral-Change-Persistence & Evidence-of-Learning | Whether behaviour change following breach is sustained over time and whether evidence of genuine learning (understanding why trust was broken, commitment to prevent recurrence) is visible. Persistence requires changed incentives and systems, not just changed rhetoric. | How long has the behaviour change persisted? Is it enforced by systems or only by effort? What would happen under pressure? Is the underlying incentive structure changed? Is there evidence of learning about why breach occurred? | Change that is temporary or effort-dependent; backsliding when pressure subsides; rhetorical change without structural change; no evidence of learning; same mistakes recurring in different forms; learning that doesn't persist through leadership transitions. |
| 29 | Redemption-Signalling & Earned-Forgiveness Dynamics | Whether the party who violated trust genuinely signals commitment to redemption and whether trust can be earned back through demonstrated change, and whether external parties who trusted the violator modify their own trust based on the recovery trajectory. Redemption signals include consistency and transparency about change. | Does the violator signal genuine commitment to change? Is the signal credible? How do previously-trusting parties respond? How much evidence of change is required before trust is restored? Is forgiveness earned or is it merely time-based? | Redemption signalled without substantive change; signals that are performative or calculated; inconsistency between signalled commitment and actual behaviour; external parties unwilling to restore trust despite changes; trust restored based on time alone without evidence of change. |
| 30 | Trust-Resilience & Relationship-Stabilization | Whether the relationship is strengthened through recovery (trust restored to pre-breach levels or deeper), remains fragile (recovered but vulnerable), or remains broken. Resilience depends on whether root causes were addressed and whether both parties commit to rebuilding. | What is the trajectory post-recovery? Is the relationship strengthened or remains fragile? Are both parties committed to rebuilding? How would a similar breach be handled? Is the relationship more transparent and robust than before, or just restored to prior state? | Recovery to prior fragility; relationship remains brittle despite apparent healing; vulnerability to re-breach; lack of commitment to preventing recurrence; return to prior patterns without learning; resilience-building efforts absent. |

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy
| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | Preserve the 6×5 structure (30 types) as the shared vocabulary for cross-case comparability and integration across the Analytical Series. |
| Domain-specific refinement | Add domain-specific subtypes under each parent type (e.g., 'algorithmic trust' under epistemic trust; 'family-system trust' under interpersonal trust; 'cross-cultural relational trust' under relational reciprocity). Tag subtypes to parent IDs and maintain mappability. |
| Audience and party mapping | For every added subtype, record: (a) which parties must extend/receive trust, and (b) which trust bases (competence, integrity, alignment, institutional structure, epistemic methodology, behavioral signals) apply in that subtype. |
| Multi-basis portfolios | Model trust as a portfolio: identify which bases are relied upon, which are compensatory, which are non-substitutable (e.g., integrity cannot be compensated by competence; institutional safeguards cannot substitute for individual character trust). |
| Asymmetry and vulnerability | Track explicitly: which parties are dependent, on what basis, and what would happen if trust were violated. Document power asymmetries and information asymmetries that interact with trust structure. |
| Temporal dynamics | Track how trust bases shift over time: inherited → earned → questioned → recovered or broken. Document path-dependent vulnerabilities and recovery trajectories. |
| Versioning & governance | Minor versions for wording/examples only; major versions for taxonomy changes (e.g., splitting a type, merging categories). Document mapping notes if types are refactored. |

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table
| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Trust Accumulation | Repeated positive interactions and predictable reliability build confidence incrementally over time. Each honoured commitment adds to trust stock. | Small positive experiences accumulate; consistency is observed; predictability enables increased delegation and vulnerability; history builds. | Trust accumulation is slow but creates resilience; relationships can weather occasional failures if the general pattern is positive. Early investment in trust is capital. | Intentional relationship building; consistent follow-through; relationship maintenance activities; recognition and celebration of reliability; regular positive interaction. |
| 2 | Trust Erosion | Repeated small failures, inconsistencies, or alignments-violations erode confidence incrementally. Trust decreases faster than it accumulates. | Small failures accumulate faster than forgiveness; inconsistencies create doubt; alignment-testing shows misalignment; predictability declines; repeated promises unsupported by behaviour. | Trust erosion is often invisible until sudden collapse; relationships can drift into low-trust states without dramatic breach; erosion can be prevented by addressing small issues quickly. | Regular check-ins about relationship quality; explicit discussion of concerns; rapid response to small failures; rebuilding through demonstrated change; relationship repair before breakdown. |
| 3 | Trust-Basis Substitution Attempts | When one trust basis becomes unavailable or questionable (competence doubted, integrity questioned), attempts are made to compensate by emphasising other bases without addressing the gap. Substitution masks the vulnerability. | Institutional safeguards invoked to compensate for individual unreliability; past performance invoked despite current incompetence; relationship warmth invoked to substitute for procedural integrity; rhetoric invokes trust without evidence. | Substitution attempts hide the real problem and delay necessary response; they can succeed temporarily but eventual reality-testing reveals the gap; substitution often precedes sudden collapse when the compensating basis also proves insufficient. | Address gaps directly without attempting substitution; explicit acknowledgment of which bases have failed; genuine repair of failed bases rather than rhetorical compensation; multi-basis portfolio design. |
| 4 | Cascade-Collapse from Asymmetric Breach | When one party violates trust that was asymmetrically granted (A trusted B, B didn't trust A), the breach doesn't just damage that dyad but cascades through networks where A had delegated on the basis of trust in B. Collapse multiplies through networks. | A trusted B and delegated decisions to B; B acted against A's interests; A now distrusts B; A also must reconsider trust in everyone to whom A delegated B; networks of dependence become problematic; information chains become unreliable. | Cascade collapses are hard to control once begun; they can destroy institutional credibility far beyond the initial breach; they reveal hidden dependencies and asymmetries; they force reconsideration of entire trust portfolios. | Clear identification of dependencies pre-breach; explicit testing of trust before granting high-stakes delegation; redundancy in critical functions; transparency about delegation chains; rapid acknowledgment and repair to contain cascade. |
| 5 | Epistemic Cascade through Trust Chains | Information is trusted through chains of sources, with verification at no stage, so false information propagates widely and creates appearance of broad consensus. Chains hide independent evaluation. | Source A claims X; Source B trusts A and relays X; Source C trusts B and relays X; audiences see three independent sources confirming X; none have verified independently. | Epistemic cascades can shape belief across entire networks without being detected until external reality contradicts the false consensus. They hide the fact that information has not been independently evaluated and that disagreement might exist. | Requirement for independent verification of critical claims; mapping of information provenance; diversity of information sources; explicit uncertainty and disagreement visibility; protection of dissenting voices. |
| 6 | Trust-Halo Generalization | Earned competence or reputation in one domain is assumed to extend to unrelated domains without new evidence. The halo allows unwarranted generalisation of trust. | Expertise in domain A (e.g., technology) assumed to apply to domain B (e.g., public policy) without testing; institutional reputation for delivering service A used to support claims about service B; individual reliability in one relationship assumed in new relationships. | Halo effects enable rapid scaling but hide competence gaps until failure occurs; they can create vulnerability to novel challenges where prior reputation is not relevant; they delay necessary capability development. | Explicit capability assessment in new domains; restraint in claiming competence beyond tested domains; humility about limits; reality-testing before high-stakes delegation; regular competence validation. |
| 7 | Betrayal-Amnesia & Pattern Repetition | Prior trust breaches are forgotten or minimised by leadership or institutional memory loss, and similar vulnerabilities are recreated. Amnesia enables repeat failure. | Institutional change or leadership transition loses knowledge of prior breach; new leadership assumes trust is warranted because historical problem is unknown; prior warnings are not consulted; same vulnerability recreated. | Betrayal amnesia enables preventable repeat failures; each repetition depletes trust capital faster because pattern is now visible; organisational learning fails to persist through transitions. | Maintain institutional memory logs of breaches and repairs; document warning patterns and lessons; require new leaders to learn institutional history; create safeguards that persist regardless of memory; regular historical review. |
| 8 | Upward-Filtering Spiral & Information Distortion | Subordinates filter bad news to protect relationships with superiors, information becomes increasingly distorted as it travels up, leaders lose touch with reality and make decisions based on fiction. Distortion accelerates when environment is hostile to bad news. | Junior staff withhold bad news to protect themselves; middle managers filter to look good; executives receive sanitised information; decisions made on false premises; reality-testing is avoided; culture punishes bad-news bearers. | Upward filtering can destroy institutional capacity to respond to crises; leaders become increasingly detached from reality; crises arrive as shocking surprises; institutional blindness persists despite ground-level visibility. | Psychological safety for bad news; reward systems that prioritise honesty; multiple reporting channels and independent observers; transparency about decision-making to enable validation; protection of whistleblowers. |
| 9 | Recovery-Facilitated Relationship Deepening | When trust is breached and genuinely repaired, the recovery process can deepen trust beyond pre-breach levels because understanding of vulnerabilities is explicit and repair demonstrates commitment. Recovery can strengthen relationships. | Breach occurs and is acknowledged; repair is thorough and includes systemic change; both parties engage in recovery; relationship dynamics become more explicit and transparent; mutual understanding deepens; post-recovery trust is more robust. | Genuine recovery can build resilience and deeper relationships than pre-breach states; it requires both parties to commit; recovery timelines require patience; relationships that successfully recover are often more durable. | Mutual commitment to recovery; explicit discussion of what failed and why; systemic change, not just apology; time and attention to rebuilding; explicit discussion of future safeguards; relationships that survive recovery often become exemplary. |
| 10 | Fragility-Without-Visibility | Trust is extended based on limited information or assumption, and fragility is not visible until external reality-testing or crisis reveals the gap. Invisible fragility creates false confidence. | Trust assumed because of proximity (family, long tenure, shared organisation) without testing current reliability; institutional safeguards assumed to exist without verification; competence assumed from credentials without validation; fragility remains invisible until crisis. | Invisible fragility enables catastrophic surprise; early warning signs are missed because reality-testing doesn't occur; interventions are delayed because the problem is not perceived; trust can be very high even as reliability is actually low. | Proactive reality-testing; competence validation in critical domains; regular feedback seeking; independent assessment of actual performance; stress-testing of trust assumptions; visibility of assumptions being made. |
| 11 | Distrust-Driven Information Suppression | Where distrust exists, information sharing is suppressed even when sharing would serve both parties, because the parties fear the information will be used against them or misinterpreted. Distrust becomes the hiding mechanism. | Community doesn't report safety concerns to institutions they distrust; employees don't share concerns upward if they fear retaliation; stakeholders don't volunteer feedback if they don't trust it will be used constructively; information is withheld to protect against perceived threat. | Distrust-driven suppression hides critical information and prevents organisations from responding to problems; it creates feedback deserts where reality is invisible; crises arrive as surprises; solving the underlying problem becomes impossible without restoring enough trust to enable information sharing. | Rebuild trust through demonstrated responsiveness to concerns; separate information-gathering from consequences; protect information sources; address root causes of distrust; demonstrate that information is used constructively; create safe channels for raising concerns. |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Hiddens Cross-Check

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how trust analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Competence Assumption | Treat credential, title, or past success as sufficient proof of current competence without reality-testing capability in the specific domain or context. Assume expertise transfers across domains. | Reality-test competence through observation or probes. Distinguish between domain expertise and adjacent domains. Assess how the person handles tasks beyond their capability. Require evidence of competence in the specific domain. |
| 2 | Integrity Halo | Assume someone is trustworthy in all domains because they have shown integrity in one domain or because they "seem like a good person." Assume moral character applies universally. | Assess integrity specifically in the domain where trust is being extended. Probe for conflicts of interest and incentives to act dishonestly. Distinguish between espoused values and demonstrated values. Test integrity under conditions of temptation and pressure. |
| 3 | Relationship Substitution | Assume that a good personal relationship substitutes for institutional safeguards or for verification of competence. Treat warmth and likeability as evidence of trustworthiness. | Separate relational quality from institutional safeguards. Verify competence and integrity independently. Use institutional design, not relationship quality, for critical functions. Design so that institutional safeguards work even if relationship quality deteriorates. |
| 4 | Single-Source Trust | Assume confidence in one source means information from that source is reliable without independent verification. Assume trusted sources are reliable across all claims. | Require independent verification of critical claims. Distinguish between source credibility and claim reliability. Build epistemic diversity. Make information provenance visible. Test claimed consensus against actual sources. |
| 5 | Asymmetry Invisibility | Assume that trust is mutual and reciprocal when asymmetry actually exists; miss one-sided dependence and hidden filtering. Treat formal cooperation as indicating mutual confidence. | Explicitly ask each party about their confidence in the other. Map who depends on whom and on what basis. Assess information flow in both directions. Look for filtering and selective disclosure. Make asymmetries visible and managed. |
| 6 | Temporal Amnesia | Assume trust holds stably over time without monitoring for erosion or trajectory change. Treat inherited trust as permanent. Forget about prior breaches or failures. Miss the fact that trust requires active maintenance. | Monitor trust trajectory over time. Track early warning signals (competence gaps, alignment shifts, communication changes). Conduct regular trust health checks. Maintain institutional memory of prior breaches and lessons. Test assumptions about trust periodically. |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)

| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I Perceptual** | Trust asymmetries and fragility can be subtle until sudden collapse; early warning signals are often missed because attention-limited observers don't see what they're not looking for. | **V Relational** | Recognition and voice depend on position; structural positions hide how asymmetric trust and dependence are distributed across different groups. | **VI Ontological** | Disputes about trust often hide category disputes: what counts as trustworthiness, competence, integrity, reliability, and alignment differ across parties. Shared vocabulary about trust is missing; metrics substitute for meaning. | Endless definitional arguments; talking past each other on trust; metrics treated as trust (e.g., process compliance as evidence of trustworthiness); category mismatches (institution claims it is competent but stakeholders wanted alignment instead). | Glossary/ontology alignment exercise. Explicit trust criteria mapping by party. Reframing exercises. Boundary object creation for translation. Definition disputes mapping. Category mapping across parties (what means trustworthiness to me vs you). Metrics critique (what are we actually measuring?). |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) **Tier 1 (Early scan):** Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (I–VI) as applied to trust. Assume trust asymmetries and fragility can be hidden. Produce a short note on each category: why it might be active, what symptoms to look for, what checks to run. (OG §D.6.10)
2) **Shortlist and rate:** Shortlist ~10–15 candidate Hiddens from the Tier 2 crosswalk (§8.3). For each, rate: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence. Prioritise high-consequence Hiddens. (OG §D.6.10)
3) **Interface mapping:** Assign at least one detection interface (A/B/C) and one remediation interface to each high-consequence Hidden. Map interaction chains and mitigation dependencies. (OG §7.2)
4) **Hiddens source analysis:** Run §8.1 hiddens source analysis and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens. Define what evidence would reduce each Unknown. (OG §D.6.10)
5) **Targeted scan decision:** If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism (e.g., upward filtering, epistemic cascade, trust asymmetry), invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan from OG §7.5; use Triage Matrix at OG §7.5.3 to select. (OG §7.5)
6) **Tier 1 re-run (Pre-closure):** After executing the framework protocol and available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas from early pass. Any new Hiddens discovered? Reduced any Hiddens? Refined understanding? (OG §D.6.10)
7) **Produce closure artefacts:** Populate Residual Unknowns (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, delegation structures, or risk assessments. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4 (five required items). (OG §7.4)

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

**Escalate to full 30-type Hiddens taxonomy run plus full detection/remediation mapping if any apply:**
- High-consequence decisions (safety, governance, existential, severe harm potential)
- Adversarial context and strategic concealment likely (evidence of deliberate hiding, cover-up, or information manipulation)
- Persistent disagreement or inconsistent evidence across credible sources about trust or competence
- Repeat failures despite prior interventions (pattern of failure suggests learning is hidden or blocked)
- Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement across hidden types (one hidden enables or masks others; asymmetry chains)
- Trust is fragile, actively contested, or experiencing polarisation
- Material trust risks to institutional function, relationship viability, or information integrity

**Tier 3 execution (per OG §D.6.10):**
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into the full 30-type Hiddens taxonomy (full-spectrum exploration).
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements for all claims; more explicit competing hypotheses; more rigorous disconfirming tests; independent corroboration required.
- When adversarial intent is plausible: maps concealment vectors (who benefits from hiding; what would reveal it); tests alternative narratives; builds independent corroboration chains.
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree branch A6 (OG §4.3); escalation and resourcing requirements increase.

### Canonical shared registers (Operating Guide v2.4 aligned; mandatory for LLM use)
**These registers are the standard information capture structures used across the Analytical Series.**

- **Hiddens Register:** Captures Hiddens identified, by meta-category, with mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence, detection interface, remediation interface, and interaction notes. Populated during Tier 1 (early and pre-closure) and Tier 2.
- **Evidence Ledger:** Captures evidence claims by type (factual/inferential/assumed/unknown), source, quality assessment, bias risks, and provenance. Used to track what we know with what confidence.
- **Hypothesis & Test Backlog:** Captures contending hypotheses about trust structure, asymmetries, basis adequacy, and dynamics, with corresponding tests to distinguish them. Executed iteratively during the framework run and updated pre-closure.
- **Information Request List:** Captures information gaps, what evidence would reduce them, priority, and owner. Used to manage evidence-gathering and to track closure constraints.
- **Residual Unknowns & Closure Note:** Captures what remains unknown, mechanism (why it's hidden or hard to know), impact (what depends on it), and next probe. Populated at closure per OG §7.4 (five required items: residuals list, acceptability rationale, monitoring plan, ownership, learning hook).

# 10. First Principles & Assumptions (Appendix A)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle | Justification | Operational implication | Failure mode if ignored |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trust is grounded in assessments of competence, integrity, and alignment, not in position or authority alone | Authority can be legitimate without being trusted; formal roles do not create confidence in actual capability or good intent. Trust judgments are made by those depending on the trusted party based on available evidence. | Reality-test competence and integrity; do not assume position creates trustworthiness; distinguish between formal authority and earned confidence. | Assumes title guarantees trustworthiness; misses competence gaps and misalignment; overdepends on formal structure without testing actual reliability. |
| Trust asymmetries create hidden influence and information filtering | When A trusts B but B does not trust A, information flows one direction with filtering at each stage, and B has hidden influence over A's decisions while being unaccountable to A. Asymmetries are structural power levers. | Make asymmetries explicit; identify who depends on whom and on what basis; assess information flow in both directions; establish mechanisms to make filtering visible. | Treats trust as mutual when asymmetry actually exists; misses hidden influence; enables information distortion through unaccountable filtering; fails to manage vulnerability. |
| Trust cannot substitute for institutional design | Personal trust between good people cannot reliably substitute for institutional safeguards, checks and balances, and accountability mechanisms. Safeguards must work whether or not people are trustworthy. | Design institutions so that critical functions are protected by structure, not by hope that key people are trustworthy; use trust as supplement to design, not substitute. | Relies on good people to maintain integrity; fails when leadership changes; creates vulnerability when key individuals leave; enables corruption and abuse. |
| Explicit incentive structures can dissolve trust relationships | When transactional incentives are made explicit, they can signal distrust and shift the relationship from "we cooperate because we trust each other" to "you benefit if you cooperate," paradoxically reducing cooperation. Incentive-explicit relationships are weaker. | Use incentives and safeguards for critical functions; preserve trust relationships for domains where they operate; be cautious about making incentive structures too explicit in valued relationships. | Attempts to control trust relationships through incentive redesign; unexpectedly destroys relationship through signalling distrust; undermines cooperation by shifting to transactional model. |
| Trust erosion is faster and less visible than trust accumulation | Small failures erode trust faster than comparable successes build it; erosion can be invisible until sudden collapse; restoration requires sustained behaviour change over time and is not symmetric with erosion speed. | Monitor for early warning signals (competence gaps, alignment shifts, communication changes); address small failures quickly; patience with trust rebuilding (does not happen as fast as erosion). | Assumes symmetry in trust building and erosion; surprised by sudden collapse after slow erosion; expects rapid restoration after brief reputation repair. |
| Epistemic trust chains hide independent reliability of information | Information trusted because its source is trusted, without independent verification, can propagate false information widely and create appearance of consensus when information has not actually been verified. Chains hide whether information is reliable. | Require independent verification of critical claims; build epistemic diversity; make source provenance visible; protect dissenting voices; test information against external reality. | Assumes trusted sources are reliable; accepts information without independent evaluation; vulnerable to cascades of false information; creates brittle consensus that collapses with external fact-checking. |
| Recovery after breach requires mutual commitment from both parties | After trust is violated, recovery is only possible with genuine commitment from both parties: acknowledgment of harm, willingness to change on the violator side, and willingness to engage in recovery on the violated party side. One-sided effort typically fails. | Assess both parties' commitment to recovery; address root causes, not just symptoms; allow time for behaviour change; maintain transparency about recovery progress; establish new safeguards. | Assumes apology is sufficient; expects rapid restoration; doesn't require systemic change; treats time as healer when behaviour change is necessary; one-sided recovery efforts often fail. |
| Trust-consciousness requires explicit management of vulnerabilities | Trusting others creates vulnerability; vulnerability must be explicitly acknowledged and managed, not assumed to be acceptable. Trust decisions should be conscious of what would happen if trust were violated. | Identify where critical dependence exists; assess trustworthiness explicitly; establish backup and redundancy for critical functions; maintain monitoring even in trusted relationships. | Assumes trust is safe; ignores vulnerability; fails to establish safeguards or backup; surprised by betrayal because vulnerability was not explicitly acknowledged. |

# 11. Glossary (Appendix B)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary
- **Competence trust:** Confidence that another party has the capability and skill to do what they claim. Grounded in evidence of effective action, not just credentials.
- **Integrity trust:** Confidence that another party will be honest and will enact their values even under temptation or pressure. Grounded in observation of character in difficult situations.
- **Alignment trust:** Confidence that another party's interests are genuinely aligned with yours and that they care about your wellbeing. Grounded in observation of care and reciprocal vulnerability.
- **Institutional trust:** Confidence in an organisation's design, safeguards, and consistency to deliver what it claims. Grounded in structural design and demonstrated performance, not just reputation.
- **Epistemic trust:** Confidence in a knowledge source, expert, or information system to provide reliable, independent, and accurate information. Grounded in methodology, track record, and freedom from conflicts of interest.
- **Trust asymmetry:** Unequal distribution of trust where one party depends on another while that dependence is not reciprocated. Creates hidden influence and information filtering.
- **Upward filtering:** Suppression of bad news as information travels upward in a hierarchy, driven by fear of consequences for the bearer of bad news. Prevents leaders from knowing ground-level reality.
- **Epistemic cascade:** Chain of information transfer where information is trusted because its source is trusted, without independent verification at any stage, allowing false information to propagate and false consensus to appear.
- **Misplaced trust:** Confidence extended to an actor or institution that has not actually demonstrated trustworthiness. The gap between assumed and actual trustworthiness remains hidden until breach.
- **Trust halo:** Reputation for competence or reliability in one domain assumed to extend to unrelated domains without testing. Competence does not universally transfer.
- **Betrayal:** Violation of trust; breaking of confidence through incompetence, dishonesty, or misalignment when those risks were not disclosed or were explicitly contracted against.
- **Trust recovery:** Process of rebuilding trust after breach through acknowledgment, repair, behaviour change, and demonstration of systemic improvement. Requires commitment from both parties.
- **Psychological safety:** Shared belief that the relationship or team is safe for taking interpersonal risks; candour about concerns, mistakes, or disagreement will not result in punishment or rejection.
- **Fragility:** Quality of trust that appears robust but is actually brittle; built on assumptions not tested under stress; vulnerable to sudden collapse under pressure or changed conditions.
- **Hiddens:** Information, mechanisms, or relationships that are not visible to relevant parties; visibility failures that prevent accurate assessment or response. Trust-based hiddens include asymmetries, cascades, filtering, and misplaced trust.
