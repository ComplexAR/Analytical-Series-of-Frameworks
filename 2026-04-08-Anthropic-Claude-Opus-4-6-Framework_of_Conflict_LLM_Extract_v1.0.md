## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Framework of Conflict identifies what contestation, adversarial dynamics, and competing interests are active, latent, or strategically concealed in a scenario. It classifies conflict into six meta-categories and thirty core types spanning forms of contestation, conflict triggers, escalation patterns, suppression mechanisms, strategic conflict behaviour, and resolution pathways. It profiles conflicts across five cross-cutting dimensions (intensity, visibility, structure, stakes, reversibility); maps escalation and suppression dynamics; surfaces latent opposition hidden by false consensus, reframing, or hierarchy-imposed silence; identifies strategic concealment in adversarial contexts; and provides interface analysis for hidden, suppressed, and reframed conflicts. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with conflict-specific blindnesses and de-escalation pathways, enabling downstream frameworks to design interventions addressing root conflict rather than surface disagreement.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Latent Conflict Suppression | Surface agreement masks actual opposition; stated support with behaviour divergence; false consensus in formal settings, real opposition in informal | Hierarchical power prevents open voice; norms enforce silence; fear of consequences suppresses expression; conflict is rational but dangerous to surface | Decisions appear well-supported until implementation reveals covert opposition; morale and retention problems signal hidden conflict; sabotage emerges when suppression breaks |
| Escalation Through Coalition Mobilisation | Conflict spreads as allies are recruited; opposing coalitions mobilise; intensity increases due to coalition pressure and competition for leadership | Primary actors' willingness to contest is amplified by coalition stakes; coalition members face pressure to support; escalation accelerates as coalitions harden | Bilateral dispute becomes multiparty conflict; contagion spreads conflict beyond original parties; coalition dynamics may drive escalation even if primary parties would prefer containment |
| Rhetorical Intensification | Positions harden; language becomes more extreme and absolute; flexibility decreases; rhetoric moves from disagreement to disrespect to delegitimisation | Public statements create commitment pressure; retreat becomes costly; emotional engagement increases; group identity becomes tied to position | Retreat from hardened position becomes publicly costly; de-escalation becomes harder as escalation proceeds; emotional lock-in prevents rational negotiation |
| Reframing as Technical | Interest conflict is reframed as technical disagreement; value conflict becomes factual dispute; political becomes operational | Reframing moves conflict to domain where objective resolution appears possible; technical experts gain legitimacy; conflict becomes depoliticised | Underlying conflict remains unresolved in technical frame; technical solutions do not stick; conflict resurfaces when technical solution is implemented; debate becomes intractable |
| Strategic Concealment & Information Asymmetry | Opposition is hidden from decision-makers; intentions and capabilities are disguised; true positions are concealed | In adversarial contexts, transparency is disadvantageous; strategic actors hide information to maintain negotiating advantage; intentional misdirection | Analysis based on visible position is systematically wrong; negotiators are blindsided; capabilities are misestimated; escalation can result from misinterpreted signaling |
| Suppression Breakdown & Eruption | Suppressed conflict suddenly surfaces; triggering event breaks suppression mechanisms; latent opposition erupts explosively | Suppression mechanism fails (hierarchy collapses, norm erodes, pain threshold reached); triggering event activates latent opposition; pent-up grievance releases | Sudden visible conflict appears to erupt without cause; surface stability was masking severe opposition; organizations face crisis when suppression breaks |
| False Consensus Performance | Surface unity masks underlying disagreement; formal agreement hides private dissent; consensus theatre obscures real support distribution | Suppressive norms or hierarchy enforce appearance of agreement; actors comply publicly while opposing privately; performance becomes investment | Decision appears well-supported until implementation reveals hidden opposition; trust erodes when hidden opposition becomes visible; locked-in poorly supported decisions create later crises |
| Commitment Tactics & Escalation Lock-in | Actors take public commitments, incur sunk costs, or make moves that eliminate retreat options; escalation path becomes harder to reverse | Public commitments create reputation costs to backing down; sunk costs are lost if position changes; ideological commitment makes retreat identity-threatening | Escalation appears inevitable but is result of strategic choices; de-escalation requires face-saving alternatives; breaking commitment tactics is path to de-escalation |
| Structural Conflict Persistence | Conflict recurs despite mediation, personnel changes, or conflict resolution attempts; appears as inevitable tension or personality clash | Conflict is built into system design, role definitions, or incentive structures; persists independent of who occupies roles; structural source remains unaddressed | Treating structural conflict as interpersonal conflict fails; personnel changes do not resolve conflict; mediation does not work; structural redesign is required |
| Interest-Value Escalation Divergence | Escalation intensity increases beyond what material interests warrant when identity or values become engaged | Value-based positions are non-negotiable; identity stakes make retreat costly; moral emotion (outrage, shame, humiliation) drives escalation | Value-based conflicts are resistant to resolution; material trade-offs do not work; escalation exceeds rational calculation; de-escalation requires addressing value and identity stakes |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What opposition and conflict are outside typical attention frame or not expected to exist in this context; what contestation is invisible to decision-makers? |
| II (Systemic) | How are conflict origins embedded in system structure, role definitions, or incentive misalignment in ways that make conflict appear inevitable or natural rather than as choice? |
| III (Informational) | What information about positions, intentions, capabilities, or strategic moves is being strategically concealed; what information asymmetries are enabling conflict? |
| IV (Temporal) | How are past grievances, historical conflicts, or prior betrayals shaping current opposition; what latent conflict is activation waiting for trigger? |
| V (Relational) | What opposition is flowing through networks; what coalitions are forming covertly; what relational dynamics of contestation are hidden from formal view? |
| VI (Ontological) | How is conflict being understood and named in this context; what forms of contestation are not even conceivable given operative conflict concepts? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Latent Conflict Suppression (incompatible interests exist but are not surfaced; suppression mechanisms keep conflict invisible until triggering event)
- False Consensus Masking Opposition (surface agreement masks actual disagreement; opposition is hidden in informal channels or through performance)
- Conflict Reframing as Technical (interest conflict is presented as technical disagreement; underlying contestation remains hidden beneath technical frame)
- Hierarchy-Imposed Silence (power asymmetry prevents open disagreement; subordinates cannot safely voice opposition; false consensus at higher levels)
- Strategic Concealment in Adversarial Contexts (actors deliberately hide true positions, capabilities, or intentions to maintain negotiating advantage)
- Suppressed Opposition Emerging as Sabotage (covert resistance and foot-dragging appear as implementation failure rather than as hidden conflict)
- Coalition-Mediated Hidden Opposition (dyadic conflict operates through coalitions; actual antagonism is between coalition structures; primary opposition is obscured)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| False consensus and suppressed opposition masking real conflict | Interests framework; Culture framework | Behaviour-first analysis; informal channel surfacing; multi-level interview | Surface underlying interests driving opposition; create safe channels for disagreement; break suppression norms carefully; provide face-saving pathways for opposition |
| Strategic concealment and information asymmetry in negotiation | Power framework; Evidence framework | Intelligence analysis; capability assessment; signal interpretation | Probe for true positions beneath surface negotiating stance; test proposals against revealed preferences; assume strategic misrepresentation; use multi-channel information gathering |
| Conflict reframed as technical; underlying dispute remaining unresolved | Interests framework; Evidence framework | Reframe the reframe; identify underlying interests/values; surface political conflict beneath technical | Move beyond technical debate; surface actual interests or values in conflict; address root conflict rather than technical proxy; create space for interest/value disagreement |
| Escalation locked in through commitment tactics; de-escalation appearing impossible | Emotions framework; Decisions framework | Commitment tactic identification; face-saving alternative design; psychological pathway analysis | Provide paths for emotional cooling; offer face-saving exits from public commitments; reduce sunk-cost perception; address underlying emotional drivers (humiliation, fear, moral outrage) |
| Suppressed conflict erupting suddenly; organisational shock from surfacing opposition | Culture framework; Governance framework | Suppression mechanism assessment; trigger event analysis; crisis communication | Manage surfacing of suppressed conflict; provide legitimate channels for grievance; address suppression norms; help organisation process suddenly visible opposition |
| Structural conflict persisting despite personnel changes and mediation | Systems framework; Governance framework | Structural incompatibility analysis; role/incentive redesign pathway | Redesign system, roles, or incentives to address structural root; surface that conflict is built into design; structural change required; personnel and mediation will not resolve |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Identify focal actors, their stated positions, and explicit opposition (overt disputes); note where positions are stated as disagreement or conflict is openly acknowledged.
2. Assess conflict visibility: lightly determine whether conflict is fully visible, partially visible (true consensus masking opposition), latent/suppressed, or reframed as technical disagreement.
3. Identify apparent conflict sources: interest incompatibility, value collision, resource scarcity, or structural misalignment; map 2–3 key conflict drivers.
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six Hiddens categories lightly; flag obvious suppression (hierarchy preventing voice, norms enforcing silence), false consensus, reframing signals.
5. Shortlist highest-consequence hidden conflict; note whether suppression is strong or escalation is likely; flag for escalation to Full Depth if strategic concealment, latent conflict, or escalation risk is high.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Map focal actors and conflict structure: dyadic, multiparty, or nested; identify coalition formations and alignment patterns; assess coalition stability and fragility points.
2. Classify conflict comprehensively across all six meta-categories for each conflict instance; profile along five dimensions (intensity, visibility, structure, stakes, reversibility).
3. Identify all conflict triggers and origins: map interest incompatibilities, value collisions, resource competition, structural misalignments, and historical grievances for each actor pair.
4. Analyse escalation dynamics: assess which escalation patterns are active (coalition mobilisation, rhetorical hardening, coercive escalation, contagion, commitment tactics); identify turning points.
5. Map suppression mechanisms: identify hierarchy-imposed silence, norm-based suppression, reframing, information control, and covert resistance; assess mechanism strength and what would break suppression.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: for each identified Hidden (latent conflict, false consensus, reframing, strategic concealment, suppressed opposition), assess mechanism, detectability, consequence; assign detection and remediation interfaces; design specific probes.
7. Analyse de-escalation and resolution pathways: understand what would reduce escalation (communication, structural change, power shifts, superordinate goals, mediation); map path dependencies and preconditions.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add conflict-specific Hiddens with meta-category tag (I–VI), conflict type, suppression/concealment mechanism, visibility (latent/reframed/suppressed/hidden), detectability, consequence, remediation interface pointer. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate any high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hidden (latent escalation, strategic concealment, false consensus masking severe opposition) to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record conflict classification, dimensional profiles, trigger identification, suppression mechanism analysis, coalition mapping, evidence quality, information gaps (what would confirm latent conflict, what reveal suppression breakdown). | After conflict mapping and suppression analysis; note gaps requiring additional evidence (interviews, observation, informal channel access). |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about conflict escalation trajectories, suppression stability, coalition fragility, and de-escalation pathways; propose tests (escalation pattern tracking, suppression mechanism testing, coalition stability assessment). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decision points. |
| Information Requests | Request access to opposition patterns (informal communication, coalition records, behaviour divergence from stated position), historical conflict data, leadership/power change effects, multi-level perspectives on conflict reality. | To reduce Unknowns and validate Hiddens detection; coordinate with Interests, Power, and Emotions frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified the focal actors, classified conflict across all six meta-categories, assessed visibility and suppression mechanisms, identified highest-consequence hidden opposition (false consensus, latent conflict, strategic concealment), run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-risk conflicts (escalation lock-in, suppression breakdown risk, structural escalation) to appropriate frameworks?

## Section 8: Cross-Framework Routing Pointers

### 8a. Upstream Inputs (frameworks commonly feeding into this one)

- Interests & Power (what actors want; what they can enforce; map interest incompatibility and power asymmetry enabling/constraining conflict)
- Agents & Relations (who are the actors; what is relationship quality; what trust exists enabling or inhibiting opposition)
- Incentives & Values (what reward structures are misaligned; what values are opposed; which conflicts are interest-based vs value-based)
- Beliefs & Cognition (what actors believe about each other; how cognition shapes conflict perception; what beliefs enable demonisation or dehumanisation)
- Culture & Norms (what norms suppress opposition; what cultural values enable or inhibit conflict; what emotional display rules shape conflict expression)

### 8b. Downstream Handoffs (frameworks commonly consuming this framework's outputs)

- Framework of Negotiations (negotiation design addressing conflict root causes; coalition management; de-escalation pathway design)
- Framework of Decisions (how conflict shapes decision options and outcomes; strategic behaviour in adversarial contexts; choice under contestation)
- Framework of Governance (governance structures addressing conflict sources; conflict institutionalisation; designing for conflict management rather than suppression)
- Framework of Risk (conflict as risk source; escalation risk assessment; suppression breakdown risk; de-escalation preconditions)
- Framework of Interventions (de-escalation interventions; structural changes addressing conflict sources; conflict transformation options)
- Framework of Diagnosis (explaining failure and persistence through conflict analysis; opposition as root cause of implementation failure)
