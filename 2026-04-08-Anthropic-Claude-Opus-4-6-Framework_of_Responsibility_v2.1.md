# Introduction

## What is Responsibility and Accountability?

Responsibility is the structured allocation of **obligations**: formal and normative expectations that specific agents will act (or refrain from acting) in ways that matter to specified stakeholders over defined time horizons. It translates analysis, risk, and values into duties. Who must maintain controls? Who must escalate when risks emerge? Who must fund mitigation? Who must explain and justify decisions? Who carries consequences when obligations are not discharged? Responsibility is the normative layer that answers these questions.

Responsibility operates on multiple bases simultaneously. Some duties flow from formal roles and mandates (Role Responsibility, Category I). Others flow from causal contribution to outcomes (Causal Responsibility, Category II). Still others are grounded in moral evaluation, law, stewardship, or systemic design (Categories III–VI). Complex organisations typically carry responsibilities across all six bases, and conflicts between them are common. This framework provides a structured method to surface these conflicts, allocate duties across bases, and ensure that allocation is backed by accountability mechanisms that make it real—not merely ceremonial.

## Why does Responsibility matter for Hiddens work?

Responsibility allocation is itself a domain of Hiddens. Many failures persist not because capability was absent, but because responsibility was ambiguous, shifted, or actively concealed. This framework surfaces several families of hiding mechanisms:

**Many-Hands Effect** (responsibility diffusion): When numerous actors are involved, each assumes someone else is accountable. Responsibility becomes "everyone's job" and therefore no one's. This hiding mechanism is especially common in complex systems and multi-organisation partnerships.

**Responsibility-Power Misalignment**: Those with power to act lack corresponding duties, or those bearing duty lack the power to discharge it. A frontline worker is "responsible" for safety but has no authority to change processes. A senior leader is accountable for strategy but insulated from implementation consequences. This misalignment creates both unowned risks and unjust accountability.

**Positional Invisibility** (Hidden-21 Positional): Responsibility is genuinely assigned but invisible from certain roles or locations. Frontline staff are blamed for failures they had no authority to prevent. Senior leaders claim ignorance of operational duties they formally owned. Responsibility becomes contestable and unequally enforced.

**Temporal Decay** (Hidden-15 Latency, Hidden-7 Mirage): Responsibilities are assigned at inception but drift over time. Stewardship ownership is documented but gradually neglected. Forward-looking duties (risk management, anticipation) are orphaned as immediate crises dominate attention. Accountability mechanisms weaken without conscious maintenance.

**Responsibility Silence** (Hidden-14 Fragmentation, Hidden-11 Secret): Responsibilities are deliberately left ambiguous or undocumented to preserve operational flexibility or to hide power asymmetries. Accountability is "theatre": blame is assigned retroactively to individuals rather than traced to systemic gaps.

Without this framework, these Hiddens remain latent. With it, responsibility allocation becomes a repeatable, testable discipline that flags diffusion and gaps early, enabling governance redesign.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises responsibility allocation through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 responsibility types** organised into six meta-categories (Role & Task, Causal & Outcome, Moral & Evaluative, Legal & Institutional, Forward-Looking & Stewardship, Collective & Systemic), enabling rapid classification and comparison of responsibilities across contexts.

2. **Eight cross-cutting dimensions** (Temporal orientation, Formalisation, Strength, Collectivity, Visibility, Basis diversity, Contestability, Enforcement mechanism) that profile any responsibility and explain why different stakeholders attribute it differently.

3. **Dynamics patterns** (drift, dumping, overload, activation, contestation, inter-category conflict) that describe how responsibility systems evolve and where systemic breakdowns occur.

4. **Seven accountability interface types** (hierarchical, professional, regulatory, public, contractual, peer, community) that map how responsibilities are tested and enforced.

5. **Responsibility-specific Hiddens analysis** with eight responsibility-centric mechanisms and mandatory tiered Hiddens scans (Tier 1 six-category audit, Tier 2 crosswalk to prominent Hiddens types, Tier 3 escalation).

6. **A 7-step application protocol** that moves from framing through inventory, classification, dimensional assessment, Hiddens scanning, redesign, and closure with explicit meta-responsibility for maintaining the responsibility system itself.

The framework populates standard registers (Responsibility Gap Register, Accountability Mechanism Map, Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation. It is designed for iterative use: baseline responsibilities are mapped, gaps are identified, governance is redesigned, and the responsibility system is periodically reviewed as organisations, risks, and incentives evolve.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework whenever accountability is contested, when repeated failures suggest "no one owned it," when governance is being redesigned, or when stewardship and forward-looking duties need to be clarified. The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (quick responsibility gap scan) and Investigative Run Mode (deep audit of all 30 types, Hiddens scanning, full governance redesign).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: scan the six meta-categories, identify key types and gaps, map accountability mechanisms, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, propose 3–5 design actions. Escalate to Full Depth when responsibility is high-consequence, when multiple stakeholders dispute who is accountable, when many-hands effects are suspected, or when governance redesign is in scope. The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all responsibility claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording gaps and residual unknowns rather than imposing false clarity.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario and the Operating Guide, producing a disciplined responsibility analysis with explicit Hiddens, accountability gaps, F/I/A/U tagging, and actionable redesign recommendations.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Responsibility is the structured allocation of **obligations**: expectations that specific agents will perform, ensure performance of, or refrain from certain actions, for specified stakeholders, on some basis, over some time horizon. It is the normative layer that translates analysis, evidence, and values into duties—and it is inherently relational, contestable, and shaped by power and incentives.

This framework provides a standardised approach to:
- Surface responsibility allocations that exist but remain undocumented or contested,
- Classify responsibilities across six distinct bases (role, causal, moral, legal, stewardship, systemic) to reveal conflicts and gaps,
- Assess each responsibility using eight dimensions to diagnose misalignments and hidden risks,
- Identify and assign responsibility for anticipating, detecting, and responding to Hiddens and latent conditions,
- Map accountability mechanisms—the structures that make responsibilities real rather than nominal,
- Redesign responsibility allocations and governance to close gaps and align with actual power and capability, and
- Establish meta-responsibility for maintaining and reviewing the responsibility system itself.

Because many failures persist through responsibility gaps (unowned risks, diffusion, power-duty misalignment, temporal decay) rather than through lack of capability, this framework is essential for governance design, incident response, risk ownership, and stewardship planning.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Responsibility (operational)**: A structured obligation for an agent to perform, ensure performance of, or refrain from certain actions, for specified stakeholders, on some basis (role, causal, moral, legal, stewardship, systemic), over some time horizon, with verifiable accountability mechanisms.

**In scope**:
- Role- and task-based responsibility allocations (who owns what, by virtue of position)
- Causal and outcome responsibility (who materially contributed; who is accountable for results)
- Moral and evaluative responsibility (blameworthiness and praiseworthiness; character-based duties)
- Legal and institutional responsibility (law, regulation, contract, fiduciary duty, mandates)
- Forward-looking stewardship and prevention responsibility (maintaining systems, managing risks, intergenerational duty)
- Collective, distributed, and systemic responsibility (group duties, many-hands conditions, meta-responsibility)
- Accountability mechanisms (hierarchical, professional, regulatory, public, contractual, peer, community)
- Responsibility for Hiddens and latent conditions (ownership of detection, anticipation, escalation)

**Out of scope** (by design; refer to other frameworks):
- Full adjudication of legal liability in any jurisdiction (use Legal Analysis framework)
- Full ethical theory resolution of contested moral claims (use Values and Legitimacy frameworks)
- Full intervention catalogue and implementation (use Interventions and Change frameworks)
- Detailed capacity and resource planning (use Resources framework)

**Common confusions** ("responsibility is not..."):
- Responsibility is not blame alone; it includes design, prevention, stewardship, and forward-looking duties.
- Accountability is not the same as responsibility; accountability mechanisms test, verify, and enforce responsibilities.
- Role assignment does not equal discharge; written ownership without enforcement becomes theatre.
- Responsibility is not always individual; collective, shared, and systemic responsibilities are equally real.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (narrative)

### Category I: Role & Task Responsibility
Responsibilities that attach to formal offices, roles, mandates, and explicit task allocations. This is the starting point for most governance structures: who holds this position, and what does the position require? Role Responsibility is highly formalised and often documented in charters, job descriptions, governance policies, and mandates. It is typically the easiest to allocate and the most difficult to dispute, because it is objective and independent of individual capability.

However, Role Responsibility can conceal hidden gaps: not all important tasks are formally allocated to anyone. Some duties are assumed to exist "somewhere" but no one has explicit accountability. When multiple roles contribute to a single outcome, each role-holder may assume someone else is also responsible. And when roles are documented but not enforced through accountability mechanisms, role responsibility becomes nominal—a paper exercise rather than a real obligation.

### Category II: Causal & Outcome Responsibility
Responsibility anchored in causal contribution to outcomes. This category is salient in incident response and post-mortems: who made decisions or took actions that, causally, shaped what happened? Unlike Role Responsibility (which is about formal allocation), Causal Responsibility is about actual influence. A junior engineer may have minimal role-based accountability but significant causal responsibility if their code design enabled a failure. Conversely, a senior executive may bear minimal causal responsibility for an operational failure even if they hold broad role-based authority.

Causal Responsibility is essential for learning: understanding who actually caused outcomes, in what ways, and under what conditions is necessary for preventing recurrence. However, causal attribution is fraught: causation in complex systems is typically non-linear, multi-factor, and counterfactual (what would have happened if X had not occurred?). Easy attribution often misses systemic factors (inadequate processes, resource constraints, information gaps) in favour of individual actions.

### Category III: Moral & Evaluative Responsibility
Responsibility grounded in moral evaluation: who is blameworthy or praiseworthy, given their knowledge, intent, capacity, and the alternatives available to them? This category asks not just what happened, but whether the person deserves blame or credit. It is closely tied to culture, values, and legitimacy: communities differ in how they attribute moral responsibility (some emphasise intent; others emphasise outcome; some attend to systemic constraints while others focus on individual choice).

Moral Responsibility is often the most contested. A person may have clear role-based and causal responsibility but claim they lacked knowledge of consequences, had no realistic alternative, or faced impossible constraints. Conversely, a person with minimal formal responsibility may deserve moral praise for whistleblowing or extraordinary care beyond their duty. This category is essential for culture and leadership, but it requires values clarity and legitimacy shared across stakeholders.

### Category IV: Legal & Institutional Responsibility
Responsibility defined by law, regulation, contract, fiduciary duty, and institutional rules. This category is enforced through formal sanctions (fines, imprisonment, contract damages, injunctions) and professional discipline. Legal Responsibility is often the most rigid and objective: what the law says is what applies, regardless of individual beliefs about fairness or appropriateness.

However, legal responsibility does not cover all important duties, and legal boundaries often lag behind emerging harms. A practice may be legal but irresponsible; a practice may be illegal but dismissed as "victimless." Legal Responsibility should be clarified early in governance design (to understand what is mandatory), but it should not be the only basis for responsibility allocation.

### Category V: Forward-Looking & Stewardship Responsibility
Responsibility for prevention, risk management, care, maintenance, and future states. Unlike backward-looking accountability (who is responsible for what happened?), stewardship responsibility asks: who is responsible for ensuring that systems remain healthy, risks are managed, and future stakeholders are not harmed by present inaction?

Stewardship Responsibility is particularly neglected in organisations dominated by short-term metrics and incentives. A person may discharge their role-based duties (deliver the project, hit the quarterly target) while systematically neglecting stewardship (maintaining the underlying system, managing emerging risks, building long-term capability). This neglect often goes unnoticed until systems break down, at which point the steward is blamed retroactively. Explicitly assigning forward-looking duties and building accountability mechanisms for them is essential for long-term resilience.

### Category VI: Collective, Distributed & Systemic Responsibility
Responsibility borne by groups, organisations, or systems as a whole, not reducible to individuals alone. This category addresses many-hands effects, ecosystem-level accountability, and responsibility for designing the responsibility system itself (meta-responsibility). In complex systems, it is often impossible (and would be unfair) to assign individual responsibility for every failure. Instead, responsibility must be designed into the system: interfaces are clarified, handoffs are managed, escalation pathways are created, and feedback loops ensure that gaps are discovered and corrected.

Collective Responsibility also includes accountability for the responsibility system's own health: meta-responsibility is the duty to review whether responsibilities are being discharged, whether new gaps have emerged, whether power-duty alignments persist, and whether the system itself requires redesign.

| 7 | Contributory Responsibility | Partial causal responsibility where an agent's contribution is one among multiple factors; the outcome would not have occurred without this contribution, but other factors were also necessary. | Root-cause analysis; fault tree; contribution assessment; multi-factor failure; partial causation. | Sole focus on one contributor while systemic factors are ignored; one contributor is blamed while equally-causal contributors are excused; contribution is uncertain or disputed. |
| 8 | Enabling Responsibility | Responsibility for creating, maintaining, or tolerating conditions that facilitated or made possible an undesired outcome (e.g. under-resourcing, weak controls, poor training, lax oversight, deferred maintenance). | Condition audit; root-cause pointing to antecedent conditions; resource inventory; control assessment; competency register. | Enabler claims they "had no authority" to change conditions; enabler was unaware conditions were inadequate; enabling conditions are structural/normalised and thus invisible; no one is assigned responsibility for changing them. |
| 9 | Omission Responsibility | Responsibility for failing to act where there was a duty or clear expectation to do so (e.g. failure to escalate, failure to maintain, failure to monitor, failure to check/verify). | Escalation procedures; maintenance schedule; inspection protocol; monitoring thresholds; documented expectation. | Omitter was unaware of the duty; omitter faced conflicting pressures; escalation pathway was inaccessible; no one monitored whether duty was discharged; duty was assumed to be "someone else's job". |
| 10 | Foreseeability Responsibility | Responsibility for failing to anticipate harms that were reasonably foreseeable, given available information, expertise, tools, and the stakes involved. | Expertise register; risk assessment; comparable prior incidents; signal history; scenario planning materials. | Foreseeable harm was outside agent's expertise; agent lacked access to relevant information; harm was considered "unlikely" even though remotely possible; no systematic process for foresight; "black swan" defence invoked. |

# 4. Eight Dimensions of Responsibility

## 4.1 Dimensions Overview
Every responsibility can be profiled across eight cross-cutting dimensions. These dimensions explain why different stakeholders attribute responsibility differently, and where responsibility is likely to fail.

| # | Dimension | Question | Scale / values | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Temporal orientation | Is responsibility backward-looking (accountability for past) or forward-looking (stewardship for future)? | Backward ← → Forward | Backward focus creates blame cycles without prevention; forward focus requires different skills and cultures. |
| 2 | Formalisation | Is responsibility documented, clear, and formally allocated, or is it informal/assumed? | Informal ← → Formal | Informal responsibility is easier to deny; formal responsibility can become theatre without enforcement. |
| 3 | Strength | How strongly is responsibility enforced? Are there material consequences for breach? | Weak ← → Strong | Weak enforcement creates nominal responsibility; strong enforcement without fairness creates resentment and gaming. |
| 4 | Collectivity | Is responsibility borne by an individual, shared among a few, or distributed across a collective? | Individual ← → Collective | Individual responsibility is clear but may be unfair; collective responsibility is fair but vulnerable to diffusion. |
| 5 | Visibility | Is responsibility visible to all relevant stakeholders, or is it hidden or contested? | Hidden ← → Visible | Hidden responsibility enables denial; visible responsibility enables scrutiny and fairness. |
| 6 | Basis diversity | Is responsibility grounded in one basis (e.g. role only) or multiple bases (role + causal + moral + legal)? | Single ← → Multiple | Single basis is clear but fragile; multiple bases provide redundancy but can conflict. |
| 7 | Contestability | Can responsibility be disputed or challenged, or is it fixed and non-contestable? | Fixed ← → Contestable | Fixed responsibility is efficient but may be unfair; contestable responsibility is legitimate but slower. |
| 8 | Enforcement mechanism | Through which accountability structure is responsibility tested and enforced (hierarchical, professional, regulatory, public, contractual, peer, community)? | See 3.4 Accountability Interfaces | Different mechanisms suit different contexts; enforcement without legitimacy fails. |

# 5. Responsibility Dynamics (7 patterns over time)

## 5.1 Dynamics Patterns Table
| # | Dynamic pattern | Description | Warning signs | Remediation approach |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Responsibility Drift | Responsibilities are formally allocated but are gradually neglected or displaced by competing pressures; accountability mechanisms weaken; duties become nominal. | Stewardship fails; maintenance is deferred; escalations are ignored; post-mortems blame individuals but do not close gaps. | Refresh responsibility allocation; strengthen accountability; add monitoring of stewardship duties; escalate unowned risks. |
| 2 | Responsibility Dumping | Responsibility is pushed from a more powerful actor to a less powerful one; the less powerful actor lacks authority, resources, or capability to discharge it. | Frontline blamed for systemic failures; accountability flows to those least able to change outcomes; victims blamed. | Audit power-duty alignment; redesign accountability mechanisms; escalate systemic issues; protect vulnerable parties. |
| 3 | Responsibility Overload | One actor or unit is given too many responsibilities to discharge them effectively; quality suffers; burnout and error rates rise. | Failures increase disproportionately; person/unit is overwhelmed; mistakes blamed to individual rather than overload; attrition rises. | Redistribute responsibilities; add capacity; clarify priorities; reduce scope; add support structures. |
| 4 | Responsibility Activation | A responsibility that was dormant or nominal becomes active due to incident, external pressure, or leadership change; accountability mechanisms suddenly tighten. | Sudden enforcement; retrospective blame; person caught by newly-activated responsibility; "moving the goalposts" perception. | Communicate responsibility clearly ahead of activation; provide transition support; grandfather existing conduct; align accountability with capability. |
| 5 | Responsibility Contestation | Different stakeholders dispute who bears responsibility; conflicting norms, laws, or cultures produce competing claims. | Blame-shifting; stakeholder conflicts escalate; decisions are delayed pending resolution; ad-hoc adjudication. | Facilitate stakeholder dialogue; articulate decision rule for conflicts; escalate to values/legitimacy frameworks; build shared understanding. |
| 6 | Inter-Category Conflict | Responsibilities grounded in different bases (role vs causal vs moral vs legal) point in different directions; person is caught in conflicting duties. | Person follows one duty while violating another; loyalty conflicts; role conflicts; ethical dilemmas. | Map all bases; articulate priority rule; escalate to values/legitimacy; redesign structures to reduce conflicts; add decision support. |
| 7 | Responsibility-Incentive Misalignment | Responsibilities point one way but incentives (pay, promotion, recognition) point another way; people face perverse incentives to violate or ignore duties. | Stated responsibilities are not followed; people pursue incentivised outcomes instead; norms are circumvented; culture becomes cynical. | Align incentives with responsibilities; audit incentive structure; add intrinsic motivation; escalate to Governance and Power frameworks. |

# 6. Accountability Interface Types (7 types)

## 6.1 Accountability Interface Table
Accountability mechanisms are the structures through which responsibilities are tested, verified, and enforced. Different mechanisms suit different contexts.

| # | Interface type | Definition | When used | Strengths | Weaknesses | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hierarchical | Direct accountability upward through a line manager or formal authority; superior has power to sanction or reward. | Command-and-control organisations; safety-critical systems; clear authority relationships. | Direct, rapid feedback; clear consequences; suitable for urgent action. | Vulnerable to abuse; suppresses dissent; does not work if superior is part of problem. | Military command; corporate hierarchy; emergency response. |
| 2 | Professional | Accountability to professional peers and professional bodies (medical board, bar association, engineering council). Based on shared professional standards and ethics. | Regulated professions (medicine, law, accounting, engineering); fields with clear expertise. | Accountability to expertise rather than authority; peer review; legitimacy from profession. | Professional bodies may lack enforcement power; professions can be self-protective; standards may lag reality. | Medical malpractice boards; legal ethics; professional licensing. |
| 3 | Regulatory | Accountability through government oversight, inspection, enforcement, and penalty; regulator has statutory power. | Industries with public interest (utilities, financial services, safety-critical); harm-prone sectors. | Protects public interest; independent enforcement; creates universal standards. | Regulatory capture risk; rules become prescriptive rather than adaptive; regulator-regulated relationship can be adversarial. | Banking regulation; environmental enforcement; workplace safety inspectorates. |
| 4 | Public | Accountability through transparency and public scrutiny; reputation is the enforcement mechanism. | Elected officials; public institutions; companies seeking legitimacy; NGOs. | Harnesses transparency and reputational incentive; enables constituent/stakeholder voice; creates democratic accountability. | Public may lack expertise to judge; media can distort; reputation operates slowly; vulnerable to information control. | Elections; social media; investigative journalism; annual reports. |
| 5 | Contractual | Accountability through contracts and commercial relationships; non-performance triggers financial or reputational consequences. | Supplier relationships; outsourcing; partnerships; service agreements. | Clear terms; economic consequences; market discipline. | Power asymmetries can produce unfair contracts; small actors squeezed; moral hazard (supplier cuts corners). | Commercial contracts; SLAs; liability clauses; escrow arrangements. |
| 6 | Peer | Accountability within a peer group or team; norms enforcement through social pressure, feedback, and inclusion/exclusion. | Team-based work; professional guilds; research communities; communities of practice. | Motivation is partly intrinsic (respect of peers); flexible and adaptive; builds culture. | In-group bias; weak toward outsiders; peer pressure can drive conformity over integrity; cliques form. | Code review; academic peer review; team retrospectives; mentoring. |
| 7 | Community | Accountability to a community (local, organisational, stakeholder community); norms and values enforcement; legitimacy through voice and participation. | Participatory governance; community-based risk management; multi-stakeholder initiatives; place-based decisions. | Legitimacy from inclusion; accountability to affected parties; values alignment. | Slow decision-making; powerful minorities can dominate; decisions may be parochial; difficult at scale. | Community policing; watershed governance; indigenous decision-making; participatory budgeting. |

# 7. Application Protocol (7 steps — mandatory)

## 7.1 Seven-Step Application Protocol Table
| Step # | Step name | Action | Key question(s) | Outputs / artefacts |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Frame the responsibility problem | Define the stakes, stakeholders, time horizon, and the purpose (allocation, accountability design, remediation, learning). | 2 | Inventory current responsibilities | Elicit formal and informal responsibilities, policies, contracts, governance mandates, and de facto duties. | 3 | Classify with the 30-type taxonomy | Using the six meta-categories and 30 types, classify responsibilities explicitly. | 4 | Assess each responsibility on eight dimensions | For priority responsibilities, profile temporal orientation, formalisation, strength, collectivity, visibility, basis diversity, contestability, enforcement mechanism. | 6 | Redesign responsibilities and accountability | Propose revised allocations, decision rights, resourcing, and enforcement mechanisms. | 7 | Closure with residual unknowns | Record unresolved responsibility questions, governance gaps that persist, and implications for decisions and redesign. What should trigger re-review? | Residual Unknowns register; escalation recommendations; closure statement. |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (responsibility-specific generators)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how responsibility analysis fails) | Responsibility focus | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Many-Hands Effect | So many actors are involved that each assumes someone else is accountable; diffusion protects all and blames none. | Collective/Systemic responsibility, Interface responsibility. | Explicitly design collective duty structures; name a meta-responsible party; create escalation pathways for unowned risks. |
| 2 | Responsibility-Power Misalignment | Those with power to act do not hold corresponding duties; those bearing duty lack power to discharge it; accountability becomes unfair or impossible. | Role responsibility, Causal responsibility, Legal responsibility. | Audit power-duty alignment; reallocate duties toward those with power; grant power to those with duties; escalate systemic misalignments. |
| 3 | Figurehead Accountability | Publicly accountable individuals lack real operational responsibility; real responsibility is hidden deeper in system; frontline blame, leadership insulation. | Role responsibility, Hierarchical accountability interface. | Map actual responsibility behind figurehead; redistribute to reflect actual decision-makers; strengthen accountability mechanisms. |
| 4 | Boundary Handoffs | Responsibilities fall between organisational, jurisdictional, disciplinary, or contractual boundaries; no one owns interfaces. | Interface responsibility, Systemic responsibility, Distributed responsibility. | Create explicit Interface responsibility roles; design handoff protocols; establish escalation for boundary issues; audit boundaries regularly. |
| 5 | Responsibility Silence | Deliberate avoidance of explicit responsibility assignment to preserve operational flexibility or to hide power asymmetries; deniability is maintained. | All categories (especially Stewardship and Moral responsibility). | Make responsibility allocations explicit and documented; require sign-off; add transparency and protected dissent channels; escalate deliberate ambiguity. |
| 6 | Responsibility Overload | One actor or unit is given too many responsibilities to discharge them effectively; overload is masked by blaming the overloaded party. | Role responsibility, Task responsibility, Stewardship responsibility. | Audit responsibility load per actor; redistribute; add capacity; reduce scope; prioritise; clarify what is truly essential. |
| 7 | Temporal Short-Termism | Responsibilities are defined only for short-term outputs; long-term stewardship, prevention, and intergenerational effects are orphaned; drift and decay persist. | Stewardship responsibility, Risk management, Intergenerational responsibility, Forward-looking responsibility. | Explicitly assign forward-looking responsibilities; build monitoring for stewardship; add review cadence; align incentives with long-term outcomes. |
| 8 | Capability–Responsibility Gap | Responsibilities are assigned without aligning capability, resources, authority, or knowledge; discharge becomes impossible; blame follows. | All categories (especially Role and Forward-looking responsibility). | Audit responsibility-capability alignment; invest in capability; grant authority; resource duties; provide training; escalate persistent gaps. |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – mandatory)
| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it matters for responsibility | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I Perceptual | If no one is responsible for noticing weak signals, risks remain hidden until crystallised as failures. | Near-miss under-reporting; "surprise" incidents after ignored warnings; risk creep goes unnoticed. | Assign explicit responsibility for monitoring and signal detection; implement sampling and audits; create protected channels for reporting. |
| II Systemic | Responsibility can be allocated around misleading models, metrics, or system designs; responsibility appears allocated but is actually subverted by system architecture. | "Dashboard says green" while harms accumulate; metrics drive wrong behaviour; system design makes some responsibilities impossible. | Audit responsibility-system alignment; disaggregate metrics; model audit; test for metric gaming; strengthen independent validation. |
| III Informational | Accountability systems can incentivise secrecy, editing, fragmentation, or distortion of information; true responsibility becomes invisible. | Missing logs; filtered reporting; silos prevent integration; information gatekeeping protects some and blames others. | Access audits; chain-of-custody for evidence; protected disclosure routes; cross-functional integration checks; transparency requirements. |
| IV Temporal | Responsibilities drift; stewardship is neglected; past lessons decay; learning is not operationalised; refresh cadence is insufficient. | Repeat failures; control erosion; stewardship failure; knowledge loss across transitions; lessons not acted upon. | Set refresh cadence for responsibility reviews; monitor stewardship indicators; legacy ownership mapping; learning capture and application. |
| V Relational | Power and position filter whose responsibilities are visible, enforced, and contested; responsibility is selectively applied; vulnerable parties are scapegoated. | Responsibility enforcement is biased by power; frontline blamed; senior leaders insulated; retaliation for raising responsibility issues. | Power–duty alignment checks; multi-level inquiry; retaliation detection and protection; hold powerful parties accountable; fair enforcement. |
| VI Ontological | New risk classes or responsibility types lack owners because categories or structures do not yet exist; emerging risks are unowned. | "We didn't know what to call it" (e.g. cyber risk, AI governance); ownerless emerging risks; new stakeholder classes lack voice. | Category-creation and naming; provisional ownership assignment; scenario exploration; stakeholder inclusion; adaptive governance. |

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full Hiddens run)
Escalate to a full 30-type Hiddens scan + comprehensive responsibility analysis if any apply:
- High consequence (safety, systemic resilience, regulatory exposure, trust)
- Strong incentives to conceal or to perform accountability theatre
- Persistent disputes over "who owned what" across levels/functions/organisations
- Repeat failures where post-mortems assign blame but do not close responsibility gaps
- Evidence of cascades (e.g., Many-Hands → Figureheading → Blame shifting → Mirage of accountability)
- Responsibility for critical forward-looking duties (stewardship, risk management, anticipation) is unclear or unowned

# 9. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 9.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| **Multi-Basis Clarity** | Responsibilities should be explicit about their basis (role, causal contribution, moral evaluation, law, stewardship, or systemic design). Conflicts between bases should be surfaced and adjudicated, not hidden. |
| **Power–Duty Alignment** | Duties must be aligned with actual capability and power. Responsibility without capability is fiction; power without duty is a hazard and source of injustice. |
| **Accountability Makes Responsibility Real** | Responsibility allocations require verification, enforcement, and feedback mechanisms; without these, responsibility becomes theatre and accountability remains illusory. |
| **Many-Hands Requires Design** | Collective and systemic duties must be designed explicitly to prevent diffusion, dumping, and unowned gaps. Interface responsibility and meta-responsibility are not optional in complex systems. |
| **Responsibility Includes the Unseen** | Assign explicit responsibility for anticipating, detecting, and responding to Hiddens and latent conditions. Forward-looking stewardship is as important as backward-looking accountability. |
| **Legitimacy Matters** | Responsibility allocations that lack legitimacy (are seen as unjust, unfair, or imposed) will not be sustained. Build legitimacy through stakeholder voice, fairness, and alignment with values. |
| **Responsibility Decays Without Maintenance** | Meta-responsibility and governance review are essential. Responsibilities drift, mechanisms weaken, and new gaps emerge without conscious maintenance and refresh. |

# 10. Glossary (recommended; mandatory for "Stable" status)

## 10.1 Glossary Table
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Responsibility** | A structured obligation for an agent (individual, role, organisation, or system) to perform, ensure performance of, or refrain from certain actions, for specified stakeholders, on some basis (role, causal, moral, legal, stewardship, systemic), over some time horizon, with verifiable accountability mechanisms. |
| **Accountability** | The requirement for an agent to explain and justify how they have discharged responsibilities, to specified forums or stakeholders, often with potential sanctions or rewards. Accountability mechanisms make responsibility real. |
| **Role Responsibility** | Responsibility that attaches to a formal position or office, independent of the individual holder. |
| **Causal Responsibility** | Responsibility tied to actual causal contribution to an outcome or failure. |
| **Moral Responsibility** | Responsibility grounded in blameworthiness or praiseworthiness, considering intent, knowledge, capacity, and alternatives. |
| **Legal Responsibility** | Responsibility defined by law, regulation, contract, fiduciary duty, or institutional rules, enforced through formal legal processes. |
| **Stewardship Responsibility** | Responsibility to safeguard, maintain, and improve systems or institutions over time; forward-looking and long-horizon. |
| **Collective Responsibility** | Responsibility borne by a group or collective as such, not reducible to individuals alone. |
| **Meta-Responsibility** | Responsibility for designing, maintaining, and improving the responsibility system itself. |
| **Responsibility Gap** | A situation where important duties are unowned, diffused, or unclear; "no one's job." |
| **Many-Hands Effect** | A hiding mechanism where responsibility is so widely distributed that each person assumes someone else will act; diffusion enables inaction. |
| **Responsibility-Power Misalignment** | A systemic condition where those with authority lack corresponding duties, or those bearing duty lack power to discharge it. |
| **Accountability Theatre** | Nominal accountability: responsibility allocations and enforcement mechanisms that appear real but lack substance or fair consequences. |
| **Accountability Interface** | A structural mechanism (hierarchical, professional, regulatory, public, contractual, peer, community) through which responsibilities are tested and enforced. |
| **Hiddens** | Relevant facts, risks, responsibilities, or dynamics that are present but not seen, not acknowledged, or not acted upon. |
| **Latent Conditions** | Existing but dormant system features (design flaws, cultural norms, misaligned incentives, resource constraints) that combine with triggers to produce failures. |
| **Responsibility for Hiddens** | Explicit duty to anticipate, detect, escalate, and address Hiddens and latent conditions before they crystallise into failures. |

## Appendix A: Notes on Rapid Implementation (optional reference)

For Rapid Run Mode (1–2 hour analysis):
1. Identify key actors and stakeholders (30 min)
2. Scan six meta-categories for obvious gaps (30 min)
3. Spot major many-hands problems and power misalignments (15 min)
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan (15 min)
5. Propose 3–5 redesign moves (20 min)
6. Record residual unknowns and escalation decision (10 min)

For Full Depth analysis (4–8 hours):
1. Complete stakeholder and governance mapping (60 min)
2. Classify all responsibilities using 30-type taxonomy (90 min)
3. Dimensional profiling of priority responsibilities (60 min)
4. Responsibility dynamics and Hiddens Source Analysis (60 min)
5. Run Tier 1 and Tier 2 Hiddens scans with ownership mapping (90 min)
6. Redesign and governance change proposals (60 min)
7. Closure with residual unknowns, escalation, and next review (30 min)

For post-incident learning:
1. Map what responsibility allocations existed at time of incident (reality, not aspirational)
2. Identify which responsibilities were discharged, which were missed, which were diffused
3. Assign explicit responsibility for detecting the latent conditions pre-incident
4. Redesign to close gaps and assign meta-responsibility for prevention of similar failures
5. Track closure over next review cycle
