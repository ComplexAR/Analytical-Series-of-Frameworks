## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Trust framework identifies patterns of confidence and dependence that actors extend to each other, institutions, and information sources. It classifies trust mechanisms into six meta-categories and thirty core types operating across three registers: interpersonal (individual relationships), institutional (organisations and systems), and epistemic (knowledge sources). It maps trust basis portfolios, asymmetries, and recovery capacity; surfaces visibility failures created by trust-based information filtering, cascade-collapse risks, and trust-halo misalignment. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with trust-structural analysis, enabling downstream frameworks to design trust-conscious interventions and detect where distrust creates information suppression.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trust Accumulation | Repeated positive interactions and predictable reliability build confidence incrementally. | Small positive experiences accumulate; consistency is observed; predictability enables increased delegation. | Trust accumulation is slow but resilient; relationships weather occasional failures if general pattern is positive. |
| Trust Erosion | Repeated small failures and inconsistencies erode confidence incrementally; trust decreases faster than it accumulates. | Small failures accumulate faster than forgiveness; inconsistencies create doubt; predictability declines. | Erosion is invisible until sudden collapse; relationships can drift into low-trust states without dramatic breach. |
| Trust-Basis Substitution Attempts | One trust basis becomes unavailable; compensation through other bases is attempted without addressing the gap. | Institutional safeguards invoked to compensate for individual unreliability; rhetoric invokes trust without evidence. | Substitution masks real problem and delays necessary response; masks eventual collapse when compensating basis also fails. |
| Cascade-Collapse from Asymmetric Breach | One party violates asymmetrically-granted trust; breach cascades through networks of delegated dependence. | A trusts B and delegates; B acts against A's interests; A must reconsider trust in everyone A delegated to B. | Cascade collapses multiply damage through networks; destroy credibility beyond initial breach; reveal hidden asymmetric dependencies. |
| Epistemic Cascade through Trust Chains | Information trusted through chains of sources with no independent verification; false information propagates as apparent consensus. | Source A claims X; Source B trusts A and relays X; Source C trusts B and relays X; no independent verification at any stage. | Cascades shape belief across networks without detection until external reality contradicts; hide that disagreement may exist. |
| Trust-Halo Generalization | Earned competence in one domain assumed to extend to unrelated domains without new evidence. | Expertise in domain A assumed to apply to domain B; reputation in service A used to support claims about service B. | Halo effects enable rapid scaling but hide competence gaps until failure; create vulnerability to novel challenges. |
| Betrayal-Amnesia & Pattern Repetition | Prior trust breaches are forgotten by leadership; institutional memory loss recreates same vulnerabilities. | Leadership transition loses knowledge of prior breach; new leaders assume trust is warranted; prior warnings not consulted. | Amnesia enables preventable repeat failures; each repetition depletes trust capital faster because pattern is visible. |
| Upward-Filtering Spiral & Information Distortion | Subordinates filter bad news to protect relationships; information becomes distorted as it travels up; leaders lose touch with reality. | Junior staff withhold bad news; managers filter to look good; executives receive sanitised information; culture punishes bad-news bearers. | Filtering can destroy institutional capacity to respond to crises; leaders become detached from reality; blindness persists. |
| Recovery-Facilitated Relationship Deepening | When trust is breached and genuinely repaired, recovery deepens trust beyond pre-breach levels. | Breach is acknowledged; repair is thorough; both parties engage in recovery; dynamics become explicit; mutual understanding deepens. | Genuine recovery builds resilience and deeper relationships; requires both parties' commitment; relationships often become more durable. |
| Fragility-Without-Visibility | Trust extended based on limited information; fragility is invisible until crisis reveals the gap. | Trust assumed due to proximity without testing; institutional safeguards assumed without verification; fragility invisible until crisis. | Invisible fragility enables catastrophic surprise; early warning signs missed because reality-testing absent; false confidence persists. |
| Distrust-Driven Information Suppression | Distrust prevents information sharing even when sharing would serve both parties. | Community fears information will be used against them; employees fear retaliation; stakeholders fear misinterpretation. | Distrust-driven suppression hides critical information and prevents response to problems; feedback deserts form. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | Which trust assumptions are agents making without testing current reliability or basis validity? |
| II (Systemic) | How do trust-based dependencies create coupling between otherwise independent systems, and where are cascade risks hidden? |
| III (Informational) | What information is agents filtering, hoarding, or distorting because of trust/distrust dynamics or information asymmetries? |
| IV (Temporal) | How are trust breaches being forgotten, trust-halo generalizations persisting despite disconfirming evidence, or trust erosion proceeding invisibly? |
| V (Relational) | Which asymmetric trust structures enable one party to control information, delegate without accountability, or exercise hidden influence? |
| VI (Ontological) | What trust mechanisms are being treated as inevitable or neutral while actually reflecting specific choices about who has power to earn trust? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Trust-Halo Misalignment (competence in one domain assumed in unrelated domains without testing)
- Cascade-Collapse Risk (hidden dependencies in trust networks create orphan risks when trust is violated)
- Upward-Filtering Invisibility (bad news suppressed at each hierarchical level; leaders lose situational awareness)
- Asymmetric Vulnerability Invisibility (one-sided dependence creates hidden influence that party with power may not acknowledge)
- Epistemic Cascade Invisibility (false consensus appears from information-chain effects without independent verification)
- Fragility-Without-Visibility (trust is extended despite brittle foundations; fragility invisible until breach or crisis)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trust-halo effects generalising across unrelated domains | Framework of Competencies; Framework of Evidence | Capability assessment in new domains; reality-testing before delegation | Explicit competence validation in target domain; restraint in claiming cross-domain expertise |
| Cascade-collapse risk from asymmetric trust relationships | Framework of Institutions; Framework of Risk | Dependency mapping; vulnerability assessment; redundancy design | Identify hidden delegation chains; establish redundancy in critical functions; transparency about dependencies |
| Upward-filtering spiral suppressing bad news | Framework of Communications; Framework of Diagnosis | Psychological safety audit; information-flow transparency; independent monitoring | Reward honest reporting; multiple reporting channels; protection of whistleblowers; independent validation |
| Epistemic cascade creating false consensus | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Epistemics | Information-provenance mapping; diversity-of-sources requirement; independent verification protocols | Require independent verification of critical claims; expose information-chain structure; protect dissenting voices |
| Fragility-without-visibility in critical relationships | Framework of Institutions; Framework of Risk | Stress-testing of trust assumptions; competence validation; monitoring of warning signs | Proactive reality-testing; validation in high-consequence domains; feedback seeking; early warning systems |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Map focal trust relationships (interpersonal, institutional, epistemic) that are decision-critical in this scenario; identify who trusts whom on what basis.
2. For each focal relationship, assess whether trust basis is sound: Is reliability track record tested? Is competence real? Is independence verified? Is information access genuine?
3. Identify obvious asymmetries: Who depends on whom? Who controls information? Who has hidden delegation? Where is one-sided vulnerability?
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six Hiddens meta-categories lightly; flag trust-halo misalignments, cascade risks, filtering spirals, information cascades, and fragility without visibility.
5. Shortlist 2–3 highest-consequence trust Hiddens; note whether asymmetries are visible to both parties or hidden from one side; escalate if high-consequence and high-uncertainty.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Inventory focal trust relationships across three registers (interpersonal, institutional, epistemic); classify by meta-category and core type (I–VI × 5).
2. For each focal relationship, assess trust-basis portfolio: which types of trust are relied on? Which are absent or weak? Assess diversity (portfolio resilience).
3. Assess five cross-cutting dimensions for each relationship: Symmetry (who depends on whom?), Visibility (is asymmetry visible to both?), Recovery Capacity (can trust be rebuilt?), Volatility (is trust stable or eroding?), Duration (is trust inherited, earned, or transitional?).
4. Analyse all eleven dynamic patterns; assess which are active (accumulation, erosion, basis-substitution, cascade risk, filtering, halo effects, amnesia, recovery potential, fragility, suppression).
5. Map cascade-collapse risks: where would information breach, competence failure, or integrity violation propagate damage through networks?
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan (structured deepening): for each high-relevance Hidden (halo misalignment, cascade risk, filtering spiral, epistemic cascade, fragility, asymmetry invisibility), identify mechanism, detectability, consequence; assign remediation interfaces.
7. Document unresolved Unknowns about trust legitimacy, competence grounding, and recovery potential; apply Escalation Rule; generate Residual Unknowns register.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add trust-structural Hiddens: asymmetry invisibility, cascade risks, filtering spirals, halo effects, fragility; tag by meta-category and register (interpersonal/institutional/epistemic). | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate cascade risks and high-consequence asymmetries to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record trust-relationship classification, basis portfolio, dimension assessments, dynamic pattern presence, evidence of competence/integrity/reliability. | After inventory and dimensional profiling; note gaps requiring trust testing (reference checks, competence validation, integrity assessment). |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about trust legitimacy (is basis sound?), asymmetry consequences, cascade risks; propose tests (competence validation, stress-testing assumptions, reality-testing). | Identified Unknowns; residual Hiddens from Tier 1 scan; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request trust basis evidence (track records, competence validation, independence verification); request information about cascade dependencies and recovery history. | To reduce Unknowns and validate trust diagnosis; coordinate with Evidence and Institutions frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we mapped focal trust relationships, assessed trust-basis legitimacy, identified asymmetries and cascade risks, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan across all six meta-categories, and escalated high-consequence trust Unknowns and cascade risks to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Institutions, Communications, Risk)?

## 5 Cross-Cutting Dimensions (Quick Assessment)

| Dimension | What to assess | Range / Values | How to determine quickly |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Trust basis mix** | Which types are relied on; compatibility | Single-basis (brittle) → Compatible portfolio → Conflicting → Substitution attempts → Non-substitutable constraints | List what each party claims to trust on; identify conflicts and gaps |
| **Relationship asymmetry** | Who depends on whom; is trust mutual? | Mutual & equal → Asymmetric (A trusts B, not vice versa) → Highly asymmetric → Polarised distrust | Ask each party: do you trust them, in what domain, on what basis? Who would suffer more if trust broke? |
| **Epistemic access** | Who can verify claims independently; information transparency | Equal access → Asymmetric access → Fragmented → Opaque | Who controls key information? Can people verify independently? What happens if someone asks? |
| **Temporal character** | Is trust inherited, earned, eroding, recovering? | Inherited (legacy) → Earned (active) → Borrowed (crisis) → Eroding (declining) → Recovering → Volatile | How long has relationship lasted? What is confidence trajectory over time? |
| **Recovery potential** | Can relationship rebuild after breach? | High (recovery demonstrated) → Moderate → Low → Impossible → Unknown | Have prior breaches been repaired? What is commitment to recovery? |

## 6 Hiddens Source Analysis (What makes trust analysis fail?)

| Generator | Pattern of failure | How to correct |
|---|---|---|
| **Competence Assumption** | Credential/title treated as proof of current competence without domain-specific reality-testing | Reality-test competence in specific domain; assess how person handles tasks beyond capability |
| **Integrity Halo** | Good character in one domain assumed across all domains; moral character treated as universal | Assess integrity specifically where trust is extended; test under temptation and pressure in that domain |
| **Relationship Substitution** | Good personal relationship assumed to substitute for institutional safeguards or competence verification | Separate relational quality from institutional design; use structure for critical functions, not relationship |
| **Single-Source Trust** | Assume trusted source is reliable across all claims without independent verification | Require independent verification of critical claims; build epistemic diversity; trace provenance |
| **Asymmetry Invisibility** | Assume trust is mutual when asymmetry actually exists; miss one-sided dependence and filtering | Explicitly map who depends on whom; assess information flow both directions; make asymmetries visible |
| **Temporal Amnesia** | Assume trust holds stably without monitoring; forget prior breaches; treat inherited trust as permanent | Monitor trust trajectory; track early warning signals; maintain institutional memory of lessons; periodic testing |

## Escalation Triggers (When to escalate to Tier 3)

Escalate to full Hiddens taxonomy run if any apply:
- High-consequence decisions (safety, governance, existential risk)
- Adversarial context and strategic concealment likely
- Persistent disagreement across credible sources
- Repeat failures despite prior interventions
- Cascades/reinforcement suspected across hidden types
- Trust is fragile, actively contested, polarising
- Material risks to institutional function or relationship viability
