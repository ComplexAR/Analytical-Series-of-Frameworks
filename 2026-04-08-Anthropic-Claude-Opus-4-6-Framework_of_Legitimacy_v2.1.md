# Introduction

## What is Legitimacy?

Legitimacy concerns the recognised rightfulness of power, action, and authority. It is distinct from effectiveness (whether something works), legality (whether it complies with rules), or raw power (whether it can be enforced). Legitimacy is fundamentally relational: it is conferred by audiences under standards that vary across groups and contexts. Because legitimacy shapes cooperation, compliance, and resistance, it often determines whether policies, programmes, and interventions succeed, fail, or trigger backlash.

Legitimacy operates as a portfolio. A single act may be legal but morally wrong, or culturally consonant but procedurally unfair. Effective authority typically invokes multiple bases simultaneously and manages audience divergence. When one basis erodes, others can provide resilience—or fail to compensate if constraints are non-substitutable. This framework provides a structured way to map legitimacy bases, audiences, and dynamics so that legitimacy risks can be detected early and addressed through portfolio interventions before crisis occurs.

## Why does Legitimacy matter for Hiddens work?

Legitimacy is a primary hiding mechanism in organisations and systems. When authority is unquestioned, bad news about legitimacy risks is suppressed. Compliance theatre masks procedural gaps. Performance claims substitute for actual remedies. Dissent is delegitimised as disloyalty. Institutional status creates immunity from challenge. Information environments become fragmented, so different stakeholders hear different rationales and lack shared ground for contestation. Alternative authority claims are dismissed as irrational or illegitimate.

The Hiddens meta-categories (I: Perceptual; II: Systemic; III: Informational; IV: Temporal; V: Relational; VI: Ontological) all manifest acutely in legitimacy contexts. Legitimacy disputes are often framed as factual disagreements when the underlying conflict is over standards and bases. Temporal distortion hides historical legitimacy breaches and their accumulated consequences. Positional blindness prevents recognition of groups excluded from voice. Shadow bargaining moves real legitimacy decisions into backchannels while formal processes remain performative. Category confusion means actors debate compliance when the real issue is social licence, or invoke legality when rights are at stake.

This framework integrates Hiddens discovery directly into legitimacy analysis through tiered scans (Tier 1 across all six Hiddens categories, Tier 2 deepening of top candidates, Tier 3 escalation for high-stakes contests) and targeted scans for legitimacy-specific hiding mechanisms (narrative capture, legitimacy theatre, suppression of alternative claims). Early warning signals are often visible in Hiddens signals: unspoken grievances, compliance without commitment, selective information, distorted narratives.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework produces a multipart legitimacy map. First, a **types taxonomy** across six meta-categories (Legal, Moral, Cultural, Procedural, Performance, Relational) with thirty operationally distinct types, enabling practitioners to diagnose which bases are relied upon, which are missing, and where contestation is likely. Second, a **five-dimensional profile** (source basis mix, audience scope and alignment, contestability and resilience, depth of internalisation, temporal character) that summarises legitimacy's structural properties in any case. Third, a **dynamics and interface audit** that identifies patterns of erosion, substitution, divergence, and crisis; maps legitimacy to three key interfaces where failures concentrate (formal authority, stakeholder recognition, public narrative); and assigns remediation actions.

Fourth, the framework produces a **tiered Hiddens cross-check** (Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens categories with detection checks; Tier 2 mapping of top 10–15 Hiddens candidates to mechanisms, detectability, agency, consequence, and remediation interfaces; Tier 3 escalation rule for high-stakes contests). Fifth, it produces an **intervention portfolio** addressing non-substitutable constraints directly, upgrading participation and remedy systems, aligning narratives with traceable reasons, and managing multi-audience divergence. Finally, it produces a **monitoring dashboard** with leading indicators (trust, grievances, compliance patterns, sentiment, signal anomalies) and escalation triggers, plus a **closure note** with residual unknowns, acceptability rationale, ownership, monitoring plan, and learning hook.

## How to use this framework

This framework is invoked when legitimacy is contested, when governance is failing due to acceptance questions, when authority is challenged, or when post-failure investigations need to understand why compliance or cooperation broke down. It is best suited to Investigative Run Mode for complex or high-stakes analysis; Rapid Run Mode for triage and early-stage diagnosis. The framework is routinely paired with Power (G3), Governance (G6), Culture & Norms (G4), Narratives (G4), Communications (G4), and Hiddens (G5).

Default execution depth is Light Depth Framework Execution (run the meta-category scan, dimension profile, and a short Tier 1 Hiddens check). Full Depth Framework Execution is triggered when routing decision logic at OG §4.3 indicates high consequence, weak/contested evidence, audience divergence, or cascade potential. The framework sits downstream of Power, Incentives, Culture & Norms, and Narratives (which feed inputs about authority structures, incentives, meaning, and communication systems), and upstream of Decisions, Governance, Risk, and Learning & Adaptation (which consume legitimacy diagnostics and intervention designs).

Within the unified iteration loop (OG §5.0: Initialise → Generate → Route → Execute → Test → Re-scan → Decide/Close), this framework contributes to the Route phase (determining if legitimacy is in scope), to the Execute phase (conducting the types/dimensions/dynamics/Hiddens analysis), to the Test phase (running legitimacy stress tests and participation trials), and to the Decide/Close phase (crafting interventions and setting monitoring thresholds). Tier 1 Hiddens scans must run at least twice: early (before main framework execution) and pre-closure (after tests and table population); if symptoms point to specific hiding mechanisms, invoke Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scans from OG §7.5.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise
Legitimacy concerns the rightfulness of power, action, and authority. It is not the same as effectiveness, legality, or raw power. Legitimacy is conferred by audiences under standards, and those standards can differ across groups and contexts. Because legitimacy shapes cooperation, compliance, and resistance, it often determines whether policies, programmes, and interventions succeed, fail, or trigger backlash. This framework provides a structured way to map legitimacy bases, audiences, and dynamics so that legitimacy risks can be detected early and addressed with appropriate interventions.

Legitimacy operates as a portfolio of bases: legal, moral, cultural, procedural, performance, and relational. Single bases alone cannot sustain authority in complex environments. Tension between bases is common and often generative of contestation. When one basis erodes (for example, trust in performance), others can provide resilience—but some constraints are non-substitutable (rights violations cannot be compensated by superior outcomes). Audience divergence is structural: different groups use different standards, occupy different positions, and have different access to information. Durable legitimacy requires explicit recognition of divergence and portfolio interventions that address distinct audiences and their specific standards.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

- **Legitimacy (operational definition):** The recognised rightfulness of an authority, decision, rule, or action, as conferred by one or more audiences under one or more standards.
- **In scope:**
  - Six legitimacy meta-categories and thirty legitimacy types
  - Five dimensions for profiling legitimacy in any case (basis mix, audience scope, contestability, depth, temporal character)
  - Legitimacy dynamics (accumulation, erosion, basis substitution, cross-audience divergence, crisis lending, delegation chains)
  - Key interfaces where legitimacy failures concentrate (Type A: formal authority; Type B: stakeholder recognition; Type C: public narrative)
  - Hiddens sources plus tiered Hiddens cross-check (Tier 1 six-category scan, Tier 2 deepening, Tier 3 escalation)
  - Application protocol and standard deliverables (claim statement, basis-by-audience map, dimension profile, dynamics audit, intervention plan, monitoring dashboard)
  - Closure discipline with residual unknowns, acceptability rationale, monitoring plan, ownership, and learning hook
- **Out of scope:**
  - Full legal advice (use domain counsel; treat legal basis here as one analytic dimension)
  - Full moral philosophy adjudication (use as structured input; do not assume universal agreement on values)
  - Full communications strategy (use Communications framework jointly; legitimacy requirements must drive content and process)
  - Detailed Hiddens taxonomy (reference Framework of Hiddens for full 30-type taxonomy; this framework focuses on legitimacy-specific generators and mechanisms)
- **Common confusions:**
  - Legitimacy is not legality: an action can be legal but delegitimised by moral, procedural, or cultural failures.
  - Legitimacy is not compliance: coerced obedience differs from endorsed authority; distinguish toleration from recognition.
  - Legitimacy is not effectiveness: an effective outcome cannot compensate for procedural injustice or rights violations.
  - Legitimacy is not consensus: it is recognition by materially relevant audiences, not universal agreement.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions

### I. Legal–Formal Legitimacy
- **Definition:** Authority and action are legitimate when they derive from and conform to formal rules, legal procedures, and the rule of law—including constitutions, statutes, charters, delegation instruments, and properly applied administrative procedures.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Reliance on formal authority, written mandate, legal justification, regulatory compliance, and procedural compliance arguments. Often framed as "we have legal authority to do this."
- **Typical failure mode:** Legalism (asserting that formal legality is sufficient for legitimacy) without attention to moral, cultural, procedural, or performance-based contestation. Scope creep, procedure-over-substance, boundary overreach.

### II. Moral–Substantive Legitimacy
- **Definition:** Authority and action are legitimate when they are substantively right by relevant moral standards (justice, rights, harm reduction, integrity, proportionality, non-corruption) regardless of formal legality.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Arguments about justice, fairness, rights, welfare, integrity, and proportionality. Moral claims often activate when legal authority is exercised in ways audiences find ethically wrong.
- **Typical failure mode:** Moralism (assuming a single universal moral standard or downplaying plural values and audience disagreement). Rights violations justified by outcomes. Disproportionate force claimed as necessary.

### III. Traditional & Cultural Legitimacy
- **Definition:** Authority and action are legitimate when they conform to or respect cultural traditions, identity narratives, sacred authority, historical continuity, and local embeddedness.
- **Diagnostic cues:** References to custom, heritage, identity, ritual, spiritual authority, and local embeddedness. Often invoked to defend continuity and resist change, or to mobilise identity-based support.
- **Typical failure mode:** Instrumentalisation of tradition to resist reform; sacred claims used selectively; loss of meaning when cultural context shifts; dismissal of newcomers or outsiders as threatening identity.

### IV. Procedural & Participatory Legitimacy
- **Definition:** Authority and action are legitimate when decision processes are fair: they provide voice, transparent reasoning, representation of affected parties, opportunities for contestation, and accessible remedies.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Process arguments: "we consulted widely," "we followed due process," "people had a chance to object." Procedural justice requires fairness both in form and in lived experience.
- **Typical failure mode:** Checkbox compliance (procedures on paper only); tokenistic participation (processes designed to confirm predetermined outcomes); slow/inaccessible remedies; decisions moving to shadow channels.

### V. Performance & Output Legitimacy
- **Definition:** Authority and action are legitimate when they demonstrably deliver valued outcomes (problem-solving, security, reliability, competence, responsiveness) over time.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Arguments about track record, capability, service delivery, effectiveness, and learned improvement. Earned legitimacy through demonstrated competence and results.
- **Typical failure mode:** Short-term wins masking structural fragility; success claimed without counterfactual assessment; sudden delegitimisation when performance fails; performance claims used to override other bases.

### VI. Relational & Contested Legitimacy
- **Definition:** Authority and action are legitimate when they are recognised by relevant audiences, and recognised bases are maintained across different groups; encompasses contestation, rival claims, and relational justice.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Recognition signals (trust, compliance, cooperation), contestation signals (grievances, protests, withdrawal), and relational quality (respect, dignity, impartiality in everyday interaction).
- **Typical failure mode:** Assuming consensus where divergence exists; missing early warning signals; elite consensus masking community rejection; lack of attention to relational experience and lived fairness.

Who bears harms? | Short-term welfare gains justify long-term erosion of trust. Harms are externalised to low-voice stakeholders. Evidence of benefit is overstated; evidence of harm is suppressed. |
| 9 | Integrity & Non-Corruption Legitimacy | Legitimacy grounded in honesty, anti-corruption, fidelity to purpose, avoidance of conflicts of interest, and trustworthy decision-making. | Are incentives and decision rights structured to avoid capture and corruption, and are integrity controls credible? Is motive transparent? | Perceived or real conflicts of interest poison legitimacy even when outcomes are acceptable. Self-dealing. Hidden beneficiaries. Undisclosed incentives. |
| 10 | Proportionality & Restraint Legitimacy | Legitimacy based on using only necessary and proportionate means, especially regarding coercion, surveillance, punishment, or extraordinary power. | Is the action necessary, least intrusive, and proportionate to the threat or objective? Are clear sunset and oversight in place? | Overreaction, mission creep, or indefinite exceptionalism creates backlash and delegitimisation. Extraordinary powers become routine. Necessity is assumed without questioning. |

|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | Customary & Precedent-Based Legitimacy | Legitimacy grounded in customary practices and precedents: what has long been done and is recognised as rightful within a community or institution. | What precedent is being invoked, and is it relevant under current conditions and stakeholder composition? ## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy
| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | Preserve the 6×5 structure (30 types) as the shared vocabulary for cross-case comparability and integration across the Analytical Series. |
| Domain-specific refinement | Add domain-specific subtypes under each parent type (e.g., 'professional licensure legitimacy' under Legal–Formal; 'social licence for infrastructure projects' under Relational). Tag subtypes to parent IDs and maintain mappability. |
| Audience and value mapping | For every added subtype, record: (a) which audiences must recognise it, and (b) which value standards (legal, moral, cultural, procedural, performance, relational) apply to that subtype. |
| Multi-basis regimes | Model legitimacy as a portfolio: identify dominant bases, compensatory bases, and non-substitutable constraints (e.g., rights constraints that performance cannot buy off). |
| Temporal dynamics | Track how bases shift over time: inherited → earned → crisis-borrowed → eroding → recovering. Document path-dependent vulnerabilities. |
| Versioning & governance | Minor versions for wording/examples only; major versions for taxonomy changes (e.g., splitting a type, merging categories). Document mapping notes if types are refactored. |

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Legitimacy

## 4.1 Legitimacy Dimensions Table

|---|---|---|---|---|

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table
| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Accumulation, Erosion & Path-Dependence | Legitimacy builds slowly through repeated confirmation and erodes through repeated violations or perceived betrayal; once eroded, recovery is slow. | Trust reserves accumulate via consistent behaviour; violations create memory and narratives that constrain future legitimacy claims. Early breaches establish low-trust baseline that is hard to escape. | Explains why identical actions are accepted in one context and rejected in another. Explains why sudden failure after long success often occurs (accumulated micro-violations finally break trust). Informs recovery horizons. | Track legitimacy debt/credit explicitly. Make repairs visible and substantial. Avoid repeated small violations. Invest in reliability and remedy systems. Acknowledge historical breaches. |
| 2 | Basis Substitution & Compensatory Logics | Actors attempt to substitute one basis for another (e.g., performance used to compensate for procedural deficits; legal authority substituted for moral legitimacy). | Justifications shift as vulnerabilities are exposed. Some bases partially compensate; others are non-substitutable constraints (rights, due process). Attempts to substitute often fail and signal weakness. | Misdiagnosing substitutability leads to failed interventions (e.g., public relations in place of rights compliance; performance claims in place of transparency). | Identify non-negotiable constraints. Design portfolio interventions across bases. Avoid 'single lever' legitimacy strategies. Acknowledge where bases conflict. |
| 3 | Cross-Audience Divergence & Polarisation | Different audiences recognise different bases; legitimacy becomes segmented or polarised as audiences separate and rival claimants emerge. | Distinct value standards, information environments, and identities produce divergent recognition. Rivals exploit splits. Echo chambers harden positions. Information isolation grows. | Creates governance deadlock and contested authority. Interventions for one audience can delegitimise in another (e.g., police reform popular with communities, seen as capitulation by traditional supporters). | Explicitly map audience standards and positions. Segment messaging and process design thoughtfully. Build bridging institutions and cross-audience forums. Create boundary objects for translation. |
| 4 | Crisis, Exception & Re-Negotiation | Crisis temporarily increases tolerance for exceptional measures ('rally-around-the-flag'), then triggers legitimacy re-evaluation as fear subsides and exceptional costs accumulate. | Emergency legitimacy is lent under necessity (Tier 2 legitimacy). If safeguards, sunset, and proportionality are weak, legitimacy is depleted post-crisis. Publics expect return to normal; if exceptionalism persists, legitimacy plummets. | Explains post-crisis backlash and the 'window of opportunity' for reform. Anticipates legitimacy cliffs when exceptionalism persists. Underpins why emergency powers are so dangerous if not bounded. | Define clear sunset clauses and proportionality thresholds. Require independent oversight and public reasoning. Publish post-crisis accountability and learning plans. Build anticipation of return to normal. |
| 5 | Delegation, Proxy & Layered Legitimacy | Legitimacy flows through delegated chains and proxies (agents act 'on behalf of' principals); agents can spend or squander delegator's legitimacy; proxy behaviour feeds back to delegator credibility. | Delegators lend legitimacy to agents; agents can build or deplete it. If proxy behaviour diverges from stated values, delegator credibility is damaged. Accountability gaps arise. | Key for outsourcing, regulators, contractors, and leaders relying on frontline execution. Explains why bad frontline behaviour can delegitimise entire institutions. Explains where accountability gaps hide. | Clarify mandates and scope. Align incentives between principal and agent. Monitor proxy behaviour and public perception. Strengthen auditability and remedies. Set revocation mechanisms and regular mandate refresh. |
| 6 | Narrative Capture & Coordinated Messaging | A single narrative or messaging discipline displaces competing accounts; institutions converge on one story; alternative framings are suppressed or delegitimised. | Shared briefing, institutional pressure, reputational incentives, or deliberate coordination produce message discipline. Alternative narratives are ridiculed or suppressed. Appears as consensus but is enforced conformity. | Creates illusion of legitimacy (echo) while masking contestation and diversity. Brittle: when the narrative breaks, it can break suddenly. Prevents adaptation and course correction. | Diversify sources and independence of validation. Create protected spaces for dissent. Disclose sources and acknowledge competing interpretations. Rotate narrators and perspectives. Build legitimacy stress tests. |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how legitimacy analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Legalism (legality equals legitimacy) | Treat formal legality and procedural compliance as sufficient, ignoring moral, cultural, performance, and recognition-based contestation. Assume law creates legitimacy; discount non-legal bases and non-legal audiences. | Assess multi-basis portfolio explicitly. Map affected audiences and their standards. Test social licence signals alongside legal validity. Acknowledge that legality is one base among six; others are material. |
| 2 | Moralism (rightness equals legitimacy) | Assume a single moral standard determines legitimacy; discount plural values, audience disagreement about rightfulness, and cultural/procedural differences. Treat moral certainty as universal. | Make moral claims explicit and audience-bound. Compare competing value frameworks. Use deliberation and representation mechanisms. Acknowledge moral disagreement is structural, not ignorance. |
| 3 | Performance substitution fallacy | Assume outcomes can compensate for procedural or rights deficits; ignore non-substitutable constraints and long-run trust erosion. Treat effectiveness as sufficient justification. | Identify non-negotiable constraints (rights, due process, fairness). Separate short-run effectiveness from legitimacy resilience. Design repairs directly where needed; performance upgrades are secondary. |
| 4 | Single-audience assumption | Assume legitimacy is uniform across audiences; miss divergence, polarisation, and boundary-crossing recognition dynamics. Treat "stakeholders" as monolithic. | Map audiences, standards, and signals explicitly. Treat divergence as a primary risk. Design segmented processes and communications. Acknowledge plural audiences will have plural standards. |
| 5 | Static legitimacy view | Treat legitimacy as a fixed property rather than a time-varying relationship that accumulates, erodes, and can be borrowed in crisis. Ignore path dependence and temporal dynamics. | Reconstruct legitimacy timelines. Track legitimacy debt/credit. Define crisis safeguards, sunsets, and recovery actions. Acknowledge temporal character of legitimacy. |
| 6 | Hiddens omission | Assume contestation is visible and good-faith. Hidden filtering, secrecy, distortion, and shadow bargaining remain untested, producing false stability. Miss legitimacy-based hiding mechanisms entirely. | Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan systematically across all six meta-categories. Shortlist Tier 2 hidden types and attach detection/remediation actions to legitimacy interfaces (Type A/B/C). Escalate if mechanisms are active. |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts; per OG §7.2)
1) **Tier 1 (Early scan):** Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (I–VI) as applied to legitimacy. Assume contestation can be hidden. Produce a short note on each category: why it might be active, what symptoms to look for, what checks to run. (OG §D.6.10)
2) **Shortlist and rate:** Shortlist ~10–15 candidate Hiddens from the Tier 2 crosswalk (§8.3). For each, rate: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence. (OG §D.6.10)
3) **Interface mapping:** Assign at least one detection interface (A/B/C) and one remediation interface to each high-consequence Hidden. Map interaction chains and mitigation dependencies. (OG §7.2)
4) **Hiddens source analysis:** Run §8.1 hiddens source analysis and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens. Define what evidence would reduce each Unknown. (OG §D.6.10)
5) **Targeted scan decision:** If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism (e.g., narrative capture, suppression, legitimacy theatre), invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan from OG §7.5; use Triage Matrix at OG §7.5.3 to select. (OG §7.5)
6) **Tier 1 re-run (Pre-closure):** After executing the framework protocol and available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas from early pass. Any new Hiddens discovered? | Reduced any Hiddens? Refined understanding? (OG §D.6.10)
7) **Produce closure artefacts:** Populate Residual Unknowns (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or remediation priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4 (five required items). (OG §7.4)

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

**Escalate to full 30-type Hiddens taxonomy run plus full detection/remediation mapping if any apply:**
- High-consequence decisions (safety, regulatory, existential, severe harm potential)
- Adversarial context and strategic concealment likely (evidence of deliberate hiding, cover-up, or narrative manipulation)
- Persistent disagreement or inconsistent evidence across credible sources
- Repeat failures despite prior interventions (pattern of failure suggests learning is hidden)
- Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement across hidden types (one hidden enables or masks others)
- Legitimacy is fragile, actively contested, or experiencing polarisation
- Material legitimacy risks to governance or social stability

**Tier 3 execution (per OG §D.6.10):**
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into the full 30-type Hiddens taxonomy (full-spectrum exploration).
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements for all claims; more explicit competing hypotheses; more rigorous disconfirming tests; independent corroboration required.
- When adversarial intent is plausible: maps concealment vectors (who benefits from hiding; what would reveal it); tests alternative narratives; builds independent corroboration chains.
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree branch A6 (OG §4.3); escalation and resourcing requirements increase.

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle | Description |
|---|---|
| **Treat legitimacy as a relationship, not a label** | Legitimacy is conferred by audiences under standards; do not assume it is inherent to the actor, action, or position. Always ask: conferred by whom, under what standards, and is recognition secure? |
| **Model legitimacy as a portfolio of bases** | Legal, moral, cultural, procedural, performance, and relational bases interact. Identify which are dominant and which are non-substitutable constraints. One basis cannot fully compensate for failure in another. |
| **Separate compliance from recognition** | Obedience can be coerced, instrumental, or normative. Distinguish toleration from endorsement and coerced compliance from willing commitment to avoid surprise defection and backlash. |
| **Prioritise procedural fairness and remedy systems** | Durable legitimacy requires accessible, credible remedies and due process that work in practice, not only on paper. Process fairness is often more important to audiences than outcomes. |
| **Avoid basis substitution errors** | Performance and messaging rarely compensate for rights violations, exclusion, or abusive processes. Identify what must be repaired directly rather than substituted. |
| **Design for multi-audience divergence** | Expect different audiences to use different standards and have different access to information. Explicitly manage divergences and avoid one-size-fits-all justifications. |
| **Use transparency with integrity** | Transparency should trace to real decisions and trade-offs; avoid performative disclosure that increases cynicism. Disclosed reasons must match actual reasons; contradictions erode trust. |
| **Plan for crisis legitimacy cliffs** | Emergency legitimacy is borrowed and time-limited. Build sunset clauses, independent oversight, and post-crisis accountability before the crisis peaks. Don't let emergency powers become normalised. |
| **Integrate Hiddens discovery into legitimacy work** | Legitimacy disputes often hide contestation and hiding mechanisms. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scans to surface what is being hidden about rightfulness, voice, information, and recognition. |
| **Establish early warning systems** | Legitimacy erosion is detectable before crisis. Monitor trust, grievances, compliance patterns, sentiment, signal anomalies, and relational quality. Act on early signals rather than waiting for backlash. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms; mandatory for "Stable" status)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary (Legitimacy terms)

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Legitimacy** | The recognised rightfulness of an authority, decision, rule, or action, as conferred by one or more audiences under one or more standards. Not the same as legality, effectiveness, or power. |
| **Legitimation** | The process of producing, sustaining, or repairing legitimacy through practices: rules, narratives, participation, performance, remedies, and relational engagement. |
| **Audience** | A group whose recognition materially affects whether an authority or action is accepted, resisted, complied with, or delegitimised. Affected stakeholders, citizens, workforce, regulators, peers, publics. |
| **Recognition** | Acceptance of a legitimacy claim by an audience, ranging from toleration (pragmatic acceptance without endorsement) to deep normative endorsement (belief in rightfulness) to identity-level internalisation (core to self/community identity). |
| **Contestation** | Active challenge, withdrawal, or denial of recognition. Includes rival claims, resistance, protest, refusal to cooperate, exit, or deflection. |
| **Legitimacy basis** | The standard invoked to justify rightfulness. Six meta-categories: legal–formal, moral–substantive, cultural–traditional, procedural–participatory, performance–output, relational–contested. |
| **Mandate** | Delegated authority to act on behalf of others, defined by scope, duration, and constraints. Basis for office-holding and delegation-chain legitimacy. |
| **Jurisdiction** | The recognised boundary of authority (geographic, functional, organisational, professional) within which an actor may legitimately act. |
| **Rule of law** | A governance condition in which power is constrained by public, general rules applied consistently with independent adjudication and due process protections. |
| **Procedural justice** | Fairness in process and interpersonal treatment: voice, neutrality, respect, and trustworthy decision-making. Experienced fairness is often more important to audiences than outcome fairness. |
| **Due process** | Procedural protections such as notice, hearing, appeal, impartial adjudication, and access to remedy. |
| **Social licence** | Informal, ongoing acceptance by affected communities and stakeholders beyond formal permits or legal compliance. Reflects whether community views authority as rightful. |
| **Legitimacy debt** | Accumulated erosion of recognition due to repeated violations, exclusion, broken promises, or perceived betrayal. Reduces future legitimacy space; makes future actions harder to justify. |
| **Legitimacy reserve** | Accumulated trust and acceptance that provides resilience and goodwill during shocks, difficult trade-offs, or performance failures. Trust reserves can be depleted rapidly. |
| **Exception / emergency power** | Extraordinary authority used under crisis conditions, legitimate only when necessary, proportionate, bounded in scope, and time-limited. Requires sunset, oversight, and post-crisis accountability. |
| **Delegation chain** | The sequence by which legitimacy flows through agents and proxies acting on behalf of principals, creating layered accountability and potential gaps. |
| **Counter-legitimacy** | A competing claim that an alternative actor, rule, norm, or narrative is the rightful authority. Rivals can be internal (dissidents) or external (challengers). |
| **Portfolio intervention** | A coordinated set of legitimacy repairs across multiple bases (e.g., legal compliance + participation + remedy + performance + narrative alignment). |
| **Basis mix** | The particular combination of legitimacy bases invoked or relied upon in a given situation; can be compatible, conflicting, or substitutive. |
| **Audience divergence** | Situation where different groups recognise different bases or standards, leading to plural legitimacy pictures and potential polarisation. |
| **Performative compliance** | Formal adherence to procedures while substance and spirit are violated. Compliance theatre: procedures observed but authority not genuinely justified. |
| **Authority filtering** | Systematic upward filtering of legitimacy risks and bad news to protect institutional legitimacy and reputation. |
| **Delegitimisation** | Active delegitimisation of dissent, alternative claims, or challenge through framing as disloyalty, irrationality, or threat. |

## 12.2 Integration Notes
Use this framework whenever:
- Resistance, backlash, or non-cooperation threatens programme or policy success.
- Legality is present but acceptance is weak (social licence risk).
- Moral claims are contested across audiences or cultures.
- Crisis conditions require exceptional measures (need to define sunset and safeguards).
- Legitimacy disputes are driving governance failure, polarisation, or institutional paralysis.
- You need to diagnose why compliance is selective or why trust has eroded.
- You are designing interventions and need to understand what makes them likely to be accepted.
- You suspect legitimacy-based hiding mechanisms are preventing early warning or remediation.
- You are conducting post-failure investigations and legitimacy is likely a factor.
