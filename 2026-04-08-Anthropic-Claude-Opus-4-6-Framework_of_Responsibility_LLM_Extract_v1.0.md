## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Responsibility framework identifies who is accountable for outcomes and decisions across six meta-categories (Role/Task, Causal/Outcome, Moral/Evaluative, Legal/Institutional, Forward-Looking/Stewardship, Collective/Systemic); classifies responsibilities into thirty core types; profiles each responsibility across eight dimensions (Basis, Scope, Directness, Time Horizon, Enforcement Strength, Power-Duty Alignment, Contestation, Visibility); maps seven dynamics patterns (Drift, Diffusion, Hiding, Transfer, Escalation, Erosion, Redesign); and surfaces hidden, denied, or misaligned responsibilities that prevent accountability and enable avoidance. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with responsibility-level blindnesses and governance redesign requirements, enabling downstream frameworks to allocate accountability aligned with true causation, power, and stewardship rather than formal role assignment.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Drift | Responsibility allocation changes gradually; role scope creeps or shrinks; standards degrade; expectations become misaligned. | Incremental changes, personnel turnover, policy evolution, informal reallocation, normalisation of deviation. | Allocation becomes obsolete; enforcement erodes; gaps widen; new harms go unaddressed. |
| Diffusion | Many-hands effect: responsibility distributed across many agents so no single agent experiences real accountability. | Shared decisions, distributed workflows, handoffs, network effects, collective anonymity. | Gaming becomes possible; escalation fails; blame shifts; learning is suppressed. |
| Hiding | Responsibility is concealed, denied, or shifted to low-power agents; true accountability owners remain invisible. | Secrecy, PR/narrative control, scapegoating, structural concealment, power suppression, theatre. | False accountability; targets wrongly blamed; real problems persist; trust collapse. |
| Transfer & Dumping | Responsibility is shifted to others (downward, outward, to future) without commensurate authority or resources. | Power asymmetry, lack of negotiation, procedural non-compliance, downstream surprise. | Recipient lacks capacity; task fails; escalation/blame follows; resistance and cynicism increase. |
| Escalation & Conflict | Responsibility boundaries conflict; multiple claimants or deniers; escalation pathways become contested. | Overlapping mandates, unclear interfaces, power asymmetry, competing values, organisational redesign. | Outcomes suffer from coordination failure; conflict consumes resources; settlement becomes political. |
| Erosion & Decay | Responsibility weakens as capacity erodes, oversight lapses, or enforcement mechanisms weaken. | Budget cuts, turnover, normalisation of minor breaches, complacency, unmet stewardship. | Responsibilities become nominal; critical failures emerge; crisis forces redesign. |
| Redesign & Evolution | Responsibility is explicitly redesigned to close gaps, rebalance power-duty, or adapt to new conditions. | Incident/failure trigger, leadership change, values shift, institutional maturation, scaling. | System is renewed; learning is institutionalised; resilience improves. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | Which formal responsibilities are missing, vague, or assumed to be "someone else's job"? |
| II (Systemic) | Which responsibilities are diffused across many agents (many-hands effect); which accountability mechanisms have eroded? |
| III (Informational) | Where is responsibility information withheld, distorted, or fragmented; where is true accountability hidden? |
| IV (Temporal) | Which responsibilities have drifted or been quietly reallocated; which stewardship duties have been deferred? |
| V (Relational) | Where are low-power agents scapegoated; where are high-power agents shielded from accountability? |
| VI (Ontological) | Where is responsibility system-wide but attributed to individuals; where does diffusion prevent accountability? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Role Confusion & Nominal Responsibility (duties assigned but not enforced)
- Many-Hands Diffusion (multiple contributors, no one responsible)
- Scapegoating of Low-Autonomy Agents (blame concentrated on accessible targets)
- Hidden Principals (true decision-makers remain invisible)
- Power-Duty Misalignment (responsibility without authority or vice versa)
- Stewardship Neglect (forward-looking duties deferred, not monitored)
- Accountability Erosion (enforcement mechanisms weaken over time)
- Responsibility Transfer & Dumping (shifted to those with no consent or capacity)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Many-hands diffusion blocking accountability | Framework of Systems; Framework of Causation | Multi-level causal analysis; responsibility diffusion audit | Map full causal chain; clarify interfaces; assign explicit meta-responsibility; strengthen escalation. |
| Scapegoating of low-power agents | Framework of Power; Framework of Governance | Power-accountability alignment audit | Identify true causation; distinguish causal from blame-worthy responsibility; redesign accountability. |
| Stewardship responsibility neglected | Framework of Risk; Framework of Learning | Forward-looking duty inventory; stewardship mechanism audit | Assign explicit stewardship duties; create monitoring; link to incentives; link to governance cadence. |
| Responsibility transfer without consent | Framework of Power; Framework of Governance | Transfer documentation; recipient capacity audit; negotiation process review | Require negotiation and acceptance; provide resources and authority; establish escalation. |
| Accountability mechanism erosion | Framework of Governance; Framework of Evidence | Enforcement audit; mechanism-compliance review; consequence testing | Reinvest in accountability infrastructure; separate measurement from reward; protect escalation. |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. List focal decisions/outcomes; identify formal role-holders and their mandates.
2. For each outcome, quickly identify: (a) who had formal role responsibility, (b) who materially caused/contributed, (c) who could have prevented.
3. Assess two dimensions: Power-Duty Alignment (does responsibility holder have authority?) and Visibility (is allocation explicit or contested?).
4. Identify obvious misalignments (responsibility without authority, diffusion without escalation).
5. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan; shortlist top 2–3 hidden responsibilities; flag for Tier 2 if high-consequence.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Map all focal decisions/outcomes; create responsibility register (six meta-categories × 5 types per outcome).
2. For each focal outcome, assess across six meta-categories: Role (who by position?); Causal (who contributed?); Moral (who deserves blame?); Legal (what law applies?); Stewardship (who must prevent recurrence?); Collective (how is it systemic?).
3. Profile each identified responsibility across eight dimensions: Basis (role/causal/moral/legal/stewardship), Scope (what exactly), Directness (direct/contributory/enabling), Time Horizon (backward-looking/forward-looking), Enforcement Strength (strong/nominal), Power-Duty Alignment (balanced/misaligned), Contestation (agreed/contested), Visibility (explicit/hidden).
4. Map all seven dynamic patterns (Drift, Diffusion, Hiding, Transfer, Escalation, Erosion, Redesign); identify active patterns; trace cascades and feedback.
5. For each focal responsibility, assess accountability mechanisms: Trigger (when is account given?), Forum (who judges?), Consequence (what is at stake?), Strength (is it credible?).
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: for each high-relevance Hidden (Many-hands diffusion, Scapegoating, Hidden principals, Power-duty mismatch, Stewardship neglect, Responsibility transfer), identify mechanism, detectability, consequence; assign remediation moves.
7. Design responsibility reallocation and accountability redesign; document residual Unknowns; apply Escalation Rule; generate closure artefacts and monitoring plan.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add shortlist of responsibility-specific Hiddens with meta-category, mechanism, visibility, consequence, remedy lever. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate any high-consequence Hidden to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record responsibility classifications, dimensional assessments, power-duty alignment, accountability mechanism strength, gaps. | After responsibility profiling; note gaps (e.g., stewardship assessment, mechanism effectiveness, enforcement history). |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about hidden responsibilities, accountability erosion, power-duty mismatches; propose tests (role audits, mechanism reviews, enforcement checks). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request responsibility policies, mandates, governance documents, decision logs, accountability mechanism audit results, stewardship records. | To reduce Unknowns and validate Hiddens detection; coordinate with Governance and Evidence frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we assessed all focal outcomes across six responsibility meta-categories, profiled each responsibility across eight dimensions, analysed seven dynamic patterns, reviewed accountability mechanisms, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-consequence hidden/misaligned responsibilities to Tier 2/3 or downstream frameworks (Governance, Power, Causation)?
