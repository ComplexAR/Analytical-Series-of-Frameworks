# Introduction

## What is Learning & Adaptation?
Learning is the reliable process by which a system—individual, team, organisation, or ecosystem—absorbs experience (via incidents, feedback, experiments, evidence), updates beliefs and mental models, and changes structure, behaviour, or governance in ways that reduce failure recurrence and improve capability. Adaptation is the implementation of learning: the observable change in rules, processes, governance, incentives, and norms that embeds lessons into reliable practice.

These are not synonyms with "improvement," "change," or "experience." A system can have abundant experience (many failures) and no learning (recurrence despite lessons). A system can undertake significant change (reorganisations, policy updates) without adapting (the changes do not address root causes, or are reversed when pressure eases). Distinguishing genuine learning from theatre is critical for incident investigation, governance design, and safety engineering.

## Why does Learning & Adaptation matter for Hiddens work?
Learning systems are primary hiding mechanisms. When feedback loops are suppressed, institutional memory is poor, or governance does not couple learning to decision-making, the organisation becomes blind to its own failure patterns. Post-mortems that assign blame rather than extract mechanisms hide causation. Defensive routines protect identity at the cost of truth-telling. Organisational amnesia—the failure to retain and retrieve lessons—creates "ghosts" in which failed patterns recur.

Moreover, learning *failures* are often intentional or structural. Power actors edit learning outputs to protect interests. Metrics are gamed to create the appearance of learning. Interventions are designed to manage blame rather than address root causes. This framework helps surface whether a learning system has integrity or is fabricating progress. Section 8 (Hiddens Source Analysis) identifies eight common generators of learning failure: defensive routines, metric myopia, blame-based post-mortems, organisational amnesia, learning capture by power, single-loop traps, over-externalisation, and unlearning deficit.

## What does this framework produce?
The framework generates six primary artefacts: (1) a learning system map, showing where learning is expected to occur and where it actually occurs (individual through ecosystem levels, plus architectural and meta-learning); (2) a learning type inventory, classifying dominant and missing learning mechanisms at each locus using the 30-type taxonomy; (3) a learning gap register, identifying where feedback loops are broken, where memory is weak, where governance does not couple learning to action, or where learning is captured or suppressed; (4) a learning architecture assessment, evaluating the design of feedback channels, observability systems, training, knowledge management, and governance linkage; (5) an adaptation backlog, converting findings into prioritised experiments and changes with owners and monitoring; and (6) a Hiddens crosswalk and escalation plan, identifying which learning failures may be deliberate, structural, or requiring investigation.

## How to use this framework
Invoke this framework when investigating incidents where root causes are known but behaviour does not change, when transformation programmes are stalling despite visible effort, when governance reviews reveal repeated failures in "previously learned" domains, or when risk assessments show that learning capacity is a material vulnerability. Default execution is Light Depth (30-minute scan: walk the six loci, identify missing types, surface top-three learning gaps, escalation check). Full Depth execution applies when learning failures are material to accountability or remediation design, when evidence of intentional suppression exists, or when the same failure recurs despite prior lessons. Section 1.6.4 provides routing instructions. Typical Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scans involving this framework include "Social Roots of Silence" (organisational suppression), "Temporal Distortion" (amnesia and latency), and "Metric Capture" (gaming and distortion).

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

The capacity to learn from experience is fundamental to safety, resilience, and performance improvement. Yet most organisations exhibit learning failures—patterns where incidents recur despite investigation, where evidence is abundant but behaviour unchanged, where reviews occur but governance is not updated, or where lessons are recorded but not retrieved or enacted. These failures are rarely accidents: they are produced by structures.

Learning is not an individual or team phenomenon only. It operates across six distinct loci: individuals (skill and mental model updates), teams (collective sensemaking and experimentation), organisations (codification, memory, and governance coupling), ecosystems (spillover, standards, and shared crisis responses), learning architectures (feedback channel design, instrumentation, training infrastructure, and governance), and meta-learning (double-loop learning, unlearning, and paradigm revision). Failure at any locus breaks the chain. If individuals learn but the organisation does not codify or govern the learning, change is episodic and fragile. If feedback channels are designed but governance does not act on signals, channels atrophy. If metrics are gamed, observability is fabricated. If lessons are suppressed by defensive routines or captured by powerful actors, the system becomes locked in patterns it no longer understands.

This framework maps learning across all six loci, identifies the 30 core learning mechanisms (five per locus), assesses architecture and governance coupling, and surfaces Hiddens—where learning is incomplete, suppressed, captured, or subject to amnesia. It then produces an adaptation backlog: experiments and governance changes designed to restore learning integrity. Critically, the framework treats learning failures as Hiddens sources: they are often deliberate or structural, not accidental, and require investigation commensurate with their consequence.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Learning & Adaptation:** A system exhibits learning when experience, evidence, or reflection produces durable change in behaviour, capability, governance, or understanding that reduces failure recurrence or improves adaptation to new conditions. Adaptation is the embedding of learning into reliable practice (rules, structures, incentives, norms). Learning without adaptation is theatre; adaptation without learning is drift.

**In scope:**
- Individual, team, organisational, and ecosystem learning mechanisms
- Feedback loop design, architecture, and governance coupling
- Institutional memory systems and their failure modes (amnesia, capture, distortion)
- Single-loop, double-loop, and triple-loop learning; unlearning
- Metrics and incentives as learning inhibitors or accelerators
- Defensive routines and learning suppression
- Blame-driven versus systemic closure and review processes
- Intervention design and post-implementation learning
- Governance structures that do or do not embed learning
- Timeline-spanning patterns (latency, ghosts, episodic spikes)

**Out of scope:**
- Generic "change management" (unless directly coupled to learning mechanisms)
- Individual talent management or career development (unless feeding learning loci)
- Technology adoption or digital transformation (unless learning is the mechanism)
- Cultural transformation as an end in itself (note cultural norms *affecting* learning architecture)
- Training content design (note training systems *enabling* learning)

**Common confusions:**
- "Learning & Adaptation" is not the same as "improvement" (systems improve without learning; learning without improvement is possible if governance does not couple).
- "We did a post-mortem" does not equal "we learned" (post-mortems are necessary but not sufficient; learning requires behaviour change and governance coupling).
- "Everyone knows what happened" does not equal "organisational learning" (individual or team knowledge differs from institutional memory; both are required).
- "We have training" does not equal "we have learning capacity" (training is input; learning requires feedback, experimentation, and governance integration).

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Individual Learning
- Definition: Skill, judgement, and mental model updates within individuals; capability building through practice, reflection, and feedback.
- Diagnostic cues: Error recognition and reflection quality; coaching uptake and skill progression; capability assessments showing improvement; individual contributors taking on more complex problems; coaching conversations referencing feedback and reflection.
- Typical failure mode: Local optimisation that does not scale; insight that does not persist through stress; learning that does not transfer to organisational level; individuals learning but team/organisation remaining stuck.

### II. Team & Group Learning
- Definition: Shared learning through collaboration, critique, experimentation, and reflection routines that improve collective practice.
- Diagnostic cues: Quality of AARs and psychological safety; peer challenge norms; experimentation with recorded results; playbook updates; team velocity and error rates improving; dissent welcomed and integrated.
- Typical failure mode: "Performative retros" producing narratives without behaviour change; blame-driven reviews suppressing truth-telling; groupthink masquerading as consensus; team-level insight not reaching organisational level.

### III. Organisational Learning & Memory
- Definition: Durable learning embedded in standards, decision routines, governance structures, and institutional memory systems that persist through turnover.
- Diagnostic cues: Standards and policies updated after incidents; incident database regularly consulted and updated; lessons integrated into onboarding and training; organisational decisions reference prior learning; changes persist through personnel rotation.
- Typical failure mode: Amnesia (lessons not retained or retrievable); "library graveyards" (knowledge recorded but unused); capture by power (learning outputs edited to protect interests); decoupling (governance does not act on learning).

### IV. Ecosystem & Inter-Organisational Learning
- Definition: Learning that occurs across organisational boundaries via regulator feedback, standards bodies, professional communities, supply chains, and shared crises.
- Diagnostic cues: Regulator findings integrated into standards; external learnings adopted selectively and tested; participation in communities and shared exercises; crisis-driven collective updates; spillover effects evident.
- Typical failure mode: Isolation (missed external signals); uncritical echo (imitation without independent verification); secrecy (supply chain/partner learning blocked by confidentiality or adversarial postures); episodic learning (crisis-driven spikes without embedding).

### V. Learning Architectures & Infrastructures
- Definition: The designed operating model for learning: how signals enter, are processed, prioritised, and become integrated into decisions and governance.
- Diagnostic cues: Clear input channels (feedback, incidents, experiments, external signals); reliable processing (analysis, hypothesis testing, prioritisation); clear output channels (decisions, standards, governance updates); resourcing and governance in place; KPI and leading indicators in place.
- Typical failure mode: Weak input (feedback channels atrophied; observability poor; signals drowned by noise); weak processing (analysis delays; competing priorities; lack of prioritisation rigor); weak output (governance does not respond; changes are cosmetic); decoupling (signals enter but do not reach decision-makers).

### VI. Meta-Learning & Unlearning
- Definition: Learning that revises assumptions, goals, decision-making processes, and learning governance itself; retiring obsolete constructs and paradigms.
- Diagnostic cues: Ability to change sacred KPIs and metrics; willingness to challenge foundational assumptions; governance redesign in response to learning; policy and rule sunset mechanisms in place; narratives and frames shifting with evidence.
- Typical failure mode: Single-loop trap (tweaks without assumption revision); unlearning deficit (obsolete rules, metrics, and narratives persisting despite evidence); organisational ghosts (legacy constraints blocking adaptation); inability to shift paradigms.

## 3.1 Types (Category I: Individual Learning)
| # | Type | Description (1–3 sentences) | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Experiential Error-Driven Learning | Learning from direct experience of success/failure by updating behaviour after outcomes. Fast but often local, confounded, and biased. | "I tried X, it broke, now I do Y." | 2 | Deliberate Practice Learning | Structured, feedback-rich practice designed to improve specific skills or judgement components over time. | 4 | Reflective Sensemaking | Learning through reflection, journaling, peer discussion, and structured review of one's own decisions and biases. | Explicit debrief rituals; journaling or reflection logs; peer discussion groups; documented bias checks. | If reflection is unsafe, defensiveness hides the true lesson; introspection becomes self-justification. |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1. **Run Tier 1 scan** across all six Hiddens meta-categories (assume Hiddens exist in learning systems—they are very common).
2. **For shortlisted Hiddens** (8–12 types from Tier 2), rate mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, and consequence.
3. **Assign at least one detection and one remediation interface** per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (e.g., Secret → Fragmentation → Mirage → Defensive routines).
4. **Run the Learning Hiddens source analysis** (§8.1) and record residual unknowns: what remains hidden about why learning is not occurring? Is there evidence of intentional suppression or structural defect?
5. **Escalation decision:** If any High-consequence Hiddens are Unverified or Alleged (status U/A), escalate to Tier 2 or Tier 3 per §8.5 rule.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full Hiddens run)
Escalate to a comprehensive Hiddens analysis (full detection/remediation matrix) if any of the following apply:
- **High consequence** (learning failure affects safety, regulatory compliance, or systemic resilience)
- **Suspected intentionality** (evidence of deliberate suppression, capture, or concealment)
- **Persistent disagreement** (stakeholders have inconsistent views of "what happened" or "whether we learned")
- **Repeat failures** despite multiple "learning" cycles (suggests amnesia, capture, or unlearning deficit)
- **Cascading Hiddens** (e.g., Secret → Fragmentation → Mirage → Defensive routines; Hidden types reinforce each other)
- **Organisational amnesia + power shifts** (combination suggests capability loss + loss of memory + no institutionalisation)

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Multi-Locus Reality | Learning must be diagnosed at the correct locus (individual, team, organisation, ecosystem, architecture, meta-learning). Individual competence cannot substitute for organisational memory, governance change, or ecosystem resilience. Gaps at any locus break the chain. |
| 2. Depth Matters | Single-loop learning can improve short-term performance while preserving the causes of failure. Double-loop (assumption revision) and triple-loop (governance redesign) learning are rarer but more durable. Stalled improvement signals single-loop trap. |
| 3. Architecture over Theatre | Learning is a system capability, not an event or report. It is built through feedback channels, observability, training infrastructure, knowledge systems, and governance coupling. Absence of architecture produces reports without change. |
| 4. Capture Resistance | Learning outputs are politically editable. Protect candour, independence, and objectivity or the system will generate mirage and anti-learning. Separate learning from accountability; protect truth-tellers. |
| 5. Unlearning as Capability | Durable adaptation requires retiring obsolete assumptions, KPIs, narratives, and processes. Without unlearning, organisations remain locked in patterns they no longer understand. Ghosts constrain options. |
| 6. Latency as Hidden Risk | Learning latency (signal → analysis → decision → change time) enables drift and makes learning retroactive. Shorten latency; add leading indicators; enable autonomous escalation for known scenarios. |
| 7. Memory as Competitive Advantage | Institutional memory (reliable retrieval of lessons) is rarer than most organisations assume. Poor memory recreates old failures and wastes remediation effort. Invest in memory infrastructure. |
| 8. Learning Governance as Binding | Learning that does not couple to governance (decision rights, policy, standards, training) is theatre. Governance changes must be traced to learning findings. Weak coupling signals learning is advice-only. |

# 11. Framework-Specific Artefacts & Knowledge

## 11.1 Glossary (series-consistent)
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Learning | Durable change in behaviour, capability, governance, or understanding resulting from experience, evidence, reflection, or experimentation. Requires behaviour change, not just knowledge transfer. |
| Adaptation | Implementation of learning; observable change in rules, processes, governance, incentives, and norms that embeds lessons into reliable practice. Without adaptation, learning is incomplete. |
| Single-Loop Learning | Correcting actions, processes, or parameters to meet existing goals and assumptions without revising underlying causes, incentives, or governance. Fast but superficial. |
| Double-Loop Learning | Revising underlying assumptions, goals, incentives, and decision procedures in response to evidence. Slower but durable; requires challenging comfortable narratives. |
| Triple-Loop Learning | Redesigning how the learning system itself operates: how we learn, how we govern learning, what counts as evidence, who has voice. Transformational and rare. |
| Unlearning | Intentional retirement of obsolete routines, metrics, assumptions, narratives, or technologies that no longer serve the organisation. Essential for adaptation to new conditions. |
| Organisational Memory | Mechanisms by which an organisation stores, retrieves, and acts on lessons: artefacts (databases, standards, playbooks), norms (integration into training, governance), and routines (mandatory retrieval, regular refreshes). |
| Organisational Amnesia | Loss or suppression of relevant knowledge through turnover, weak codification, poor retrieval mechanisms, or deliberate forgetting. Creates repeat failures. |
| Defensive Routine | Pattern of suppressing or distorting feedback that would threaten identity, hierarchy, or comfortable narratives. Blocks learning. |
| Learning Architecture | End-to-end configuration of feedback channels, governance coupling, and capability systems (training, knowledge management, instrumentation) that make learning reliable. |
| Learning Infrastructure | Concrete tools and systems enabling learning: dashboards, databases, training platforms, communication systems, governance structures. |
| Lessons Identified | Insights and hypotheses produced by reviews and analyses following events. Not yet embedded. |
| Lessons Learned | Lessons embedded into behaviour, structures, and governance so practice changes reliably. Coupled to architecture and governance. |
| Anti-Learning | Patterns where experience is converted into worse behaviour: scapegoating, mythologising, propaganda, ritual compliance without understanding, defensive routines. |
| Ecosystem Learning | Learning that occurs across organisations via regulators, standards bodies, professional communities, shared crises, and supply chains. Enables collective resilience. |
| Learning Latency | Time from signal emergence → analysis → decision → implementation of change. Long latency makes learning retroactive and demoralising; short latency enables adaptation. |
| Metric Myopia | Over-reliance on narrow metrics that distort reality, hide high-risk areas, and drive gaming. Creates false confidence. |
| Learning Capture | Editing or suppression of learning outputs by powerful actors to protect interests or narratives. Distorts truth and prevents adaptation. |
| Psychological Safety | Belief that one can speak up with concerns, admit errors, or challenge assumptions without fear of retaliation or embarrassment. Essential for learning. |

## 12.2 Integration Notes (optional)
Use this framework when an organisation can describe problems but cannot reliably change behaviour, when incidents repeat despite "lessons learned," or when improvement is episodic and crisis-driven. Treat Section 8 (Hiddens Source Analysis) as mandatory in high-stakes environments: learning failures commonly co-occur with secrecy, fragmentation, latency, and mirage formation.

When designing interventions (Framework of Interventions), treat changes as experiments with monitored outcomes and explicit learning-to-learning feedback loops. Integrate learning reviews with post-implementation audits. Ensure unlearning is governe (not ad-hoc): schedule KPI retirements, rule sunsets, and narrative refreshes alongside learning. Couple learning governance tightly to decision authority: governance changes must follow findings, or learning becomes theatre.

In high-stakes contexts (safety-critical, regulated, or where repeated failures indicate systemic learning gaps), escalate to Tier 2 or Tier 3 Hiddens analysis. Look especially for organisational amnesia (memory systems gaps), learning capture (power editing findings), and single-loop traps (symptoms treated without root cause revision).
