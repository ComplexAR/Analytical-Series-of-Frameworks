# Introduction

## What is Emotions?

Emotions are affective states — subjective feelings with somatic and neurological signatures — that drive behaviour, shape perception, influence decision-making, and structure social dynamics. In this framework, emotions are understood not as irrational obstacles to analysis, but as primary data. They reveal what agents fundamentally care about, what threatens them, what they aspire toward, and what conflicts exist between stated values and actual emotional commitments. Fear, anger, grief, pride, shame, solidarity, anxiety, resentment, hope, and joy all carry information about the landscape that a scenario analyst must see.

Yet emotions are persistently hidden — not by accident, but by design. Organisations suppress emotions through norms ("stay professional," "don't bring feelings to work"), managers rationalise emotional resistance as "lack of buy-in" or "misunderstanding of strategy," and individuals suppress their own emotional awareness to maintain self-image or meet social expectations. When these hiding mechanisms work, they render invisible some of the most powerful drivers of behaviour and the deepest sources of conflict in organisations and communities. This framework treats emotions as both analytical objects and as mechanisms of concealment.

## Why does Emotions matter for Hiddens work?

Emotions operate across multiple Hiddens meta-categories. Perceptually, emotional valence shapes what agents notice and what they ignore — a fear-driven workforce may not see novel opportunities; grief-suppressed teams may fail to grieve necessary losses and instead deny change entirely. Systemically, emotional suppression norms become control mechanisms embedded in organisational practice, making certain emotions literally unspeakable. Informationally, emotional undercurrents may directly contradict stated information — "we're excited about the merger" rings false when underlying anxiety is palpable. Temporally, emotional memory shapes what is forgotten and what is retained; collective grief, if unprocessed, can become dormant resentment that activates at unexpected moments. Relationally, emotions flow between people through contagion, creating invisible group-level affects that drive collective behaviour independently of individual preferences. Ontologically, the way emotions are understood — as natural, controllable, shameful, unmanageable — shapes what forms of emotional experience are even possible in a given context.

The Framework of Emotions is designed to surface what is hidden by emotional suppression, rationalisation, collective denial, and affective framing. Without it, a scenario analyst may see surface compliance and interpret it as alignment, see emotional resistance and classify it as irrationality, or see crisis escalation and miss the emotional drivers underneath.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework produces five interrelated artefacts. First, an affective driver map that identifies the primary emotions present in a scenario, their triggers, and their relation to behaviour and decision-making. Second, a dynamics register showing how emotions evolve over time, escalate, cascade, or are suppressed, including the presence of emotional contagion and emotional labour. Third, a hidden emotions register populated through the Hiddens scan, identifying which emotional states are being suppressed, rationalised, or made collectively invisible, along with the mechanisms maintaining that suppression. Fourth, detection and remediation interfaces — the specific diagnostic questions, channels, and probes that can surface hidden emotions and the communication pathways that might help re-integrate suppressed affect into organisational awareness. Fifth, a set of working hypotheses about what would change in the scenario if key emotional drivers were acknowledged or suppressed differently.

The framework integrates upstream with the Situations, Agents, Personas, Incentives, Culture and Norms, Cognition, and Beliefs frameworks (which provide context, actor profiles, rational structures, and normative settings). It provides outputs downstream to Decisions (emotional drivers of choice), Interventions (how to work with rather than against emotional dynamics), Communications (emotional valence of messages), and Governance (designing for emotional alignment, not just rational compliance).

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework when the scenario exhibits any of these features: stated incentives do not explain observed behaviour; managers report "resistance" without clear rational basis; teams show rapid escalation on minor triggers; decisions that should be straightforward become polarised; explicit cultural values do not match affective climate; repeated interventions fail despite alignment on strategy; loyalty or commitment is questioned despite surface compliance. This framework is typically executed at Light Depth in Rapid Run Mode (screening scan of six meta-categories, key emotions, obvious suppression signals) and at Full Depth in Investigative Run Mode (detailed emotional driver map, Hiddens Tier 2 exploration of suppression mechanisms, emotional contagion chains, and interface mapping for probes).

The framework does not attempt to diagnose mental illness or provide therapeutic intervention — it is an organisational and scenario-level analytical tool. For situations involving severe trauma, psychological crisis, or mental health concern, reference appropriate clinical domains. The framework is designed to make emotions visible as data within complex scenario analysis.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Emotions are affective drivers of behaviour that operate independently of, alongside, and sometimes against the incentive structures, cultural norms, and rational calculations analysed by other frameworks. They reveal what agents actually care about, what they fear, what causes them shame or pride, and what losses they grieve. In complex scenarios, emotions are typically hidden by three interlocking mechanisms: rationalisation (affective drivers explained away with rational justifications), suppression norms (cultural or organisational taboos that make certain emotions unspeakable), and collective denial (groups suppressing uncomfortable emotions to maintain group cohesion or desired identity).

The presence of hidden emotions often explains apparent irrationality, failure of rational interventions, sudden escalation, and persistent conflict. An engineer resisting automation may be experiencing grief at the loss of craft identity, not resisting "on the facts." A team failing to adopt a new system may be operating under shame-driven compliance norms that make questions seem disloyal. A crisis response may be driven by collective fear that masks as procedural rigour. When emotions are invisible, the analyst misdiagnoses the problem, misidentifies the leverage points, and proposes interventions that bypass the actual drivers of behaviour.

This framework provides a systematic method to: (1) identify the primary affective states present in a scenario, (2) map their triggers and their relation to behaviour, (3) discover which emotions are being suppressed or rationalised, (4) trace the mechanisms maintaining suppression, (5) identify emotional contagion and cascade effects, and (6) design detection and remediation interfaces that can help surface and integrate hidden emotional content into organisational awareness and decision-making.

The framework treats emotions as both analytical objects (what explains this behaviour?) and as hiding mechanisms (what is this emotional norm preventing us from seeing?). It does not treat emotions as pathology or as obstacles to overcome, but as information that must be integrated into scenario understanding.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Emotions:** Affective states — subjective, typically conscious or semi-conscious feelings with somatic and neurological correlates — that carry valence (pleasant/unpleasant), intensity, duration, and typically motivate action or shape perception. In this framework, emotions include primary affects (fear, anger, sadness, joy, surprise, disgust), secondary emotions (shame, pride, guilt, jealousy, hope, anxiety), and complex emotions that integrate moral, existential, or relational dimensions (grief, solidarity, despair, love). Emotions are distinguished from moods (longer-duration, lower-intensity affective states), and from temperament (stable trait-level emotional tendencies). They are also distinguished from values (what agents care about in principle) and from incentives (external or internal reward structures), though emotions are shaped by both.

**In scope:**
- Individual emotional states (fear, anger, shame, pride, grief, joy, anxiety, resentment, hope, solidarity) and their relation to behaviour, decision-making, and perception
- Emotional suppression norms and cultural/organisational emotional rules (what emotions are speakable, performable, legitimate)
- Rationalisation mechanisms (how emotional drivers are re-framed as rational justifications)
- Emotional contagion (how emotions spread through groups and organisations)
- Emotional escalation and cascade effects (how small emotional triggers lead to large group responses)
- Emotional labour (the work required to suppress, perform, or regulate emotions to meet organisational/social expectations)
- The gap between private emotional states and public emotional displays (mask-wearing)
- Collective emotions and emotional climates (group-level affective states that shape collective behaviour)
- Emotional memory (how past emotional events shape current emotional responses and group narratives)

**Out of scope:**
- Clinical diagnosis of mood disorder, personality disorder, or emotional/behavioural pathology (refer to mental health frameworks)
- Therapeutic or clinical intervention in emotional regulation (refer to clinical psychology and therapeutic practice)
- Neurophysiology of emotion (refer to neuroscience frameworks)
- Emotional development or emotional intelligence training (refer to learning and competency frameworks)
- Ethical questions about emotional authenticity or emotional "right-ness" (refer to Ethics framework)

**Common confusions ("Emotions is not…"):**
- Not Incentives: Incentives framework covers rational reward structures (salary, status, promotion); Emotions covers affective drivers that operate independently of, alongside, or against incentive logic (you may rationally want a promotion but emotionally dread the responsibility)
- Not Culture and Norms: Culture and Norms covers shared emotional norms and display rules; Emotions covers the actual emotional states that may conflict with or be suppressed by cultural norms
- Not Cognition: Cognition covers information processing and reasoning; Emotions covers affective states that shape what agents attend to and how they reason, often pre-rationally
- Not Beliefs: Beliefs covers what agents think is true; Emotions covers what agents feel, which may conflict with or reinforce beliefs
- Not Interests: Interests covers what agents fundamentally want or care about; Emotions covers the affective states that arise when interests are engaged, threatened, or frustrated
- Not Responsibility: Responsibility covers who is accountable; Emotions covers the shame, guilt, or resentment that may drive or complicate accountability and blame dynamics

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Primary Negative Emotions

**Definition:** Base-level affective responses to perceived threat, violation, loss, or unmet need. These emotions are evolutionarily old, highly conserved across cultures, and typically motivate defensive or aggressive responses.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Verbal: "I'm afraid," "I'm furious," "I'm disgusted," "I hate this," "That's unbearable"
- Somatic: tension, elevated heart rate, flushed face, withdrawn posture, clenched fist, shutdown or freezing
- Behavioural: defensive behaviour, aggression, avoidance, shutdown, resistance, sabotage
- Pattern: rapid onset, clear trigger, often disproportionate to objective threat level (suggesting deeper vulnerability)

**Typical failure mode:** Fear and anger are dismissed as "irrationality" or "emotional immaturity" rather than as legitimate signals about what an agent experiences as genuinely threatened. This leads to interventions that suppress emotion rather than address the actual threat.

---

### II. Primary Positive Emotions

**Definition:** Base-level affective responses to perceived opportunity, reward, alignment, or satisfaction of need. These emotions motivate approach, engagement, and continued investment.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Verbal: "I'm excited," "I'm proud of this," "I love working on this," "That brings me joy," "I feel hopeful"
- Somatic: open posture, energised movement, brightness in eyes and voice, relaxed facial expression
- Behavioural: increased investment, creativity, risk-taking, persistence, voluntary engagement
- Pattern: sustained energy, resilience in face of setbacks, willingness to go beyond minimum requirements

**Typical failure mode:** Positive emotions are assumed to be present (morale assumed to be good) or are actively suppressed ("don't get too excited, manage expectations"). This leads to missed opportunities to amplify what's actually working.

---

### III. Secondary Social Emotions

**Definition:** Affective responses to one's own or others' social positioning, group belonging, and relational standing. These emotions are fundamentally about how agents perceive themselves within groups and communities.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Verbal: "I feel ashamed," "I feel like I don't belong," "I feel disloyal saying this," "I envy their position," "I feel a kinship with them"
- Somatic: hunched posture (shame), restlessness and scanning (anxiety about belonging), warmth and openness (solidarity)
- Behavioural: withdrawal from group, hyperconformity, boundary-testing, defensive group loyalty, seeking allies
- Pattern: sensitive to perceived judgment or exclusion; rapid shifts in behaviour based on social signal; emotional contagion (emotions spread through group)

**Typical failure mode:** Shame and social anxiety are suppressed by norms that mandate "professionalism," creating invisible group fragmentation and loyalty conflicts. Agents act loyal while feeling alienated, or act confident while experiencing shame.

---

### IV. Secondary Self-Evaluative Emotions

**Definition:** Affective responses to agents' own appraisal of their capability, performance, character, and identity coherence. These emotions shape resilience, learning, and adaptation.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Verbal: "I don't trust my judgment," "I'm proud of what I've built," "I feel incompetent," "I'm satisfied with who I've become," "I regret that choice"
- Somatic: slump (low confidence), upright bearing (confidence), tension or tremor (self-doubt)
- Behavioural: risk-aversion or risk-seeking (confidence-dependent), learning eagerness or defensive shutdown, identity-protecting behaviour
- Pattern: self-evaluative emotions shape whether agents take on stretch assignments, admit mistakes, or ask for help

**Typical failure mode:** Low confidence or self-doubt is seen as a "skills gap" rather than as emotional signal that agents need reassurance, visibility of progress, or structure to succeed. High confidence is sometimes misread as competence and goes unquestioned until failure.

---

### V. Complex/Moral Emotions

**Definition:** Affective responses integrating personal values, responsibility, fairness, and right conduct. These emotions shape ethical decision-making and determine whether agents experience moral alignment or moral distress.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Verbal: "This feels wrong," "I'm outraged by the injustice," "I feel complicit," "I respect their integrity," "This compromises everything I stand for"
- Somatic: chest tightness (moral distress), fire in belly (outrage), lightness or relief (moral alignment), heaviness or weight (guilt)
- Behavioural: ethical boundary-setting, whistleblowing, moral disengagement, compensatory good acts, withdrawal from morally distressing situation
- Pattern: moral emotions are often suppressed in hierarchical settings where questioning authority is seen as disloyalty; agents experiencing moral distress may leave rather than speak

**Typical failure mode:** Moral emotions are framed as "personal values" that agents should check at the door of the organisation. Moral distress is invisible; agents leave silently, and institutions lose the people with the strongest integrity.

---

### VI. Complex/Existential Emotions

**Definition:** Affective responses tied to meaning-making, mortality awareness, fundamental belonging, and transcendence. These emotions are often activated by major transitions, losses, or encounters with scale/beauty/fragility.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Verbal: "This is bigger than all of us," "I feel utterly alone," "That took my breath away," "What does any of it matter?," "I feel held by this community"
- Somatic: shivers or chills, tears (sometimes of joy, sometimes of sorrow), trembling, sense of expansion or contraction, profound stillness
- Behavioural: seeking meaning (often through narrative, spirituality, or community); withdrawal or isolation (existential despair); acts of transcendence or legacy-building
- Pattern: often emerges in crisis, transition, or loss; can catalyse profound shifts in perspective and priorities

**Typical failure mode:** Existential emotions are categorised as "not relevant to business" or as signs of depression/need for clinical intervention. Organisations miss the opportunity to acknowledge shared mortality, meaning-seeking, and legacy concerns that often matter more than operational metrics.

| 4 | Disgust / Contempt | Aversive negative emotion triggered by perceived contamination, moral corruption, or unworthiness. Disgust is visceral (bodily reaction); contempt is social (judgment that someone is beneath regard). Both motivate withdrawal or rejection. | "That's revolting," "How could they…," gag reflex, nose wrinkling, dismissive language, "I won't be part of that," closed-off body language | Suppressed disgust at organisational practices becomes demoralisation and talent loss; teams become cynical and internally contemptuous; individuals silently judge leadership while performing loyalty |
| 5 | Hopelessness / Despair | Chronic negative emotion triggered by repeated loss, insurmountable barriers, or loss of agency. Hopelessness is belief-driven (conviction that things cannot improve); despair is affective (emotional weight of meaninglessness). Both motivate shutdown, passive acceptance, or self-destructive behaviour. | "What's the point?," flat affect, withdrawn posture, fatalistic language, dark humour, giving up on problems, "I've tried everything" | Suppressed hopelessness manifests as quiet quitting, performative engagement without real investment, erosion of standards; teams become functionally dysfunctional (going through motions); individuals burn out silently |

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)

| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | All 30 core types in this framework map directly to the canonical 6 meta-categories × 5 types baseline. No extension of the core taxonomy is required. |
| Domain-specific refinement | Domain-specific sub-types may be identified in particular organisational or cultural contexts (e.g., "fear of automation," "pride in legacy systems," "shame at immigrant status"). These should be captured as instances of the core types, not as new types, to preserve comparability. |
| Preserving mappability | If a new emotion type is proposed, it must map clearly to one of the six meta-categories and must address a gap in the existing 30-type taxonomy. New types should be added only if a core emotional experience is missing. |
| Structural invariants | The 6 meta-category structure should be preserved. If a new meta-category is proposed, it must be theoretically justified and must cluster a distinct family of emotions not already covered. |
| Periodic reassessment | Framework users should review the 30-type taxonomy annually against their own scenario work to identify gaps or over-specification. Recommend raising extensions to the primary maintainer for consideration in the next major version update. |
| Versioning & governance | Core type taxonomy changes increment the framework to v2.0 (major version change). Domain-specific sub-type identification in application notes increments to v1.1 (minor version). Coordinate extension proposals with the Operating Guide maintainer to ensure consistency across the 50-framework series. |

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Emotion

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)

| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. **Intensity** | The strength or magnitude of the emotional feeling state, from barely perceptible to overwhelming. High intensity emotions are more likely to drive behaviour, escalate conflict, or override other considerations; low intensity emotions are easier to ignore or dismiss. | Scale of 1–10 (subjective report) or observable: subtle (1–3), moderate (4–6), strong (7–10) | Ask directly ("On a scale of 1–10, how afraid are you?") or infer from somatic cues (trembling, facial flushing, vocal strain); observe behaviour persistence | High-intensity emotions are invisible in organisations because they're suppressed; mapping intensity reveals what's being hidden. Low-intensity chronic emotions (simmering resentment, background dread) may be more consequential than dramatic spikes |
| 2. **Duration** | How long the emotional state persists, from momentary spike (seconds) to chronic baseline (weeks or longer). Momentary emotions shape immediate responses; chronic emotions reshape worldview and behaviour patterns. | Moment (seconds), acute (minutes to hours), subacute (hours to days), chronic (weeks+) | Ask "How long have you felt this way?" or observe whether emotion resets with context change or persists across situations | Chronic emotions that persist across contexts signal either deep activation or entrenched patterns. Suppressed chronic emotions become invisible because individuals adapt to them as "baseline" rather than as affective state |
| 3. **Focal Clarity** | How clear the emotion's trigger or object is: am I afraid *of something specific* (clear) or just *anxious in general* (diffuse)? Clear emotions permit targeted action; diffuse emotions create persistent unease without clear exit. | Focused (clear object/trigger), semi-focused (possible triggers but uncertainty), diffuse (no clear focus) | Ask "What specifically are you afraid of?" If answer is clear, emotion is focused; if vague or multiple, it's diffuse | Organisations prefer focused emotions (can be addressed) over diffuse ones (seem irrational). Mapping focal clarity reveals what's being successfully avoided or concealed |
| 4. **Social Visibility** | How openly or covertly the emotion is displayed/experienced: is this emotion publicly visible and acknowledged (high visibility) or privately felt and hidden (low visibility)? High visibility emotions shape group dynamics; low visibility emotions create hidden drivers. | Highly visible (open expression, acknowledged), semi-visible (expressed selectively), invisible (suppressed, hidden) | Observe verbal/non-verbal expression in group settings vs 1:1 settings; ask "Do you show this feeling at work?" or "Have you told anyone you feel this way?" | Organisations suppress emotions through norms that make certain emotions "unprofessional." Mapping visibility reveals what's being actively hidden and what channels permit authentic expression |
| 5. **Action Readiness** | How activated the emotion is to motivate behaviour or cognition: is the person ready to act on this emotion (high readiness) or is the emotion suppressed and disconnected from action (low readiness)? High readiness emotions shape decisions; low readiness emotions are interior struggles. | High readiness (motivated to act, ready to do something), moderate readiness (pulled to act but held back), low readiness (emotion present but disconnected from action) | Ask "What do you want to do about this?" or observe whether emotion manifests in behaviour change. If emotion is high-intensity but low readiness, suppression is likely present | Organisations suppress action readiness through hierarchy and norms. High-intensity, low-readiness emotions indicate conflict between what agents feel and what they can or will do |

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)

| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Emotional Escalation / Spiral | Emotions intensify over time despite lack of new triggers; minor incident triggers disproportionate response; emotional temperature rises in group over days/weeks | Negative emotion triggers defensive behaviour that triggers reciprocal negative emotion; cycle amplifies. Often fuelled by unacknowledged underlying threat or need. Prior triggering events create sensitised nervous system. | Unaddressed emotional drivers amplify and eventually force escalation into crisis; intervention becomes harder as emotions intensify | (1) Acknowledge the underlying trigger/threat explicitly; (2) Break the reciprocal cycle by de-escalating response; (3) Create space for processing underlying emotions; (4) Establish emotional reset rituals (breathing, break, restart) |
| 2 | Emotional Suppression & Leakage | Emotions are actively suppressed to meet organisational norms ("stay professional"); suppressed emotion leaks out sideways through passive-aggressive behaviour, tension, sudden outbursts, or somatic symptoms (illness, exhaustion) | Organisational/cultural norms make certain emotions unspeakable; individuals suppress them; neurobiological cost of suppression (effort, activation, wear) manifests in physical or behavioural symptoms. Eventually emotion emerges in displaced form. | Chronic suppression leads to burnout, illness, disengagement, and eventual crisis (the suppressed emotion finally breaks through). Teams appear compliant but are running on empty. | (1) Name and legitimise the suppressed emotion ("It's normal to feel afraid about this change"); (2) Create safe channels for emotional expression; (3) Acknowledge cost of suppression; (4) Examine whether suppression norm serves anyone |
| 3 | Emotional Contagion | Emotions spread through groups rapidly, especially in high-arousal states (fear, outrage, joy); individuals adopt emotional states of those around them (mirror neurons, social signalling); group emotion emerges that is not necessarily the sum of individual emotions | Social/neurobiological mechanism: emotions are contagious; high-arousal emotions spread faster than low-arousal; proximity, repetition, and trust accelerate contagion. Visible leaders' emotions spread fastest. Emotional intensity is amplified in group. | A leader's fear or rage spreads through an organisation; a moment of collective joy creates momentum; collective grief or despair can paralyse. Emotional contagion can trigger cascade effects. Understanding contagion allows strategic use of emotional leadership. | (1) Map emotional contagion pathways (who is emotionally influential?); (2) Manage leader emotional visibility and authenticity; (3) Use positive emotional contagion (joy, solidarity) to shift group affect; (4) Interrupt negative contagion through deliberate breaks/resets |
| 4 | Emotional Polarisation | Emotions split groups into opposing affective positions (fear vs hope, outrage vs resignation, shame vs defiance) that reinforce tribal boundaries; each side's emotional certainty prevents perspective-taking | Emotions activate tribal/group identity; opposing emotional positions become markers of group membership; people choose emotional position as proxy for choosing group. Emotions then reinforce the group boundary. | Polarised emotional states prevent collaboration and mutual understanding; escalate conflict; make consensus impossible; create fragile teams of isolated individuals. Can lead to organisational split. | (1) Acknowledge legitimacy of different emotional positions ("Fear and hope are both understandable"); (2) Find the deeper shared emotional need beneath the split (both sides want safety, respect, etc.); (3) Create cross-tribe dialogue that permits different emotions; (4) Work with shared emotion (solidarity, shared grief) |
| 5 | Emotional Labour | Individuals perform emotions they don't feel (smiling through grief, appearing excited about unwanted change, hiding fear when required to lead) to meet organisational/social expectations; high cognitive and somatic cost; eventual exhaustion | Organisational norms require emotional display that doesn't match internal state; individuals regulate emotions (suppress true feeling, perform expected feeling) to maintain job/status/relationships; regulation requires constant effort and creates fatigue. Long-term emotional labour → burnout. | Chronic emotional labour leads to burnout, authenticity loss, health issues (somatic symptoms), and eventual departure. Teams characterised by high emotional labour are brittle and vulnerable to crisis. | (1) Reduce gap between expected and authentic emotion (permit authentic expression, reduce performing); (2) Acknowledge emotional labour cost; (3) Create boundaries (permission to show fatigue, sadness, struggle); (4) Build in recovery/restoration time |
| 6 | Emotional Aftermath / Trauma | Major events (crisis, loss, failure, violence, humiliation) leave emotional residue that shapes behaviour, decisions, and risk-taking long after the event. Team/organisation carries trauma; subsequent decisions are influenced by trauma response (over-caution, defensive posturing, hypervigilance) | Acute emotional event creates lasting imprint (amygdala encoding, narrative integration failure, unprocessed grief/rage). Event becomes reference point for all subsequent risk assessment. Trauma survivors become defensive; teams become risk-averse. | Organisations carrying unprocessed trauma make overly cautious decisions, miss opportunities, and are brittle (any reminder of trauma triggers defensive response). Key decisions are shaped by past emotional wounds, not current reality. | (1) Acknowledge the traumatic event explicitly; (2) Create container for processing grief, rage, shame; (3) Create ritual to mark recovery/integration; (4) Distinguish past trauma from current situation; (5) Re-evaluate risk decisions through lens of current context |
| 7 | Emotional Substitution / Displacement | One emotion substitutes for another (anger masks shame, busy-ness masks grief, aggression masks fear); the performed emotion obscures the underlying emotion, making diagnosis difficult | Emotions are linked in chains: shame triggers anger (to escape shame), grief triggers busy-ness (to avoid feeling), fear triggers aggression (to feel powerful). When surface emotion is addressed but underlying emotion isn't, the intervention fails. | Addressing the surface emotion while the underlying emotion persists leads to failed interventions and continued dysfunction. Teams address anger but don't address shame; resolve appears temporary. | (1) Ask "What's underneath the anger?" or "What would happen if you sat with this grief?"; (2) Map the emotional chain; (3) Permit the underlying emotion; (4) Address root cause, not surface expression |
| 8 | Emotional Amnesia / Forgetting | Emotional events are forgotten or downplayed (collective amnesia about crisis, past failure, loss); emotional learning is lost; teams repeat same mistakes; emotional meaning of past events is erased | Trauma or shame can trigger forgetting or minimisation as a survival response (consciously or unconsciously). Organisational culture may suppress historical memory to maintain desired identity. Succession of personnel means emotional learning is not carried forward. | Without emotional memory (what we learned from failure, what we grieve about past change), organisations are condemned to repeat emotional patterns. Teams don't learn from trauma. | (1) Explicitly record emotional history (what happened, what we felt, what we learned); (2) Onboard new people into the emotional as well as factual history; (3) Create annual rituals acknowledging past losses; (4) Archive stories/lessons from emotional events |
| 9 | Emotional Resonance & Activation | Events trigger emotional responses in proportion to prior emotional activation or sensitisation; same event triggers different emotions in different people based on their emotional history and vulnerability. Something "minor" suddenly triggers major response because it resonates with prior emotional wound | Neurobiological mechanism: prior emotional activation lowers the threshold for re-activation. Prior loss sensitises to loss. Prior shame sensitises to perceived judgment. Individual emotional history shapes current emotional response. | Apparent irrationality ("Why did they react so strongly to that small thing?") is explained by emotional resonance with past events. Judging reactions as irrational misses the emotional intelligence of the response. | (1) Ask about emotional history ("Has something like this happened before?"); (2) Recognise that emotional response is proportional to resonance, not to current event magnitude; (3) Acknowledge the pain being re-triggered; (4) Distinguish past from present; (5) Work with the deeper activation |
| 10 | Emotional Breakthrough / Integration | Suppressed emotions break through (suddenly, often in crisis); individual or group experiences emotional release; can be followed by new clarity, re-commitment, or transformation if integrated consciously | Suppression requires constant energy; when suppression breaks (often triggered by crisis, safety increase, or fatigue), emotions emerge rapidly; if permitted and processed, emotional breakthrough can catalyse change; if judged or re-suppressed, damage remains. | Emotional breakthroughs can be turning points (team grief allows team healing) or crises (rage erupts in inappropriate channels). Managing breakthrough — permitting it in safe containers — determines whether it catalyses transformation or escalates conflict. | (1) Create safe container when breakthrough emerges (private space, trustworthy witness); (2) Permit the emotion fully (don't interrupt, fix, or re-suppress); (3) Help integrate the emotional meaning; (4) Extract the learning/clarity; (5) Rebuild on new footing |
| 11 | Emotional Alignment / Coherence Shift | Team or individual emotional state shifts from fragmentation/conflict to alignment/coherence; emotions synchronise around shared meaning or shared challenge; morale and effectiveness increase; sense of being "on the same page" emotionally | Emotional coherence emerges when team has clear purpose, faces shared challenge, feels safe, and can be authentic. Often happens in crisis (team members show up fully) or in high-meaning work. Requires permission to be emotionally present. | Emotionally coherent teams are resilient, adaptive, and capable of bold action. Emotionally fragmented teams are brittle and conflict-prone. Building emotional coherence is as important as building structural alignment. | (1) Clarify and communicate shared purpose/meaning repeatedly; (2) Create rituals that synchronise emotional experience (all-hands, team gatherings); (3) Permit authentic expression; (4) Lead with visible emotional authenticity; (5) Acknowledge shared challenges and victories |

# 7. Relationship to Other Frameworks (Integration)

## 7.1 Primary Integration Links (recommended)

**Inputs expected:**
- From Situations Context Classification: scenario framing, triggering events, environmental changes that activate emotional responses
- From Agents and Personas: actor inventory, individual emotional dispositions and temperament, stress responses and coping styles
- From Incentives: rational reward structures that emotions may override, misalign with, or reinforce
- From Culture and Norms: cultural emotional display rules, suppression norms, emotional labour expectations
- From Cognition: perception biases and attention patterns shaped by emotional states
- From Beliefs: belief systems and tribal identities with which emotions become entangled
- From Communications: communication patterns, emotional valence of messages, contagion pathways through communication channels
- From Hiddens: visibility failures and suppression mechanisms relevant to emotional domain

**Outputs provided:**
- To Decisions: affective drivers of choices, emotional escalation pathways that shape decision-making, emotional resistance signals
- To Interventions: understanding of what emotional changes are needed alongside rational/structural changes, emotional alignment requirements, resistance sources
- To Communications: information about emotional valence of messages and channels, emotional contagion pathways, emotional meaning of different communication approaches
- To Governance: design guidance for systems and norms that align emotional climate with stated values, feedback loops for monitoring emotional acceptance of changes

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Rationalisation Blindness | Emotional drivers are explained away as rational justifications; analyst accepts the stated reason ("I'm concerned about resource constraints") without noticing the affective driver underneath ("I'm afraid of automation; losing my identity as an engineer"); misses the real driver entirely | (1) When a rational justification is offered, ask "What feeling is underneath this reason?"; (2) Test consistency: "Would this reason apply if the stakes were lower?" or "Is this the real reason or a covering reason?"; (3) Map discrepancies between stated reasons and behaviour patterns; (4) Invite emotional exploration without judgment |
| 2 | Suppression Norm Invisibility | Cultural/organisational suppression norms make emotions literally unspeakable; analyst normalises the suppression ("Of course people don't talk about feelings at work") and fails to see the cost of suppression; assumes emotional silence means emotional absence | (1) Document suppression norms explicitly: "What emotions are acceptable here? Which are forbidden?"; (2) Map enforcement: "Who punishes emotional expression? What are the penalties?"; (3) Look for leakage (passive-aggressive behaviour, illness, turnover) as sign of suppression; (4) Create safe channels to surface suppressed emotion (anonymous surveys, 1:1 interviews with explicit safety framing) |
| 3 | Contagion Attribution Error | Emotional contagion is invisible in how it spreads; analyst attributes emotional state to individual choice or trait ("They're just negative people") rather than to contagion from emotional leaders or emotional climate; misses systemic emotional drivers | (1) Map emotional contagion pathways: "Whose emotions influence this person's emotional state?"; (2) Compare emotion across contexts: "Does this person show different emotions in different groups?"; (3) Look for synchronised emotional responses across unconnected individuals as sign of contagion; (4) Identify emotional leaders whose affect spreads fastest |
| 4 | Emotional Labour Invisibility | Work required to suppress, regulate, or perform emotions is invisible; analyst sees compliance ("They're supportive of the change") without noticing the emotional labour cost (they're exhausted, burnt out, disengaged underneath); misses the fragility | (1) Ask about emotional authenticity: "Do you feel what you're expressing?"; (2) Look for somatic signs of emotional labour (exhaustion, illness, tension); (3) Compare emotional display (what they show) vs private emotional state (what they report 1:1); (4) Map emotional labour costs and unsustainability |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)

| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| I – Perceptual | Emotional suppression norms prevent agents from noticing/acknowledging their own emotions; fear or shame blocks emotional awareness; affective climate is misperceived | Agents report being "fine" despite clear somatic/behavioural indicators of distress; emotional awareness is absent despite emotional activation; emotional denial ("I'm not angry, I'm just being factual") | Ask agents directly about emotional experience; observe non-verbal cues (posture, facial expression, vocal tone); compare private vs public emotional displays; test emotional awareness through hypotheticals |
| II – Systemic | Organisational structures and processes suppress emotions by design; suppression norms are embedded in hierarchy, formalisation, or control systems; emotional labour is built into roles; system makes certain emotions structurally impossible | Roles require emotional performance (remain calm, appear confident, hide doubt); hierarchy suppresses vulnerability in leaders; formalisation suppresses authenticity; emotional labour is high and visible (burnout, turnover, cynicism) | Document suppression norms and structures; map emotional labour by role; identify structural barriers to emotional expression; look for burnout and turnover as signs of suppression cost |
| III – Informational | Emotional information is not surfaced or is actively suppressed; emotional data is treated as invalid or irrelevant; emotional meaning of information is ignored; emotional truths are invisible in formal communications | Communications focus only on rational content ("Here's the plan") without acknowledging emotional meaning ("I know you're scared and grieving"); emotional concerns are not on agendas; emotional feedback is not collected; emotional signals are misread as "resistance" or "irrationality" | Create forums where emotional information surfaces (anonymous surveys, safe 1:1 interviews, emotional check-ins); examine communications for emotional content; ask "How does this land emotionally?" alongside "What does it mean rationally?" |
| IV – Temporal | Emotional memory is lost (amnesia about prior emotional events); emotional aftermaths are not processed (unresolved grief, unhealed trauma); current emotional responses are disproportionate to current triggers but proportionate to past emotional wounds | Emotional responses seem irrational ("Why are they so upset about this small change?") but are actually proportionate to prior emotional trauma; repeat failures of similar interventions because emotional learning wasn't extracted; historical emotional events are downplayed or forgotten | Document emotional history (what emotional events happened, what was felt, what was learned); help agents connect current responses to past emotional activation; create rituals that acknowledge past losses and emotional learning; onboard new people into emotional as well as factual history |
| V – Relational | Emotional contagion is invisible; emotional climate driven by leaders' emotions is unacknowledged; emotions flowing through relationships are not surfaced; tribal emotional identities (in-group/out-group emotional affiliations) are hidden | Emotional state of key leaders shapes organisation's emotional state, but connection is invisible; group-level emotional patterns (team pride, team shame, team fear) are not discussed; relationships characterised by mistrust carry suppressed anger or fear; in-groups vs out-groups defined by emotional affiliation not rational criteria | Map emotional contagion pathways (who influences whose emotional state?); identify emotional leaders; surface group-level emotions explicitly; examine relationships for suppressed emotional content (trust, mistrust, alliance); track emotional in-groups and out-groups |
| VI – Ontological | The nature of emotions themselves is hidden or misunderstood in this context; emotions are treated as pathology, irrationality, or irrelevant; emotions are not seen as valid data; emotional experience is delegitimised | Culture that denies emotions exist or matter ("We don't do emotions here"); emotions treated as weakness or incompetence; emotional expression is pathologised ("You need therapy") rather than normalised; emotional knowledge is not valued; organisational identity explicitly excludes emotion ("We're rational, professional") | Examine how emotions are framed in this culture (valid data vs pathology? strength vs weakness?); ask "What would happen if emotions were seen as important information?"; identify agents whose emotional experience is delegitimised or pathologised; assess whether emotional authenticity is possible in this context |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

1) Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (early pass; assume Hiddens exist) — identify which categories are likely to be active in this scenario given the emotional landscape (OG §7.1).

2) Shortlist candidate Hiddens (start with 3–5 high-relevance items); for each, rate: what mechanism is hiding this emotion (suppression norm? rationalisation? contagion invisibility?), how reducible is it (can we surface this with detection interface X?), how detectable is it (would agents admit it in safe 1:1 interview or only in anonymous channel?), what agency shapes the hiding (organisational suppression vs individual denial?), what consequence if this remains hidden (will it drive failed intervention, sustained conflict, talent loss?) (OG §7.2).

3) Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains: e.g., if we surface fear of automation (Detection A: anonymous survey), what remediation interfaces would address it (Remediation B: career development plan; Remediation B: role redesign; Interaction C: connect to Interventions framework to design change approach that addresses fear) (OG §7.2).

4) Run the hiddens source analysis from §8.1 — check whether your emotional analysis is blind to rationalisation, suppression norms, contagion, labour invisibility, or emotional amnesia; record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens with high confidence in mechanism but uncertainty in visibility or agency.

5) If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism (e.g., strong evidence of emotional contagion, or clear suppression norm, or rationalisation chain), invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan from OG §7.5 — map which frameworks the mechanism touches and follow the emotional thread through them (e.g., Belief Persistence scan if tribal emotional identity is protecting suppressed emotion; Narrative Capture scan if stories are preventing emotional reframing) (OG §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3).

6) After executing the full framework protocol (mapping emotions, dynamics, dimensions, interfaces) and after running any detection probes, re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas: which Hiddens were surfaced? which remain? what new Hiddens emerged? (OG §D.6.10).

7) Produce Residual Unknowns listing: what emotional drivers remain invisible and why (mechanism, detectability limit, agency barriers); what is the impact if we proceed without understanding these (decision risk, escalation risk, failed intervention risk)?; what next probe or investigation would reduce the residual Unknown; apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, emotional alignment, or remediation priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4 (residual unknowns list, acceptability rationale, monitoring plan, ownership, learning hook).

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:

- **High consequence:** Emotional drivers are shaping decisions with safety, regulatory, existential, or severe harm implications (e.g., emotional fear is driving over-caution in safety-critical decision; emotional outrage is driving risky retaliation; emotional despair is driving abandonment of critical work)
- **Adversarial or strategic concealment likely:** Emotions are being deliberately suppressed or weaponised as part of organisational politics (hidden emotional alliances, emotional manipulation, deliberate culture of emotional denial for control)
- **Persistent emotional disagreement or contradictory evidence:** Different agents report radically different emotional realities; emotional data contradicts behaviour patterns (surface compliance with deep emotional resistance); emotional narratives conflict
- **Repeated failure despite prior interventions:** Prior attempts to address emotional drivers have failed; emotional escalation continues despite rational/structural changes; suggests deeper emotional mechanism not yet surfaced
- **Strong suspicion of cascades or reinforcement:** Emotional patterns are cascading across groups (contagion), or are self-reinforcing (fear drives caution drives fear), or are amplifying through feedback loops
- **Decision-critical residual unknowns:** Emotional Hiddens remain after Tier 2 that could determine whether key decisions are emotionally sustainable, achievable, or will trigger escalation

**Tier 3 execution (per OG §D.6.10):**
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories (six Hiddens meta-categories) into full Hiddens taxonomy run (all Hiddens types across all 36 frameworks, searching for emotion-driven Hiddens in power structures, narratives, beliefs, causation, responsibility, communications, governance, decisions, and interventions)
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements for emotional claims (multiple data sources, triangulation), more explicit competing hypotheses about what emotion is actually present, more rigorous disconfirming tests ("What would disprove this emotional attribution?")
- When adversarial intent is plausible: adds concealment vectors (how might this emotion be deliberately hidden? what strategic benefit does suppression provide?) and independent corroboration paths (does the emotion show up in different data sources, different channels, different contexts?)
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6 frameworks) when emotional Hiddens are suspected to be driving mechanisms in multiple frameworks simultaneously (e.g., emotion drives belief persistence, which shapes decision-making, which shapes governance, which shapes power dynamics)

| 5 | Interfaces & Hiddens Source Analysis | Map detection interfaces (how can we surface hidden emotions safely?) and remediation interfaces (what would addressing these emotions require?); run Hiddens source analysis (§8.1) to check for blindspots (rationalisation, norm invisibility, contagion attribution error, labour invisibility); run Tier 1 scan pre-closure (compare to early scan; what new Hiddens emerged?); if indicated, run Tier 2 deepening on top 5–10 Hiddens | Interfaces map (detection and remediation channels), Hiddens candidate register (Tier 1 and Tier 2), source analysis blindspot check, residual Unknowns list |
| 6 | Translate to Decisions / Interventions / Governance | Map implications for downstream frameworks: what do these emotional drivers tell us about feasibility/acceptability of decisions under consideration?; what emotional alignment is required for interventions to stick?; what governance changes would support emotional coherence rather than just rational compliance?; surface emotional escalation risks that could derail plans | Decision options analysis with emotional meaning and emotional resistance signals; intervention design with emotional support components; governance recommendations addressing emotional drivers; escalation scenario identification |

## 9.2 Standard Deliverables (recommended)

**Minimum deliverable (1–2 pages):**
- Scenario summary with key emotional drivers (3–4 primary emotions, their triggers, their impact on behaviour/decision)
- One key suppression mechanism identified with detection/remediation approach
- Downstream implications (which decisions are emotional-risk or emotional-requiring-alignment)

**Full deliverable pack:**
- Emotional landscape report (20–30 pages): scenario framing, six-category emotional activation map, detailed emotion mapping for 5–8 key emotions, dimensions and dynamics analysis, suppression mechanisms analysis, Hiddens Tier 1–2 registers
- Emotional interfaces & detection plan: detection interfaces for surfacing hidden emotions, remediation interfaces for addressing emotional drivers, who needs to be involved, what channels permit authentic expression
- Downstream routing summary: emotional implications for decisions under consideration, emotional alignment requirements for proposed interventions, emotional acceptance monitoring plan for post-intervention
- Residual unknowns & escalation recommendation: what emotional Hiddens remain despite analysis, what would reduce them, whether Tier 3 escalation is warranted, what learning hooks exist for post-decision monitoring

**Monitoring indicators & review cadence:**
- Emotional climate pulse (monthly or quarterly): emotional experience of key cohorts (are suppressed emotions surfacing? are people more or less authentic? is contagion shifting?), affective acceptance of decisions, emotional acceptance of interventions
- Emotional labour indicators (ongoing): burnout scores, absenteeism rates, retention rates, somatic health indicators (stress-related illness)
- Escalation triggers (immediate): sudden emotional outbursts, rapid emotional polarisation, collective despair or rage, silent mass disengagement (quiet quitting)
- Emotional learning: post-decision retrospectives that surface emotional drivers of success/failure, emotional memory preservation (stories of how emotions shaped outcomes)

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table

| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Emotions are data, not pathology | Treat emotional states as legitimate information about what agents care about, what they experience as threatened or desired, what values they hold. Emotions are not problems to be solved but signals to be understood. Judging emotions as "irrational" or "unprofessional" closes off information; treating them as valid data opens understanding. |
| 2. Suppression has costs | Suppressing emotions through organisational norms, leadership silence, or individual denial requires constant effort and creates somatic and psychological strain. Visible emotional labour (the work of performing emotions you don't feel) is associated with burnout, illness, and eventual failure. Organisations that permit authentic emotional expression are more resilient, not less. |
| 3. Contagion is real | Emotions spread through groups and organisations through multiple mechanisms (mirror neurones, social signalling, communication channels, leadership visibility). Emotional contagion can escalate conflicts or catalyse transformation depending on which emotions are spreading. Understanding contagion pathways permits strategic use of emotional leadership. |
| 4. Rationalisation is ubiquitous | Agents use rational justifications to explain emotional drivers, often without conscious deception. Stated reasons are frequently covering reasons; the real driver is affective. Accepting surface-level rationalisation blocks access to the actual mechanism; probing for underlying emotion permits diagnosis and intervention. |
| 5. Emotional alignment is different from rational alignment | An individual can intellectually agree with a decision while emotionally opposing it. Achieving genuine buy-in requires both rational alignment and emotional alignment. Interventions that achieve rational agreement without emotional alignment will fail or be undermined by hidden resistance. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms; recommended)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Affective state / Emotional state | A subjective feeling with valence (pleasant/unpleasant), intensity, and typically neurological and somatic correlates. In this framework, emotions are subjective, typically conscious or semi-conscious, and distinguishable from moods (lower-intensity, longer-duration affective states) and temperament (stable trait-level emotional tendencies). |
| Affective driver / Emotional driver | An emotional state that shapes behaviour, perception, decision-making, or social dynamics. Example: "Fear of job loss is the emotional driver of the resistance to automation." |
| Emotional suppression | The active or norm-based prevention of emotional expression or emotional awareness. Can be organisational (norms that forbid certain emotions), relational (social pressure to hide emotion), or individual (internal denial of emotional experience). |
| Suppression norm | An organisational or cultural rule (explicit or implicit) that makes certain emotions unspeakable, unperformable, or illegitimate. Example: "In this organisation, showing doubt or vulnerability is seen as weak." |
| Rationalisation | The substitution of an emotional driver with a rational justification. Example: "I'm resisting the change because I'm concerned about resource constraints" (rationalisation) when the actual driver is fear of obsolescence (emotional). |
| Emotional labour | The work required to suppress, regulate, or perform emotions in order to meet organisational or social expectations. High emotional labour is associated with burnout and eventual failure. |
| Emotional contagion | The spread of emotions through groups, organisations, or networks via neurobiological mechanisms (mirror neurones), social signalling, or communication channels. |
| Emotional climate | The collective affective state of a group or organisation, shaped by leader emotions, norms, and recent events. |
| Focal clarity | The degree to which an emotion has a clear trigger or object. A clear emotion is "I'm afraid of automation." A diffuse emotion is "I'm just anxious about the future." |
| Social visibility | The degree to which an emotion is openly expressed (public) vs privately experienced and hidden (private). High social visibility emotions are acknowledged in group settings; low visibility emotions are suppressed or hidden. |
| Action readiness | The degree to which an emotion is activated to motivate behaviour or thought. High action readiness: "I want to do something about this." Low action readiness: "I feel afraid but I'm not doing anything differently." |
| Emotional resonance | The activation of emotions proportional to emotional history, not to current trigger magnitude. Example: A small change about role structure triggers disproportionate emotional response because it resonates with prior trauma of role loss. |
| Emotional breakthrough / Emotional release | The sudden emergence of suppressed emotion, often triggered by crisis, safety increase, or fatigue. If integrated consciously, emotional breakthrough can catalyse transformation; if re-suppressed, damage remains. |
| Emotional coherence | Alignment of individual emotional states and group emotional states around shared meaning, purpose, or challenge. Emotionally coherent teams are resilient and adaptive. |
| Emotional authenticity | Alignment between internal emotional state and external emotional expression or performance. Low authenticity occurs when emotional labour is high (performing emotions you don't feel). |
| Moral emotion | Emotions tied to values, fairness, justice, and right conduct. Includes outrage, guilt with complicity, respect, and moral despair. |
| Existential emotion | Emotions tied to meaning, mortality, transcendence, and fundamental belonging. Includes awe, existential despair, belonging, mortality awareness, and acceptance. |

## 12.2 Integration Notes (optional)

This is the first release of the Framework of Emotions as part of the new 50-framework Analytical Series expansion. The framework is designed to bridge a significant gap in the original 36-framework series: the near-complete absence of systematic emotional analysis. While Incentives captures rational reward logic and Culture and Norms captures shared emotional norms, there was no framework to address actual emotional states as they drive, shape, and suppress behaviour in complex scenarios.

The Framework of Emotions works most closely with:
- **Incentives:** Showing where emotional drivers override or diverge from rational incentive logic
- **Culture and Norms:** Revealing actual emotional states that conflict with or are suppressed by cultural emotional display rules
- **Cognition:** Mapping how affective states shape attention and reasoning (emotion before cognition)
- **Beliefs:** Tracing how emotions reinforce belief persistence and tribal identity (emotional attachment to beliefs)
- **Hiddens:** Using emotional suppression as a primary lens for discovering visibility failures and concealment mechanisms

The framework is compatible with all 36 existing frameworks and provides emotional dimension to analysis in any of them. Integration is mechanical: add emotional questions to any framework's integration checklist (OG §7.3).

### Appendix C: Worked Example — Emotional Analysis of a Merger Integration Scenario

**Scenario:** A technology company (Acquired) has been acquired by a larger consulting firm (Acquirer). Three months post-close, integration is proceeding "rationally" (systems merged, reporting lines clear, incentives aligned) but stakeholder surveys show low engagement, retention risk is high, and informal feedback suggests people are "waiting to see" rather than committed.

**Situation framing:** Acquired employees valued their identity as agile, mission-driven innovators; Acquirer represents larger, process-heavy, client-service orientation. Decision point: Proceed with planned integration of development practices (faster time-to-market) or revisit approach based on people feedback.

**Quick Emotional Scan (Light Depth):**

**Step 1 — Situation & Goal:** Understand emotional drivers of apparent disengagement despite rational alignment; determine whether integration can proceed or requires emotional reckoning.

**Step 2 — Meta-Category Activation:**
- I. Primary Negative: **Grief (loss of organisational identity)** and **Fear (fear of loss of voice, autonomy, technical leadership in larger organisation)** — highly active
- II. Primary Positive: **Hope** — very low; **Pride** — declining (feeling absorbed, made invisible)
- III. Social: **Shame** at being acquired (seen as failure by some), **Longing** for community (feeling isolated in larger firm), **Solidarity** with former team-mates (in-group forming against "outsiders")
- IV. Self-Evaluative: **Confidence** declining in ability to influence outcomes in new structure
- V. Moral: **Respect** for Acquirer values unclear (perception they're profit-driven, not mission-driven); **Moral alignment** questioned
- VI. Existential: **Belonging** questioned (do I belong here?), **Meaninglessness** in larger organisation where individual contribution feels invisible

**Key emotion:** **Suppressed grief over loss of identity.** Grief is not named in formal communications; integration is framed as "exciting opportunity" and "best of both worlds." Actual emotional reality is: people are grieving what they've lost (autonomy, community identity, technical leadership) and performing excitement they don't feel.

**Step 3 — Suppression Mechanisms:**
- **Norm-based:** Organisational culture on both sides expects "professionalism," which means hiding grief and appearing committed
- **Leadership visibility:** Leaders (both sides) are performing excitement; this emotional contagion is spreading to teams, but inauthentically
- **Rationalisation:** "Of course we're excited about scale," "This will help us reach more clients," "We'll maintain our values but have more resources" (rational framings that obscure the grief)
- **Collective denial:** Group acknowledges "There will be an integration curve" but doesn't permit actual mourning of what's lost

**Step 4 — Emotional Dynamics:**
- **Escalation pattern:** Grief suppression → resentment of "forced optimism" → cynicism about leadership authenticity → disengagement and hidden exit planning
- **Contagion pattern:** Team leads perform optimism from Acquirer; teams sense the performance and mirror back performance without authenticity; emotional climate becomes performative throughout

**Step 5 — Detection & Remediation Interfaces:**

**Detection (Type A):**
- Safe 1:1 conversations with explicit framing: "We're interested in how you're *really* experiencing the integration, not just the official story" → reveals the grief
- Anonymous pulse survey: "What have you lost in this integration?" and "What would you want acknowledged?" → surfaces what's unsaid
- Observation: Attendance at "integration celebration" events, engagement level, informal conversation topics (high focus on "how things used to be") → indicates grief

**Remediation (Type B):**
- **Grief ritual:** Dedicated time (half-day or full-day event) explicitly titled something like "Honoring What We Built: Reflecting on Acquired's History & Identity Before Moving Forward" — tells stories of what was proud about old organisation, acknowledges losses, permits visible emotion
- **Identity continuity:** Explicit commitment from Acquirer that core values and ways of working will be carried forward (not erased) — reduces grief by showing continuity
- **Autonomy protection:** Explicit decisions about what decisions Acquired teams will retain authority over (technical leadership in certain domains, cultural norms in certain areas) — restores some of what was lost
- **Time-bounded transition:** Permission for people to grieve ("This will take 6 months") rather than expecting immediate optimism — normalises the emotional timeline

**Integration (Type C):**
- **Decisions:** "Given the grief we're seeing, is proceeding with aggressive integration pace wise? Are we sacrificing emotional alignment for speed?"
- **Interventions:** Redesign integration approach to include emotional components (grief ritual, identity continuity, autonomy restoration) alongside rational components (systems integration, reporting structure)
- **Governance:** Create integration governance that explicitly includes emotional feedback (are people emotionally engaged or performatively compliant?); slow decisions if emotional alignment is at risk

**Outcomes:**
- If remediation happens: Grief is processed, identity is acknowledged, people can grieve what was lost and invest in what's being built. Authentic commitment increases. Integration proceeds with emotional alignment, not just rational alignment.
- If remediation doesn't happen: Grief suppression deepens; emotional performance becomes exhausting; retention of key talent declines; integration stumbles because people are not actually committed, just compliant.
