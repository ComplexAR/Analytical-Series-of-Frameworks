# Introduction

## What is an Agent?

An **agent** is any entity—individual, collective, institutional, artificial, technological, ecological, or abstract—capable of acting, making decisions (consciously or functionally), and producing outcomes for which responsibility or causal attribution can meaningfully be asserted. Agents range from individual humans with full autonomy and intentionality to markets, ecosystems, and culture as emergent systems; from artificial intelligences to historical or hypothetical actors treated analytically as agents for explanatory work.

In complex scenarios, the identity of agents matters profoundly. A failure can be attributed to a single operator, a dysfunctional team, institutional rules, an AI system's constraints, emergent market dynamics, or tacit cultural norms—or combinations of these. Misidentifying agents or conflating collective and individual responsibility creates accountability gaps, enables scapegoating, and produces ineffective remediation. Conversely, systematic agent mapping prevents these errors, clarifies where agency is constrained versus autonomous, and enables governance designs that survive strategic adaptation.

---

## Why Does Agency Matter for Hiddens Work?

Agency—who acts, what they control, and how—is frequently hidden or distorted. Six primary mechanisms make agency invisible:

1. **Perceptual Hiddens (I):** Key actors are overlooked, especially intermediaries, marginal stakeholders, and agents operating at system interfaces or across boundaries.

2. **Systemic Hiddens (II):** Incentive structures, accountability rules, and role definitions systematically obscure true agency—blame is displaced to scapegoats with low autonomy; control is exercised indirectly through proxy targets; automation is blamed for organisational choices made by humans.

3. **Informational Hiddens (III):** Information about who decided, acted, or delegated control is withheld, fragmented across systems, or distorted in logs and testimony. Undocumented exceptions, deleted communications, and late discovery of decision-paths obstruct agency clarity.

4. **Temporal Hiddens (IV):** Agency can persist through identity shifts—rebranding, turnover, succession, or model updates—while accountability gaps widen. Conversely, momentary actors can escape accountability through rapid reconstitution.

5. **Behavioral Hiddens (V):** Agents may conceal their actual goals, expertise, or constraints; compliance theatre and espoused-versus-operative behaviour masks true intentionality; collusion and coordinated deception create false narratives of individual action.

6. **Structural Hiddens (VI):** Agency emerges from distributed interaction (markets, ecosystems, organisations) in ways no single actor intends. Macro patterns 'act back' on constituents, creating the appearance of unified agency where none exists—or conversely, fragmenting responsibility across unknowable networks.

Without explicit agent mapping and Hiddens auditing, investigators miss co-conspirators, blame concentrates on accessible low-autonomy agents, principal–agent relationships remain unmapped, and emergent system-level agency is attributed falsely to human intention. The Framework of Agents, combined with Tier 1–3 Hiddens scans and Targeted Discovery Scans, corrects these failures by making agency visible, testable, and governable.

---

## What Does This Framework Produce?

This framework produces five primary artefacts:

1. **Agent Register and Profiles:** A systematic map of actors across all six meta-categories, with dimensional profiles (Autonomy, Intentionality, Consciousness, Accountability, Temporal Persistence) characterising their capacities and constraints.

2. **Interaction Dynamics Map:** Documentation of how agents interact (cooperation, competition, domination, delegation, emergence) and the failure modes each pattern creates.

3. **Interface Map:** Explicit identification of Communication, Action, and Constitution interfaces where agency is mediated, constrained, distorted, or made invisible.

4. **Hiddens Register and Evidence Ledger:** Candidate hidden agents, masked agency, and accountability gaps with detection methods and remediation levers.

5. **Accountability and Governance Map:** Clear assignment of decision rights, control loci, escalation paths, and monitoring points, enabling governance design that reflects true agency rather than assumed structures.

These artefacts integrate upstream from Evidence/Failures (which expose agency visibility breakdowns) and feed downstream to Responsibility/Governance (accountability design), Decisions (anticipation of agent responses), and Interventions (adaptation-robust lever selection).

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Agency is the capacity of an entity to act, decide, and produce outcomes. In simple systems, agency is concentrated in identifiable individuals or organisations with clear authority and intention. In complex scenarios—organisations under stress, socio-technical systems, markets, ecosystems, post-incident investigations—agency is distributed, delegated, constrained, and often concealed. A single outcome may result from human intention, institutional role-based action, artificial system autonomy, emergent system dynamics, or combinations of these.

The Framework of Agents provides a systematic taxonomy and profiling method to answer three operational questions:

1. **Who or what is acting?** (Agent identification and classification across six meta-categories and 30 core types)
2. **What capacities and constraints define this agent?** (Five-dimensional profiling: Autonomy, Intentionality, Consciousness, Accountability, Temporal Persistence)
3. **How do agents interact, and where do accountability and governance need to be designed?** (Interaction patterns, interface analysis, hidden agency detection)

Without explicit agent analysis, investigations and decision-making suffer predictable failures:

- **Scapegoating:** Blame concentrates on accessible, low-autonomy agents (operators, junior staff) while principals, rule-makers, and emergent system constraints remain unexamined.
- **Principal–Agent Gaps:** Delegated agents act misaligned with principals' intentions; incentive mismatches, information asymmetries, and control failures persist undetected.
- **Emergent System Blindness:** Macro patterns (market prices, cultural norms, network effects) produce outcomes no actor intended; responsibility is falsely attributed to central control or unified intention.
- **Hidden Agency:** Concealed principals, masked intermediaries, and distributed decision-making remain invisible; accountability structures designed on false assumptions fail under scrutiny.

The framework corrects these by operationalising agent identification, dimensional profiling, and interface-level accountability design, integrated with Hiddens detection and governance robustness testing.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Agency:** The capacity of an entity to act (intentionally, functionally, or emergently) in ways that produce outcomes for which the entity can be said to bear causal responsibility or accountability. Agency includes conscious human action, role-based institutional behaviour, algorithmic decision-making, and emergent system dynamics.

**In scope:**
- Identification and classification of agents (six meta-categories, 30 core types)
- Dimensional profiling of agent capacities (Autonomy, Intentionality, Consciousness, Accountability, Temporal Persistence)
- Interaction pattern analysis (cooperation, competition, domination, delegation, emergence)
- Interface mapping (communication, action, constitution)
- Agent-level accountability design and principal–agent alignment
- Hidden agency detection and remediation
- Agent response anticipation for intervention design

**Out of scope:**
- Moral philosophy of agency or consciousness (framework accepts plurality of theories; focuses on operational profiling)
- Technical details of specific AI systems (refer to Systems/Technology frameworks)
- Organisational culture or values (refer to Culture framework)
- Individual psychological profiles (refer to Personas framework; this framework treats individual agents at the level of bounded roles and decision rights)
- Detailed causal mechanism analysis (refer to Causation framework; this framework identifies causal candidates and their interaction patterns)

**Common confusions ("Agency is not…"):**
- **Agency ≠ Causation:** An agent may cause an outcome without intending it; conversely, an agent may intend an outcome that another agent causes. Causation must be analysed separately (Causation framework).
- **Agency ≠ Intention:** Agents can be purely functional (plants, markets, algorithms) with no consciousness or explicit goals. Intentionality is a dimension, not a prerequisite.
- **Agency ≠ Moral Responsibility:** An agent may produce an outcome for which accountability can be assigned without moral blame (e.g., a natural disaster, an AI system following its design). This framework focuses on causal and institutional accountability, not moral philosophy.
- **Agency ≠ Visibility:** Agency is frequently invisible (hidden principals, emergent dynamics, masked intermediaries). This framework is designed to make hidden agency visible.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Individual Agents
**Definition:** A bounded entity—human, biological non-human, posthuman, artificial, or hybrid—capable of sensation, decision-making, and action.

**Diagnostic cues:** Identity is localised; action can be traced to a specific decision-maker; capability can be assessed in an individual; intention (or functional goal-directedness) can be attributed to the entity itself.

**Typical failure mode:** Over-attribution of causation to individual actors while ignoring systemic constraints, collective dynamics, or emergent system effects. Conversely, under-attribution when distributed agency is mistaken for individual decision-making.

---

### II. Collective Agents
**Definition:** A group, community, movement, network, or swarm of entities acting together such that outcomes cannot be reduced to individual choice.

**Diagnostic cues:** Coordination is observable; norms or identity bind the collective; behaviour emerges from group dynamics rather than central direction; members identify as part of a "we."

**Typical failure mode:** Assuming coordination when members are merely responding to external incentives; conflating network presence with causal influence; treating the collective as a unified actor when it is actually fractured or heterogeneous.

---

### III. Institutional Agents
**Definition:** A formal organisation (corporation, government, NGO, school, religious body) acting through defined roles, rules, and decision structures.

**Diagnostic cues:** Legal status or formal recognition; published governance structure; defined roles with decision rights; formal accountability channels.

**Typical failure mode:** Assuming institutions act as unified intentional agents when they are actually sites of multiple competing agents (departments, factions, principals). Role-based accountability can mask true decision-makers.

---

### IV. Artificial Agents
**Definition:** A computational or embodied system (AI, robot, software agent, multi-agent system, human-AI hybrid) capable of autonomous or semi-autonomous action.

**Diagnostic cues:** System is designed to make decisions or take actions with limited real-time human control; behaviour is mediated by algorithms, training, or programmed rules.

**Typical failure mode:** Over-attribution of blame to AI systems while ignoring designer choices, training data biases, or human supervision failures. Conversely, treating AI as passive tools without recognising autonomous decision-making.

---

### V. Distributed Agents
**Definition:** A socio-technical, ecological, or economic system (market, ecosystem, infrastructure, culture) where agency emerges from the interaction of many entities rather than central control.

**Diagnostic cues:** Outcomes arise without central direction; price signals, ecological adaptation, or cultural norms coordinate behaviour; the system has memory (path dependence, history).

**Typical failure mode:** Reifying the system as a unified agent ("the market decides"; "evolution optimises") while ignoring heterogeneity, contingency, and failures of coordination. Alternatively, denying emergent agency by reducing everything to individual choice.

---

### VI. Abstract Agents
**Definition:** Analytical constructs treating non-present, hypothetical, historical, or theoretical entities as agents for explanatory or modelling purposes.

**Diagnostic cues:** The entity does not exist now, may not exist at all, or exists only in theory. The agent is introduced to explain outcomes, explore scenarios, or model counterfactuals.

**Typical failure mode:** Confusing analytical convenience with causal reality; reifying personifications (e.g., "history judges"; "the unconscious decided") as real agents. Hindsight bias in historical agent assignment.

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Agent

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)

| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Autonomy** | How self-directed is the agent in selecting goals and actions, versus being externally controlled or constrained? | **Full Autonomy:** Complete self-direction; acts on own goals without external control. **High Autonomy:** Largely self-directed; operates within broad constraints; meaningful discretion. **Moderate Autonomy:** Acts within significant constraints but retains meaningful choice. **Limited Autonomy:** Highly constrained; acts mainly within parameters set by others. **Minimal Autonomy:** Little self-direction; actions largely determined by external factors. | Identify who sets goals (the agent or others?); who constrains choices (formal rules, incentives, direct control?); whether the agent can refuse or redirect action; distinguish policy constraints from real-time control. | Determines where to look for control levers and who owns decisions. Informs whether agency is direct (agent controls), delegated (principal controlling via agent), or constrained (external system determines behaviour). Critical for accountability design. |
| **2. Intentionality** | To what extent does the agent have goals/purposes (conscious or functional) that organise its behaviour? | **Full Intentionality:** Conscious purposes, explicit goals, deliberate planning, self-reported intent. **Functional Intentionality:** Behaves as if goal-directed without requiring consciousness; optimises for observable objective. **Derived Intentionality:** Goals set by others; acts to achieve externally assigned purposes; principal's goals, not agent's own. **Emergent Purposiveness:** Apparent goal-directedness arising from non-intentional processes; system behaves purposively without any component intending it. **Non-Intentional:** No goals or purposes; behaviour without direction; mechanistic operation. | Ask: Is behaviour goal-directed? Are goals endogenous (agent's own), assigned by others, or emergent? Document objective functions, incentives, fitness landscapes, or selection pressures. Check for evidence of planning or goal-adjustment. | Prevents projection of human motives onto non-intentional systems; clarifies principal–agent dynamics and responsibility; distinguishes designed purpose from emergent adaptive behaviour. |
| **3. Consciousness** | Whether and how the agent has subjective experience and self-awareness (where relevant and contestable). | **Full Consciousness:** Rich subjective experience, self-awareness, phenomenal states, qualia, sense of self. **Partial Consciousness:** Some subjective experience; limited self-awareness; intermittent or domain-limited phenomenal states. **Uncertain Consciousness:** Unknown whether subjective experience exists; contested cases; reasonable disagreement about status. **Functional Without Consciousness:** Information processing and sophisticated behaviour without subjective experience (philosophical zombies, unconscious algorithms). **Non-Conscious:** No subjective experience; purely mechanistic operation; insentient. | Separate behavioural capability (agent can learn, respond, adapt) from subjective experience (does it feel like something to be the agent?). Where uncertain, record uncertainty explicitly. Avoid moral/legal conclusions that assume consciousness without evidence. Use proxy indicators (neural activity, self-report, adaptive behaviour) but recognise limits. | Avoids category mistakes (treating consciousness as necessary for agency or vice versa); clarifies when rights, blame, or moral standing arguments apply; prevents anthropomorphising non-conscious systems; informs ethical accountability design. |
| **4. Accountability** | Whether and how the agent can be held responsible (morally, legally, institutionally) for actions and outcomes. | **Full Accountability:** Can be praised, blamed, rewarded, punished; fully responsible; culpable for outcomes; duty-bearing. **Partial Accountability:** Some responsibility; mitigated by constraints, intentions, or circumstances; shared with other agents. **Distributed Accountability:** Responsibility shared across multiple agents; no single locus of accountability; network or collective responsibility. **Derived Accountability:** Responsibility traced to designers, owners, operators, or principals; agent is instrument of someone else's accountability. **No Accountability:** Cannot be held responsible; purely causal without moral/legal standing; natural force or mechanism. | Locate the locus of responsibility: the agent itself, its designers/operators, its principals, distributed across a system, or no clear locus. Check legal status (can it be sued?); contractual relationships; governance structures. Assess incentive alignment and control (did the agent have genuine choice?). | Supports accountability design (where to place responsibility); reduces scapegoating and "automation blame" failure modes; clarifies when governance mechanisms are misaligned; informs investigation and remediation priority. |
| **5. Temporal Persistence** | How does the agent's identity persist through time? What counts as "the same" agent across time? | **Continuous Identity:** Same agent persists continuously; clear unbroken identity through time; no reconstitution. **Persistent Pattern:** Pattern persists though components change; "ship of Theseus" identity; organism replacing cells, organisation replacing staff. **Episodic Existence:** Exists only intermittently; reconstituted each time; task force disbanded and reformed, algorithm redeployed. **Generational Persistence:** Persists through succession; identity passes to successors; organisation continues despite complete turnover; reincarnation concept. **Momentary:** No persistence; exists only in single moments of action; fleeting coalitions; one-off decisions. | Define identity criteria: what counts as "the same" agent across time? Track versioning (is v2.0 the "same" AI system as v1.0?); succession and turnover; reconstitution; pattern persistence despite component change. Assess continuity of goals, relationships, and capabilities. | Prevents accountability gaps from drift, rebranding, turnover, and model updates. Supports long-horizon planning by clarifying whether future version of agent bears responsibility. Enables detection of identity-shift evasion (agent rebrands or reconstitutes to escape accountability). |

# 5. Dynamics (Agent Interaction Patterns)

## 5.1 Interaction Patterns Table (mandatory)

| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Cooperation** | Agents coordinate toward shared goals, pooling capabilities and aligning actions. Visible in joint projects, coalitions, alliances, mutual aid. | Agents working together toward shared goals; coordinated action; shared understanding or enforcement of norms; positive-sum dynamics; alignment of incentives or values. | Explains coalitions, joint capability, and shared enterprise; also creates shared failure modes (both agents fail if shared assumption collapses), collective analytical gaps (group-think, pluralistic ignorance), and distributed accountability challenges. | Clarify shared goals explicitly; align incentives; define joint accountability and escalation; create shared evidence loops and learning mechanisms; protect dissent/disagreement channels. |
| 2 | **Competition** | Agents pursue incompatible goals or scarce resources, creating selection pressures and strategic behaviour. Visible in markets, arms races, sports, adversarial adaptation. | Agents pursuing incompatible goals; striving for scarce resources; zero-sum or negative-sum dynamics; strategic behaviour (agents anticipate and counter each other); winners and losers. | Explains arms races, adversarial adaptation, and strategic concealment; often amplifies distortion and secrecy; can produce innovation (selection pressure) or destructive cycles (escalation); creates winner-take-all dynamics and market concentration. | Model adversaries explicitly (know their goals, constraints, capabilities); add monitoring and transparency mechanisms; reduce exploitable interfaces; diversify resource sources; test strategic responses via red-teaming; manage escalation spirals. |
| 3 | **Domination** | Asymmetric control: one agent or class can constrain or compel others via authority, coercion, resources, or dependency. Visible in hierarchies, colonialism, market monopolies, information asymmetries. | Asymmetric control relationships; power differentials; ability to force, constrain, or incentivise compliance; structural inequality; vulnerability of dominated agent. | Explains compliance and suppression; critical for understanding legitimacy, resistance, and risk (dominated agents may comply outwardly while resisting covertly); reveals hidden vulnerability and fragility (system depends on continued domination). | Map power and dependencies explicitly; ensure institutional checks/balances; protect reporting channels and dissent; review legitimacy (is domination accepted as rightful?); reduce exploitable dependencies; monitor for covert resistance. |
| 4 | **Delegation** | Principal–agent relations: one agent acts on behalf of another under mandates, incentives, and accountability. Visible in employment, representation, outsourcing, automation. | One agent acting on behalf of another; principal-agent relationship; separation of principal's goals from agent's incentives; information asymmetries; monitoring and enforcement challenges. | Explains accountability gaps, misaligned incentives, automation blame (agent blamed for principal's choice), and governance failures; central to understanding organisational structure and control design. Delegation over many layers can obscure true decision-maker. | Specify mandates clearly; align agent incentives with principal's goals; instrument monitoring and audit rights; define escalation and review mechanisms; trace accountability through delegation chains; regularly test principal-agent alignment. |
| 5 | **Emergence** | System-level patterns arise from interactions without central control; macro outcomes 'act back' on micro agents. Visible in flocks, markets, cultures, traffic flow, cities, organisations. | Collective patterns arising from individual interactions without central coordination; no agent intends the macro pattern; pattern has properties not present in individuals; feedback: pattern shapes individual behaviour | Explains outcomes no actor intended; requires multi-level modelling and careful attribution (macro pattern ≠ individual agent intention); can produce robustness (decentralised systems adaptive) or brittleness (small perturbations cascade); reification risk (pattern treated as agent intent). | Use systems maps and multi-level analysis; monitor leading indicators; avoid single-actor attribution for system outcomes; run scenario and stress tests; design robust feedback mechanisms; detect tipping points. |

## 7.3 Integration Questions by Framework (all 36 canonical frameworks)

The 36 frameworks in the Analytical Series are organised across six groups (G1–G6) per OG v2.5, §3.2. Agent analysis integrates with all frameworks as follows:

**G1 — Framing & Scope-Setting:** Situations & Context Classification, Boundaries & Scope, Dimensions & Scales, Realities & Assumptions, Time & Sequencing. These provide scope and framing constraints for agent mapping.

**G2 — Structure, Dependencies & Mechanism:** Systems & Process Interdependencies, Relations & Connectivity, Processes & Causal Pathways, Resources & Capability, Causation & Attribution. These reveal system structure where agents operate and dependencies between agents.

**G5 — Evidence & Epistemics:** Evidence & Claim Quality, Failures & Failure Patterns, Uncertainties & Unknown Unknowns, Hiddens & Visibility Gaps, Diagnosis & Root-Cause Analysis. These enable agent profiling and surface visibility failures in agent identification.

**G6 — Choice, Action & Control:** Decisions & Decision-Making, Interventions & Change Levers, Governance & Institutional Design, Risk & Resilience, Learning & Adaptive Capacity. These operationalise agent insights into governance and intervention design.

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

This section identifies the primary visibility failures (Hiddens generators) that this framework is designed to detect and correct.

| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Agent Misidentification | Wrongly identifying who or what is acting—overlooking key actors, false attribution, conflating agent categories | Systematic scan across six meta-categories (§2); evidence-based actor mapping; triangulation across multiple evidence sources; probe for missing intermediaries and marginal actors |
| 2 | Capacity Mismatch | Over- or underestimating agent capacities; confusing role-prescribed capacity with actual capability; assuming agent can do what rules say it should | Explicit dimensional characterisation (§4); competency assessment against evidence; ask "has this agent actually done this before?"; distinguish capability from authority |
| 3 | Category Neglect | Ignoring whole categories of agents; defaulting to individual or institutional frames while missing collective, distributed, or abstract agency | Systematic scan §3 (all 30 types); apply No-Early-Exclusion Rule (touch all six categories before exclusion); audit for category presence in conclusion |
| 4 | Interaction Blindness | Missing how agents interact; treating agent as isolated rather than coupled, delegating, competing, or emerging from collective dynamics | Explicit attention to five interaction patterns (§5); interface mapping (§6); trace information flow (Communication Interfaces), action coupling (Action Interfaces), and constitution (Constitution Interfaces) |
| 5 | Hiddens Omission (core failure) | Agent analysis assumes visibility and stable categories; hidden agency, concealed principals, distorted agency, and positional invisibility remain untested, leading to misattribution of intent and false accountability | **Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan (§8.2) for any non-trivial agent mapping.** Shortlist Tier 2 hidden types and attach detection/remediation actions to each Communication, Action, and Constitution Interface. Escalate Tier 3 if agent-level Unknowns could change decisions |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)

Apply the following scan at least twice: once at initial agent mapping, and once at pre-closure validation. This is the mandatory baseline for agent-level Hiddens discovery.

| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I. Perceptual Hiddens** | Agent identification is vulnerable to attention limits; key actors can be overlooked, especially intermediaries, marginal stakeholders, and agents operating at interfaces and in distributed systems. | Missing actors in maps; surprises during interviews ("we forgot to ask about X"); unobserved intermediaries (brokers, translators, gatekeepers); 'it just happened' narratives (suggests distributed agency, not individual); role-holders who don't act as roles prescribe | Independent agent mapping (ask different teams); interface walk-throughs (trace where information and action cross boundaries); anomaly prompts (ask "who benefited? who was ignored? who was surprised?"); role-based evidence comparison (do logs match role descriptions?) |
| **II. Systemic Hiddens** | Incentives and structures systematically misrepresent agency (proxy targets, scapegoating, automation blame, role-based appearance masking true power). Formal roles conceal informal decision-making; metrics distort what agents optimise for. | Accountability gaps and blame shifted to low-autonomy agents; automation blamed for organisational choices; formal decision rights don't match observed power; metric gaming observed; compliance theatre visible (espoused policy ≠ observed practice) | Incentive and governance audits (what is actually rewarded?); principal–agent analysis (trace decisions from stated policy to actual authority); red-team assumption testing (what if the formal structure is a lie?); metrics audit (what do agents actually optimise for?) |
| **III. Informational Hiddens** | Information about who acted can be withheld, distorted, or fragmented across systems and organisations. Undocumented exceptions, deleted communications, late discovery of decision paths obstruct agency clarity. | Conflicting accounts of who decided (different witnesses tell different stories); missing logs or communications (suspiciously incomplete records); undocumented exceptions or workarounds; late discovery of decision paths; testimony that contradicts logs | Access mapping (who had access to information and decision points?); provenance/logging review (are logs complete? Were records altered?); triangulation across artefacts (cross-check testimony against logs, emails, approvals); exception registers (what was done outside procedure?); audit trails (can decisions be traced?) |
| **IV. Temporal Hiddens** | Agency can persist through identity shifts (rebranding, turnover, succession, model updates, organisational restructure) while accountability gaps widen. Conversely, momentary actors can escape accountability through rapid reconstitution. | Accountability gaps widening at identity transitions (turnover, rebranding, model updates); previous actor's responsibility assigned to current actor despite different intent; successor organisation claims no connection to predecessor; rapid reconstitution allowing evasion | Identity tracking (is this the "same" agent across changes?); succession analysis (who follows whom? What was passed on?); responsibility continuity check (does accountability follow identity shift?); reconstitution tracking (how quickly do agents reassemble? What allows escape?); version/generation mapping (is v2.0 responsible for v1.0's failures?) |
| **V. Behavioral Hiddens** | Agents may conceal their actual goals, expertise, knowledge, or constraints; compliance theatre and espoused-vs-operative behaviour masks true intentionality; collusion and coordinated deception create false narratives of individual action. | Compliance theatre (espoused policy ≠ observed practice); undisclosed expertise or knowledge (agent knows more/less than visible); goal concealment (agent's real goal diverges from stated objective); collusion undetected (multiple agents coordinate secretly); silent sabotage or covert resistance | Espoused vs operative analysis (compare stated policy to observed behaviour); expertise audit (what does agent actually know?); goal inspection (is stated goal the real goal?); collusion detection (are there coordinated deviations from expected behaviour? Economic tests: do actor payoffs suggest coordination?); testimony comparison (do different actors give consistent accounts?) |
| **VI. Structural Hiddens** | Agency emerges from distributed interaction in ways no single actor intends. Macro patterns 'act back' on constituents, creating the appearance of unified agency where none exists—or conversely, fragmenting responsibility across unknowable networks. | Outcomes no actor intended (surprises at system level); attribution difficulty (who caused this? Everyone and no one); patterns misidentified as unified agents (the "market" vs individual traders; "evolution" vs selection pressures); responsibility fragmentation (who is liable?); system-level patterns acting as constraints or affordances on individuals | Systems-level modelling (what patterns emerge at system level?); attribution analysis (can we trace outcomes to individual agents or is it emergent?); reification detection (are we treating patterns as agents?); cascade modelling (how do individual decisions combine?); network analysis (is there a single locus of control or truly distributed agency?) |

# 9. The 7-Step Rapid Protocol for Agent Analysis (optional accelerant)

When time or context constrains Full Depth execution, apply this rapid protocol to generate a minimum viable agent map and Hiddens validation:

1. **Identify focal question and scope.** What decision or investigation is this agent analysis for? Who is in/out of scope?

2. **Rapid actor list (5 min).** Brainstorm all actors mentioned in scenario, logs, interviews. Don't worry about exhaustiveness; capture obvious ones.

3. **Assign six meta-categories (3 min).** For each actor, assign to one of six meta-categories (Individual, Collective, Institutional, Artificial, Distributed, Abstract). Are any categories missing entirely?

4. **Quick dimensional sketch (5 min).** For top agents, estimate: Autonomy (High/Medium/Low), Intentionality (Yes/Functional/No), Accountability (Clear/Partial/Distributed). Note uncertainties.

5. **Interaction pattern check (3 min).** Do any of the five interaction patterns dominate (Cooperation/Competition/Domination/Delegation/Emergence)? Are any interactions hidden?

6. **Tier 1 Hiddens scan (5 min).** For each meta-category and interaction pattern, ask: "What agents might be invisible here?" Shortlist top 3–5 Hiddens.

7. **Probe priorities (2 min).** What one or two probes would most reduce uncertainty about hidden agents? Escalate if probes are high-impact and feasible.

**Output:** 1-page agent map (actors, categories, dimensions, top Hiddens, probe priorities). Escalate for Investigative Run Mode if any Hiddens are material to the decision.

# Conclusion & Closure Discipline

This framework operationalises agent identification, dimensional profiling, and accountability design for complex scenarios. Its core value is making agency visible—both hidden actors and the distributed, delegated, and constrained forms of agency that resist simple attribution.

**For closure**, use the five required items from OG v2.5, §7.4:

1. **Residual unknowns about agents.** What remains unclear? Why? Can it be reduced? At what cost?

2. **Acceptability rationale.** Is the residual uncertainty acceptable for the decision at hand? What is our risk tolerance?

3. **Monitoring plan.** How will we detect if our agent map was wrong? What early-warning indicators should we track?

4. **Ownership.** Who is accountable for maintaining the agent analysis and updating it as conditions change?

5. **Learning hook.** What will we do if the agent analysis proves incorrect? How will we feed back into future analysis?

Record these explicitly in your Residual Unknowns + Closure Note. Do not close prematurely on agent identity, accountability, or principal–agent alignment if evidence is weak or contested.
