# Introduction

## What is Scenarios?

Scenarios is the disciplined practice of constructing and reasoning across alternative possible futures—not just the most likely future, but the range of plausible futures including surprising, discontinuous, and uncomfortable ones. A scenario is a internally coherent narrative of how the world could unfold over a specified time horizon, built from explicit assumptions about drivers (technological, market, social, political, environmental), discontinuities (breaks from past patterns), and feedback loops. Scenarios are distinct from predictions (which claim likelihood) and from wild speculation (which ignores coherence and evidence). They are tools for making the invisible visible: revealing hidden assumptions about the future that are embedded invisibly in current strategy, current risk estimates, and current organizational worldviews.

Scenarios are analytically important because organizations routinely make strategic and operational decisions while implicitly assuming a single future. This future-as-continuation bias (treating incremental change as the only path forward) creates systematic blindness to discontinuous change, to tail risks, to mode shifts in markets or geopolitics or technology, and to the false confidence that comes from planning around one expected future. When that expected future does not arrive, organizations discover—often too late—that their strategy was brittle, that their risk mitigations were designed for the wrong risk space, and that they had made invisible assumptions about what was possible.

Scenarios make these assumptions visible and testable. By constructing two or more fundamentally different futures from the same current moment, an organization can discover: (1) What assumptions are driving the current strategy? (2) What futures would make that strategy fail? (3) What weak signals are being dismissed because they contradict the expected future? (4) What possibilities are being treated as impossible because they violate current worldviews? (5) What strategic options would be robust across multiple futures rather than brittle in one?

## Why does Scenarios matter for Hiddens work?

Scenarios is foundational to Hiddens work because it surfaces the hidden assumptions embedded in organizational narratives about the future. These assumptions are particularly invisible because they operate at the level of what-is-deemed-possible: assumptions about which futures are plausible and which are not, about which discontinuities are "realistic" and which are "extreme," about the trajectory of technologies, markets, or geopolitical stability. An analyst dismissing a future as "unlikely" or "extreme" may be importing unexamined assumptions about technological limits, about social change speeds, about political stability, or about institutional inertia.

Scenarios directly addresses four major Hiddens meta-categories:

1. **Assumptions about futures and possibility spaces (Category I – System-Level Hiddens):** Organizations embed assumptions about what is possible, inevitable, or impossible into their strategies. These are often invisible precisely because they feel like facts rather than assumptions. Scenarios makes them visible by constructing alternatives.

2. **Forecast bias and single-future framing (Category II – Cognitive & Perceptual Hiddens):** The mind naturally settles on one expected future and treats it as inevitable. This bias is so strong that organizations routinely fail to prepare for predictable alternative futures. Scenarios disrupts this bias by forcing explicit construction of multiple futures.

3. **Normality bias and discontinuity invisibility (Category III – Denial & Distortion):** Discontinuous change is routinely dismissed as extreme, unrealistic, or impossible—particularly when it contradicts the dominant narrative about how things work. Scenarios specifically targets discontinuities and makes them structurally visible.

4. **Strategy selection bias (Category IV – Organizational & Institutional Hiddens):** Organizations select scenarios that confirm existing strategy or that are flattering to current decision-makers. Scenarios frameworks that include explicit bias checks and uncomfortable-future forcing mechanisms help surface this distortion.

Additionally, Scenarios connects to weak signal discovery (Category V): Many weak signals are dismissed because they point toward futures that violate current worldviews. By constructing alternative futures first, weak signal detection becomes more systematic.

## What does this framework produce?

The Framework of Scenarios produces a structured toolkit for futures thinking and assumption discovery. Its primary artefacts are:

1. **Scenario type taxonomy:** A categorization of scenario archetypes (continuity scenarios, cyclical scenarios, transformative scenarios, discontinuous scenarios, adversarial scenarios) that helps practitioners recognize the patterns they are implicitly using.

2. **Scenario logic structures:** Driver matrices (explicit mapping of key uncertainties and their interactions), causal logics (feedback loops and tipping points), discontinuity maps (where and how past patterns break).

3. **Alternative future narratives:** Fully constructed scenarios with internally coherent stories, not just matrices of variables. These narratives make assumptions visible and testable in ways that matrices alone cannot.

4. **Robustness analysis:** Systematic testing of current strategy across scenarios, identification of brittle points and failure modes, discovery of scenarios where strategy fails.

5. **Assumptions registers:** Explicit cataloguing of assumptions hidden in current framings, with testing protocols and monitoring plans.

6. **Evidence integration:** Mapping of weak signals to scenarios, identification of which scenario is becoming visible in current data, early warning protocols.

These artefacts feed into the broader Analytical Series through the Hiddens Register (assumptions discovered), the Evidence Ledger (signals mapped to scenarios), the Hypothesis/Test Backlog (assumptions to be tested), and the Information Requests register (data needed to distinguish between scenarios).

## How to use this framework

Scenarios is typically invoked when strategic decisions are being made under uncertainty about futures, when risk estimates seem to be missing tail risks or discontinuities, when organizational strategy appears brittle or oversensitive to one assumed future, or when weak signals suggest that assumed futures may be shifting. The Operating Guide §4.3 routing rules specify when Scenarios should be combined with Risk, Uncertainties, and Learning frameworks.

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: construct two or three contrastive scenarios, identify key assumptions, test strategy robustness. Full Depth Framework Execution (including comprehensive driver analysis, discontinuity mapping, weak signal integration, and Tier 2/3 Hiddens scans) is recommended when decision consequences are high, when organizational strategy has high sensitivity to future assumptions, or when hidden selection bias is suspected.

Scenarios integrates with the Operating Guide's iterative investigation loop (§5.0) by anchoring the initial framing question: "In what future would the current view be radically wrong?" This question surfaces hidden assumptions and directs the investigation toward either confirming or refuting them through evidence gathering and Hiddens discovery.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Scenarios operates in the space where the future possibility space itself is uncertain or contested. This is distinct from Risk (which estimates probability distributions over known outcome types) and from Uncertainties (which classifies types and levels of not-knowing rather than constructing alternative futures). Scenarios asks: What alternative futures are internally coherent, plausible from current evidence, and would substantially alter the validity of current strategy?

A scenario is not a forecast. Forecasts claim likelihood; scenarios claim coherence and internal logical consistency. A scenario is not speculation; it is built from explicit drivers (technological, market, social, political, environmental), causal logics (how the future unfolds from the present), and discontinuities (where and how past patterns break). A scenario makes assumptions visible: about what is possible, about the speed of change, about the stability of institutions, about feedback loops and tipping points.

The core premise rests on two operational findings:

First, organizations systematically underestimate the range of plausible futures. This underestimation is not random; it reflects predictable biases: future-as-continuation bias (treating recent past as the future), normality bias (discontinuous change as impossible), and single-future overconfidence (treating the expected future as inevitable). These biases are particularly strong in mature organizations with stable business models and long planning horizons, precisely where discontinuous disruption is most dangerous.

Second, hidden assumptions about the future are embedded invisibly in organizational strategy, risk estimates, and narratives. These assumptions are invisible because they feel like facts or constraints rather than assumptions. An analyst saying "that future is unrealistic" may be importing unstated assumptions about technological limits, social stability, or organizational inertia. An organization planning around one expected future has made invisible assumptions about which alternatives are too improbable to warrant preparation.

Scenarios makes these assumptions visible and testable by forcing explicit construction of fundamentally different futures from the same current moment. By asking "What would have to be true for this alternative future to unfold?" and "What assumptions about the present world does this future require?", scenarios analysis surfaces assumptions that would otherwise remain invisible. By testing current strategy across multiple scenarios and asking "In which futures does this strategy fail?", scenarios analysis discovers hidden strategy vulnerabilities.

The framework contributes three core products to the broader investigation:

1. **Assumption visibility:** Explicit cataloguing of assumptions about what is possible, probable, and impossible, with identification of which assumptions are contested, weak, or hidden.

2. **Robustness testing:** Systematic testing of current strategy, risk mitigations, and organizational capabilities across scenarios, with identification of brittle points and failure modes.

3. **Weak signal integration:** Systematic mapping of current weak signals to scenarios, with early warning protocols for when one scenario is becoming visible in the data and when key assumptions are being violated.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Scenarios:** Scenarios are internally coherent narratives of alternative possible futures, constructed from explicit drivers, causal logics, and discontinuities, designed to surface hidden assumptions about what is possible and to test strategy robustness. Scenarios are not predictions (they do not claim likelihood), not speculation (they maintain coherence with evidence), not long-range planning (they are designed to be disruptive), and not forecasts (they do not estimate probabilities).

**In scope:**
- Construction of alternative possible futures from current evidence and uncertainties
- Identification of scenario drivers (technological, market, social, political, environmental, ecological)
- Mapping of discontinuities and tipping points where past patterns break
- Explicit surfacing of assumptions about what is possible and how the future unfolds
- Scenario logics: internal causal chains, feedback loops, reinforcing patterns
- Testing of current strategy across scenarios; identification of robustness and brittleness
- Discovery of futures in which current strategy fails or becomes irrelevant
- Weak signal detection and scenario-signal mapping
- Assumptions registers and testing protocols
- Governance of scenario use; prevention of scenario selection bias
- Integration with Risk, Uncertainties, Decisions, and Learning frameworks

**Out of scope:**
- Probability estimation or likelihood forecasting
- Long-range strategic planning around a single expected future
- Scenario construction from insufficient evidence (without explicit caveat)
- Academic futures studies separated from decision-making context
- Predictive analytics or statistical forecasting
- Organizational change management (separate from scenario implications)
- Technology roadmapping (unless explicitly scenario-grounded)

### 1.6.2 Near-neighbour disambiguation

**Scenarios vs. Risk:**
- **Risk** answers: "What are the possible bad outcomes, their probabilities, and their impacts?"
- **Scenarios** answers: "What are the fundamentally different futures that are possible, what assumptions make them plausible, and how does strategy perform in each?"
- **Disambiguation rule:** If the question is about probability distribution over known outcome types, use Risk. If the question is about whether the possibility space itself is correct, use Scenarios. Both frameworks are often used together: Scenarios defines the futures, Risk estimates probability and impact within each scenario.

**Scenarios vs. Uncertainties:**
- **Uncertainties** answers: "What don't we know? How certain or uncertain is our knowledge? How should we handle different types of not-knowing?"
- **Scenarios** answers: "What different ways could the future unfold? What would have to be true for each?"
- **Disambiguation rule:** Uncertainties is primarily epistemic (about knowledge states); Scenarios is primarily futures-oriented (about possibility spaces). Scenarios often uses Uncertainties as input (mapping uncertainties onto scenario branching), but they are distinct frameworks.

**Scenarios vs. Decisions:**
- **Decisions** answers: "What should we choose at this decision point? What choice logic applies?"
- **Scenarios** answers: "In what futures would this decision be right? In what futures would it fail?"
- **Disambiguation rule:** Scenarios frames the futures context; Decisions makes the choice within a future (or across futures if robust strategy is the goal). Use Scenarios first to define the futures, then Decisions to evaluate choices in each scenario.

**Scenarios vs. Learning and Adaptation:**
- **Learning and Adaptation** answers: "How does understanding update from experience? How should we learn as the future unfolds?"
- **Scenarios** answers: "What futures could unfold? What would we see if each futures were becoming true?"
- **Disambiguation rule:** Scenarios identifies what-to-watch-for; Learning and Adaptation designs the feedback loops and monitoring protocols to detect scenario shifts. They are sequential: Scenarios first, then Learning design based on Scenarios outputs.

### 1.6.3 Routing triggers

The Scenarios framework should be invoked when:

1. **Strategic decisions are being made under deep uncertainty about futures** — when decision consequences are high and future assumptions are not explicit
2. **Risk estimates appear to be missing discontinuities or tail risks** — when current risk models assume continuity and may miss regime shifts
3. **Organizational strategy appears brittle or oversensitive to one assumed future** — when small changes in future assumptions would invalidate the strategy
4. **Weak signals suggest the assumed future may be shifting** — when current data contradicts the expected future trajectory
5. **Scenario selection bias is suspected** — when the organization appears to be using scenarios selectively to confirm strategy
6. **Multiple competing narratives about the future coexist in the organization** — when different groups hold incompatible assumptions about what is possible
7. **Planning horizons are long and discontinuity risk is high** — when 5+ year horizons make future assumptions particularly consequential
8. **Organizational learning loops need to be configured** — when early warning systems and scenario-shift detection are required

**Default routing (from Operating Guide §4.3):** Scenarios is typically run in combination with Uncertainties (to identify key branching uncertainties), Risk (to estimate impact and cascade risk within scenarios), and Decisions (to evaluate strategic choice across futures). Full Depth execution is recommended when routing is from risk/strategy review processes; Light Depth is appropriate for iterative scenario refinement.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Continuity Scenarios

Continuity scenarios assume that past patterns will continue: that market structures will persist, that institutions will remain stable, that the speed of change will be similar to recent experience, that current assumptions about what is possible remain valid. These scenarios are not conservative (in the sense of defensive); they are the default projection of recent trends into the future.

Continuity scenarios are essential because they establish the baseline assumptions embedded in current strategy. Most organizations' strategic plans are implicitly continuity scenarios: they assume their market structure will persist, their competitive advantages will endure, their regulatory environment will remain stable. Making these assumptions explicit (through formalized continuity scenarios) creates the opportunity to stress-test them and to discover what would break if they proved false.

The primary hiding mechanism that continuity scenarios surface is future-as-continuation bias: the strong cognitive tendency to assume that the future will resemble recent experience. This bias is particularly dangerous in mature organizations precisely because past continuity (which is real and recent) is used as evidence for future continuity (which is assumed but not certain). Continuity scenarios also surface assumptions about what is inevitable: the view that, for example, digital transformation will continue to accelerate, or that emerging markets will continue to grow, or that interest rates will remain near zero. These are assumptions, not facts.

### II. Cyclical Scenarios

Cyclical scenarios assume that the world operates through recurring patterns: market cycles (boom, peak, contraction, recovery), political cycles (progressive, conservative, regressive, progressive), technology cycles (novelty, growth, saturation, displacement), adoption cycles (innovators, early adopters, majority, laggards). These scenarios focus on variations in cycle phase, amplitude, and frequency rather than on fundamental discontinuity.

Cyclical scenarios are valuable for organizations whose fortunes are tightly coupled to cycles: financial services (market cycles), real estate (property cycles), consumer technology (adoption cycles), energy (commodity price cycles). They are also valuable for detecting assumptions about cycle stability: assumptions that cycles will continue to operate, that cycle amplitudes will remain within historical bounds, that phase transitions will be predictable.

The primary hiding mechanism is cycle-phase blindness: the difficulty of identifying which phase of a cycle the present moment occupies. Organizations in the euphoria phase of a market cycle often make strategic decisions as if boom conditions will continue indefinitely. Cyclical scenarios surface these phase assumptions and force explicit consideration of what happens when phases transition.

### III. Transformative Scenarios

Transformative scenarios assume that fundamental structures or paradigms will shift: that market structures that have persisted for decades will be reconfigured, that institutional forms will change radically (e.g., centralized vs. decentralized, hierarchical vs. network), that dominant technologies will be displaced, that new organizing principles will emerge. These scenarios move beyond cyclical disruption (which assumes cycles continue) to fundamental restructuring.

Transformative scenarios are particularly valuable in technology-driven industries, in policy domains where institutional forms are being challenged, and in mature industries facing sustained disruption. They are also valuable in detecting normalcy bias: the difficulty of taking seriously the possibility that "how things have always been" will fundamentally change. A transformative scenario in retail, for example, might imagine a world in which physical stores disappear entirely or are reconfigured as logistics hubs or customer experience centers—not because this is inevitable, but because ignoring this possibility has costs if it occurs.

The primary hiding mechanisms are: (1) normalcy bias (treating current structures as inevitable or nearly inevitable), (2) institutional inertia assumption (assuming structures change slowly), and (3) technological determinism (assuming that because technology has always driven change, current technologies will continue to do so). Transformative scenarios surface these assumptions and force explicit consideration of what organizing principles could replace current structures.

### IV. Discontinuous Scenarios

Discontinuous scenarios assume breaks from past causal patterns, tipping points, cascade effects, or non-linear amplification. These scenarios are distinct from transformative scenarios in that they focus on specific rupture points (where causation breaks or reverses) rather than on wholesale institutional restructuring. A discontinuous scenario might involve a sudden loss of confidence in a currency, a pandemic shutdown, a critical technology failure, a political upheaval, or a geopolitical realignment—events that violate assumptions about the stability of current systems.

Discontinuous scenarios are essential for stress-testing against black swan events and for identifying where organizational models are brittle. They are also essential for identifying scenarios where competitive advantages or risk mitigations become suddenly irrelevant. If, for example, a business model depends on consumer confidence or on particular regulatory frameworks, discontinuous scenarios can explore what happens if those assumptions rupture.

The primary hiding mechanisms are: (1) normalcy bias and tail-risk dismissal (treating discontinuous change as impossible or too extreme to warrant planning), (2) assumption of linear causation (assuming that causal relationships will continue as in the past), and (3) invisibility of tipping points (not recognizing that some small changes in conditions can trigger large, non-linear effects). Discontinuous scenarios surface these assumptions and force explicit consideration of where organizational models are sensitive to ruptures.

### V. Adversarial Scenarios

Adversarial scenarios assume that intelligent actors will deliberately act against organizational interests: that competitors will make specific moves, that hostile governments or factions will implement damaging policies, that supply chain partners will defect or fail, that users will find ways to circumvent organizational controls. These scenarios are distinct from other scenario types in that they explicitly model human agency and intentional opposition.

Adversarial scenarios are valuable in competitive industries, in regulated sectors where policy adversaries exist, in cybersecurity and defense contexts, and in business domains where supply chain or partnership risks are high. They force organizations to think about their strategy from the perspective of actors who want it to fail.

The primary hiding mechanism is invisibility of intelligent opposition: the assumption that competitors or adversaries will behave passively or predictably, or the assumption that past competitive moves are a guide to future competitive moves when competitive landscapes are shifting. Adversarial scenarios surface these assumptions and force explicit consideration of what smart competitors or hostile actors might do in response to organizational strategy.

### VI. Counterfactual Scenarios

Counterfactual scenarios reconstruct the past or the present by changing one or more key assumptions about what actually happened or what is currently true. These scenarios are used not primarily to predict futures, but to understand causation: if we changed X, what would follow? These scenarios are powerful tools for causal reasoning and for testing assumptions about current state.

A counterfactual scenario might ask: "What if a founding regulation had been written differently?" or "What if our major competitor had not failed?" or "What if supply chains had not been disrupted by the pandemic?" By exploring what would follow from a different X, counterfactual scenarios help isolate causal effects and test whether current assumptions about causal relationships are robust.

The primary use is in assumption testing and causal learning. Counterfactual scenarios are also valuable for understanding path dependence: recognizing that current state depends on specific historical contingencies and that small changes earlier would have led to fundamentally different current states.

# 3. Core Types Taxonomy

## 3.0 Canonical Rule & Deviations

**Canonical rule:** This framework follows the standard 6 meta-categories × 5 core types structure = 30 core types, as specified in the Master Rewrite Template v3.0.

**Deviation:** This framework deviates slightly to accommodate scenario-specific complexity. Each category contains 5 core scenario types that are distinct in how they construct or reason about possible futures. These types are not mutually exclusive at the level of individual scenarios (a real scenario might combine elements of continuity and cyclical reasoning), but they are distinct as analytical archetypes.

**Mapping rule:** All 30 core scenario types map back to the canonical Hiddens framework (6 categories × 5 types), with explicit connection to assumptions made visible by each type.

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)

The 30 core types provide a comprehensive taxonomy for scenario construction. However, specific domains and investigation contexts may require additional types or combinations. Domains may add types by:

1. **Appending domain-specific scenario archetypes** that are common in that domain (e.g., "supply chain localization" in manufacturing, "regulatory regionalization" in energy). Add these as additional rows in the relevant category table; document the justification and the hiddens they surface.

2. **Combining core types** when the scenario under investigation blends multiple core types (e.g., "Transformative Scenarios + Adversarial Scenarios" for a scenario in which market structure changes are driven by coordinated competitor moves).

3. **Creating category-crossing scenarios** when a scenario does not fit neatly into one meta-category. Document the mapping to multiple meta-categories and note which category is primary.

4. **Registering domain-novel assumptions** that are surfaced by domain-specific scenario types. Document these in the Assumptions Register and ensure they are tracked through Tier 2 Hiddens analysis.

When extending the taxonomy, maintain the structural invariant: each type should have a clear definition, a specific use case, and an assumption it surfaces. Types that simply repeat existing types under different names should be consolidated.

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Scenario

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)

The following five dimensions apply to every scenario, regardless of type. These dimensions structure how scenarios are described, compared, and tested for robustness.

| Dimension | Definition | Operative Choices | Why it matters | How to assess |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Scope:** What systems or domains does this scenario affect? | The breadth of change: does the scenario affect one organization, an industry, a region, global systems, or some combination? | Single organization / industry / region / global / multi-domain | Scope frames which risks are internal vs. external, which exposures are company-specific vs. systemic, which responses are local vs. require external coordination | Map scenario drivers to their scope; identify which are company-specific, industry-wide, regional, or global; flag cascades across scopes |
| **Causation:** What are the causal drivers and feedback loops? | The logic by which the scenario unfolds: what causes lead to what effects; which feedback loops reinforce or dampen the scenario | Primary drivers (technological, market, social, political, environmental) / feedback loops (reinforcing or damping) / tipping points / lag structures | Causation clarifies whether the scenario is self-reinforcing or self-correcting, where tipping points exist, how slow or fast the scenario unfolds, and which interventions might alter the trajectory | Explicitly trace causal chains from initial drivers through to endpoints; identify feedback loops; mark tipping points; specify lag structures |
| **Timeline:** Over what time period does this scenario unfold? | The temporal extent: does the scenario unfold over 1 year, 5 years, 10 years, or longer? | Near-term (1-2 years) / medium-term (3-5 years) / long-term (5-10 years) / very-long-term (10+ years) / non-linear timeline (slow then sudden) | Timeline matters because it determines which organizational changes are possible, which data can inform preparation, which early signals are visible, and which interventions have time to work | Specify explicit timeline for each scenario; identify inflection points; note which signals would appear when; distinguish slow-then-sudden vs. linear timelines |
| **Reversibility:** Can the scenario be reversed or recovered from if it occurs? | The degree to which states or paths in the scenario are reversible or locked-in: can you exit the scenario if you recognize it's happening, or are decisions/changes irreversible? | Fully reversible / partially reversible / largely locked-in / point-of-no-return | Reversibility determines whether strategy should focus on prevention or recovery, whether options preservation is critical, whether there are safe points to wait-and-see | Identify which transitions in the scenario are reversible; mark points of no return; flag irreversible decisions or changes; assess recovery costs |
| **Surprise / Discontinuity:** Does this scenario involve unexpected or discontinuous change, or does it emerge recognizably from current trends? | The degree to which the scenario contradicts current expectations or involves surprise: is it a surprise scenario or a plausible-extrapolation scenario? | Predictable extrapolation / surprising but plausible / highly discontinuous / black swan | Surprise matters because it determines whether current monitoring systems will detect the scenario shift, whether organizational readiness is adequate, whether early warning is possible | Assess how much the scenario violates current expectations; specify what data would make it visible; identify how early warning would operate; note surprise points |

## 4.2 Dimension Assessment Protocol

When constructing or assessing any scenario, complete the following protocol for each dimension:

1. **Scope:** Explicitly identify which organizations, industries, regions, and global systems are affected. Draw a diagram if helpful. Flag which effects are contained vs. cascade.

2. **Causation:** Create a causal logic diagram for the scenario showing primary drivers, feedback loops, reinforcement points, damping points, and tipping points. Specify lag structures (how quickly effects propagate).

3. **Timeline:** Specify the time horizon for scenario construction (5-year, 10-year, etc.). Mark inflection points (when major transitions occur). Specify which transitions are slow-then-sudden vs. linear. Identify when key signals would become visible.

4. **Reversibility:** For each major state transition or decision in the scenario, mark whether it is reversible, partially reversible, or locked-in. Identify points of no return. Assess recovery costs for key transitions.

5. **Surprise/Discontinuity:** Assess how much the scenario contradicts current organizational expectations and current base-case forecasts. Identify what data would make the scenario visible to observers. Assess readiness.

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)

| Pattern | Definition | When it appears | Organizational implications | Detection signals |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Slow-then-sudden | Change is undetectable for extended period, then accelerates non-linearly into rapid transformation; common in technology adoption, institutional collapse, geopolitical shifts | Often appears in scenarios involving tipping points, threshold effects, or phase transitions; particularly in discontinuous and transformative scenarios | Organizations optimized for linear forecasting may be completely unprepared; early warning systems tuned for linear change miss the pattern until too late | Invisible slow phase followed by sudden emergence of signals; by definition, signals appear very late in the pattern; post-hoc analysis typically shows observable slow phase that was dismissed as insignificant |
| Oscillating / cycling | Change oscillates between states without clear directional trend; examples: market booms and crashes, political swings, competitive intensity cycles, confidence cycles | Cyclical scenario types explicitly assume oscillation; but oscillation can also appear in transformative or discontinuous scenarios as an interim pattern before a regime shift | Strategies optimized for one cycle phase become brittle when the cycle shifts; early in a new cycle, current state may feel familiar from earlier cycles, creating false confidence | Visible oscillation around a trend line; phase transition indicators; change in cycle amplitude or frequency |
| Accelerating / decelerating | Change accelerates or decelerates over time; acceleration common in technology adoption and disruption, deceleration common in institutional change and market saturation | Can appear in most scenario types; particularly prominent in technology adoption and transformation scenarios | Extrapolation of current trends may seriously mis-estimate where change will be in 5-10 years if acceleration is underestimated; conversely, assuming deceleration when acceleration is occurring leads to being caught off-guard | Change in the rate of change; data that shows non-linear progression; leading indicators that accelerate or decelerate |
| Lock-in / irreversibility | Change becomes increasingly locked-in or path-dependent; early choices constrain later choices; switching costs increase over time | Appears in scenarios involving technological standards, institutional evolution, supplier relationships, regulatory entrenchment, or ecological tipping points | Early choices about strategy, partnerships, or investments may become increasingly costly to undo; options preservation becomes critical in scenarios where lock-in is high | Increasing switching costs; entanglement of systems; path dependence becoming visible in strategic options |
| Contagion / cascade | Change in one system triggers change in adjacent systems; contagion spreads through networks or supply chains; effects amplify through feedback loops | Appears prominently in discontinuous scenarios, in supply chain disruption scenarios, in confidence collapse scenarios, and in scenarios with high coupling between systems | Strategies that assume isolation or that are insensitive to upstream or downstream effects become exposed; single-point-of-failure risks become critical | Spread of impact across system boundaries; amplification of effects beyond direct cause; network or supply chain contagion |
| Bifurcation / divergence | At some point, two distinct futures diverge from a common present; which future emerges depends on which side of a threshold the system lands | Occurs at decision points and tipping points in scenarios; marks moments where small differences lead to large outcome differences | Organizations must identify bifurcation points clearly; strategy before bifurcation differs from strategy after; ability to navigate toward preferred bifurcation outcome is critical | Existence of threshold or tipping point; small interventions having large effects; system behavior changing qualitatively across threshold |
| Emergence / self-organization | Order or coordination emerges without centralized direction; new organizational forms or collective behaviors emerge from distributed actors following simple rules | Can appear in transformative and discontinuous scenarios; particularly when market structures or organizational forms are reshaping | Centralized control strategies become ineffective; need for strategy that works with emergent order rather than against it; identification of what simple rules are driving emergence | Appearance of novel order or coordination that was not explicitly designed; patterns that cannot be explained by centralized direction |
| Hysteresis / non-reversibility | System behavior differs depending on path taken to current state; return to prior state does not restore prior behavior | Appears in scenarios involving institutional or technological lock-in, learning and adaptation, or ecological thresholds | Strategies that assume you can "go back to how things were" if a scenario unfolds are wrong; transitions that occur must account for hysteresis | Different system behavior at same parameter values depending on direction of approach; inability to return to prior equilibrium even if conditions reverse |

## 7.3 Integration Questions by Framework (recommended)

### Risk Framework

1. **Scenario-Risk coupling:** Which risk factors dominate in which scenarios? Do Risk's probability estimates assume a particular scenario implicitly, or are they scenario-independent?

2. **Tail risk visibility:** Are scenarios helping identify tail risks that Risk analysis alone might miss? Which scenarios concentrate previously-scattered risks into new combinations?

3. **Cascade and contagion:** Do Risk's cascade models identify cascade patterns that should shape scenarios? Or do scenarios reveal cascade paths that Risk analysis has not captured?

4. **Risk appetite:** Is the organization's stated risk appetite realistic in all scenarios, or does it implicitly assume a particular scenario (e.g., assume good economic times)?

5. **Risk monitoring:** Which risk indicators should shift based on which scenarios? What does risk monitoring look like in each scenario?

### Uncertainties Framework

1. **Scenario branching:** Do the key scenarios branch on the highest-impact uncertainties? Or are important uncertainties being overlooked in scenario construction?

2. **Uncertainty interaction:** Which uncertainties have strong interactions that shape which scenarios are plausible together? Are scenarios correctly reflecting these interactions?

3. **Uncertainty handling:** For each key uncertainty in the scenarios, is the organization's uncertainty handling strategy (reduce, robustify, option-create, deliberate, precaution) appropriate?

4. **Extreme uncertainties:** Are scenarios including the extreme ends of uncertainty ranges? Or are scenarios clustering toward the middle, missing tail scenarios?

5. **Uncertainty evolution:** As uncertainties are resolved (through evidence gathering or time passage), which scenarios become more/less plausible? Is the organization monitoring this evolution?

### Decisions Framework

1. **Scenario-contingent decisions:** Which decisions should be deferred until scenarios clarify? Which decisions are robust across scenarios?

2. **Contingent strategies:** For each scenario, what is the optimal strategy or decision? Are contingent strategies being prepared?

3. **Decision reversibility:** Does the decision allow for recovery if the wrong scenario unfolds? Or does the decision lock the organization into one scenario outcome?

4. **Decision triggers:** What signals would trigger a shift from one contingent strategy to another? Are these signals being monitored?

5. **Decision authority:** In different scenarios, who has decision authority and how does that affect strategy? Are governance structures scenario-dependent?

### Learning and Adaptation Framework

1. **Scenario detection:** Which metrics and indicators would signal that a particular scenario is becoming visible in data? Is monitoring configured to detect these?

2. **Scenario shift indicators:** As the organization moves from one scenario to another, what are the early indicators of the shift?

3. **Learning protocols:** In each scenario, what should the organization be learning? What monitoring and feedback loops are required?

4. **Adaptive strategy:** As scenarios become clearer, how should strategy adapt? What is the process for detecting scenario clarification and adapting strategy?

5. **False scenario detection:** How will the organization distinguish between a weak signal that is a false positive vs. the beginning of scenario shift?

### Hiddens Framework

1. **Assumption visibility:** Which assumptions about the future are embedded in current strategy? Are scenarios helping surface these?

2. **Possibility space assumptions:** What is assumed to be possible vs. impossible? Are scenarios challenging these boundaries?

3. **Scenario selection bias:** Which scenarios are being avoided or rationalized away? What selection biases exist in which scenarios are constructed?

4. **Comfort bias:** Are scenarios being constructed to validate current strategy rather than to challenge it? How can construction be de-biased?

5. **Hidden discontinuities:** What discontinuities or disruptions is the organization implicitly assuming cannot happen? Are scenarios explicitly including these?

### Time Framework

1. **Temporal framings:** Do scenarios use the same temporal framings as other frameworks? Are horizon choices consistent?

2. **Speed assumptions:** Do scenarios reflect realistic assumptions about how fast change occurs? Is speed of change being used as an excuse to dismiss scenarios?

3. **Lag structures:** Are lags in causal chains being accurately modeled? When do effects appear relative to causes?

4. **Temporal reversals:** Are there moments in scenarios where causation reverses or changes direction? Are these marked explicitly?

5. **Long-term coupling:** How do scenarios at different time horizons (5-year, 10-year, 20-year) couple? Does the 5-year scenario set you up for the 10-year scenario?

### Systems Framework

1. **System boundaries:** Are scenarios assuming the right system boundaries? Or are they missing cross-system effects?

2. **Coupling and emergence:** Do scenarios account for emergence and self-organization? Are causal assumptions too linear?

3. **Feedback loops:** Are reinforcing and damping loops being modeled correctly? Are tipping points being identified?

4. **Scale effects:** How do scenarios at different scales (organization, industry, ecosystem) couple? Are scale effects being ignored?

5. **System resilience:** In each scenario, how resilient are critical systems? What is the margin to system failure?

### Causation Framework

1. **Causal mechanisms:** Are scenario causal chains consistent with causal mechanisms documented in the Causation framework?

2. **Contested causation:** Where causation is contested in the Causation framework, are scenarios exploring alternative causal models?

3. **Causal loops:** Are feedback loops and causal loops being modeled correctly? Are there circular causalities or simultaneous causation?

4. **Causal strength:** Are weak vs. strong causal links being distinguished? Are scenarios overestimating or underestimating causal strength?

5. **Causal reversals:** Are there moments in scenarios where causation reverses (e.g., what was a risk becomes an opportunity)? Are reversals being marked?

### Metrics Framework

1. **Scenario indicators:** For each scenario, what are the leading indicators that the scenario is becoming visible?

2. **Metric shifts:** Do the metrics that matter shift across scenarios? What metrics are you tracking in one scenario but should abandon in another?

3. **Brittleness indicators:** Are there metrics that would signal when strategy is becoming brittle in the current scenario?

4. **Scenario sensitivity:** How sensitive are key metrics to scenario transitions? What metric movements would be expected in each scenario?

5. **Monitoring configuration:** Based on scenarios, how should the metrics/monitoring portfolio be configured? Are all critical scenario signals being monitored?

### Evidence Framework

1. **Scenario signals:** Which current weak signals map to which scenarios? Are early indicators of scenarios being monitored?

2. **Evidence base:** Is the evidence base sufficient to distinguish between scenarios? What evidence would reduce scenario uncertainty?

3. **Signal dismissal:** Are signals that point toward uncomfortable scenarios being dismissed? Are there hidden dismissal mechanisms?

4. **Evidence updates:** As evidence emerges, which scenarios become more vs. less plausible? Is probability weighting of scenarios being updated?

5. **Evidence gaps:** For each scenario, what evidence is missing? What information requests should be prioritized?

### Other Frameworks

For each of the 36 total frameworks in the Analytical Series, ask: (1) Does this framework provide inputs that should shape scenarios? (2) Do scenarios reveal assumptions embedded in this framework? (3) Are there bidirectional dependencies or feedback loops?

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

Scenarios is itself a Hiddens framework—it is designed to surface hidden assumptions about the future embedded in organizational strategy, risk models, and decision-making. This section specifies the hiding mechanisms that this framework is designed to detect, and the protocols by which the framework itself is checked for distortion or bias.

**Primary hiding mechanisms that Scenarios is designed to surface:**

1. **Future-as-continuation bias:** The automatic cognitive tendency to assume the future will resemble the recent past. This bias is so strong that organizations routinely fail to prepare for predictable alternative futures. Scenarios disrupts this bias by forcing explicit construction of multiple futures.

2. **Possibility space assumption invisibility:** Organizations embed invisible assumptions about what is possible, probable, and impossible into strategy. These assumptions often feel like facts or constraints rather than assumptions. Scenarios makes them visible by asking "What would have to be true for this alternative future to unfold?"

3. **Normalcy bias and discontinuity dismissal:** Discontinuous change is routinely dismissed as extreme, unrealistic, or impossible—particularly when it contradicts the dominant narrative about how things work. Scenarios specifically targets discontinuities and makes them structurally visible through discontinuous scenario types.

4. **Single-future overconfidence:** Planning around one expected future makes range of possibilities invisible. The organization becomes brittle to the one assumption on which the future rests. Scenarios uses robustness testing to expose this brittleness.

5. **Scenario selection bias:** Organizations select scenarios that confirm existing strategy or that are flattering to current decision-makers. Uncomfortable or disconfirming scenarios are avoided or rationalized away. Scenarios frameworks that include explicit governance and bias-checking mechanisms help surface this distortion.

6. **Weak signal dismissal:** Weak signals that point toward uncomfortable futures are routinely dismissed because they contradict current worldviews. By constructing alternative futures first, weak signal detection becomes more systematic.

7. **Assumption entrenchment:** Organizations become locked into particular assumptions about the future; these assumptions become institutionalized and hard to challenge. Scenarios frameworks can help surface and challenge entrenchment.

**Protocols for checking Scenarios frameworks for distortion:**

1. **Discomfort check:** Which scenarios is the organization most uncomfortable discussing? These are likely to be the most important scenarios. If all constructed scenarios feel compatible with current strategy, scenario selection bias is probable.

2. **Discontinuity check:** Are scenarios including sufficient discontinuity? Or are they clustering around continuity and incremental change? Discontinuity avoidance is common.

3. **Assumption clarity check:** For each scenario, can the organization articulate what assumptions would have to be true for the scenario to unfold? If assumptions are vague, scenarios are likely not surfacing hidden assumptions.

4. **Robustness clarity check:** Can the organization clearly articulate where strategy fails in each scenario? Or are failures being rationalized away? Rationalization is common.

5. **Evidence integration check:** Are weak signals pointing toward uncomfortable scenarios being dismissed or minimized? Signal dismissal suggests hidden selection bias.

6. **Governance clarity check:** Are governance processes for scenario use transparent? Or are scenarios being used selectively to support pre-existing strategy? Transparency enables detection.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| **I. | **II. ## 8.3 Hiddens Crosswalk (Tier 2 – structured deepening layer)

When Tier 1 indicates potential hiding, shift to Tier 2. Complete the following for focused deepening into specific Hiddens mechanisms:

| Mechanism | Definition | How it operates in Scenarios work | Detection test | Remediation protocol |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Future-as-continuation bias** | Automatic assumption that the future will resemble the recent past | Scenarios cluster near expected future trajectory; discontinuities dismissed as too extreme | Construct scenarios that explicitly violate recent trend; measure resistance and discomfort; track which scenarios feel "unrealistic" | Force construction of at least one discontinuity scenario; require assumption articulation for each scenario |
| **Possibility space entrenchment** | Assumptions about what is possible become institutionalized and hard to challenge | Scenarios avoid certain categories of futures as inherently impossible; stakeholders cannot articulate why certain futures are impossible | Ask stakeholders to explain why specific futures are impossible; track use of "that can't happen" language; examine history of past dismissals | Challenge each "impossible" classification; construct counterfactual explaining what would have to be true for the dismissed future; track how views change over time |
| **Scenario selection bias** | Scenarios are constructed or selected to confirm existing strategy | Comfortable scenarios are emphasized in decision-making; disconfirming scenarios are avoided or rationalized away; governance is weak | Track which scenarios are cited in decisions; monitor language (disconfirming scenarios get defensive, qualified language; confirming scenarios get direct language); analyze documentation | Establish governance requiring that all scenarios are engaged with equally; track scenario usage patterns; escalate selective usage |
| **Weak signal dismissal** | Signals pointing toward uncomfortable scenarios are dismissed as noise or anomalies | Weak signals supporting expected future are collected and tracked; weak signals pointing toward uncomfortable futures are dismissed; search is biased | Systematically track which signals are collected, emphasized, and dismissed; compare treatment of signals supporting vs. contradicting expected future | Establish neutral signal governance; treat all signals equally; escalate disconfirming signals rather than dismissing them; create feedback loops |
| **Normalcy bias** | Discontinuous change is assumed to be impossible or too extreme to warrant preparation | Discontinuity scenarios are rarely constructed; when constructed, they are treated as theoretical exercises rather than serious possibilities; change is assumed to be gradual | Count discontinuity content in scenarios; assess how seriously discontinuity scenarios are treated; track language about "realism" vs. "theoretical" | Force explicit discontinuity scenario construction; require treating at least one discontinuity scenario as plausible; track how beliefs shift over time |
| **Assumption invisibility** | Assumptions are stated as facts and are therefore invisible as assumptions | Organization cannot articulate the assumptions driving current strategy; assumptions are defended as facts or constraints | Ask stakeholders to state assumptions explicitly; track how many can be articulated; measure defensiveness when assumptions are questioned | Create explicit assumptions register; require assumptions to be tested and monitored; escalate contested assumptions |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

When executing Scenarios analysis, use the following prompts to surface Hiddens in real time:

1. **"What would have to be different in the future for the current strategy to fail?"** — This prompt surfaces assumption invisibility by forcing explicit articulation of what the strategy depends on.

2. **"Which of these scenarios makes you most uncomfortable, and why?"** — This prompt surfaces distortion and denial by identifying which scenarios are being avoided emotionally.

3. **"What are you assuming about what is possible vs. impossible in the future?"** — This prompt surfaces possibility space assumptions.

4. **"In which of these futures would you not see it coming?"** — This prompt surfaces weak signal dismissal and early warning gaps.

5. **"What changes would make you believe this scenario is happening?"** — This prompt surfaces signal thresholds and belief-change triggers.

6. **"What are you assuming will NOT happen, and how confident are you about that?"** — This prompt surfaces invisibility of absence.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

**Tier 3 escalation is required when:**

1. Tier 1 scan reveals hiding in more than two categories (II, III, IV, V)
2. Tier 2 deepening confirms significant distortion in scenario selection, signal dismissal, or assumption invisibility
3. Organization is unable to engage seriously with any discontinuous or disconfirming scenarios
4. Governance failures are preventing scenarios from being used in decision-making
5. Evidence of systematic weak signal dismissal pointing to uncomfortable futures

**Tier 3 protocol:**

1. **Governance escalation:** Engage executive sponsors and governance bodies to explicitly address scenario governance, usage rules, and escalation protocols
2. **Investigative deepening:** Conduct targeted investigation into: (1) Which scenarios are being avoided? (2) What would trigger belief change? (3) What signals are being dismissed? (4) Which assumptions are contested?
3. **Assumption stress-test:** Identify the most critical and contested assumptions embedded in current strategy and stress-test them explicitly
4. **Signal governance:** Establish explicit governance for signal collection, treatment, and escalation, with independent oversight
5. **De-bias intervention:** If evidence of systematic distortion, consider whether external facilitation or independent scenario construction is warranted

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

This framework is built on the following principles and should be used in a manner consistent with these principles:

1. **Coherence over likelihood.** Scenarios are not predictions or forecasts. A scenario is internally coherent and plausible from current evidence, but may or may not be the most likely future. Evaluating scenarios on likelihood misses the entire point—scenarios are designed to surface what would happen if something that seems unlikely actually occurred.

2. **Possibility space discovery.** Scenarios are tools for discovering hidden assumptions about what is possible and impossible. The framework is designed to surface assumptions that are invisible precisely because they feel like facts or constraints rather than assumptions. Before dismissing a scenario as unrealistic, ask what assumptions would have to be true for that scenario to be unrealistic.

3. **Robustness over optimization.** Strategy should be tested for robustness across scenarios, not optimized for one expected future. A strategy that works brilliantly in one scenario but fails catastrophically in others is not a good strategy—it is a brittle strategy vulnerable to scenario shifts.

4. **Discomfort as data.** Which scenarios the organization is most uncomfortable discussing are likely to be the most important scenarios. Discomfort is a signal of hidden assumptions, hidden vulnerabilities, or hidden denial. Name the discomfort explicitly and investigate it.

5. **Assumption visibility and testing.** All scenarios should make explicit the assumptions on which they rest. Assumptions should be testable (at least in principle) and should be monitored. Contested assumptions should be escalated to governance and decision-making. Weak assumptions should be treated as threats to strategy robustness.

6. **Governance against selection bias.** Scenarios are vulnerable to selection bias: organizations construct scenarios that confirm existing strategy and avoid scenarios that challenge it. Effective scenario work requires explicit governance, diverse scenario construction teams, cross-functional challenge, and mechanisms to prevent comfortable scenarios from crowding out uncomfortable ones.

7. **Integration with evidence and learning.** Scenarios are not academic exercises. Scenarios should drive creation of early warning indicators, should shape monitoring and learning loops, and should be updated as evidence clarifies which scenarios are becoming visible. Scenario-signal mapping is essential.

8. **Explicit discontinuities.** The framework includes explicit scenario types for discontinuity and surprise. Discontinuous change is real and historically frequent. Organizations should not assume continuity by default; continuity should be a scenario choice, not an unstated assumption.

9. **Time and reversibility matter.** Scenarios should specify timelines and should assess reversibility of key transitions. Some decisions or transitions are reversible; others are locked-in or point-of-no-return. Strategy should be designed with reversibility and options-preservation in mind.

10. **Scenarios as Hiddens tools.** Scenarios are fundamentally tools for surfacing hidden assumptions. This framework includes explicit Hiddens integration (§8.0) to ensure that scenario work is not itself distorted by hidden biases and selection mechanisms. Tier 1/2/3 Hiddens scanning should be applied to scenarios analysis itself.
