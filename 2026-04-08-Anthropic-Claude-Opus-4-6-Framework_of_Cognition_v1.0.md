## At-a-Glance: Core Premise Summary

Cognition analyses how mental processes — mental models, heuristics, cognitive biases, attention allocation, and bounded rationality — shape what agents perceive, what they miss, and what conclusions they reach. Its distinctive contribution is addressing hiddens hidden from actors themselves due to how their minds work: an actor's mental model of a system determines what anomalies are even visible to them; the expert cannot see what they take for granted; the novice cannot see what they lack knowledge to notice. Cognition works with Beliefs (which covers belief content and formation), Emotions (which covers affective drivers), and Knowledge (which covers what is known and distributed). Cognition covers the information-processing and reasoning processes that determine what agents can even perceive as possible or relevant.

# Introduction

## What is Cognition?

Cognition is the framework for analysing mental processes through which agents perceive, attend to, and reason. It examines mental models (the internal models agents use to understand systems), cognitive biases (systematic deviations from normative reasoning), heuristics (mental shortcuts that are efficient but error-prone), attention allocation (what agents notice and what remains in the periphery), and bounded rationality (the constraints on how much information agents can process). The framework does not ask "what do agents believe?" (that is Beliefs) or "what do agents feel?" (that is Emotions) or "what do agents know?" (that is Knowledge). It asks: "How do the mechanisms of perception and reasoning determine what agents can see as real, possible, or relevant?" Its primary analytical contribution is exposing hiddens that arise because of how minds work—not because information is missing, but because mental processes make certain realities functionally invisible to actors themselves.

## Why does Cognition matter for Hiddens work?

Three core mechanisms create perceptual and reasoning blindness that no amount of information provision can solve:

**Confirmation Bias and Selective Attention.** An actor's current mental model determines what counts as an anomaly. If the mental model does not include space for a particular type of event, that event is perceptually invisible even when it occurs repeatedly. A manager whose mental model assumes that "frontline staff always resist change" will see defensive pushback as confirmation of resistance rather than as valid concern about implementation; the staff's substantive feedback remains invisible because it does not fit the model. Confirmation bias ensures that evidence confirming the model is attended to and remembered; disconfirming evidence is filtered out. The result is hiddens generated by the actor's own selective attention, locked in place by the mental model.

**Expert Blind Spots and Novice Blindness.** Experts cannot see what they have automated. A radiologist expert who has instantly pattern-matched thousands of X-rays develops fast, accurate perception for anomalies—but cannot see obvious confounders that novices might notice because the expert's perception has been optimised for speed and pattern recognition at the expense of deliberate, step-by-step logic. Conversely, novices cannot see what they lack knowledge to perceive: a junior programmer cannot see the architectural implications of a coding decision because the required conceptual framework is absent. Both are locked into blindness by their level of expertise. The hiddens here arise not from deception or suppression but from the structure of human cognition itself.

**Mental Model Lock-In.** Once a mental model is formed and successfully guides action, it becomes resistant to revision. The model structures how new information is interpreted; information that fits the model is integrated, information that contradicts it is reinterpreted or rejected. A system operator's model of "normal" operations becomes so embedded that genuine anomalies go undetected until catastrophic failure. The mental model is invisible to the agent as a model—it is experienced as "how things are," making it impossible for the agent to see alternatives or to notice that reality has drifted from the model.

Cognition's distinctive hiddens contribution is recognizing that these are not failures of information, willpower, or moral intent. They are features of how human minds work. They can be mitigated but not eliminated. They require intervention designs that work with cognitive structure, not against it.

## What does this framework produce?

The Framework of Cognition produces:
- A **cognitive type inventory**: Thirty core types of mental processes organized across six meta-categories (mental models, cognitive biases, heuristics, attention allocation, bounded rationality, and reasoning processes)
- A **dimensional profile** of any cognitive process (scope, systematicity, reversibility, contextuality, and automaticity)
- A **dynamics map** of how cognitive processes shift, adapt, entrench, and cascade over time
- A **Hiddens Register entry** for each material cognitive process, including: the mechanism, what becomes invisible as a result, detection interfaces, and remediation interfaces
- A **cognitive risk map** showing which cognitive processes are most likely to produce material hiddens in this situation
- Integration interfaces with frameworks covering upstream inputs (mental models depend on Experience, Knowledge, Beliefs) and downstream outputs (cognitive blindness affects Diagnosis, Decisions, and Interventions)

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework when cognitive processes are diagnosis-critical: when perception gaps are suspected (actors do not see what is happening), when attention allocation is selective (some risks are peripheral while others are overattended), when mental models may be misaligned with current reality, when expert blindness is plausible, when heuristic-driven errors are shaping judgment, or when reasoning processes are constrained by bounded rationality. Invoke it at middle stages (after Situations and Evidence are defined) because cognition requires understanding what information is available and what agents' current mental models are. The framework operates at Light Depth by default (identify top 3–5 cognitive processes and map their hiddens); escalate to Full Depth when high-consequence perception gaps are suspected, when multiple cognitive biases interact, when expert blind spots are affecting safety-critical decisions, or when mental model lock-in is preventing adaptation. # 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Cognition is the framework for understanding how mental processes shape what agents perceive, attend to, and conclude. It rests on four foundational observations.

First, **perception is not passive reception of reality; it is active construction by the agent's mental models and existing frameworks**. An actor's mental model of a system determines what counts as normal, what is an anomaly, and what is invisible because it does not fit the model's categories. A supply chain manager whose mental model includes "suppliers are opportunistic" will see price increases as confirmation of opportunism rather than as responses to real cost pressures; the supplier's explanations remain perceptually unavailable because they do not fit the framework. Mental model lock-in produces **model-based blindness**: anomalies outside the model are not merely hard to see, they are cognitively unavailable.

Second, **attention is allocated selectively according to what agents consider relevant, salient, or threatening**. Limited attention is a biological fact: human working memory holds 5–9 items at once; skilled performance requires automation (delegating tasks to unconscious processing). This efficiency gain—automation—comes at a cost: what is automated is invisible. The experienced pilot who flies on autopilot can miss novel failures because the normal pattern-matching that guides safe flying has been automated away. The manager who uses a rapid heuristic ("is this person similar to people I trust?") to judge credibility cannot see that the heuristic is substituting for evidence evaluation. What is attended to is foregrounded, shaped by salience and emotion; what is not attended to is peripheral or invisible. **Attention blindness** is not negligence—it is a necessary consequence of efficient cognition.

Third, **reasoning under bounded rationality systematically deviates from normative rationality in predictable ways**. Agents cannot evaluate all options, cannot compute perfect probabilities, cannot hold unlimited information in working memory. They use heuristics—mental shortcuts—that are fast and usually effective but error-prone. Availability heuristic (judging frequency by how easily examples come to mind) works well in familiar domains and breaks down when recent or emotionally salient events distort perception. Representativeness heuristic (judging probability by similarity to a category) works for rough categorization and produces base-rate neglect and conjunction fallacies when agents ignore statistical structure. These are not mistakes caused by laziness or low ability; they are systematic features of how human reasoning works. They affect all agents, including experts. They produce **heuristic-driven hiddens**: conclusions that seem rational given agents' bounded reasoning processes but are systematically misaligned with truth.

Fourth, **experts and novices are each blind in complementary ways**. Experts have automated perceptual and reasoning processes that produce fast, usually accurate judgments—but automatization creates blindness to what the expert takes for granted. An experienced engineer cannot see the problem-design interactions that a novice might notice because the engineer's perception has been optimised for speed and integration with domain knowledge. The novice sees details the expert misses—but cannot see implications the expert understands implicitly. Neither blindness can be overcome by information provision alone; they require different interventions: for experts, explicit articulation of assumptions and periodic perspective-taking with outsiders; for novices, guided learning and domain knowledge acquisition. **Complementary blindness** is a fact of expertise structure, not a flaw in individual experts.

The Framework of Cognition does not replace Beliefs or Knowledge or Emotions. It works alongside them: Beliefs covers what is held as true (content); Cognition covers how that content is formed and processed. Knowledge covers what is known and distributed; Cognition covers how knowledge is used in reasoning under constraints. Emotions covers affective states; Cognition covers how affect shapes attention and judgment. The boundary is maintained throughout.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Core definitions:**
- **Cognition (operational):** the set of mental processes—perception, attention, memory, reasoning, and judgment—through which agents construct their understanding of situations and make decisions; includes mental models, heuristics, biases, and bounded rationality constraints.
- **Mental Model:** an agent's internal representation of how a system, process, or domain works; determines what is recognized as normal, what is an anomaly, and what is invisible because it does not fit the model.
- **Cognitive Bias:** a systematic deviation from normative reasoning or perception that arises from how the mind processes information; includes confirmation bias, availability bias, anchoring, representativeness, and dozens of others.
- **Heuristic:** a mental shortcut or rule of thumb used to simplify reasoning under uncertainty; efficient but error-prone; examples include availability (judge frequency by ease of recall), representativeness (judge probability by similarity to category), and anchoring (adjust insufficiently from first number seen).
- **Attention Allocation:** the selective direction of cognitive resources toward some information and away from other information; shaped by salience, prior beliefs, emotion, and automatization; limited by working memory capacity.
- **Bounded Rationality:** the condition that agents cannot evaluate infinite options, cannot process unlimited information, and cannot compute perfect probabilities; agents make good-enough decisions using available cognitive resources, not optimal decisions.
- **Expert Blind Spot:** what experts cannot see because they have automated perception and reasoning; produces fast, usually accurate judgments but creates invisibility to what the expert takes for granted.
- **Novice Blindness:** what novices cannot see because they lack domain knowledge or conceptual frameworks required for perception; produces cautious, deliberate reasoning but creates invisibility to implications and patterns the expert perceives implicitly.
- **Mental Model Lock-In:** resistance to revising a mental model even when evidence contradicts it; caused by confirmation bias, identity protection, and the model's success in past contexts; produces continuing blindness despite reality change.
- **Model-Based Blindness:** events or anomalies that do not fit the agent's mental model and therefore remain perceptually unavailable; the agent cannot see what the model does not include.

**In scope:**
- Six meta-categories and thirty core cognitive types (mental model types, bias types, heuristic types, attention types, reasoning types, and reasoning constraint types)
- Five dimensions for profiling any cognitive process (scope, systematicity, reversibility, contextuality, and automaticity)
- Ten cognitive dynamics (patterns of entrenchment, revision, cascade, expert-novice complementarity, heuristic substitution, etc.)
- Three interface types (perception interface, reasoning interface, and intervention interface)
- Hiddens sources and tiered Hiddens cross-check
- Application protocol and standard deliverables
- Explicit integration with Evidence, Beliefs, Knowledge, Diagnosis, Interventions, and Hiddens frameworks

**Out of scope (by design):**
- Neuroscience and detailed cognitive psychology (use as inputs where available; do not treat as required)
- Judgment and decision-making optimization (that is Decisions framework)
- Complete catalogue of cognitive biases (focus on decision-critical and hiddens-relevant biases; reference cognitive science literature for comprehensive coverage)
- Interventions beyond mapping (use Interventions and Learning & Adaptation frameworks for designing change)
- Individual cognitive development or learning (use Competencies and Learning & Adaptation frameworks)

**Common confusions:**
- Cognition ≠ Beliefs (Cognition covers processes; Beliefs covers content—what is held as true)
- Cognition ≠ Knowledge (Cognition covers reasoning with what is known; Knowledge covers what is known and distributed)
- Cognition ≠ Emotions (Cognition covers information processing; Emotions covers affective states—though they interact)
- Cognitive bias ≠ irrationality (biases are systematic, predictable features of reasoning under constraints, not failures of rational capacity)
- Mental model ≠ belief (a belief is a specific propositional attitude; a mental model is a framework for understanding a system or domain)
- Expert blind spot ≠ incompetence (expertise creates both capability and blindness; both are real)
- Attention blindness ≠ negligence (attention is limited; selectivity is necessary, not a flaw)

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Mental Models

**Definition:** An agent's internal representation of how a system, process, or domain works; determines what is recognized as normal, what is an anomaly, and what is invisible because it does not fit the model's categories.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Agent makes confident predictions about system behaviour based on unstated logic
- Agent recognizes anomalies that fit the model's category scheme and misses anomalies that do not fit
- Agent explains outcomes by reference to causal structures the model specifies
- Agent shows surprise when events contradict the model; shows confirmation of existing beliefs when events fit the model
- When interviewed about "how the system works," agent articulates a coherent (but potentially incorrect) model

**Typical failure mode:**
- Model-based blindness: events, risks, or changes that the model does not include space for remain invisible until catastrophic failure forces model revision
- Model lock-in: agent continues to reason from a model even after reality has changed; model's success in the past makes revision resistance strong
- Automation blindness: agent's perception has been optimised for the model; what the model takes for granted is invisible to the agent

### II. Cognitive Biases

**Definition:** Systematic deviations from normative reasoning or perception that arise from how the mind processes information; predictable, recurring, and not caused by lack of ability or information.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Agent exhibits the same error pattern across multiple situations and over time
- Error pattern is predictable from knowledge of the bias mechanism (not random)
- Error persists despite agent being intelligent and well-intentioned
- Error produces consistent directional bias (not just noise)
- Providing additional information does not correct the error if confirmation bias or motivated reasoning is driving it

**Typical failure mode:**
- Confirmation bias: agent selectively attends to confirming evidence and filters out disconfirming evidence; perception becomes increasingly distorted from reality
- Availability bias: recent or emotionally salient events dominate judgment; less available but relevant information is underweighted
- Motivated reasoning: agent shifts evidential standards to defend preferred conclusions; rational discourse becomes impossible because standards move with preferences

### III. Heuristics and Substitution

**Definition:** Mental shortcuts that agents use to simplify reasoning under uncertainty; they are efficient but error-prone; they include biases in how agents estimate probabilities, judge similarity, and substitute one judgment for another.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Agent uses a visible rule of thumb ("if it looks like X, it probably is X")
- Agent makes quick judgments that would require extensive calculation if done normatively
- Agent's errors are predictable from the heuristic mechanism (e.g., base-rate neglect from representativeness heuristic)
- Heuristic works well in familiar, structured domains and breaks down in unfamiliar or novel domains
- Agent is unaware of using a heuristic and experiences judgment as direct perception

**Typical failure mode:**
- Heuristic substitution: agent uses one heuristic (e.g., similarity to category) for a different judgment (e.g., probability) and produces systematic error
- Base-rate neglect: agent uses representativeness heuristic and ignores base rates; probability estimates are wrong
- Anchoring: agent's judgment is pulled toward an initial number even when that number is arbitrary or irrelevant

### IV. Attention Allocation

**Definition:** The selective direction of cognitive resources toward some information and away from other information; shaped by salience, prior beliefs, emotion, automatization, and working memory limits.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Agent notices some risks or opportunities and misses others that are objectively equally important
- Attention shifts when emotion is triggered or when salience increases
- Agent's attention is tunnel-focused under stress (perceptual narrowing)
- What is attended to is foregrounded, vivid, memorable; what is not attended to is peripheral or forgotten
- Agent can easily recall information they attended to; cannot recall information that was not attended to even if it was present

**Typical failure mode:**
- Attentional blindness: agent does not see events occurring in the periphery of attention even when looking directly at them
- Inattentional deafness: agent does not hear information that is not expected or salient
- Automation blindness: agent's attention has been automated away from the task; novel failures in automated processes go undetected
- Tunnel vision: agent focuses narrowly on threat or goal; peripheral information is invisible

### V. Bounded Rationality

**Definition:** The condition that agents cannot evaluate infinite options, cannot process unlimited information, and cannot compute perfect probabilities; agents make good-enough (satisficing) decisions using available cognitive resources.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Agent makes decisions quickly despite incomplete information (cannot afford to wait for complete information)
- Agent uses satisficing rule: "find an option good enough for the goal" rather than searching for optimal option
- Agent's reasoning depends on what information is available; availability of alternatives shapes choice
- Agent's decision improves with more time/information but decisions are made before information is complete
- Agent acknowledges constraints ("we had to decide with the information we had")

**Typical failure mode:**
- Satisficing traps: agent settles for good-enough option early and misses better options that would be found with longer search
- Framing effects: how options are presented affects choice even though the decision problem is the same
- Status quo bias: agent defaults to current option because changing requires effort; inertia locks in suboptimal choices
- Information overload: agent shuts down deliberation when information exceeds processing capacity

### VI. Reasoning Processes

**Definition:** The structures through which agents reason (deductive, inductive, abductive, analogical, intuitive); includes both valid reasoning and reasoning that breaks down through invalid premises, false logic, or hidden assumptions.

**Diagnostic cues:**
- Agent articulates a reasoning chain (if asked to explain)
- Agent's logic appears sound but depends on hidden or false premises
- Agent's reasoning is valid in form but unsound because premises are wrong
- Agent's intuitive judgment contradicts deliberate reasoning; agent trusts intuition over logic
- Agent's reasoning is correct for past contexts but invalid for changed contexts

**Typical failure mode:**
- False premises: agent reasons correctly from wrong premises; invalid conclusion is hard to correct without addressing premises
- Hidden premises: agent's reasoning depends on unstated assumptions that might not hold
- Analogical reasoning error: agent maps analogy from past case and misses ways the current case differs
- Intuitive override: agent's intuition overrides logic; agent cannot articulate why intuition should be trusted but acts on it

# 3. Core Types Taxonomy

## 3.0 Canonical Rule & Deviations

- Canonical baseline: 6 meta-categories × 5 types = 30 core types.
- This framework: 30 types. No deviations.
- Mapping back to baseline: Direct 1:1 mapping; all 30 types are deployed.

## 3.1 Types (Category I: Mental Models)

| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Causal Model | Agent's internal representation of what causes what; determines what interventions agent believes will produce desired effects | Agent articulates causal links ("X causes Y"); agent predicts outcome of intervention based on model; agent is surprised when intervention produces unexpected result | Misspecified causal model; agent intervenes with confidence based on wrong causal theory; false confidence in intervention effectiveness; reverse causality or confounding embedded in model |
| 2 | System Structure Model | Agent's representation of how system components relate, interact, and constrain each other; includes architecture, feedback loops, and constraints | Agent maps system components and their relationships; agent predicts cascade effects from single intervention; agent understands why system resists certain changes | Model misses nonlinear effects, feedback loops, or delayed consequences; agent intervenes at wrong leverage point; agent is surprised by cascade or emergent effects |
| 3 | Normalcy Model | Agent's representation of what counts as normal operation, routine variation, and expected ranges; determines what is recognized as an anomaly | Agent distinguishes normal variation from signal; agent has confidence bounds for normal operation; agent shows surprise when values fall outside normal range | Normalcy model is outdated; agent fails to recognize genuine anomalies because they fall within normalcy bounds; agent treats normal variation as anomaly (false alarm) |
| 4 | Expert Intuitive Model | Agent's rapidly-accessed, tacit model developed through extensive experience; enables fast pattern recognition and decision-making without explicit reasoning | Agent makes fast, accurate judgments in domain; agent claims "I just know this"; agent's judgment is accurate but agent cannot articulate reasoning | Automation has rendered reasoning invisible; expert cannot articulate expertise; expert perception may be optimised for past contexts and fail in novel situations; expert cannot see what they take for granted |
| 5 | Paradigm/Framework Model | Agent's broadest background assumptions about what kinds of things exist, what counts as evidence, what questions are askable; filters interpretation of all evidence and observation | Agent treats paradigm as "how things are" rather than as a choice; agent cannot conceive of questions outside paradigm; certain hypotheses are rejected a priori as unthinkable | Paradigm boundary is invisible to inhabitant; paradigm excludes consideration of critical realities; agent cannot revise paradigm without major cognitive event; paradigm lock-in is deep and slow to change |

## 3.2 Types (Category II: Cognitive Biases)

| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 6 | Confirmation Bias | Agent selectively attends to, seeks, and remembers evidence that confirms existing beliefs; disconfirming evidence is filtered out or reinterpreted | Agent seeks confirming evidence; agent remembers cases that confirm and forgets disconfirming cases; agent interprets ambiguous evidence as confirming | Perception becomes increasingly distorted from reality; agent becomes overconfident in beliefs because only supporting evidence is attended to; disconfirming evidence accumulates but is unseen |
| 7 | Availability Bias | Agent judges frequency or probability by how easily examples come to mind; recent or emotionally salient events distort perception of likelihood | Agent's probability estimates are biased by recent events; emotionally vivid cases dominate judgment; agent overestimates frequency of memorable events | Rare but memorable events are overestimated; common but boring events are underestimated; risk perception is distorted by salience; decisions are biased toward recent experience |
| 8 | Anchoring Bias | Agent's judgment is disproportionately influenced by an initial number (anchor) even when the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant; agent adjusts insufficiently from anchor | Agent's estimate is pulled toward initial number even when agent knows the number is irrelevant; agent reports initial number influenced their thinking | Anchoring distorts estimates in negotiations, audits, and forecasts; arbitrary anchors shape consequential judgments; agent does not adjust far enough away from anchors |
| 9 | Hindsight Bias | Agent, observing an outcome, believes that outcome was more predictable than it actually was; agent thinks "I should have known"; agent rewrites history to make outcome seem inevitable | Agent claims outcome was obvious in hindsight; agent blames actors for not predicting outcome; agent's retelling changes to fit outcome; agent cannot remember their pre-event predictions | Outcomes are misjudged as more predictable than they were; actors are unfairly blamed for not predicting the unpredictable; agent learns wrong lessons from failures because outcome seems inevitable in hindsight |
| 10 | Motivated Reasoning | Agent shifts evidential standards based on preferred conclusions; agent demands more proof for disliked conclusions than liked ones; rational discourse becomes difficult | Agent's argument is internally consistent but standards change depending on whether argument supports or challenges preferred view; agent notices flaws in opposing argument but not in own argument | Evidence-based dialogue is impossible because standards move with preferences; agent appears to "see" different facts from same evidence; agent is not persuadable by evidence when conclusion is threatened |

## 3.3 Types (Category III: Heuristics and Substitution)

| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | Representativeness Heuristic | Agent judges probability or likelihood by similarity to a category; ignores base rates and statistical structure; substitutes category similarity for probability calculation | Agent judges "is this person like a banker?" rather than "what is the probability this person is a banker?"; agent's estimate ignores base rates; agent shows base-rate neglect | Agent systematically misjudges probabilities when base rates differ from category appearance; agent makes conjunction fallacy errors (judges specific case as more probable than general case) |
| 12 | Availability Heuristic | Agent judges frequency or probability by how easily examples come to mind (differs from availability bias by mechanism; this is rule-of-thumb, bias is selective attention) | Agent estimates frequency by ability to recall examples; agent uses ease-of-recall as proxy for frequency; agent's estimates track ease-of-recall not actual frequency | Frequency estimates are biased by how memorable examples are; agent overestimates frequency of things that are easy to recall; memorable but infrequent events are overweighted |
| 13 | Anchoring Heuristic | Agent uses an initial value as starting point and adjusts from it; adjusts insufficiently; uses any available number as anchor even if irrelevant | Agent reports adjustment from initial number; agent acknowledges initial number influenced judgment; agent claims to have adjusted but adjustment is small | Judgments are systematically biased toward anchor; arbitrary initial numbers distort final estimates; agent does not make sufficient adjustment |
| 14 | Substitution Heuristic | Agent substitutes an easier question for a harder question without noticing the substitution; answers easy question and reports it as answer to hard question | Agent appears to be answering the question asked but is actually answering different question; substitution is unconscious; agent does not notice the substitution | Agent answers wrong question confidently; agent does not realise the error; wrong answer shapes decisions based on ostensibly relevant analysis |
| 15 | Recognition Heuristic | Agent judges that items that are recognised are more likely/probable/valuable than items that are not recognised; uses recognition as proxy for importance | Agent judges recognised item as more likely; agent's judgments track recognition not actual probability; agent prefers recognised options | Judgments are biased toward familiar options; unfamiliar but superior options are underestimated; recognition becomes a proxy for quality or probability |

## 3.4 Types (Category IV: Attention Allocation)

| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 16 | Selective Attention Filter | Agent filters incoming information based on prior beliefs and current goals; attends to information consistent with expectations, misses information that is unexpected | Agent notices information matching expectations; agent misses information outside expectations even when present; agent is surprised by unexpected information; filtering is unconscious | Unexpected anomalies go unnoticed because they do not match expectation filter; agent continues in previous mode because new information does not trigger attention shift; reality change is invisible |
| 17 | Salience-Driven Attention | Agent's attention is captured and held by vivid, recent, emotionally arousing, or personally relevant information; mundane but important information remains peripheral | Agent's attention shifts to emotionally arousing event; agent remembers salient information better; agent weights salient information more in judgments | Attention is captured by salient but less important information; less salient but critical information is overlooked; emotional salience distorts priority |
| 18 | Working Memory Limit | Agent can hold approximately 5–9 items in working memory; exceeding this capacity produces errors, forgotten information, or shutdown; efficiency requires automation | Agent makes errors when juggling too many items; agent forgets information when capacity exceeded; agent trades off different items as new information arrives | Information overload causes decision quality to degrade; information beyond capacity is forgotten; agent experiences cognitive load and may shutdown deliberation; critical information is lost |
| 19 | Automatization Blindness | Agent's perception and decision-making have been automated through repetition; automation is efficient (fast, parallel, low effort) but renders reasoning invisible and creates blindness to what automation assumes | Agent makes fast, accurate decisions in routine situations; agent cannot articulate how they decide; agent's decisions are guided by implicit pattern recognition; agent is unaware of what assumptions guide the automated process | Automation creates blindness to novel situations that violate automation's assumptions; automated process fails in edge cases; agent cannot explain or revise the automated logic |
| 20 | Attentional Tunneling | Agent focuses narrowly on primary threat or goal; attention narrows to exclude peripheral information; information outside primary focus is invisible even if it is important | Agent focuses narrowly on primary task; agent misses peripheral information even when important; agent reports inability to attend to peripheral information while focused; attentional narrowing is extreme under stress | Tunnel vision produces critical information loss; agent is unaware of important context because attention is locked on primary focus; attentional tunnel narrows further under stress (stress makes blindness worse) |

## 3.5 Types (Category V: Bounded Rationality)

| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 21 | Satisficing Decision Rule | Agent searches for options that meet minimum criteria (satisfice) rather than optimising; stops search when acceptable option is found; does not search through all options | Agent finds option that is "good enough" and stops searching; agent does not continue search for better options; agent acknowledges trade-off between search cost and decision quality | Agent may settle for suboptimal option early; agent misses superior options that would be found with continued search; early good option locks in subsequent choices |
| 22 | Information Incompleteness | Agent makes decisions despite incomplete information; cannot wait for information to be complete; bases decision on available subset; decision quality depends on what information happened to be available | Agent decides "with the information we have"; agent acknowledges missing information; agent's decision would change if missing information were available; decision quality varies based on what information was available | Decision is based on incomplete information; decision would likely be different if missing information were incorporated; agent does not know what is missing; decisions are vulnerable to unknown unknowns |
| 23 | Option Overload and Reduction | Agent cannot evaluate infinite options; uses heuristics to reduce option set (elimination, satisficing, defaulting); what options remain in consideration depends on reduction method | Agent uses rule to eliminate options ("if less than X, eliminate"; "if already tried, skip"); agent acknowledges not evaluating all options; agent's choice depends on what survived reduction | Optimal option is eliminated in reduction phase; agent never considers it; option space is narrowed in systematic ways that can miss superior options |
| 24 | Framing Effect | How options are presented (frame) affects choice even though the decision problem is the same; agent is loss-averse in gain frame, risk-seeking in loss frame; frame shapes choice non-rationally | Agent's choice differs depending on how problem is presented; agent chooses differently when problem is framed as gains vs losses; agent does not recognise frames as arbitrary | Choice is affected by how problem is presented, not just by objective structure of decision; arbitrarily different frames produce different choices; agent cannot evaluate decision problem independent of frame |
| 25 | Status Quo Bias and Default Effect | Agent defaults to current option because changing it requires effort; inertia locks in current state even when better alternatives are available; default option is chosen simply because it is default | Agent stays with current option despite acknowledging better alternatives; agent claims "I'd have to change everything" so defaults to status quo; agent defaults without explicit evaluation | Current state persists despite being suboptimal; changes are not made because inertia is strong; defaults lock in options without comparison |

## 3.6 Types (Category VI: Reasoning Processes)

| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 26 | Hidden Premise Reasoning | Agent's reasoning is logically valid but depends on unstated premises that may not hold; premises are invisible to agent and to audience | Agent's reasoning appears sound until one examines hidden premises; when premises are made explicit, their questionability becomes apparent; premises are treated as self-evident | Reasoning is correct in form but unsound in substance; premises are false but invisible; reasoning cannot be corrected without surfacing premises |
| 27 | Analogical Reasoning | Agent reasons from past precedent by mapping analogy to new situation; reasoning is valid if analogy holds, fails if analogy breaks down; agent may miss ways current case differs | Agent invokes precedent ("it's like when…"); agent maps structure of precedent onto new situation; agent's confidence is high if analogy seems to fit | Analogy maps incorrectly; current case differs in ways that matter; agent misses disanalogies and reasons from false precedent; overconfidence in analogical reasoning |
| 28 | Deductive Reasoning Validity | Agent reasons by formal logic; reasoning is valid (conclusion follows from premises) or invalid (does not follow); validity is independent of truth of premises | Agent articulates logical chain; agent's reasoning follows or does not follow valid form; invalid reasoning may be subtle (affirming consequent, denying antecedent) | Reasoning appears to follow from premises but violates valid form; logic errors are subtle and easy to miss; conclusions seem sound but reasoning is invalid |
| 29 | Inductive Reasoning Weakness | Agent generalises from cases to universal claims; strength depends on sample size, representativeness, and whether exception is possible; overgeneralisation is common | Agent generalises from examples ("this is always true because I've seen it happen"); agent's generalisation goes beyond what evidence supports; agent's confidence is higher than sample strength warrants | Agent overgeneralises from limited experience; agent treats cases as universal when exceptions exist; agent is overconfident in generalisations |
| 30 | Intuitive Judgment Dominance | Agent's quick, holistic judgment overrides deliberate reasoning; intuition is based on implicit pattern recognition (expertise) but can be wrong in novel domains; agent trusts intuition more than logic | Agent reports "gut feeling" or "intuition"; agent chooses based on intuition despite logic pointing elsewhere; agent trusts intuition and acts on it | Intuition is wrong in domains where agent lacks expertise; agent overconfidently trusts intuition; intuitive judgment overrides evidence-based reasoning |

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)

| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | 30 core types derived from cognitive science literature on mental models, cognitive biases, heuristics, attention, bounded rationality, and reasoning. Types are organised by mechanism, not exhaustively (cognitive science identifies 100+ biases; framework focuses on decision-critical and hiddens-relevant types). |
| Domain-specific refinement | Extend within domains by adding types specific to domain reasoning (e.g., medical decision-making may require "diagnostic anchoring" or "premature closure" types). Maintain mappability back to the six meta-categories. Document additions and rationale in version history. |
| Preserving mappability | If adding types, ensure new type maps back to one of the six meta-categories. If creating new meta-category, document and provide mapping back to canonical baseline. Do not create types that cannot be mapped back to core cognitive science. |
| Structural invariants | Maintain the invariant that types within a meta-category are distinguishable and non-overlapping. Avoid duplication across categories. Keep descriptions concise and actionable. |
| Periodic reassessment | Reassess taxonomy annually against emerging cognitive science literature. If new high-consequence cognitive mechanisms are identified, integrate into next major version. Flag changes in version history. |
| Versioning & governance | v1.0 baseline is fixed. v1.1 and later may add types or refine descriptions without changing core structure. Major version (v2.0) required if meta-categories are restructured or baseline types are removed. All changes must be reversible (no destructive modifications). |

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Cognitive Process

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)

| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Scope | How wide is the range of situations in which the cognitive process operates? Narrow-domain vs broad-domain applicability | Narrow (domain-specific; operates only in specialised contexts) ↔ Broad (domain-general; operates across contexts); typical: 1–5 scales | Observe whether bias/heuristic/blindness appears across multiple situations or only in specific domain; ask agent whether pattern occurs in other contexts | Narrow-scope biases are easier to mitigate through domain-specific interventions; broad-scope biases require deeper cognitive work |
| 2. Systematicity | How predictable and consistent is the cognitive process? Produces same error/blindness repeatedly or varies | Random/idiosyncratic ↔ Highly systematic and recurring (produces same error across cases and agents); typical: 1–5 scales | Look for pattern repeatability; does agent make same error across cases or is error variable; do multiple agents exhibit same bias | Systematic biases are actionable; idiosyncratic cognition is harder to address; systematicity makes bias predictable |
| 3. Reversibility | How easily can the cognitive process be overridden once awareness exists? Can agent correct through effort or is blindness/bias persistent? | Highly reversible (agent can override with awareness and effort) ↔ Persistent (awareness does not enable override; automatic bias persists); typical: 1–5 scales | Test whether providing information about the bias enables agent to correct it; test whether slowing down deliberation enables better reasoning | Reversible biases can be mitigated through awareness and decision aids; persistent biases require structural/environmental interventions |
| 4. Contextuality | How much does the cognitive process depend on context (stress, time pressure, emotion, framing)? Context-invariant or context-dependent | Context-invariant (bias operates regardless of context) ↔ Context-dependent (bias is strong in some contexts, weak in others); typical: 1–5 scales | Observe whether bias strength varies with time pressure, stress, emotion, or framing; test whether removing context-drivers reduces bias | Context-dependent biases can be mitigated by managing context; context-invariant biases are harder to eliminate through context change alone |
| 5. Automaticity | How automatic is the cognitive process? Is it deliberate and effortful or automatic and implicit? | Deliberate/effortful (agent can articulate reasoning) ↔ Automatic/implicit (agent is unaware of reasoning; appears intuitive); typical: 1–5 scales | Observe whether agent can articulate reasoning or claims judgment is automatic/intuitive; test whether deliberation changes outcome | Automatic processes are harder to override; they require environment/structure change rather than awareness alone; deliberate processes can be slowed and checked |

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)

| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Mental Model Entrenchment | Model becomes increasingly resistant to revision despite contradicting evidence; agent interprets new evidence as confirming model | Confirmation bias applied to model; model's past success makes agent confident; identity fusion with model; sunk costs (agent invested in model) | Entrenchment can prevent adaptation to changed reality; agent continues reasoning from outdated model; model lock-in produces persistent blindness | Explicit model articulation; periodic model testing against reality; perspective-taking with alternative models; identity-safe revision contexts |
| 2 | Bias Reinforcement Cycle | Bias produces action; action produces evidence seemingly confirming bias; evidence strengthens bias | Self-fulfilling prophecy; confirmation bias applied to self; action based on biased belief produces evidence confirming bias | Cycles entrench biases through action-consequence feedback; agent sees "evidence" confirming bias and becomes more confident | Test whether action alone produces evidence or whether agent interprets evidence selectively; break cycle by changing action, not just belief |
| 3 | Attention Capture Escalation | Salient or emotionally charged event captures attention; attention narrows; peripheral information is lost; narrow attention makes problem worse; escalation continues | Attentional tunneling + stress-induced narrowing + loss of situational awareness + poor decisions due to incomplete information produce cascading problems | Escalation can produce crisis from manageable problem; tunnel vision during stress prevents corrective action; attention narrowing accelerates failure | Widen attention deliberately; periodic "step back" checks; assign monitoring role to manage peripheral awareness; pre-crisis attention protocols |
| 4 | Heuristic Substitution Chains | Agent uses heuristic for one judgment; heuristic error produces misestimate; downstream judgments compound the error; chain of errors from single heuristic substitution | Representativeness heuristic error produces wrong probability estimate; probability error drives resource allocation; resource allocation produces outcome confirming initial wrong estimate | Single heuristic error can cascade; each downstream step seems reasonable but compounds the original error; outcome seems to confirm reasoning | Identify initial heuristic and error; test where in chain the error is recoverable; break chain early |
| 5 | Expert Blind Spot Persistence | Expert automated reasoning prevents expert from seeing what expert takes for granted; expert resists revision because reasoning is implicit; expert becomes increasingly confident over time | Automation → invisibility → confidence without deliberation → resistance to feedback; expertise reinforces automation further | Expert confidently makes errors in edge cases or changed contexts; expert cannot learn from feedback because reasoning is invisible | Pair expert with novice; require expert to articulate reasoning; periodic outside review; expert rotation to expose blind spots |
| 6 | Novice Option Expansion | Novice, lacking domain knowledge, considers options expert would discard; novice considers unconventional approaches; some produce surprising success | Novice does not "know" what is impossible; considers paths expert would never try; some options are genuinely better for changed contexts | Novice can find novel solutions expert would miss; but novice usually chooses poorly because lacks understanding of consequences | Leverage novice options carefully; evaluate options on merit not source; combine novice option space with expert evaluation |
| 7 | Framing Cascade | Initial framing of problem shapes attention and option set; option set shapes reasoning; reasoning shapes decision; decision outcomes reinforce original framing | Framing effects produce different choice; choice commits resources; resources produce outcomes confirming framing; framing appears validated | Arbitrary initial framing can lock in suboptimal path; reframing is hard because path-dependent costs make change costly | Reframe problem periodically; question framing explicitly; design decision processes that test framing-dependence |
| 8 | Paradigm Lock-In | Paradigm boundaries make certain hypotheses unthinkable; unthinkable hypotheses are not tested; absence of testing is treated as evidence paradigm is correct; paradigm becomes increasingly rigid | Paradigm excludes hypotheses → hypotheses untested → absence of evidence for hypotheses → evidence for paradigm → paradigm strengthens | Paradigm can lock out critical realities that fall outside paradigm boundaries; learning is impossible until paradigm shift | Expose paradigm through alternatives; test paradigm boundaries; design paradigm-challenge sessions; bring in outsiders with different paradigms |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Mental Model Invisibility Blindness | Analysis assumes agents perceive available information without recognising that mental models determine what counts as visible; assumes information provision is sufficient to change perception | Map agents' mental models explicitly through interviews, behavioural prediction tasks, and anomaly detection tests; compare mental model to actual system; identify what mental model makes invisible; test perception against ground truth |
| 2 | Expert Blind Spot Underestimation | Analysis assumes expertise implies accurate perception when expertise creates both capability and blindness; misses what expert has automated and cannot articulate | Compare expert performance to edge cases and novel situations; ask expert to articulate implicit reasoning; test expert in unfamiliar contexts; pair expert with novice to surface expert assumptions |
| 3 | Bias Underestimation as "Just Error" | Analysis treats biases as mistakes caused by information deficit or low ability when biases are systematic features of reasoning under constraints; assumes bias provision of information corrects bias | Test whether providing information corrects bias (confirmation bias persists despite contrary evidence); test whether slowing deliberation helps (some biases are automatic); identify bias mechanism before designing intervention |
| 4 | Attention Allocation Invisibility | Analysis assumes agents attend to information that is available without recognising that attention is selective and limited; assumes if information is present, agents perceive it | Observe what agents attend to; measure working memory load; test peripheral perception; identify what salience and expectations drive attention; map what is peripheral to agents despite being present |
| 5 | Bounded Rationality Denial | Analysis assumes rational actors can process all available information and evaluate all options when agents are bounded rational; expects optimisation when agents satisfice | Test option search depth; identify what drives satisficing vs continued search; map information overload points; understand why agents stopped processing information; design decisions for bounded rationality not perfect rationality |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)

| II — Systemic | Cognitive processes are embedded in role positions, expertise structures, and organisational norms; blindness is reinforced by institutional structures that reward expertise confidence | Expertise-based authority prevents questioning; certain questions are "not for people in this role"; expert consensus closes off inquiry; novices lack legitimacy to raise concerns | Map expertise hierarchy; test whether questioning expert is psychologically safe; compare private and expert-challenged positions; assess whether organisation can learn when expert blind spots matter |
| III — Informational | Cognitive biases filter information systematically; confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, and availability bias ensure disconfirming information is filtered out or reinterpreted | Agents selectively seek confirming evidence; agents dismiss disconfirming evidence; agent's conclusions shift with framing; information provision does not change perception | Test evidence uptake directly; present disconfirming evidence and observe response; map information filters and standards; identify selective attention and memory patterns; test confirmation bias persistence |
| IV — Temporal | Cognitive processes are locked to past contexts; mental models are based on historical periods; temporal lags between reality change and model update; models persist past their applicability | Beliefs and models are based on past situations; agents are surprised by changes they did not anticipate; adaptation lags behind reality change; agents continue reasoning from outdated models | Timeline agents' mental model formation; compare model formation to current reality; test whether mental models track current state or historical state; check for temporal drift without re-examination |
| V — Relational | Cognitive processes are maintained through social enforcement and epistemic trust networks; certain ways of perceiving are enforced; alternative perceptions are delegitimized or suppressed | Perceptions appear stable across contexts but are externally maintained; agents report different perceptions in private vs public; authority figures dominate perception; questioning is socially costly | Compare private perception (anonymous) to public expression; map epistemic trust networks and source credibility; identify conformity pressure and retaliation risks; assess psychological safety for alternative perception |
| VI — Ontological | Paradigm boundaries make certain realities unthinkable or inadmissible; background mental models exclude entire domains from consideration; frame-work boundaries are invisible to inhabitants | Certain questions are not asked because they are outside the paradigm; agents cannot conceive of alternatives; hypotheses outside paradigm are rejected a priori; paradigm boundaries are invisible | Expose paradigm through interaction with alternatives; test whether hypotheses outside paradigm are evaluated or rejected a priori; probe admissibility rules; design paradigm-challenge sessions; bring in outsiders |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

1) **Run Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories** (early pass; assume Hiddens exist). For each meta-category, ask: "Why might cognitive processes be generating hiddens here? What symptoms would indicate it?" Record findings in Tier 1 scan table (§8.2).

2) **Shortlist candidate Hiddens**; for each, rate: mechanism (how is it produced/maintained), reducibility (how fixable), detectability (how observable), agency (who maintains it), consequence (what impact). Use Tier 2 crosswalk table (§8.3) to develop working model for each candidate.

3) **Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface** per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (Type C in Tier 2 table). Detection interface specifies how to surface the Hidden; remediation interface specifies how to address it.

4) **Run the Hiddens source analysis** (§8.1) and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens in Hiddens Register (standard register at OG §D.4.1). Explicitly mark which Hiddens remain opaque and why.

5) **If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan** (OG v2.4, §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3). Standard Targeted Scans that commonly apply to Framework of Cognition: (a) **Positional Blindness Scan** (Family B, Scan 3) — when expertise creates role-based cognitive constraints or novice blindness is preventing information from being perceived; (b) **Paradigm Boundary Scan** (Family D, Scan 5 / Ontological VI) — when mental model or paradigm boundaries are preventing consideration of critical realities; (c) **Cognitive Cascade Scan** (Family B, Scan 2) — when multiple cognitive biases and bounded rationality constraints interact to produce compounding errors.

6) **After executing the framework protocol and any available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1** (pre-closure pass) and note deltas. Have new cognitive Hiddens emerged? Have candidate Hiddens been reduced or disconfirmed? Record changes explicitly.

7) **Produce Residual Unknowns** (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and **apply the Escalation Rule** (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or remediation priorities. **Produce closure artefacts per OG v2.4, §7.4:** (1) residual unknowns list, (2) acceptability rationale, (3) monitoring plan, (4) ownership assignment, (5) learning hook.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- **High consequence** (safety, regulatory, existential, severe harm potential; strategy/transformation at risk; precedent-setting decision)
- **Expert blind spot plausibility** (decisions are being made by experts in areas where expert blind spots are plausible; edge cases or novel situations are involved)
- **Mental model misalignment likely** (models are based on past contexts; reality has changed significantly; model lock-in is strong)
- **Persistent perception gaps** (agents continue to miss information despite its availability; anomaly goes undetected despite evidence)
- **Multiple cognitive biases interact** (confirmation bias + availability bias + motivated reasoning cascade; compounding effects suspected)
- **Decision-critical residual unknowns** (closure would be false; Unknowns could change decisions, accountability, or remediation)

**Tier 3 execution (per OG v2.4, §D.6.10):**
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into full Cognition taxonomy (all 30+ cognitive types and mechanisms)
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements for claims, more explicit competing hypotheses for mental models, more rigorous disconfirming tests
- When adversarial intent is plausible: adds alternative mental models and tests whether agents are reasoning from deliberately false models
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree (OG §4.3, branch A6 high-consequence escalation)
- Produces more detailed Residual Unknowns and stronger escalation recommendation if material Unknowns remain

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table

| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Cognition is mechanism, not error | Cognitive processes (biases, heuristics, attention limits, bounded rationality) are systematic features of how human minds work, not failures of intelligence or intent. They affect all agents including experts. Treat them as mechanisms to work with, not defects to eliminate. Interventions must work with cognitive reality. |
| 2. Mental models determine what is visible | What agents perceive is determined by their mental models; information outside the model's categories is invisible even when present. Perception is not passive reception of reality; it is active construction by the agent's frameworks. Change mental models to change perception. |
| 3. Expertise creates complementary blindness | Experts have automated perception enabling fast, accurate judgment in domain—but automation creates blindness to what they take for granted. Novices see what experts miss, but cannot see implications experts understand implicitly. Neither blindness can be overcome by information alone. Leverage expert-novice complementarity. |
| 4. Attention is allocated selectively and limits reasoning | Limited attention is a biological necessity; selective allocation is required. What is attended to is foregrounded; what is not attended to is invisible or forgotten. Working memory limits what can be held simultaneously. Design decisions and interventions for actual attention limits, not idealised unlimited attention. |
| 5. Hiddens from cognition require cognitive intervention | Information provision does not automatically correct perception shaped by mental models, attention allocation, or cognitive biases. Cognitive blindness requires intervention targeting the cognitive mechanism: mental model revision, bias awareness, attention widening, reasoning improvement. Diagnosis of cognitive mechanism must precede intervention design. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Automatization | The process through which frequently-performed tasks are delegated to unconscious processing, enabling fast and parallel performance but rendering reasoning invisible and creating blindness to what has been automated |
| Base-Rate Neglect | The bias of ignoring base-rate (population-level) information when making probability judgments; produced by representativeness heuristic when judging probability by similarity to category |
| Bounded Rationality | The condition that agents cannot evaluate infinite options, cannot process unlimited information, and cannot compute perfect probabilities; agents make good-enough (satisficing) decisions given cognitive constraints |
| Causal Model | An agent's internal representation of what causes what; determines what interventions the agent believes will produce desired effects; misspecified causal models produce failed interventions |
| Cognitive Bias | A systematic deviation from normative reasoning or perception arising from how the mind processes information; predictable, recurring, not caused by low ability, but by cognitive structure |
| Cognitive Blindness | What becomes invisible to agents because of how their minds work; includes model-based blindness (events outside mental model), attention blindness (peripheral information not attended to), expert blindness (automated reasoning), novice blindness (lack of conceptual framework) |
| Confirmation Bias | The tendency to selectively seek, attend to, and remember evidence that confirms existing beliefs while filtering out disconfirming evidence |
| Expert Blind Spot | What experts cannot see because they have automated perception and reasoning; includes things experts take for granted, things experts perceive too fast to articulate, things experts optimised perception to exclude |
| Framing Effect | The phenomenon that how a choice or decision problem is presented (framed) affects choice even though the decision problem is objectively the same; frames determine attention to different aspects |
| Heuristic | A mental shortcut or rule of thumb used to simplify reasoning under uncertainty; efficient but error-prone; includes availability (judge frequency by ease of recall), representativeness (judge probability by similarity), anchoring (adjust insufficiently from initial number) |
| Hindsight Bias | The tendency, after observing an outcome, to believe that outcome was more predictable than it actually was; outcomes appear inevitable in hindsight even though they were uncertain before |
| Inattentional Blindness | The failure to perceive a stimulus that is present in the environment because attention is focused elsewhere; agent does not see the event even when looking directly at it |
| Mental Model | An agent's internal representation of how a system, process, or domain works; determines what is recognised as normal, what is an anomaly, and what is invisible because it does not fit the model |
| Mental Model Lock-In | Resistance to revising a mental model even when evidence contradicts it; caused by confirmation bias, identity protection, sunk costs, and the model's success in past contexts |
| Model-Based Blindness | Events or anomalies that do not fit the agent's mental model and therefore remain perceptually unavailable; the agent cannot see what the model does not include space for |
| Motivated Reasoning | The shifting of evidential standards based on preferred conclusions; agent demands more proof for disliked conclusions than liked ones; rational discourse becomes difficult because standards move |
| Novice Blindness | What novices cannot see because they lack domain knowledge or conceptual frameworks required for perception; novices see details experts miss but cannot see implications experts understand |
| Satisficing | The decision-making strategy of choosing an option that meets minimum criteria (good enough) rather than searching for the optimal option; results from bounded rationality constraints |
| Working Memory | The cognitive system that holds information in mind during reasoning; limited capacity (approximately 5–9 items); exceeding capacity produces errors, forgotten information, or shutdown |
