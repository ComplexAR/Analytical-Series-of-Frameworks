## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Narratives framework identifies stories, plot structures, agency attributions, frames, and master narratives that organise meaning and justify action in a scenario; classifies them into six meta-categories and thirty core types; profiles them across five dimensions (temporality, agency, valence, coherence, embeddedness); maps narrative dynamics and ecology (drift, collapse, capture, fragmentation, emergence); and surfaces visibility failures (distortion, silencing, closure, amplification bias). It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with narrative-specific blindnesses, evidence-narrative mismatches, and governance gaps, enabling downstream frameworks to design remediation and narrative integrity.

## Section 4: Condensed Core Taxonomy

| Meta-category | Core types (names only) | Diagnostic question (1 sentence) |
|---|---|---|
| I. Plot-Structural Narratives | Linear-Causal, Quest/Progress, Fall/Decline, Cyclical/Return, Fragmented/Non-Linear | How are events ordered into causal sequence, and what does the temporal structure reveal about contingency vs. inevitability? |
| II. Character & Agency Narratives | Hero, Victim, Villain/Blame, Redemption/Transformation, Collective-Agent | Who is depicted as acting; how is agency and responsibility distributed; and what moral standing is assigned? |
| III. Framing & Interpretation Narratives | Crisis/Emergency, Opportunity/Innovation, Threat/Enemy, Normalisation/Business-as-Usual, Trade-off/Dilemma | How is the situation defined and what action set is foregrounded; what is bracketed as inevitable or routine? |
| IV. Master & Systemic Narratives | Progress/Modernisation, National/Identity, Market/Technocratic, Civilisational/Religious, Inevitable Trend/Destiny | What large-scale cultural or institutional logics organise meaning and anchor legitimacy across many local stories? |
| V. Counter-, Alternative & Subaltern Narratives | Counter-Narrative, Resistance/Emancipatory, Conspiracy/Hidden-Forces, Marginalised/Subaltern, Satirical/Parodic | Which narratives challenge dominant accounts, reveal hidden harms, emerge from non-dominant groups, or expose contradictions? |
| VI. Narrative Ecology & Competition | Dominant/Hegemonic, Fragmented/Multiplicity, Polarised/Camp, Narrative Capture/Monopolisation, Narrative Vacuum/Silence | How do narratives coexist, compete, dominate, or suppress alternatives; what is the configuration of the information environment? |

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Narrative Drift | Meaning shifts gradually; audiences diverge; retellings accumulate; semantic drift in key terms across contexts. | Reaccumulation of small retellings; context shifts; audience differences; selective emphasis. | Drift is invisible until discontinuity surfaces; system operates under radically different understandings; decisions compound on false premises. |
| Narrative Collapse | Dominant narrative fails; predictions fail; contradictions accumulate; lived experience diverges sharply from story. | Evidence contradicts narrative; event falsifies prediction; insider accounts diverge from official story. | Collapse triggers crisis and opportunity for course-correction or risk of vacuum and extremist takeover. |
| Narrative Entrenchment | Narrative becomes rigid and resistant to evidence; questioning becomes identity-threatening; moral investment increases. | Identity-protective reasoning; sunk costs; institutional reinforcement; generational transmission. | Entrenchment disables learning; error correction becomes disloyalty; institutional flexibility decreases; cascades and brittleness increase. |
| Narrative Capture | Small set of actors controls production and circulation; alternatives marginalised or suppressed. | Power imbalance; gatekeeping over channels; incentive structure rewards conformity. | Narrowed thinking; disabled error-correction; delegitimised alternative voices; shadow narratives; regime surprise when truth emerges. |
| Narrative Fragmentation | Narratives diverge across audiences and communities; no shared baseline; incompatible stories operative. | Audience segmentation; channel silos; platform algorithms; power imbalances limiting translation. | Coordination failure; markets malfunction; policies fail; Babel conditions; no shared facts. |
| Narrative Emergence | New narratives emerge from marginalised groups and compete with dominant stories; previously silent voices become articulate. | Changed incentives for voice; network effects amplify emerging narratives; crisis creates space for alternatives. | Enables correction and adaptation but risks polarisation and capture; new narratives may not be more truthful. |
| Narrative Echoing & False Consensus | Confirmations are echoes; multiple outlets repeat one upstream account, creating appearance of broad corroboration. | Provenance concentration; media consolidation; platform amplification of early sources; lack of independence check. | False consensus drives policy and resource allocation; error correction delayed; minority dissent invisible. |
| Shadow Narratives & Performative Compliance | Real story negotiated in backchannels; formal narrative becomes performative, incomplete, or misleading. | Fear of reputational risk; political pressure; punitive governance; lack of psychological safety. | Gap between formal and informal narratives indicates hidden problems; shadow narratives become hostile; institution loses credibility. |
| Narrative Amnesia | Institutional memory loss deletes past narratives, promises, and failures; system repeats cycles. | Staff turnover; archive loss; intentional erasure; generational discontinuity. | Repeated failures; cycle detection fails; precedents lost; institutional trust erodes; crises seem unexpected. |
| Narrative Crisis & Recovery | Crisis breaks confidence in dominant narrative; system enters uncertainty; new narrative stabilises or remains in vacuum. | Sudden event falsifies narrative; accumulation of evidence; insider breach; loss of key authority. | Critical juncture for learning and adaptation but vulnerability to hostile narratives and opportunistic manipulation. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What narratives are invisible due to inattention or low salience; which stories are drowned out by noise; what weak signals are missed? |
| II (Systemic) | How do narratives couple with institutions, incentives, and norms in ways not fully modelled; what emergent effects arise from narrative-system interaction? |
| III (Informational) | What evidence contradicts narrative claims; what data are withheld or distorted; what measurement games support narrative over outcome; what categories are missing from narrative schema? |
| IV (Temporal) | How are timescales distorted in narrative (compression, amnesia, repetition); what slow variables and path dependencies are erased; what reversibility assumptions are unexamined? |
| V (Relational) | Who controls narrative production and circulation; whose voices are excluded; who bears reputational risk for dissent; whose accountability is obscured or scattered? |
| VI (Ontological) | What categories and moral orders does the narrative foreclose; whose lived realities cannot be represented in the narrative schema; what alternative sense-makings are not conceivable? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Temporal Compression (long histories erased into simple linear arcs; contingencies and reversals hidden)
- Agency Flattening (distributed or structural causes obscured by hero/villain/victim attribution)
- Frame Naturalisation (chosen frames treated as situation-inherent facts; alternatives dismissed as misunderstanding)
- Narrative Dominance & Suppression (alternative and counter-narratives marginalised; monopolisation crowds out plurality)
- Narrative Capture & Shadow Stories (formal narrative masks backchannels; performative compliance hides real negotiations)
- Evidence-Narrative Mismatches (narrative claims unexamined against evidence; measurement gaming favours narrative over outcome)
- Audience Invisibility (divergent audience interpretations assumed away; echo chambers and polarisation invisible)
- Institutional Amnesia (past narratives, failures, promises deleted; system repeats cycles without learning)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Temporal compression and path-dependency erasure | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Systems | Timeline reconstruction; slow variable identification; regime shift detection | Build explicit timelines; surface reversals and contingencies; test assumptions about inevitability; incorporate historical depth |
| Agency attribution and scapegoating | Framework of Governance; Framework of Responsibility | Attribution audit; accountability mapping; systemic driver analysis | Separate individual from structural drivers; establish accountability for decisions not just outcomes; audit proportionality of blame |
| Evidence-narrative mismatch and measurement gaming | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Metrics | Evidence quality audit; metric-outcome divergence analysis; gaming vulnerability test | Conduct evidence audit and publish gaps; redesign metrics to match outcomes; establish independent verification; escalate anomalies |
| Narrative capture and shadow stories | Framework of Governance; Framework of Hiddens (Tier 2) | Governance structure audit; dissent safety assessment; channel and narrative committee design | Reduce gatekeeping power; create protected dissent channels; formalise decision-relevant narratives; establish diverse narrative committees; increase transparency |
| Narrative fragmentation and Babel conditions | Framework of Framing; Framework of Scale | Audience mapping; translation cost analysis; baseline definition for decision-critical domains | Create translation forums; develop canonical narratives with translation guidance; establish shared baselines; audit for semantic drift across audiences |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. List focal narratives in the scenario; identify stories told to whom and by whom (official accounts, counter-narratives, emergent voices).
2. Classify each narrative into one or more of the six meta-categories (I–VI) and assign a core type (1–30); note if type is uncertain or hybrid.
3. For each focal narrative, profile two critical dimensions: Plot Structure & Temporality (linear/cyclic/fragmented, compressed/extended) and Agency & Responsibility Distribution (concentrated/distributed, individual/systemic).
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six Hiddens meta-categories lightly; flag obvious perceptual blindspots (inattention), temporal distortions, agency flattening, or evidence-narrative gaps.
5. Shortlist the 2–3 highest-consequence Hiddens from Tier 1 scan; note detection/remediation interfaces; flag for escalation if high-consequence and high-uncertainty.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. List focal narratives; create narrative inventory with versions, audiences, institutional reinforcement, emergence timeline, and provenance.
2. Classify each narrative (meta-category + core type 1–30); note taxonomy deviations and domain-specific refinements if needed.
3. Profile all five dimensions for each focal narrative: Temporality (linear/cyclic/compressed), Agency Distribution (concentrated/distributed), Valence & Normativity (positive/negative, prescriptive/descriptive), Coherence & Closure (coherent/fragmented, closed/open), Embeddedness & Institutionalisation (formal/informal, central/peripheral).
4. Analyse all ten dynamics patterns; for each focal narrative, assess which patterns are active (nascent, present, high-risk) and why; map cascade risks and feedback loops.
5. Map narrative ecology: identify dominant/hegemonic narratives, fragmentation or polarisation, capture or monopolisation, vacuum or silence, emerging alternatives; assess translation and bridge opportunities.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan (structured deepening): for each high-relevance Hidden (e.g., temporal compression, agency flattening, evidence-narrative mismatch, narrative capture), identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; assign detection and remediation interfaces.
7. Document unresolved Unknowns; apply Escalation Rule if any Unknown could change decisions or accountability; generate Residual Unknowns register and closure artefacts.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add shortlist of narrative-specific Hiddens with meta-category tag (I–VI), mechanism (distortion, silencing, closure, amplification), detectability, consequence. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate any high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hidden to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record narrative type classifications, dimensional profiles, ecology mapping, evidence-narrative match/mismatch, provenance and corroboration patterns, gaps and anomalies. | After narrative classification and dimensional profiling; note gaps (e.g., evidence audit, temporal reconstruction, audience diversity testing). |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about narrative effects, drift, entrenchment, capture trajectories; propose tests (temporal reconstruction, evidence audit, audience testing, shadow narrative elicitation, ecology mapping). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request narrative versions and variants across audiences, temporal archive of narrative changes, evidence for key narrative claims, audience segmentation and reach data, internal/shadow narrative accounts. | To reduce Unknowns and validate Hiddens detection; coordinate with Evidence and Governance frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified focal narratives, classified them into the six meta-categories, profiled critical dimensions (temporality, agency, valence, coherence, embeddedness), assessed narrative ecology and dynamics, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-consequence Unknowns to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Evidence, Governance)?

## Section 8: Cross-Framework Routing Pointers

### 8a. Upstream Inputs (frameworks commonly feeding into this one)

- Situations Context Classification (what situation type generates which narratives?)
- Agents & Personas (who are the narrators; what are their contexts, goals, and credibility?)
- Evidence (what evidence exists about narrative claims, quality, provenance, gaps, contested claims?)
- Scale & Time (what is the deployment timescale of narratives; how stable or volatile is the narrative environment?)
- Power & Institutions (who has power to shape narratives; how do institutions reinforce or suppress stories?)
- Framing & Sensemaking (how do frames and attention patterns shape what narratives are visible and salient?)

### 8b. Downstream Handoffs (frameworks commonly consuming this framework's outputs)

- Framework of Hiddens (narrative-specific Hiddens detection and remediation design)
- Framework of Evidence (evidence audit against narrative claims; narrative-evidence mismatch remediation)
- Framework of Governance (narrative governance, authority over narrative production, accountability for narrative integrity)
- Framework of Interventions (narrative redesign, reframing, retirement, protection, or emergence support)
- Framework of Responsibility (accountability mapping for narrative production, circulation, and correction)
- Framework of Risk (risk register from narrative dynamics, capture, collapse, fragmentation, vacuum scenarios)
