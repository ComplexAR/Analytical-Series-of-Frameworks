# Introduction

## What are Hiddens?

In complex environments, failures are frequently driven less by absence of intelligence and more by *structured invisibilities*: key facts are missing, distorted, fabricated, delayed, compartmentalised, suppressed, or conceptually unavailable. These are **hiddens**—not merely "unknowns" in the sense of unacquired knowledge, but identifiable patterns by which reality becomes unavailable to an analyst, organisation, or system at the moment it matters. A blindspot is different from a mystery; a mirage is different from honest uncertainty; a concealed truth is different from an undiscovered one. Each involves a distinct hiding mechanism, requires a different detection approach, and justifies different remediation moves.

The Framework of Hiddens provides a **canonical visibility layer** for the Analytical Series. It gives a stable vocabulary (30 types) for classifying *what is hidden* and *by what mechanism*, plus a repeatable protocol for mapping those hiddens into (a) detection moves, (b) remediation moves, and (c) interaction maps showing how hiddens cascade, reinforce, evolve, resist, or reveal. This is a backbone framework because it governs the visibility assumptions underneath every other analytic object in the Analytical Series. Whether mapping realities, diagnosing causation, assessing evidence, designing interventions, or assigning responsibility, each output is only as reliable as what it can—and cannot—see.

## Why does Hiddens matter for the Analytical Series?

The Analytical Series is built on the premise that complex situations require structured analysis across multiple dimensions. Yet every analysis rests on visibility assumptions. Is the evidence base adequate? Are the measurement systems biased? Is information being withheld? Are the categories we are using sufficient to capture what is real? These visibility failures are not marginal; they can undermine entire investigations and drive cascading failures. The Framework of Hiddens makes visibility assumptions explicit, testable, and governable. It provides the disciplines that prevent:

- **Overconfidence in partial visibility** (treating available data as complete truth),
- **Failure to triangulate** (missing the same distortion across multiple sources),
- **Misattribution of uncertainty** (conflating "unknowns" with "structured hiddens" that could be remedied),
- **Missed interactions and cascades** (fixing one hidden while inadvertently creating others),
- **Paradigm blindness** (believing that deeper investigation can resolve what is actually an ontological boundary), and
- **Reflexive failures** (assuming that the framework analyzing hiddens is itself exempt from visibility limits).

This framework integrates with every other framework in the series: it surfaces the visibility conditions required for trustworthy outputs, flags likely failure modes, identifies candidate hiddens for systematic checking, and triggers escalation to deeper scans when the cost of error, adversarial pressure, or uncertainty warrants it.

## What does this framework produce?

The Framework of Hiddens operationalises visibility mapping through seven core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 hidden types** organised into six meta-categories (Perceptual, Systemic, Informational, Temporal, Relational, Ontological) that recur across domains and cover mechanisms from immediate perception to fundamental limits.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence) that profile any hidden and explain why different analysts classify the same visibility failure differently.

3. **Dynamics and transition pathways** describing how hiddens evolve, cascade, reinforce, resist, and reveal—enabling detection of risk patterns and early warning of systemic vulnerability.

4. **Three interface types** (detection, remediation, interaction) that translate hidden classifications into action: what tests would surface this hidden, what interventions would reduce it, and what other hiddens depend on it.

5. **Hiddens source analysis** mapping the four systematic sources of visibility failure (misclassification, distortion/bias, concealment/asymmetry, paradigm/ontological limits) and the eight families of hiding mechanisms.

6. **Tiered scanning protocol** (Tier 1 mandatory audit; Tier 2 structured mapping for high-stakes; Tier 3 full-spectrum escalation) integrated with the Operating Guide's investigation loop.

7. **Standard registers and application protocol** (seven steps, table-driven, emitting Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests, and closure artefacts) that integrate across the entire Analytical Series.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework as part of every other framework's work (via each framework's mandatory §8 Hiddens cross-check) and in standalone mode when visibility is contested, stakes are high, evidence is weak, adversarial concealment is suspected, or deep uncertainty requires systematic governance. It works in both Rapid Run Mode (Tier 1 visibility audit only; 15–30 minutes) and Investigative Run Mode (Tier 1 + Tier 2 mapping; 2–4 hours for typical scenarios). Default execution is Light Depth: run Tier 1 once, spot candidate hiddens, assign owners, design at least one probe per high-consequence hidden. Escalate to Full Depth when routing rules (OG §4.3) apply, consequence is material, evidence is contested, or a Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG §7.5) is triggered.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for complete specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework produces disciplined visibility maps with explicit F/I/A/U tagging, bounded closure discipline, and residual-unknowns logs that surface what remains hidden and why.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

In complex environments, failures are frequently driven less by a lack of intelligence and more by *structured invisibilities*: key facts are missing, distorted, fabricated, delayed, compartmentalised, suppressed, or conceptually unavailable. These are **hiddens**—not merely "unknowns", but identifiable patterns by which reality becomes unavailable to an analyst, organisation, or system at the moment it matters.

It gives a stable vocabulary (30 types) for classifying *what is hidden*, plus a repeatable protocol for mapping those hiddens into (a) detection moves, (b) remediation moves, and (c) interaction maps showing how hiddens cascade, reinforce, evolve, resist, or reveal.

This is a backbone framework because it governs the visibility assumptions underneath every other analytic object in the Analytical Series. Whether mapping realities, diagnosing causation, assessing evidence, designing interventions, or assigning responsibility, each output is only as reliable as what it can—and cannot—see. The Framework of Hiddens makes visibility assumptions explicit, testable, and governable; the other frameworks, in turn, provide structured lenses that help surface candidate hiddens for classification and action.

**Interaction rule:** Other frameworks may surface hiddens; Hiddens governs visibility across them.

### Two-direction use of the Analytical Series (recommended)

The Analytical Series can be used in two complementary directions:

**Top-down (Backbone / governance direction)**
Hiddens imposes visibility discipline on the work of every other framework. It specifies visibility requirements, forces cross-checks, identifies likely failure modes, and triggers escalation to deeper scans when the cost of error, adversarial pressure, or uncertainty warrants it.

**Bottom-up (Elicitation / discovery direction)**
Other frameworks can be used as structured lenses that generate candidate hiddens ("what might be missing, distorted, concealed, or misbounded here?"). They help surface hiddens, but they do not define the taxonomy of hiddens, determine completeness, or set the visibility governance rules.

### Relationship model: bidirectional coupling with asymmetric authority

- **Other frameworks → help elicit Hiddens (inputs/signals):** they generate structured prompts, anomalies, and hypotheses that point to potential hiddenness.
- **Hiddens → validates and governs other frameworks (cross-checking, confidence, remediation):** it classifies what is hidden and why, evaluates the visibility conditions required for trustworthy outputs, and prescribes detection and remediation actions that qualify or correct downstream conclusions.

The framework is intentionally **interaction-aware**. Hiddens rarely occur in isolation: secrets create fragmentation; fragmentation sustains mirages; echoes harden mirages; legacy ghosts constrain remediation; positional and scalar limits create systematic disagreements; ontological boundaries determine what questions can even be asked. A correct Hiddens application therefore includes both (1) classification of specific hiddens and (2) modelling of hidden systems.

Finally, the framework is **reflexive**. It applies to its own use: the act of classifying hiddens is itself vulnerable to visibility failures, framing, echoes, and paradigm boundaries. This is not a weakness; it is a design constraint that keeps the series epistemically disciplined.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

- **Definition of Hidden**: a reality-relevant factor whose presence, structure, or implications are not available to the analyst/agent in the relevant moment, due to identifiable hiding mechanisms.
- **In scope:**
  - Perceptual failures (missed signals; false positives; cognitive bandwidth and habituation)
  - Systemic distortions (measurement/method/model/framing artefacts)
  - Informational pathologies (concealment, amplification, noise, fragmentation, latency)
  - Temporal residues (repression, legacy constraints, memory loss, future misreads, wrong-era categories)
  - Relational/scale invisibilities (position effects; level mismatch; emergence; context loss; perspective limits)
  - Ontological/epistemic limits (unasked questions, paradigm boundaries, category absence, ineffability, fundamental unpredictability)
- **Out of scope** (by design):
  - A complete causal model of any domain (handled by Diagnosis/Causation and domain models)
  - A values-resolution or ethics method (handled by Values/Governance/Responsibility)
  - A complete decision procedure (handled by Decisions/Interventions)
- **Common confusions** (what "Hidden" is not):
  - Hidden ≠ "unknown" (**foundational guardrail**): unknown is a knowledge state; hidden is a visibility condition caused by mechanisms.
  - Hidden ≠ "uncertainty": uncertainty is a knowledge state/representation range; hidden mechanisms often *create* or *widen* uncertainty.
  - Hidden ≠ "risk": risk is outcome-relevant possibility; hiddens are visibility failures that shape risk assessment and control.

### 1.3.1 Foundational guardrail: Hidden ≠ "unknown" (mandatory)

Hiddenness is **not** a synonym for "unknown." The distinction is structural:

- **Unknown** is a *knowledge state*: an observer does not (yet) know X.
- **Hidden** is a *visibility condition*: under current constraints, X is not reliably observable/inspectable/measurable for the relevant observer/decision process, due to identifiable mechanisms (e.g., absence of instrumentation, distortion by models/metrics, concealment by agents, boundary choices, fragmentation, latency).

This guardrail is essential. If "hidden" is collapsed into "unknown," Hiddens is misused as a generic information-gathering method ("collect more data"), and it ceases to function as the Analytical Series' **visibility-governance layer**.

**Operational test (fast):**
- If the missing information is readily accessible with ordinary effort, it is primarily **unknown** → retrieve it.
- If the system prevents *reliable* observation even with effort (or yields systematically biased signals), it is **hidden** → change the visibility conditions.

**Illustrative cases:**
1. **Unknown but not hidden:** You have not read a public document, policy, or dataset that is readily accessible. The information is not hidden; it is simply unretrieved.
2. **Hidden (and therefore unknown):** True incident rates are suppressed by incentives, under-reporting, and metric gaming. The information is not merely missing; the system structure prevents reliable seeing without changing measurement and governance conditions.
3. **Nominally known but still hidden:** A KPI value is "known" and repeatedly reported, yet the underlying reality remains hidden because the proxy is systematically distorted (e.g., Goodhart effects, selection bias, boundary mis-specification). The number exists; trustworthy visibility does not.

**Implication for action:**
- **Unknown → retrieval:** research, access requests, document discovery, direct measurement.
- **Hidden → visibility redesign:** instrumentation, triangulation, independent verification, incentive redesign, boundary revision, provenance controls, auditability, and governance safeguards.

### 1.3.2 Knowledge state vs hidden type (recommended)

The 30-type taxonomy classifies **how** something is hidden (dominant mechanism) and includes a canonical **epistemic failure mode** (how knowing tends to fail). This does **not** uniquely determine the *degree* of knowledge in a particular case.

**Rule:** treat "knowledge state" (epistemic status) as a property of the **(hidden instance × observer/role × moment)**, not as a fixed property of the hidden type.

Why this matters:
- The same hidden instance can be well-known to one actor (e.g., the concealing party) and unknown to another (e.g., an external analyst).
- The same analyst may move through states over time (unrecognised gap → recognised gap → partial recovery → false certainty → corrected model).
- Many hiddens are *relational*: visibility depends on access, position, and scale, not only on the mechanism label.

**What the hidden type does constrain:** it narrows the *likely* epistemic failure pattern (e.g., recognised gaps, false knowns, strategic ignorance, paradigm exclusion). That mapping is the second axis of the dual-axis system (Section 3.8).

**How to operationalise:** in the hidden register, add an **Epistemic status** field per high-priority hidden instance, alongside confidence and evidence basis. A practical scale is:
- **Unrecognised gap:** no awareness that something relevant is missing.
- **Recognised gap:** awareness of missingness, but content remains unavailable (classic "known unknown").
- **False known:** a confident belief/model exists but is wrong (hallucination/mirage patterns).
- **Contested/unstable:** multiple incompatible models persist; evidence does not settle the issue.
- **In-principle limited:** limits remain even under best-available methods (unknowable/irreducible uncertainty).

Record (a) who holds this status, (b) what evidence supports it, and (c) what visibility interventions would move the status toward reliable seeing—or toward robustness-by-design where seeing is not possible.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Perceptual Hiddens
- **Definition:** Hiddens produced at the interface between reality and awareness—missed signals or false positives due to perceptual/attentional limits.
- **Diagnostic cues:** "We didn't see it" despite availability; "we saw it" but cannot replicate; obvious-in-hindsight misses.
- **Typical failure mode:** Assuming that more data automatically eliminates perceptual failure.

### II. Systemic Hiddens
- **Definition:** Hiddens produced by measurement, modelling, and interpretive systems—warped lenses and emergent illusions.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Stable directional bias; conclusions depend heavily on framing; key variables are "out of model".
- **Typical failure mode:** Treating the model as reality rather than an instrument with exclusions.

### III. Informational Hiddens
- **Definition:** Hiddens produced by information channel structure—blockage, amplification, noise, distribution, or lag.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Information asymmetry; high-volume repetition without independence; silos; stale reporting.
- **Typical failure mode:** Treating "data availability" as equivalent to "knowledge availability".

### IV. Temporal Hiddens
- **Definition:** Hiddens produced by history and time—repression, legacy constraints, forgetting, projection, and temporal misapplication.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Unspoken topics; constraints with no rationale; repeated rediscovery; confident extrapolation; outdated categories.
- **Typical failure mode:** Treating the present as self-contained and ahistorical.

### V. Relational Hiddens
- **Definition:** Hiddens produced by relational structure and scale—what can only be seen from other positions, levels, or perspectives.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Stakeholders perceive different "realities"; patterns appear/disappear when zooming; the whole behaves unlike the parts.
- **Typical failure mode:** Over-privileging one vantage point or scale as "the truth".

### VI. Ontological Hiddens
- **Definition:** Hiddens produced by limits of conception and knowability—unasked questions, paradigm boundaries, category absence, ineffability, and irreducible unpredictability.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Persistent anomalies with no available category; consistent predictive failure; phenomena recognised only indirectly.
- **Typical failure mode:** Treating absence of concepts as absence of reality.

## 3.1 Types (Category I: Perceptual Hiddens)
| # | Type | Description (1–3 sentences) | Diagnostic cues | Epistemic failure mode |
|---:|---|---|---|---|

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)
| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | Treat the 30 types as the stable "visibility vocabulary" across domains; do not rename IDs without governance. |

| Versioning & governance | Changes require an explicit rationale, mapping impact assessment, and version-history entry. |

## 3.8 Dual-Axis Classification System (recommended)

The taxonomy is best understood as a **dual-axis system**:

- **Axis 1 — Mechanism family (how hiding is produced):** the six meta-categories (Perceptual, Systemic, Informational, Temporal, Relational, Ontological).
- **Axis 2 — Epistemic failure mode (how knowing fails):** the characteristic way an analyst/organisation fails to know in that mechanism family (e.g., Known Unknowns, False Knowns, Strategic Ignorance).

Each of the 30 types is a *unique intersection* of these axes. This has two benefits:

1. **Teachability:** users can learn to distinguish hiddens by both causal mechanism and knowledge-failure signature.
2. **Actionability:** detection and remediation choices become less error-prone because they are keyed to mechanism and failure mode rather than surface symptoms alone.

**Important:** an *epistemic failure mode* describes the characteristic way knowing tends to fail for a given hidden type. It is **not** the same as the current **knowledge state** of an observer. Two analysts can face the same hidden type while occupying different epistemic statuses (e.g., one holds a recognised gap; another holds a false known), depending on access, instrumentation, incentives, and time position.

## 3.9 Completeness Rationale (recommended)

The framework aims for *practical completeness*: a stable set of mutually distinct hiding mechanisms sufficient to classify visibility failures encountered across domains. The completeness rationale rests on:

- **Mechanism coverage:** the six meta-categories span the primary ways visibility fails: perception, analytic systems, information channels, time/history, relational structure/scale, and conceptual boundaries.
- **Non-overlap by dominant mechanism:** types are assigned by the dominant hiding mechanism; when multiple mechanisms are present, interaction mapping (Section 6, Type C) captures the compound system.
- **Hierarchical progression:** the ordering reflects increasing epistemic difficulty and decreasing eliminability (Perceptual → Systemic → Informational → Temporal → Relational → Ontological).
- **Epistemic coverage:** each type is paired with a characteristic knowledge-failure mode, ensuring classification does not rely solely on causal story but also on how the analyst's knowing breaks.
- **Diagnostic closure via symmetric checks:** symmetric pair logic (Appendix A) provides a structured completeness prompt: if you see A, check B; if you remediate A, ensure you did not create B.

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Hidden

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)
| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Mechanism | The "how" of hiding: which causal pathway produces invisibility (absence, false presence, distortion, concealment, boundary-limits, or combinations). | Absence ←→ Boundary | Identify the dominant hiding mechanism and match to type family. | Prevent type-confusion; select valid detection/remediation methods. |
| 2. Reducibility | Whether the hidden can be removed, reduced, or only managed (robustness/containment). | Eliminable ←→ Irreducible | Ask: "Can we remove the cause, or only adapt?" | Choose remediation posture (fix vs. mitigate vs. robustify). |
| 3. Detectability | How easy it is to surface the hidden with available methods and access. | Obvious ←→ Invisible | Review monitoring, access, and methodological feasibility. | Prioritise investigative effort and instrumentation. |
| 4. Agency | Whether the hidden is natural/accidental vs. deliberate/adversarial (or negligent). | Natural ←→ Adversarial | Ask who benefits, who controls channels, who can block disclosure. | Determine threat model, governance, and protections. |
| 5. Consequence | Severity if the hidden remains (or if misclassified). | Negligible ←→ Catastrophic | Evaluate downside, reversibility, systemic exposure, and time-to-harm. | Prioritise what to surface first; trigger escalation. |

## 4.2 Dimension Value-Sets (recommended)

### 4.2.1 Mechanism
| Value | Definition |
|---|---|
| Absence | Something exists but is not perceived or recorded; a gap in observation/awareness. |
| False Presence | Something is perceived/recorded that does not exist; spurious detection. |
| Distortion | Something exists but is perceived inaccurately; systematic warping. |
| Concealment | Something is actively hidden by agents, incentives, or enforced secrecy. |
| Boundary | Something is beyond the current limits of perception, modelling, or conception. |

### 4.2.2 Reducibility
| Value | Definition |
|---|---|
| Eliminable | Can be removed with known methods (instrumentation, governance, redesign). |
| Reducible | Can be significantly diminished but not fully removed. |
| Manageable | Cannot be reliably reduced, but impact can be mitigated. |
| Resistant | Difficult to reduce; requires deep structural change or sustained pressure. |
| Irreducible | Cannot be reduced in principle; requires robustness/acceptance strategies. |

### 4.2.3 Detectability
| Value | Definition |
|---|---|
| Obvious | Apparent once attention is directed; low effort. |
| Discoverable | Findable with systematic search and standard methods. |
| Hidden | Requires significant effort, specialist methods, or privileged access. |
| Obscure | Very difficult; may require breakthrough or fortuitous disclosure. |
| Invisible | Not directly detectable; only inferable indirectly or not at all. |

### 4.2.4 Agency
| Value | Definition |
|---|---|
| Natural | Arises from limits of perception/cognition/reality. |
| Accidental | Unintentional by-product of systems and processes. |
| Negligent | Due to failure to take reasonable preventative measures. |
| Deliberate | Intentionally created to control visibility. |
| Adversarial | Intentionally created to deceive/harm; active counter-detection expected. |

### 4.2.5 Consequence
| Value | Definition |
|---|---|
| Negligible | Minimal practical impact. |
| Minor | Limited harm potential; absorbable. |
| Moderate | Notable harm potential; requires management. |
| Severe | Major harm potential; urgent mitigation. |
| Catastrophic | Irreversible or existential-level harm potential; highest priority. |

## 4.3 Practical Scoring Guidance (recommended)
- **Score twice:** once for the *current state*, and once for the *expected state after intervention* (to avoid creating new hiddens).
- **Separate detectability from reducibility:** something can be detectable yet not reducible (e.g., Unknowable), or reducible yet hard to detect (e.g., certain Secrets).
- **Treat agency as a threat model:** deliberate/adversarial hiddens justify stronger protections for investigators and stronger evidence requirements.

# 5. Dynamics: Patterns Over Time

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)
| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cascade | One hidden triggers others in a chain (e.g., Secret → Fragmentation → Mirage). | Dependencies across channels, models, and actors propagate invisibility. | Fixing a downstream hidden may fail; sequence and upstream leverage matter. | Map chains; intervene upstream; monitor for secondary hiddens. |
| 2 | Reinforcement | Hiddens mutually stabilise (e.g., Echo strengthens Mirage; Mirage justifies Secret). | Feedback loops, incentives, defensive routines, and institutional lock-in. | Hiddens become self-sustaining; evidence and governance degrade. | Break loops; create independent evidence; add accountability and audits. |
| 3 | Evolution | A hidden changes type over time (e.g., Blindspot becomes Ghost through legacy). | Context and structure shift; temporary gaps become persistent constraints. | Remedies must anticipate transformation, not only current form. | Re-scan periodically; design for drift; treat as moving target. |
| 4 | Revelation | The hidden becomes visible (audit, incident, whistleblowing, new method). | New interfaces or shocks expose the hidden and its hiding mechanism. | Revelation can destabilise systems; can trigger secondary risks. | Build safe disclosure; plan response; preserve evidence; communicate carefully. |
| 5 | Resistance | The hidden resists detection/removal (deflection, noise, denial, retaliation). | Strategic behaviour or complexity barriers block detection and remediation. | Investigation can be attacked; naive methods fail or backfire. | Use multi-method detection; protect sources; avoid single points of failure. |

## 5.2 Common Transition Pathways (recommended)

Transition pathways are recurring sequences in which one hidden tends to transform into another. They are diagnostic (what to check next) and operational (where to intervene).

| Pathway | Typical context | What to check next | Intervention leverage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unknown Unknown → Blindspot → Hallucination → Echo → Mirage | Novel domain + weak instrumentation + social amplification | When a surprise occurs, check whether false positives and social feedback are filling the gap. | Instrument early; reward disconfirmation; enforce provenance and independence. |
| Secret → Fragmentation → Mirage | Compartmentalisation + complex systems + decision pressure | If information is withheld, check whether the picture exists nowhere and is being filled by coherent stories. | Reduce asymmetry; build integration layers; require traceable evidence. |
| Secret → Shadow → Ghost | Long-term concealment where concealment becomes taboo and then legacy constraint | If no one speaks, check whether constraints have become structural and "unquestionable". | Create safe disclosure; external audit; legacy reversal programme. |
| Blindspot → Distortion → Mirage | Gaps create biased proxies, which accumulate into a coherent but wrong model | If monitoring gaps exist, check for biased measurement and narrative closure. | Close measurement gaps; triangulate methods; track anomalies explicitly. |
| Latency → Projection | Stale data encourages straight-line extrapolation and regime-miss | If decisions use lagging indicators, check for future assumptions and missed discontinuities. | Shorten feedback loops; use scenarios; build leading indicators. |
| Scalar → Emergent Invisibility | Wrong scale blocks emergence recognition | If local optimisation fails, check for system-level behaviour and non-linear coupling. | Shift to system-level observation; run multi-scale analysis. |

# 6. Interface Types: Detection, Remediation, and Interaction

## 6.1 Interface Types Table (mandatory)
| Type | Name | Description | Key questions | Typical examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Detection Interface | Methods/tools that can reveal a hidden (tests, audits, replication, triangulation, sensing, red teaming). | "How could we observe, test, or falsify this?" | Replication; anomaly detection; distribution checks; red teaming; cross-position inquiry; independent audit |
| B | Remediation Interface | Interventions that reduce/remove/mitigate a hidden (governance, redesign, transparency, robustness, incentives). | "How do we remove the cause or reduce impact?" | Expand monitoring; disaggregate; transparency requirements; redesign incentives; robustness-building; governance safeguards |
| C | Interaction Interface | How hiddens connect—dependencies, cascades, reinforcements, transformations. | "Which other hiddens does this produce or depend on?" | Secret → Fragmentation; Aggregation → Mirage; Latency → Projection; Echo → Mirage |

## 6.2 Interface Selection Heuristics (recommended)
- **Start with detectability:** select at least one Type A interface per high-consequence hidden; prefer independent methods.
- **Match remediation to reducibility:** eliminable/reducible hiddens justify direct fixes; resistant/irreducible hiddens justify robustness and governance.
- **Always map at least one interaction chain:** hiddens are frequently upstream/downstream of each other; treat single-hidden fixes as provisional.

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Hiddens Cross-Check (Self-Referential Application)

## 8.1 Four Systematic Sources of Visibility Failure

This framework applies to itself: the act of classifying hiddens is vulnerable to the same visibility failures it is designed to detect. The four systematic sources below define where Hiddens analysis itself can fail.

| Source | Description | Typical hiding patterns | Detection moves | Remediation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Misclassification of Mechanism** | Hidden is assigned to wrong type/meta-category; downstream detection/remediation choices become invalid. | Type confusion (Perceptual vs Systemic; Temporal vs Relational); forcing fit to canonical 30 when extension needed | Re-rate the same hidden with a different team; compare dimensional profiles; test detection methods against predicted signatures | Map back to ground truth (evidence, diagnostics); iterate with domain expert; propose type extension if needed |
| **2. Structural Distortion / Bias** | The Hiddens taxonomy itself contains blindspots or systematic biases; entire classes of hiddens remain unmapped. | Missing meta-categories; paradigm bias in type definitions; underrepresentation of certain domain hiddens | Track "cannot classify" frequency; cross-domain comparison (similar cases, different domains); invite criticism from edge case practitioners | Assess whether 30-type taxonomy is sufficient (Tier 3 assessment); propose new meta-category or type if needed; update v3.0 |
| **3. Asymmetric Visibility in Analysis** | Analysts occupy different positions; what one sees, another misses (e.g., concealer sees secret; outsider sees fragmentation). | Hidden-types vary by analyst role; inside view vs outside view diverge; incentive-driven disagreement on what is "hidden" | Explicitly identify analyzers' roles and positions; run role-specific Hiddens scans; compare outputs; surface disagreement as a candidate hidden | Maintain role-tagged Hidden register; trigger Perspectives framework; design disclosure mechanisms that overcome asymmetry |
| **4. Paradigm Boundary Blindness** | Entire domains of hiddenness lie outside current analytic paradigm; the framework cannot see what it has no category for. | Ontological hiddens that the framework itself cannot name; paradigm-mismatch between framework and domain; ineffability that resists tabulation | Use Tier 3 full-spectrum Hiddens scan; invoke Targeted Scans (OG §7.5); monitor for persistent anomalies that cannot be classified; convene cross-paradigm review | Maintain a "Unknowns Unknown" register; schedule periodic paradigm audits; preserve option to extend framework to new v3.0 if warranted |

## 8.2 Tier 1 Hiddens Scan (Self-Referential) — Early Application

Apply this checklist early in a Hiddens analysis to audit the Hiddens work itself:

| Check | Question | If yes, then… | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scope creep** | Have we drifted from the focal decision/situation without explicitly re-scoping? | Re-anchor to the original problem statement. Note what expanded. | Record scope changes explicitly; track consequences for hidden inventory. |
| **Monolithic classification** | Have we forced all candidate hiddens into the 30 types, or missed domain-specific variants? | Spot-check with domain experts; cross-reference with analogous domains. | Allow domain subtypes; update mapping (§3.7); consider v3.0 extension. |
| **Analyst blind spot** | Are all analysts looking from the same role/position? | Explicitly invite counterparty or outside-view analyst. | Maintain role-tagged register; trigger Perspectives framework. |
| **Self-serving narrative** | Are we unconsciously filtering hiddens to support preferred conclusions? | Subject candidate hiddens to disconfirming tests; invite criticism. | Apply Prompt Discipline Rules (PD-01–PD-10, OG §D.3); tag all claims F/I/A/U. |
| **Legacy assumptions** | Are we inheriting hidden-type classifications from prior work without re-testing? | Spot-check top 3 high-consequence hiddens; run new diagnostic. | Treat all claims as provisional until re-confirmed; document reasoning shifts. |
| **Paradigm capture** | Have we adopted the current organisational paradigm as unquestionable? | Invite outside-view facilitator; convene cross-disciplinary review. | Schedule paradigm audit (Tier 3 if material stakes). |

| **Paradigm Audit** | Convene cross-domain/cross-paradigm review; ask: "What would be hidden to a (AI system / physicist / artist / opponent)?" | Paradigm-boundary assessment; Category VI inventory | §3.6 (Type 26–30) |

## 8.4 Tier 3 Escalation Triggers & Full-Spectrum Assessment

Escalate to Tier 3 if any apply:

- **High consequence and weak evidence:** decisions involve safety, regulation, existential risk; evidence is contested or sparse.
- **Adversarial context:** strategic concealment, active counter-detection, retaliation risk.
- **Persistent disagreement:** stakeholders hold incompatible hidden-type classifications; cannot converge.
- **Repeat failures:** interventions have failed despite prior Tier 1 scan; suggests misclassification or hidden cascade.
- **Suspected hidden cascade:** fixing one hidden plausibly creates others; requires full interaction modeling.
- **Residual unknowns could change accountability/remediation:** what remains hidden directly affects governance and decision authority.

**Tier 3 activities:**
1. Full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy review (all 30 types × all 5 dimensions).
2. Multi-method detection (at least 3 independent methods per hidden type represented).
3. Role and paradigm audits (compare across inside/outside and cross-paradigm analysis).
4. Cascading remediation sequencing (design interventions to avoid secondary hiddens).
5. Closure discipline per OG §7.4 (residual unknowns list, acceptability rationale, monitoring plan, ownership, learning hook).

## 9.2 Standard Deliverables (recommended)

### Minimum deliverable (Tier 1; 1–2 pages)
- **Summary table:** top 5–10 hiddens with type, meta-category, consequence, and one suggested probe/test per hidden.
- **One-page residual-unknowns log:** what remains hidden and why; what action is recommended.
- **Governance embedding:** cadence for re-scan (e.g., monthly, at decision gates, upon trigger breach).

### Full deliverable pack (Tier 2; 4–8 pages)
- **Hiddens Register:** all identified hiddens with meta-category, type, dimensional scores, epistemic status, owner, status.
- **Evidence Ledger:** key claims, supporting/contradicting evidence, quality, gaps, next probes.
- **Hypothesis/Test Backlog:** high-priority probes with expected discriminators and dependencies.
- **Information Requests:** missing inputs needed to reduce decision-critical unknowns.
- **Interaction & Cascade Map:** pathways showing how hiddens depend, trigger, mask, or reinforce.
- **Visibility Audit by Role:** what different stakeholders can and cannot see; asymmetry mapping.
- **Residual Unknowns + Closure Note:** per OG §7.4 (five required items).
- **Escalation Recommendation:** triggers for Tier 3 if present; governance change recommendations.

### Monitoring indicators and review cadence
- **Tier 1 re-scan:** Recommended after major decision, incident, or threshold breach; at minimum quarterly.
- **Interaction monitoring:** Track when one remediated hidden affects others; update cascade map.
- **Anomaly tracking:** Monitor persistent surprises (potential new hiddens or type misclassifications).
- **Indicator selection:** For each hidden, identify 1–2 leading indicators that would signal emergence or escalation (e.g., Latency → build leading indicators; Amnesia → knowledge-retention metrics).

# 10. Framework Principles (Mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. **Visibility Governance** | Hiddenness is not a data problem; it is a visibility-governance problem. Treat detection/remediation as changes to measurement conditions, incentives, access, and transparency structures—not only as information collection. |
| 2. **Dual-Axis Classification** | Classify each hidden by both mechanism (how it is hidden) and epistemic failure mode (how knowing fails). Both matter for detection and remediation selection. |
| 3. **Asymmetric Visibility** | What is hidden depends on position, role, time, and access. Maintain role-tagged registers. Do not collapse "unknown to me" into "hidden in the system." |
| 4. **Interaction-Aware** | Hiddens rarely occur in isolation. Map cascades, reinforcements, and transformations. Treat fixes as provisional until interaction effects are modelled. |
| 5. **Reflexivity** | The framework applies to itself. Audit Hiddens work for misclassification, analyst asymmetries, and paradigm boundaries. Build self-correction into governance. |
| 6. **Robustness Over Revelation** | When hiddens reflect ontological limits (Category VI), shift from detection to robustness-by-design, safety margins, and option creation. Do not over-promise to reveal what cannot be revealed. |
| 7. **Closure Discipline** | Do not prematurely close Hiddens analysis. Record residual unknowns, acceptability rationale, monitoring plan, ownership, and learning hooks (OG §7.4). Embed re-scan into governance rhythm. |

# 11. Glossary (Local Domain Terms; Mandatory)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary (framework-specific terms)
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Hiddenness** | The structural condition (produced by identifiable mechanisms) by which a reality-relevant factor is not available to an analyst/agent at the moment it matters, due to perceptual, systemic, informational, temporal, relational, or ontological limits. |
| **Hidden (noun)** | A specific instance of a reality-relevant factor whose presence, structure, or implications are not available due to hiding mechanisms; identified by type (1–30) and characterized by five dimensions. |
| **Meta-category** | A family of hiding mechanisms (Perceptual, Systemic, Informational, Temporal, Relational, Ontological); each contains 5 canonical types. |
| **Hiding mechanism** | The causal pathway by which visibility fails: Absence (gap), False Presence (spurious), Distortion (warping), Concealment (agency), or Boundary (ontological limits). |
| **Epistemic failure mode** | The characteristic way knowing tends to fail for a given hidden type (e.g., Known Unknowns, False Knowns, Strategic Ignorance, Paradigm Exclusion). |
| **Dimensional profile** | Characterization of a hidden by its five cross-cutting dimensions: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence. |
| **Detection interface (Type A)** | Methods, tests, and tools that can reveal or falsify a candidate hidden; examples: replication, audit, triangulation, red teaming. |
| **Remediation interface (Type B)** | Interventions that reduce, remove, or mitigate a hidden; examples: instrumentation, governance change, transparency, incentive redesign. |
| **Interaction interface (Type C)** | The pathways and dependencies through which hiddens connect, cascade, reinforce, mask, or resolve; modeled as a directed graph. |
| **Tier 1 scan** | Mandatory early-stage Hiddens audit: rapid 6-category visibility pass to spot candidate hiddens and preliminary type assignments; O(15–30) min. |
| **Tier 2 mapping** | Structured deepening for Investigative Run Mode: detailed dimensional profiling, interaction mapping, targeted scans, multi-method detection. Deployed for top hiddens or escalation scenarios. |
| **Tier 3 full-spectrum** | Full Hiddens taxonomy review with high rigor (multiple methods, role/paradigm audits, cascading analysis); deployed when escalation triggers apply or stakes are existential. |
| **Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan** | Mechanism-specific cross-framework scan (OG §7.5) invoked when Tier 1 symptoms point to specific hiding mechanism or stakeholder disagreement pattern; 13 scans across 4 families. |
| **Hidden register** | Running log of candidate hiddens, including type, meta-category, dimensional scores, epistemic status, owner, status (F/I/A/U), probes, remediation, interactions. |
| **Residual unknowns log** | Record of what remains hidden after Hiddens analysis, including reason, acceptability rationale, monitoring plan, owner, learning hook. |
| **Visibility audit by role** | Mapping of what different stakeholders/roles can and cannot see; identifies asymmetric visibility and detection opportunity gaps. |
