# Introduction

## What is Interests?

Interests are what agents fundamentally need, want, or care about beneath the positions they explicitly state, the demands they make, or the preferences they profess. An interest is an underlying substantive concern that persists across contexts and shapes behaviour even when stated positions shift. Where an agent says "We need stricter compliance rules," the underlying interest might be control, resource protection, liability reduction, professional legitimacy, or protection from rival departments. Where another says "We oppose regulation," the interest might be autonomy, cost minimisation, or status preservation.

The Framework of Interests operationalises the distinction between positions (what agents say they want) and interests (what they actually fundamentally need or want). This distinction is analytically critical because agents often conceal, misrepresent, or remain unconscious of their own true interests—whether through strategic calculation, social convention, self-deception, or genuine opacity about their own motivations. Interests lie beneath the surface of stated demands and explain persistent behaviour, coalition patterns, and the willingness or unwillingness to accept solutions that appear to address stated positions but leave underlying interests unsatisfied.

Interests are distinct from Incentives (which capture what agents are rewarded or punished for in transactional terms) and from Values (which capture what agents profess to care about normatively). An agent may have incentives to pursue behaviour A, profess to value behaviour B, but have a fundamental interest in outcome C—all three in tension. This framework maps the territory of C: what agents actually fundamentally care about.

## Why does Interests matter for Hiddens work?

Interests are primary hiding sites. Strategic concealment (Hidden-11) occurs when actors deliberately hide true interests behind plausible positions—a supplier claims cost pressures when the real interest is strategic market dominance. Interests-positions confusion (a core Hiddens generator) occurs when stated positions are systematically mistaken for interests: analysts recommend solutions addressing stated demands, unaware that the true underlying interest remains unaddressed, leaving actors dissatisfied despite apparent "win."

Conflicting interests within actors (Hidden-13) create another hiding site: an executive may have individual interests (promotion, autonomy, reputation) that conflict with organisational interests (efficiency, sustainability, risk reduction), producing observable contradiction and incoherence until the interest conflict is surfaced.

Organisational interest aggregation (Hidden-19) hides individual interests behind formal positions: frontline staff have fundamentally different interests from leadership, yet both speak in the name of "the organisation," creating systematic mismatch between what leadership believes is motivating frontline behaviour and what actually is.

Unconscious or latent interests (Hidden-6) exist when actors themselves are not fully aware of what fundamentally motivates them—they pursue security but cannot articulate why, or seek identity validation without recognising it. Positional blindness (Hidden-21) means actors in different structural positions face fundamentally different interest landscapes but assume others operate in their interest-frame.

Without this framework, these hiddens remain latent and invisible. With it, interest analysis becomes a disciplined probe for surfacing what agents actually fundamentally care about, what they are willing to sacrifice for, and what solutions would actually satisfy them—enabling negotiation, conflict resolution, and intervention design that addresses real rather than apparent motivations.

## What does this framework produce?

This framework operationalises interest analysis through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 interest types** organised into six meta-categories (material interests covering resources and security; procedural interests covering recognition and voice; identity interests covering belonging and coherence; relational interests covering status and positioning; temporal interests covering optionality and legacy; and institutional interests covering role security and organisational continuity) that recur across contexts and enable rapid initial classification.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (materiality level, consciousness/awareness, sustainability across contexts, scope and scale, transparency and admission) that profile any interest and explain why different actors prioritise the same interest type differently.

3. **Twelve dynamic patterns** (interest alignment and coalition formation, interest drift and goal shift, hidden interest revelation, interest conflict escalation, interest substitution and migration, interest amplification through framing, suppression and denial, interest cascade across levels, temporal interest shift, interest clustering and correlation, unconscious-to-conscious conversion, interest satiation and plateau effects) that describe how interests emerge, change, interact, and reveal themselves over time.

4. **Three interface types** (Detection Interfaces—how true interests become visible; Remediation Interfaces—how to address interests in intervention design; Interaction Interfaces—how interest conflicts are mediated and negotiated) that make visible where interest analysis can change outcomes.

5. **Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** (Tier 1 six-category visibility audit, Tier 2 structured deepening for interest-specific hiding mechanisms, Tier 3 full-spectrum escalation for contested or strategically concealed interests) that surface systematic sources of interest misreading and strategic concealment.

6. **A seven-step application protocol** that moves from interest mapping through meta-category scan, dimensional characterisation, dynamics analysis, interface analysis, Hiddens checking, conflict mapping, and redesign/negotiation implications.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework whenever actors' behaviour does not match their stated positions, negotiations stall despite apparent compromise offers, coalitions form or dissolve unexpectedly, solutions addressing stated demands are rejected, accountability assignments provoke resistance that appears disproportionate, or organisational incoherence suggests internal interest conflict.

The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch interest scan and type-mapping) and Investigative Run Mode (deep dimensional profiling, full Hiddens scanning, interest conflict analysis, coalition mapping, multi-level evidence synthesis). Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: assign interest types from stated positions and observed behaviour, profile key dimensions, spot obvious conflicts, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, interests are contested or hidden, strategic concealment is likely, multi-stakeholder coalitions are involved, or negotiation failure is costly.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario and the Operating Guide, producing a disciplined interest analysis with explicit identification of hidden interests, interest conflicts, F/I/A/U tagging, and bounded closure.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Interests are what agents fundamentally need, want, or care about beneath the positions they explicitly state. They are substantive concerns that persist across contexts and shape behaviour even when stated preferences shift, positions are abandoned, or negotiating tactics change. Interests answer the question: "What would have to be true about an outcome for this agent to genuinely accept it?"—whereas positions answer: "What is the agent currently claiming to want?"

The Framework of Interests provides a systematic way to infer, map, characterise, and address underlying motivations. Where the Framework of Incentives asks "What is the agent rewarded or punished for?" and the Framework of Values asks "What does the agent profess to care about normatively?", the Framework of Interests asks: "What does the agent actually fundamentally need or want, irrespective of what they say or what their immediate incentives are?"

The critical distinction is this: agents may pursue actions that are incentivised but contrary to their interests (moral hazard), may profess values they do not actually care about (performative commitment), and may state positions that conceal their true interests (strategic negotiating stance). Interests capture what agents would choose if they had full clarity and freedom—a counterfactual that enables diagnosis of why observed choices diverge from what we would expect if incentives and values were the only drivers.

Interests operate at three primary levels: material (security, resources, welfare, survival), procedural (recognition, voice, fairness, due process), and identity (belonging, dignity, coherence, self-respect). An agent might have a material interest in cost reduction, a procedural interest in being heard in decision-making, and an identity interest in remaining recognised as a professional—all three shaping behaviour, and all three potentially hidden or competing.

The primary output of this framework is an interest inventory across all six meta-categories; profiles of priority interests using five dimensions; identification of interest conflicts and coalition patterns; hidden interest register with tiered scans; detection and remediation interface analysis; and redesign options that address real interests rather than apparent positions.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Interests:** Fundamental needs, wants, or substantive concerns that agents care about at a level beneath stated positions, demands, or professed preferences. Interests are what agents would act to secure or defend if they had full clarity and freedom, and what must be addressed (or consciously sacrificed) for an agent to genuinely accept a solution or outcome.

**In scope:**
- Six interest meta-categories and thirty core interest types (material, procedural, identity, relational, temporal, institutional)
- Five cross-cutting dimensions for profiling and comparing interests
- Common interest dynamics (coalition formation, conflict escalation, drift, concealment and revelation)
- Three high-leverage interface types (Detection, Remediation, Interaction)
- Hiddens source analysis + tiered Hiddens cross-check (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3 escalation)
- Interest conflict analysis and coalition mapping
- Integration with Negotiation, Conflict, Interventions, and Governance frameworks

**Out of scope** (handled by adjacent frameworks):
- Full causal mechanism modelling (Causation; Systems)
- Full accountability allocation (Responsibility; Governance)
- Full uncertainty quantification (Uncertainties)
- Detailed negotiation strategy design (Negotiation; Conflict)
- Full intervention design (Interventions)

**Common confusions ("Interests is not…"):**
- Not the same as Incentives. Incentives are structured reward/punishment patterns; interests are what agents fundamentally care about beneath those structures.
- Not the same as Values. Values are what agents profess to care about normatively; interests are what they actually pursue when forced to choose.
- Not the same as Preferences. Preferences are revealed moment-to-moment choices; interests are more fundamental and persistent.
- Not the same as Emotions. Emotions are affective states; interests are substantive concerns that may or may not be accompanied by affect.
- Not motivation in the momentary sense. Interests persist; momentary motivation fluctuates.
- Not just "what people want." Interests include needs (what people require for functioning) and wants (what they desire for fuller life).

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions

### I. Material Interests
**Definition:** What agents fundamentally need or want in terms of physical resources, economic welfare, security from harm, and stability. These are the most basic substrate interests—what enables survival, functioning, and comfort. Material interests form the foundation upon which other interests are built.

**Diagnostic cues:** Agents consistently prioritise resource acquisition or protection; behaviour shifts sharply when resource availability changes; resistance to proposals that threaten material security; patterns of accumulation or protection behaviour; willingness to sacrifice other interests (time, relationships) for material gain; focus on tangible outcomes and measurable gains; anxiety or urgency in resource-scarce conditions

**Typical failure mode:** Material interests are assumed to be transparently stated when in fact they are concealed (resource hoarding, hidden costs, informal arrangements); material security is assumed to be solved when it is merely provisioned temporarily; material interests are dismissed as "just money" when they are actually substrate needs for functioning; inequality in material access is not connected to other interest conflicts; informal economic systems (side-deals, gifts, favours) hide true material interest patterns

### II. Procedural Interests
**Definition:** What agents fundamentally need or want in terms of voice in decisions affecting them, recognition as worthy of consultation, fair and transparent process, and accountability. Procedural interests are about being included in how things are decided and having recourse when decisions affect them. They are distinct from material interests (what is decided) and relational interests (status relative to others).

**Diagnostic cues:** Resistance to decisions despite accepting the substance (suggests procedural interest violation); willingness to accept less-favourable outcomes if consulted; emphasis on "process" and "fairness" language; satisfaction/dissatisfaction unrelated to outcome materiality; focus on being "heard" and "acknowledged"; resentment about exclusion from decisions affecting them; demands for transparent criteria and appeal mechanisms; emphasis on legitimacy and due process

**Typical failure mode:** Procedural interests are overlooked or called "political posturing"; consultation is done perfunctorily without genuine influence; stakeholders are informed of decisions already made; transparency is promised but not delivered; appeals mechanisms are unavailable or do not work; procedural fairness is assumed to be solution when substance is what actually matters; dominance of "efficient decision-making" over inclusive process; power imbalances mean some voices are genuinely heard and others are not

### III. Identity Interests
**Definition:** What agents fundamentally need or want in terms of self-respect, group belonging, professional standing, and self-narrative coherence. Identity interests are about maintaining or enhancing the sense of self and being recognised by others as worthy of respect. They encompass professional identity, cultural identity, moral coherence, and dignity in the eyes of important others.

**Diagnostic cues:** Resistance to outcomes or processes that imply criticism or lack of respect; alignment of choices with identity narratives ("that's not who we are"); investment in professional standing and peer recognition; anxiety about loss of status or belonging; willingness to sacrifice material or relational interests for dignity; sensitivity to language and framing that affects self-respect; alignment of resistance with identity-protective motivations; group identity activation in responses

**Typical failure mode:** Identity interests are dismissed as "soft" or non-rational; identity-based resistance is misread as obstruction rather than legitimate interest; proposals that solve material problems are rejected because they imply failure or lack of respect; scapegoating and blame-shifting emerge as identity-protective reactions; cultural or professional identity conflicts are not surfaced as root causes; framing that activates identity threats produces disproportionate resistance

### IV. Relational Interests
**Definition:** What agents fundamentally pursue in terms of status, social positioning, dominance, and relative advantage compared to reference groups or competitors. Relational interests are about where agents sit in status hierarchies and comparative position relative to peers. They can align with or diverge from material interests (wanting more relative advantage even when material needs are met) and identity interests (wanting recognition that one is superior or special).

**Diagnostic cues:** Sensitivity to comparisons with peers or competitors; focus on relative position and ranking; willingness to sacrifice absolute gain if it means gaining relative advantage; coalition choices that improve competitive positioning; sensitivity to loss of status or face; emphasis on competitive advantage or dominance; resistance to peer equality or parity arrangements; focus on who is "better" or "ahead"; investment in reputation and visibility

**Typical failure mode:** Relational interests are misread as narcissism or pathology when they are normal status-seeking; positional competition is not recognised as a driver of coalition formation; arrangements that are equal on material dimensions produce dissatisfaction because they do not improve relative position; status hierarchies are assumed to be fixed when they are actually contested; face-saving is dismissed as "saving face" rather than protecting legitimate relational interests

### V. Temporal Interests
**Definition:** What agents fundamentally preserve in terms of autonomy over future choices, optionality, flexibility, and avoiding lock-in. Temporal interests are about maintaining the freedom to adapt choices as circumstances change and avoiding irreversible commitments that foreclose alternatives. They reflect both the value of flexibility itself and the uncertainty about future needs and circumstances.

**Diagnostic cues:** Reluctance to commit to long-term arrangements despite accepting the proposed content; preference for reversible or renegotiable terms; retention of alternatives even at cost; resistance to "finality" language and permanent decisions; emphasis on optionality and flexibility; willingness to pay to preserve choices; anxiety about being locked-in; preference for time-limited trials or pilot arrangements; focus on early exit options or modification clauses

**Typical failure mode:** Temporal interests are misread as indecisiveness or lack of commitment; reversibility and optionality are removed from agreements to force commitment; lock-in is imposed as a control mechanism without understanding resistance; long-term contracts are assumed to be binding when agents experience them as temporary; flexibility needs are dismissed as "keeping options open to defect"; escalation paths and modification mechanisms are absent from governance

### VI. Institutional Interests
**Definition:** What agents fundamentally pursue in terms of their role or organisational security, institutional continuity, system preservation, and legitimacy. Institutional interests reflect that agents are embedded in roles and organisations that have persistence and legitimacy interests beyond any individual. These interests can align with or diverge from individual interests (an executive might sacrifice personal material interest for institutional legitimacy, or vice versa).

**Diagnostic cues:** Focus on "the organisation" or "the system" as protagonist; resistance framed in terms of institutional survival; alignment of choices with institutional protection; concern with legitimacy and formal approval; investment in structure, governance, and procedural formalisation; resistance to changes that threaten institutional continuity; emphasis on sustainability and long-term viability; concern with external legitimacy (regulatory, professional, social approval)

**Typical failure mode:** Institutional interests are assumed to be aligned with individual interests when they may conflict; organisational survival interests are treated as overriding when they are actually contested; formal institutional goals are assumed when actual priorities diverge; system legitimacy concerns are invisible (organisations pursue goals they cannot publicly justify); institutional persistence interests obscure need for change; scapegoating and sacrifice of individuals to preserve institutional legitimacy

# 4. Five Dimensions of Interests

The 30 core types can be further profiled and compared along five cross-cutting dimensions. Every interest can be located somewhere on each dimension, and these dimensions help explain why the same objective situation (e.g., a proposed process change) generates different interest responses across actors.

## 4.1 Five Dimensions Table

| Dimension | Range / Poles | What it captures | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A. Materiality Level** | Subsistence-based → Actualisational | How concrete/physical vs abstract/self-expressive is the interest? Is it about survival vs about realising potential? | Determines urgency, negotiability, and what kinds of compensation or trade-offs are possible. Subsistence interests are non-negotiable; actualisational interests are more flexible. |
| **B. Consciousness & Awareness** | Unconscious/latent → Explicitly stated | How aware is the agent of this interest themselves? Is it something they can articulate and defend, or something they pursue without full awareness? | Determines whether interest can be addressed through negotiation vs must be inferred and surfaced. Unconscious interests generate contradiction and resistance that appear irrational. |
| **C. Sustainability Across Contexts** | Volatile/situational → Fundamental/persistent | How stable is this interest across different contexts and time? Does it emerge only in specific situations or does it persist across most situations? | Determines whether interest is stable basis for prediction vs situational. Stable interests can anchor agreements; volatile interests require flexibility and monitoring. |
| **D. Scope & Scale** | Individual → Collective/institutional | Is this interest pursued by individuals or aggregated at group/organisational level? | Determines what unit must be satisfied (individual vs group) and whether satisfaction of individual interests aggregates to collective interest satisfaction. |
| **E. Transparency & Admission** | Privately concealed → Publicly stated & defended | How openly does the agent acknowledge and defend this interest in public discourse? | Determines whether interest is strategically hidden vs openly bargainable. Openly stated interests can be negotiated; concealed interests generate surprise and distrust. |

## 4.2 Dimensional Profiling Examples

**Example 1:** Income (Material Interest 1.2)
- Materiality: Subsistence-to-actualisational (depends on income level; survival-level income is subsistence; discretionary income is actualisational)
- Consciousness: Explicit (most agents are very aware they have income interests)
- Sustainability: Fundamental/persistent (income interests persist across virtually all contexts)
- Scope: Individual aggregated to household and organisational level
- Transparency: Often publicly stated, though exact figures may be concealed; relativised (concern with "fair" income relative to peers is often hidden)

**Example 2:** Professional Identity & Competence (Identity Interest 3.1)
- Materiality: Actualisational (about self-expression and realisation, not subsistence)
- Consciousness: Mixed (agents know they value professional standing but may not fully understand how much it affects their choices and resistance)
- Sustainability: Fundamental/persistent (career interests persist throughout working life)
- Scope: Individual (though connected to professional community reputation)
- Transparency: Often publicly stated but can be concealed if implications are identity-threatening

**Example 3:** Optionality & Future Flexibility (Temporal Interest 5.1)
- Materiality: Actualisational (preserving freedom and future possibilities)
- Consciousness: Often latent (agents may not articulate "I need optionality" but show it through behaviour)
- Sustainability: Moderate (increases under uncertainty; decreases when path is clear)
- Scope: Individual
- Transparency: Often concealed (agents may reluctant to say "I want to keep my options open to leave")

# 5. Cross-Cutting Interest Dimensions

This section extends the dimensional analysis by examining five key themes that cut across multiple interest types and meta-categories.

## 5.1 Dimension A: Interest Concealment vs Revelation Patterns

Some interests are openly stated (procedural interests in voice and consultation are often explicitly demanded). Others are strategically concealed (relational interests in dominance or status are often hidden behind claims of efficiency or fairness). Still others are unconsciously pursued (agents may have strong identity interests but not articulate them as such).

**Interest Concealment Patterns:**
- Strategic concealment (agent deliberately hides interest to negotiate advantage)
- Social concealment (interest is culturally legitimate but not publicly spoken; "one doesn't talk about money" or "image management")
- Self-deception (agent pursues interest without admitting it to themselves, rationalising through other language)
- Structural concealment (system or hierarchy makes it illegitimate to state certain interests; "you can't say that here")

**Revelation Patterns:**
- Crisis revelation (interests become explicit only when threatened)
- Coalition revelation (interests emerge publicly when group stakes are raised)
- Resistance revelation (observed refusal patterns reveal interests not stated)
- Comparative revelation (interests emerge in contrast to others' stated positions)

## 5.2 Dimension B: Interest Conflict Within Actors (Internal Multiplexing)

Most agents have multiple interests, and these interests frequently conflict. An executive may have material interests in cost reduction, identity interests in being seen as humane, and relational interests in dominance. A frontline worker may have material interests in income stability, procedural interests in voice, and temporal interests in autonomy. When interests conflict, agents face real dilemmas rather than straightforward choices.

**Types of Internal Conflicts:**
- Material vs Identity (cost reduction conflicts with professional pride; income maximisation conflicts with dignity)
- Procedural vs Material (voice in decisions conflicts with speed of decision-making; fair process takes time and resources)
- Identity vs Temporal (maintaining group belonging may require locked-in commitment; exercising autonomy may require separating from group)
- Relational vs Institutional (individual status advancement may threaten institutional coherence; individual dominance may undermine organisational collaboration)

**Strategic Responses to Internal Conflict:**
- Prioritisation (one interest is explicitly prioritised when conflict forces choice)
- Sequential satisfaction (satisfy interests in sequence rather than simultaneously)
- Reframing (redefine interests so they appear compatible rather than conflicting)
- Delegation (assign internal conflict to different agents or levels; "I want X but the organisation requires Y")

## 5.3 Dimension C: Interest Satisfaction Scaling and Threshold Effects

Not all interests operate on a linear scale. Some interests have threshold effects: below the threshold, the interest is highly salient and shapes behaviour powerfully; above the threshold, further satisfaction produces diminishing returns. Other interests show satiation: once satisfied to a sufficient level, additional satisfaction has declining value.

**Threshold Effects:**
- Material security: has a sharp threshold (subsistence level); below it dominates all behaviour; above it, further wealth produces diminishing satisfaction effects
- Voice and consultation: has threshold (being completely excluded is intolerable; token inclusion may be seen as insufficient; genuine influence may be threshold)
- Status and hierarchy position: has relative threshold (being significantly lower-status than peers is highly salient; minor differences matter less)

**Satiation Effects:**
- Material wealth beyond security level: diminishing returns on satisfaction
- Status when already high: further status improvement produces smaller satisfaction gains
- Optionality: beyond certain point, additional options produce little benefit (paradox of choice)

**Interaction Effects:**
- As material security increases, procedural and identity interests become more salient
- As status increases, temporal interests (optionality, flexibility) sometimes increase (ability to afford variability)
- Satisfaction of some interests (inclusion, recognition) can increase or decrease other interests (autonomy—recognition can come with expectations)

## 5.4 Dimension D: Interest Alignment and Coalition Patterns

Interests cluster and align in complex ways. Agents with aligned interests form coalitions and coordinate. Agents with cross-cutting interests face dilemmas about coalition choice. Understanding coalition patterns requires mapping not just individual interests but also where interests align and diverge.

**Coalition Alignment Patterns:**
- Identity-based coalitions (group belonging interests align frontline workers with each other despite different material interests)
- Material-based coalitions (resource and cost interests align competing departments against others)
- Procedural-based coalitions (voice and recognition interests align subordinate levels against hierarchical authority)
- Power-based coalitions (agents with strong relational interests in dominance align with agents with weak procedural interests)

**Cross-Cutting Interest Conflicts:**
- Material vs procedural: cost reduction interests conflict with voice and fairness interests
- Identity vs relational: group belonging interests conflict with status differentiation interests
- Individual vs institutional: individual material interests conflict with institutional survival interests
- Temporal vs institutional: autonomy and flexibility interests conflict with role security and system preservation

## 5.5 Dimension E: Interest Dynamics Across Time

Interests are not static. They emerge, persist, decline, are hidden and revealed, and interact with other interests and external events. Understanding interest dynamics is critical for predicting coalitions, identifying escalation risks, and designing interventions that address interests rather than just momentary positions.

**Types of Interest Change:**
- Interest emergence (latent interests become active when triggered by context)
- Interest intensification (interests grow more important as circumstances change)
- Interest suppression (interests are pushed to background by urgent competing demands)
- Interest displacement (one interest is replaced by another as priorities shift)
- Interest cycling (interests reappear periodically as situations recur)

**Patterns of Interest Activation:**
- Threat activation (interests become highly salient when threatened; security interests activate when threatened with job loss)
- Cascade activation (satisfaction or frustration of one interest activates related interests)
- Coalition activation (interests become more salient when coalition forms around them)
- Identity activation (interests become salient when associated identity is activated)

# 6. Interest Dynamics Patterns

This section maps twelve dynamic patterns that describe how interests emerge, interact, evolve, and reveal themselves over time.

## 6.1 Twelve Core Dynamics

| # | Pattern name | Description | Observable signature | Intervention implications |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Interest Alignment & Coalition Formation | Agents with aligned interests coordinate and coalesce; coalitions form around clusters of shared interests | Rapid coalition formation; public alignment statements; coordinated action by previously separate actors | Identify underlying interest alignment; make visible what is driving coalition; address shared interests to break or stabilise coalition |
| 2 | Interest Conflict Escalation | As interests collide, escalation occurs through commitment, group mobilisation, and hardening of positions | Positions harden; actors bring in allies; public statements become more extreme; willingness to compromise declines | Address underlying interest conflicts rather than position conflicts; create space for interests to be negotiated rather than positions fought over |
| 3 | Hidden Interest Revelation | Concealed interests become visible when actors are under pressure, fail to achieve stated goals, or face situations that activate hidden interests | Behaviour shifts when incentives change; resistance appears disproportionate to stated stakes; contradictions in what actors say and do | Run interest-first analysis when behaviour is opaque; don't assume stated positions are actual interests; probe for hidden interests driving resistance |
| 4 | Interest Drift & Goal Shift | Interests shift subtly over time as contexts change; what was primary interest becomes secondary as other interests activate | Actor's priorities are not what they were previously; resistance that was absent now appears; stated goals shift without explanation | Monitor for interest drift; don't assume interests are static; escalate governance and reviews when drift is detected; adapt targets and expectations |
| 5 | Interest Substitution & Migration | When one interest is blocked or thwarted, agents pursue alternative interests; energy transfers from blocked path to alternative path | Resistance to specific solution is replaced by support when path opens elsewhere; energy that was in one domain appears in another | Understand full range of interests; don't assume only one path to interest satisfaction; be alert to where substituted interests might cause unintended consequences |
| 6 | Interest Amplification Through Framing | How interests are framed (as threat vs opportunity, as individual vs collective, as moral vs practical) shapes how salient and urgent they become | Same underlying situation generates different urgency depending on how it is framed; threat-framing amplifies urgency; collective framing amplifies alignment | Pay attention to framing; understand that interest amplification through framing is real and powerful; use framing consciously in design rather than allowing it to be imposed |
| 7 | Interest Suppression & Denial | Actors may suppress or deny interests that conflict with self-image or group identity; interests become invisible when admission would be identity-threatening | Behaviour that reveals interests is accompanied by denial; rationalisation; narrative reframing; public explanation inconsistent with observed choices | Don't challenge explicit denial; instead work with observed interests and allow actors to maintain face; address interests while allowing denial of their importance |
| 8 | Interest Cascade Across Levels | Interest satisfaction or frustration at one level cascades to create demands at other levels; individual interest frustration can activate institutional interest in representation | Individual interests in fairness cascade to demands for procedural legitimacy; institutional interests in role security cascade to individual job security concerns | Track cascades across levels; don't assume level-specific analysis is sufficient; design interventions that address cascade points and allow resolution across levels |
| 9 | Temporal Interest Shift | As time horizons extend, interest priorities shift; immediate interests (security) give way to longer-term interests (optionality, legacy); crisis mode shifts priorities | Under crisis, subsistence and immediate security interests dominate; as stability increases, higher-order interests (identity, learning) become salient | Understand where actors are in temporal space; crisis response is different from steady-state; help move from crisis to stable-state interest activation |
| 10 | Interest Clustering & Correlation | Some interests tend to cluster together; certain interests activate related interests; interests have correlation structures | Identity interests and procedural interests often cluster (those with identity stakes want voice); relational interests and institutional interests can align | Map interest clusters; don't treat interests as independent; understand correlation structures in your context |
| 11 | Unconscious-to-Conscious Interest Conversion | Latent, unconscious interests become conscious when surfaced through dialogue and are then explicitly acknowledged and negotiated | Resistance that was attributed to obstruction is reframed when underlying interest is named and acknowledged; actors then explicitly affirm and defend interest | Create space for interests to be named and acknowledged; don't force consciousness, but enable it; conscious interests are more negotiable than unconscious ones |
| 12 | Interest Satiation & Plateau Effects | As interests are satisfied, additional satisfaction produces diminishing returns; interest can become latent if sufficiently satisfied; can re-activate if threatened | Interest dominates attention when pressing; recedes to background when satisfied; reactivates when satisfaction is threatened; new interests become salient as others plateau | Understand satiation patterns; don't assume constant interest intensity; design systems that detect interest activation and respond accordingly |

# 7. Interface Types: Detection, Remediation, and Interaction

The Framework of Interests identifies three primary interface types where analysis can drive change: Detection Interfaces (how true interests become visible), Remediation Interfaces (how interventions address interests), and Interaction Interfaces (how interest conflicts are negotiated and mediated).

## 7.1 Interface Type A: Detection Interfaces

How do hidden interests become visible? The following interfaces are where true interests emerge from concealment and become available for analysis and negotiation.

| Interface | Purpose | Observable patterns | Engagement approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Behaviour-first analysis** | Interests reveal themselves through what actors actually do when forced to choose, not what they say | Revealed preferences; patterns of sacrifice; where actors protect interests; coalition choices; persistence despite stated goals | Analyse choices and sacrifices, not rhetoric; identify what actors defend regardless of cost; track behaviour across different domains and times |
| **Resistance & refusal patterns** | When proposed solutions are rejected, the rejection reveals what interests remain unaddressed despite apparent problem-solving | Disproportionate resistance; resistance that persists despite compromise; refusal of solutions addressing stated demands | Don't treat resistance as obstruction; analyse what interests are implied by what is refused; ask "what would have to be true for this solution to be acceptable?" |
| **Coalition formation & dissolution** | Who coalesces with whom reveals aligned interests; who breaks coalition reveals cross-cutting interests and shifting priorities | Surprising coalitions; unexpected conflicts within groups; shifts in coalition membership; alignment across normally separate actors | Track coalitions as signals of aligned interests; understand coalition fragility as sign of cross-cutting interests; use coalition shifts as evidence of interest change |
| **Stated exceptions & qualifications** | What actors will accept or reject (stated exceptions to general opposition, qualifications on demands) reveals the actual interest beneath the position | "We oppose change, except in X domain"; "We need cost reduction, but not if it affects Y"; qualifications that reveal where interests are flexible | Listen carefully to exceptions; they reveal true interests more reliably than general statements; ask about exceptions and their rationale |
| **Multi-level contradiction & disagreement** | What different levels say about priorities and what matters reveals where interests differ across hierarchy | Frontline says X, leadership says Y; different departments have different accounts of what is important; contradiction about priorities | Don't treat contradiction as error; treat it as evidence of different interest configurations across levels; make explicit that levels have different interests |
| **Crisis & pressure situations** | When actors are under real pressure to choose, interests reveal themselves because there is no option to hide or negotiate | Under crisis, true interests emerge; actors reveal what they actually prioritise when forced to choose; behaviour under pressure differs from behaviour when relaxed | Use pressure situations carefully (not to create unnecessary crisis); pay attention to what actors prioritise when constraints are real; understand this as signal of true interest |
| **Irreversible commitment patterns** | What actors will lock into vs what they insist remain flexible reveals interests in autonomy, optionality, and risk | Willingness to commit permanently vs insistence on reversibility, pilots, trial periods; what is locked in and what is negotiated | Listen carefully to reversibility requests; they signal temporal interests and autonomy interests; respect them as signals of legitimate concerns |

## 7.2 Interface Type B: Remediation Interfaces

How can interventions be designed to address interests rather than just positions? The following interfaces are where interest analysis drives intervention design.

| Interface | Purpose | Application approach | Design questions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Interest-aware negotiation design** | Negotiations that explicitly address underlying interests rather than positions; problem-solving around interests rather than positional bargaining | Frame negotiation explicitly as "let's understand what you each fundamentally need and see if we can find solutions that address all those needs" | What interests need to be satisfied for each party to genuinely accept outcome? Can we find solutions addressing all interests, or must we prioritise and compensate? |
| **Multi-interest solution design** | Design interventions that acknowledge and satisfy multiple interests simultaneously, not just the primary one (security) | When designing governance, process, or system changes, explicitly map whether solution addresses all relevant interests or only some; compensate where interests are sacrificed | What interests does this design address? What interests does it frustrate? What would addressing frustrated interests require? What trade-offs are we making? |
| **Interest-based coalition building** | Rather than fighting coalitions, understand aligned interests and build counter-coalitions or negotiated solutions addressing multiple interest clusters | Understand why coalition formed; acknowledge interests driving it; offer alternative paths to interest satisfaction that break coalition alignment while addressing interests | What interests drive this coalition? Are there ways to satisfy those interests that don't require the coalition structure? Can we address multiple interests in ways that make coalition less necessary? |
| **Procedural legitimacy & voice** | Design processes that satisfy procedural interests in voice, consultation, recognition, and transparency, making outcomes more acceptable even if material outcomes are unchanged | Invest in meaningful consultation, transparent criteria, appeal mechanisms, and recognition of contributions; these address interests not addressed by content outcomes | Are stakeholders genuinely consulted or is this theatre? Are criteria transparent? Are appeals possible? Is contribution recognised? Do actors feel they had genuine voice? |
| **Identity-protective framing** | Frame solutions in ways that protect rather than threaten identity interests, allowing actors to accept solutions without loss of face or professional identity | Language, framing, and narrative matter; solutions framed as "this protects professional judgment" will be accepted where the same solution framed as "you couldn't be trusted to decide" will be resisted | Can this solution be framed in identity-consistent language? Can the actor's role be protected and respected in this transition? What narrative allows acceptance without loss of face? |
| **Interest-accommodating governance** | Design governance systems that acknowledge and accommodate multiple interests rather than suppressing them; create space for interests to be articulated and addressed | Build in mechanisms for interest to be heard (voice), assessed fairly (procedural), and addressed (remediation); expect that interests will emerge and have paths for them to be addressed | What governance structure allows interests to be articulated? What mechanisms allow interests to be addressed before they escalate to conflict? How do we handle interest conflicts when they arise? |
| **Temporal flexibility & optionality** | Where temporal interests in optionality and autonomy are strong, design reversibility, renegotiation clauses, exit options, and pilot/trial arrangements rather than permanent lock-in | Rather than fighting for permanent commitments, offer time-limited pilots, renegotiation points, and exit options; this satisfies temporal interests while preserving ability to evolve | Can this be structured as a pilot or trial? Are there renegotiation points and modification mechanisms? Are there realistic exit options if things change? How flexible can we be about implementation pace? |

## 7.3 Interface Type C: Interaction Interfaces

How are interest conflicts mediated and negotiated? The following interfaces are where interest-based conflicts are engaged and resolved.

| Interface | Purpose | Application approach | Interaction questions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Interest articulation & naming** | Create safe space for interests to be articulated and named without judgment; naming interests explicitly changes negotiation dynamic from positional to interest-based | Rather than debate positions, ask actors what they fundamentally need; create norms where interests can be stated without shame; acknowledge legitimate interests even if you disagree with solutions | Can you tell me what you fundamentally need from this situation? What would have to be true about the outcome for you to genuinely accept it? What are you worried about losing? |
| **Interest legitimacy assessment** | Assess whether interests are legitimate (needs/wants that have merit) vs illegitimate (wants based on harm to others); acknowledge legitimate interests while refusing illegitimate ones | Acknowledge that material security interests, identity interests, and voice interests are legitimate; refuse interests based on dominating others or harming third parties | Is this an interest in something for yourself, or is it an interest in preventing others from having something? Is this a fundamental need or a preference for relative advantage? |
| **Interest trade-off dialogue** | When interests genuinely conflict, engage actors in explicit dialogue about what they would trade off and what they would not; map trade-off space | Instead of pretending conflicts don't exist, acknowledge them explicitly; explore whether some interests can be sacrificed, sequenced, or partially satisfied; map the space of acceptable compromise | What are you willing to sacrifice to get your core interest satisfied? What would you not compromise on? Are there interests we can address in sequence rather than simultaneously? Can we satisfy some interests partially? |
| **Interest-based incentive design** | Rather than designing incentives divorced from interests, design incentive structures that reward behaviours consistent with genuine interest satisfaction | Incentives that force trade-offs against genuine interests (e.g., incentivising speed when autonomy interests are strong) create perverse results; design incentives that are interest-aligned | What incentive structures would be consistent with the actual interests we're trying to satisfy? Are there ways to reward the behaviour we want that don't require agents to sacrifice core interests? |
| **Multi-level interest integration** | When interests differ across levels, create mechanisms for lower-level interests to be aggregated and represented at higher levels; prevent suppression of interests through hierarchy | Frontline interests in autonomy and voice need mechanisms to reach decision-makers; individual interests in fairness need aggregation to organisational level; ensure voices from all levels are heard | What mechanisms allow frontline interests to reach leadership? How do individual interests aggregate to organisational interests? Are lower-level interests being suppressed through hierarchy? |
| **Interest conflict escalation paths** | Design escalation mechanisms where interest conflicts can be addressed at appropriate level; prevent escalation to conflict where negotiation is possible | Create forums where interest conflicts can be discussed and negotiated before they become positional disputes; escalate only when negotiation is not possible | Can this interest conflict be addressed through dialogue? What forum would be appropriate for negotiating this conflict? When should it escalate to senior leadership? |
| **Coalition-aware negotiation** | Recognise that negotiation is often multi-party with aligned interests forming coalitions; design negotiation processes that allow coalitions to be formed, understood, and negotiated with | Don't treat coalitions as obstacles; understand aligned interests; negotiate with coalition while acknowledging both individual and collective interests | Who are the natural coalition partners around this set of interests? Can we negotiate with coalitions as units? How do we address both collective and individual interests? |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Hiddens Cross-Check

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

The Framework of Interests is specifically designed to surface hidden interests. The following are the primary mechanisms through which interests remain hidden and how analysis can overcome them.

| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Interests-Positions Confusion | Analysis treats stated positions as actual interests; solutions address stated demands while true interests remain unaddressed; negotiation fails because underlying interests are not surfaced | Explicitly distinguish positions from interests; assume stated positions are negotiating stances, not interests; infer interests from behaviour, resistance, and what actors protect regardless of cost |
| 2 | Strategic Concealment | Actors deliberately hide true interests behind plausible positions to negotiate advantage; intelligence about true interests gives negotiating edge to those who know it | Use multi-level detection interfaces; analyse behaviour and resistance, not just statements; interview multiple levels and observe coalitions; assume strategic misrepresentation in high-stakes contexts |
| 3 | Interests-Incentives Conflation | Attributes behaviour to incentive structures when actual driver is underlying interests that conflict with incentives; incentive change produces unexpected resistance because interests are not addressed | Analyse interests separately from incentive structures; when observed behaviour conflicts with incentive predictions, probe for interest conflicts; run interest analysis in parallel with incentive analysis |
| 4 | Interests-Values Conflation | Attributes behaviour to stated values when actual driver is unstated interests; actors may profess values they don't prioritise when forced to choose, while pursuing true interests | Analyse interests separately from value statements; when observed behaviour conflicts with professed values, probe for interest conflicts; use behaviour-first analysis to identify true priorities |
| 5 | Unconscious/Latent Interests | Actors themselves are not fully aware of what fundamentally motivates them; interests are pursued without explicit acknowledgment; observers may mistake latent interests for irrational behaviour | Create safe spaces for interests to be articulated and surfaced; ask probing questions ("what are you worried about?", "what would have to be true for you to accept this?"); use behaviour analysis to infer latent interests |
| 6 | Organisational Interest Aggregation Opacity | Individual actors speak in name of "the organisation" while pursuing individual interests; organisational position is aggregate of conflicting individual interests, creating incoherence | Separate individual interests from organisational interests; understand that "the organisation says" masks conflicting individual interests; interview across levels to understand individual interest diversity |
| 7 | Conflicting Interests Within Actors | Same actor has multiple interests that conflict; when asked about "their interest," actors give one answer, but behaviour reveals multiple competing interests; internal conflict creates contradiction | Assume actors have multiple interests and that these interests conflict; ask about all relevant interest domains (material, procedural, identity, relational, temporal, institutional) explicitly; understand internal conflict as normal |
| 8 | Positional Blindness & Different Interest Landscapes | Actors in different structural positions face fundamentally different interest landscapes but assume others operate in their own interest frame; misattribution of motivations to others | Map interest landscapes by role/position; understand that different roles face different interests; don't assume uniform interest experience; investigate how interests differ across hierarchy and domains |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)

| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| I  Perceptual | Interests are invisible to decision-makers or analysts because they are outside typical attention frame or are not salient | Behaviour appears irrational or contradictory; resistance appears disproportionate; actors focus on issues that seem minor; stakeholders express surprise at what matters to actors | Map actor interest landscapes explicitly; interview across roles; ask "what are the constraints and pressures you face?" and "what matters most to you here?"; analyse behaviour for revealed interests |
| II Systemic | Interest conflicts are embedded in structure but treated as individual problems or intractable differences | Persistent conflict despite mediation attempts; coalitions form and dissolve in predictable patterns; same conflicts recur in different domains | Map interest distributions across the system; identify where interests are systematically opposed; understand structural interest conflicts vs individual personality clashes |
| III Informational | Interests are hidden because key information about stakes or constraints is not circulated; incomplete knowledge leads to misunderstanding of what is actually driving behaviour | Surprise at actor responses; actors appear to know things not shared; apparent inconsistency in positions reveals missing information | Request information from actors about what they understand about stakes and constraints; map information asymmetries; identify where missing information is driving interest misunderstanding |
| IV Temporal | Interest shifts over time are not tracked; interests that were primary become secondary or vice versa; timing of interest activation is not understood | Resistance that was absent now appears; priorities that were stated are abandoned; new coalitions form unexpectedly; behaviour changes without apparent cause | Track interests over time; ask about how priorities have shifted; understand whether new interests are activated or old interests are satiated; monitor for interest drift |
| V  Relational | Interests are hidden because stating them would be socially inappropriate or would violate group norms or hierarchy; actors pursue interests covertly through subtle means | Status interests are pursued but not stated; coalition building happens behind the scenes; preferences are inferred through body language and subtle cues rather than stated | Create norms where different interest types can be acknowledged; use anonymous formats where cultural norms prevent public statement; allow actors to pursue interests through socially acceptable channels |
| VI Ontological | Interests are hidden because the concepts or language for articulating them are not available; interests exist but there is no framework for naming or discussing them | Actors express frustration about inability to articulate concerns; behaviour reveals interests but actors cannot name them; analyst assumes interests don't exist because they are not stated | Introduce language for naming interests explicitly; help actors articulate what they care about; use probing questions to surface interests that exist but are unnamed; normalise discussion of different interest types |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

1) Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (early pass; assume Hiddens exist) (OG §7.1). Specifically: check whether interests are perceptually invisible, systemically embedded, informationally hidden, temporally unstable, relationally suppressed, or ontologically unnamed.

2) Shortlist candidate Hiddens; for each, rate: mechanism (what is hiding this interest?), detectability (how visible is it?), agency (intentional or structural?), consequence (how much does this hidden interest matter?). Focus on high-consequence hidden interests.

3) Assign at least one Detection Interface and one Remediation Interface per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (OG §7.2). Example: if interest is strategically concealed (mechanism), use behaviour-first analysis (detection interface) and interest articulation dialogue (remediation interface).

4) Run the hiddens source analysis (§8.1) and record which mechanisms are likely active; what this reveals about how interests are being hidden in this scenario.

5) If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism (strategic concealment, organisational aggregation, positional blindness), invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3).

6) After executing the framework protocol (interest mapping, dimensional analysis, dynamics, interfaces) and any available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas. Have previously hidden interests been surfaced? Have new Hiddens been discovered?

7) Produce Residual Unknowns (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, conflict resolution, or intervention priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:

- **High consequence:** Negotiation failure is costly; interests in play could determine coalition success or failure; wrong analysis of interests could lead to wrong intervention design
- **Adversarial context / strategic concealment likely:** Multi-party negotiation where parties have incentive to misrepresent; competitive context where interests must be protected; history of deception or strategic gaming
- **Persistent contradiction:** Stated positions conflict with observed behaviour; actors' accounts of their own interests are internally inconsistent; different observers report different interest maps
- **Repeat failures:** Prior negotiation attempts failed; prior interest-based analyses did not lead to sustainable agreement; same conflicts keep re-emerging
- **Organisational interest aggregation complexity:** Multi-level organisation where individual interests clearly differ from organisational statements; significant inter-level disagreement about what matters
- **Interest mapping creates surprise:** Analysis reveals interests that were invisible to decision-makers; actors express surprise at what others care about; understanding of motivations shifts dramatically as interests are surfaced

Tier 3 execution (per OG §D.6.10):

- Expands beyond mechanical interest inventory into deep interest dialogue and testing; requires sustained engagement with actors
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance (direct statement from actors, not inference); explicit competing hypotheses (multiple possible interest maps); rigorous disconfirming tests (what would prove this interest wrong?)
- When adversarial context: adds concealment vectors (how is this interest being hidden and why?) and independent corroboration paths (do other evidence streams support this interest map?)
- May justify extended engagement (multiple dialogue cycles) when critical interests are in play

## 9.2 Standard Deliverables (recommended)

**Minimum deliverable (1–2 pages):**
- Actor map with role-level interest assignment (which interests matter most to which roles)
- Interest conflict matrix (where interests fundamentally conflict)
- Top 3–5 hidden interests and how they affect outcomes
- One key implication for intervention or governance design

**Full deliverable pack:**
- Complete 30-type interest inventory with confidence levels and evidence basis for each assignment
- Five-dimensional profiles for priority interests (high-consequence, high-conflict, or highly hidden)
- Twelve dynamics assessment (which patterns are active; what evidence supports each)
- Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens analysis with detection/remediation interfaces mapped
- Interest conflict matrix with coalition implications
- Three interface analyses (Detection, Remediation, Interaction) with worked examples
- Interest-based negotiation or governance design recommendations
- Residual unknowns register with escalation assessment
- Timeline of interest evolution and monitoring plan

**Monitoring indicators and review cadence:**
- Interest activation signals (what would indicate that an interest has become more salient?)
- Coalition stability signals (what would indicate that aligned interests are fragmenting?)
- Interest drift signals (what would indicate that priorities have shifted?)
- Satisfaction assessment (are interests actually being addressed by implemented solutions?)

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table

| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. **Interests-First Analysis** | Start with interests, not positions. Do not assume stated positions are actual interests. Infer interests from behaviour, choices, and what actors protect regardless of cost. Let positions be tentative until interests are understood. |
| 2. **Multiplicity & Internal Conflict** | Assume actors have multiple interests across all six meta-categories and that these interests frequently conflict. Expect internal contradiction. Do not try to reduce to a single "true" interest; acknowledge legitimate pluralism. |
| 3. **Hidden Interests Are Normal** | Expect that significant interests are hidden (strategically concealed, unconscious, suppressed by hierarchy or norms, or simply not articulated). Invest in surfacing hidden interests rather than assuming visibility. Treat incomplete interest maps as baseline assumption. |
| 4. **Behaviour Over Rhetoric** | When behaviour and statements diverge, trust behaviour over statements. Revealed preferences show actual interests; verbal commitments show intended identity. Analyse what actors actually do when forced to choose. |
| 5. **Legitimacy & Acknowledgment** | Acknowledge that actors have legitimate interests across all domains (material, procedural, identity, relational, temporal, institutional). You may disagree about how to address interests, but refuse the stance that interests themselves are irrational or unworthy of negotiation. Acknowledgment does not mean agreement; it means taking interests seriously. |
| 6. **Interest Accommodation Not Elimination** | The goal is not to eliminate interests (impossible) but to design solutions that accommodate or explicitly trade off interests with awareness and compensation. Interests that are suppressed resurface; interests that are addressed can be negotiated. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Interest** | What an agent fundamentally needs, wants, or cares about beneath stated positions. A substantive concern that persists and shapes behaviour even as positions change. More foundational than momentary preference or affect. |
| **Position** | What an agent explicitly claims to want; stated demand or objective. Positions are often negotiating stances that conceal or reframe underlying interests. |
| **Revealed Preferences** | What agents actually choose when faced with constraints and trade-offs; inferred interests from observed behaviour. More reliable indicator of true interests than verbal statements. |
| **Interest Conflict** | Situation where two or more interests are genuinely incompatible (satisfying one requires unsatisfying the other). Different from positional conflict, which may mask compatible underlying interests. |
| **Hidden Interest** | Interest that is not explicitly stated or observable; concealed through strategy, suppressed through hierarchy/norms, unconscious, or unnamed. Present as driver of behaviour but not visible to analysis. |
| **Interest Alignment** | Situation where two or more actors' interests are sufficiently compatible that they can coordinate or form coalitions. Basis of coalition stability. |
| **Strategic Concealment** | Deliberate hiding of true interests behind plausible positions to negotiate advantage. Assumes misrepresentation gives negotiating edge. |
| **Unconscious Interest** | Interest pursued without explicit awareness; actor cannot articulate it but behaviour reveals it. Often becomes conscious through dialogue and questioning. |
| **Procedural Interest** | Interest in fair process, voice, recognition, and transparency independent of outcome content. Often neglected in favour of material outcome focus. |
| **Identity Interest** | Interest in self-respect, belonging, professional standing, and self-narrative coherence. Can override material interests when threatened. |
| **Material Interest** | Interest in resources, economic welfare, security, and survival-level provisions. Foundation for other interests. |
| **Relational Interest** | Interest in status, social positioning, comparative advantage, and face in social interactions. Often hidden but powerful driver of coalition and conflict. |
| **Temporal Interest** | Interest in autonomy, optionality, flexibility, and avoiding irreversible lock-in. Reflects both value of flexibility and uncertainty about future. |
| **Institutional Interest** | Interest in organisational or role security, institutional continuity, and system legitimacy. Can align with or diverge from individual interests. |
| **Interest Drift** | Shift in interests over time as circumstances change; what was primary becomes secondary or vice versa. Normal and expected; requires monitoring. |
| **Interest Cascade** | Effect where satisfaction or frustration of interests at one level creates demand or pressure at other levels. Individual interest frustration can activate institutional representation demands. |
| **Coalition** | Group of actors with aligned interests coordinating to advance shared interests. Coalition stability depends on interest alignment continuing. |
| **Trade-Off** | Situation where one interest can be satisfied only by partially or fully unsatisfying another. Requires explicit dialogue and negotiation to identify acceptable trade-offs. |
| **Interest Satiation** | State where additional satisfaction of an interest produces diminishing returns; interest recedes to background once sufficiently satisfied. |
| **Detection Interface** | Method by which hidden interests become visible (behaviour analysis, resistance patterns, coalition formation, stated exceptions, contradiction, crisis/pressure, irreversible commitment patterns). |
| **Remediation Interface** | Method by which interventions are designed to address interests (interest-aware negotiation, multi-interest solution design, coalition building, procedural legitimacy, identity-protective framing, governance design, temporal flexibility). |
| **Interaction Interface** | Method by which interest conflicts are mediated and negotiated (interest articulation, legitimacy assessment, trade-off dialogue, incentive design, multi-level integration, escalation paths, coalition-aware negotiation). |

### Appendix C: Worked Example (Micro-Case)

**Scenario:** A public agency proposes to consolidate three regional offices into one central location to reduce costs (the stated decision goal). Leadership frames this as efficiency improvement. All three regional offices have expressed opposition to the consolidation.

**Stated Positions:**
- Regional office leaders: "We oppose consolidation. It will damage service delivery to our communities."
- Leadership: "This is a cost-efficiency measure. Opposition is predictable resistance to change."

**Interest Inference (Behaviour-First Analysis):**

Rather than accepting these positions at face value, analyse what is actually being protected. Notice that all three regional offices oppose consolidation despite different stated rationales. Run coalition analysis: the three offices are normally competitive, yet they have coordinated opposition. This alignment suggests interests beyond "service delivery quality" are driving resistance.

Examine revealed preferences and resistance patterns:
- Regional office leaders have significant tenure in their communities
- They express concern about local relationships and knowledge
- They show reluctance to relocate despite offered relocation packages
- They focus repeatedly on "knowing the community" as core to their role

**Interest Mapping:**

| Meta-category | Interest | Type | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Material | Economic welfare (job security, income continuity) | 1.2 | Regional leaders reluctant about relocation despite compensation offers suggests non-monetary aspects matter more |
| Procedural | Voice in decisions affecting them | 2.1 | Regional leaders say they were not consulted; they claim to have local knowledge decision-makers don't have |
| Identity | Professional identity rooted in community | 3.1 | Regional leaders repeatedly emphasize "knowing the community" and local relationships as core to professional role |
| Relational | Status and authority in local context | 4.1 | Regional leaders have high status locally; centralisation would subordinate them to central office hierarchy |
| Temporal | Autonomy and decision-making authority | 5.2 | Regional leaders would lose autonomy over local decisions; central office would make decisions for their region |
| Institutional | Role security and legitimacy | 6.2 | Regional office role would change fundamentally; existing role legitimacy is tied to regional autonomy |

**Coalition Analysis:**

The three regional offices normally compete but have aligned interests in:
- Professional identity rooted in locality
- Autonomy over local decisions
- Relational status in local community
- Role as "regional expert" rather than "central office subordinate"

This explains why they coordinate despite competitive history.

**Hidden Interests:**

The primary hidden interests are relational and identity-based, not the material or efficiency arguments in the surface debate. Regional leaders experience consolidation as:
- Loss of professional identity (no longer "regional expert")
- Loss of status in local hierarchy (subordinate to central office)
- Loss of autonomy (decisions made centrally)

These hidden interests explain why material compensation (relocation package) does not satisfy resistance—the actual interests at stake are not material.

**Interest Conflict Analysis:**

- Leadership interest: Cost reduction (material: 1.2)
- Regional leaders' interests: Professional identity (3.1), local autonomy (5.2), local status (4.1), role security (6.2)

These are not incompatible in principle. The problem is that current consolidation design addresses leadership's interest while ignoring regional leaders' interests.

**Remediation Approach (Interest-Based Design):**

Rather than force consolidation, explore designs that:
1. Preserve regional autonomy over local decisions (satisfy temporal interest 5.2)
2. Maintain professional identity as "regional expert" (satisfy identity interest 3.1)
3. Preserve local status and authority (satisfy relational interest 4.1)
4. Achieve cost savings through centralised back-office functions rather than decision-making consolidation

Example design: "Hub and spoke" model where back-office (finance, HR, IT) is centralised (cost savings) but regional offices retain autonomy over local service delivery decisions and maintain regional brand/identity.

This design:
- Addresses leadership's material interest in cost reduction
- Preserves regional leaders' identity interests, autonomy, status, and role security
- Acknowledges and accommodates multiple interests rather than suppressing regional interests

**Predicted Outcome:**

With this interest-aware design, regional opposition likely decreases significantly because actual interests are addressed, not just positions acknowledged.

- [ ] Have we designed intervention options that address actual interests rather than just stated positions?
- [ ] Have we assessed whether our proposed solution satisfies, frustrates, or leaves unaddressed each significant interest?

**Workshop Prompts for Interest Surfacing:**

When interviewing actors about interests:

1. **Position-to-Interest Bridge:** "You've said you oppose consolidation. What specifically would you lose if consolidation happened? What would you need to preserve?"

2. **Behaviour-First Probe:** "I notice you've taken X action. That action isn't necessary to achieve your stated goal. What else might be driving this choice?"

3. **Trade-Off Exploration:** "If we could achieve cost reduction without consolidating regional offices, would you support that? If not, what else is really at stake for you?"

4. **Unconscious Interest Surfacing:** "What would have to be true about this outcome for you to feel it was genuinely acceptable, not just 'tolerable'?"

5. **Multi-Level Interest Mapping:** "What matters most to you personally in this decision? What matters most to your team? What matters most to the organization? Are these different?"

6. **Temporal Interest Probe:** "How important is it that you maintain flexibility to change course if this doesn't work out as expected?"

7. **Identity Interest Exploration:** "How would this decision affect how you see yourself professionally? How would others see you?"
