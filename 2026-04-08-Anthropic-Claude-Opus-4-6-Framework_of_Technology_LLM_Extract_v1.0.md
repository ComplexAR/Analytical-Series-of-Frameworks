## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Technology framework identifies what designed artefacts, systems, platforms, algorithms, and protocols shape a scenario; classifies them into six meta-categories and thirty core types; maps their affordances, constraints, and embedded values; and surfaces the visibility failures (black-box opacity, infrastructural invisibility, design assumptions treated as neutral) they create. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with technology-specific blindnesses and governance requirements, enabling downstream frameworks to design remediation and governance.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Obsolescence & Replacement | Technology becomes outdated; older artefacts abandoned; rapid platform shifts; migration waves. | Newer technology offers superior functionality or lower cost; user preferences shift; standards change; investment cycles drive replacement. | Disrupts dependent systems; strands investments; migration costs underestimated; skills become obsolete; data loss during transitions. |
| Lock-in & Network Effects | Once adopted, switching becomes prohibitively costly; network value increases with scale; complementary investments multiply. | Data lock-in; network effects (value grows with adoption); switching costs exceed benefits; complementary investments trap users. | Vendor can change terms/prices; better alternatives cannot be adopted; power concentrates; competitive pressure reduces; vulnerability to vendor changes. |
| Emergent Coupling & Sociotechnical Drift | Social practices and institutions couple tightly with technology over time; changes to one require changes to others; unintended consequences emerge. | Users adapt practices to affordances; institutions change policy/process to match technology; feedback loops amplify coupling; effects compound. | Changes become brittle; unintended cascades; resilience decreases; adaptation hardens; crisis becomes necessary; flexibility lost. |
| Algorithmic Amplification of Bias | ML systems trained on historical data amplify biases; distributional bias invisible to model creators; feedback loops reinforce discrimination. | Training data reflects historical inequality; optimization metrics ignore fairness; feedback loops reward popular items; scale amplifies small biases. | Harms accumulate at scale; bias invisible to designers; affected populations excluded; systemic discrimination at deployment scale. |
| Infrastructure Invisibility & Assumed Neutrality | Infrastructure becomes ubiquitous and disappears from analysis; treated as neutral/inevitable; assumptions about reliability go unexamined. | Ubiquity makes infrastructure invisible; claims of neutrality mask value choices; assumptions inherited rather than questioned; failures surprising. | Hidden failures become catastrophic; neutral claims mask power; alternatives not considered; design choices treated as inevitable. |
| Feature Creep & Scope Drift | Additional features added over time; scope expands beyond original design; system becomes larger, more complex, harder to maintain. | Each feature individually justified; cumulative effects untracked; sunk costs prevent saying no; market pressures drive additions. | Complexity reduces reliability; performance degrades; unintended interactions increase; maintenance costs rise; original purpose obscured. |
| Measurement Gaming & Proxy Drift | Systems optimized for metrics diverge from actual goals; metrics gamed; proxies drift from original intent; incentives misaligned. | Metric becomes optimization target; short-term gaming easier than long-term goal; feedback loops reinforce metric over goal; measurement is imperfect proxy. | System optimizes wrong target; actual performance diverges from reported; incentives misaligned; accounting looks good while business fails. |
| Vendor Dependency & Proprietary Escalation | Dependency grows over time; vendor increases prices/forces upgrades; switching becomes impossible; vendor captures increasing value share. | Switching costs increase; vendor has monopoly power; path dependency traps customer; vendor under competitive pressure extracts more value. | Organization dependent on vendor decisions; vendor forces unwanted changes; costs increase; negotiating power decreases; alternatives no longer viable. |
| Cascading Failure & Fragility | Systems become fragile over time; small failures cascade; resilience decreases; critical failures become more likely. | Tight coupling increases; redundancy eliminated for cost; feedback loops amplify shocks; monitoring insufficient for early warning. | Single failure propagates widely; crisis response necessary; slow recovery; prevention opportunity missed; brittleness increases. |
| Designed Obsolescence & Planned Degradation | Technology designed to fail or become obsolete; vendors intentionally limit lifespan to drive replacement; support withdrawn. | Vendor wants repeat purchases; lock-in is intentional; updates unavailable; repairability designed out; software support limited. | Drives replacement cycles and e-waste; total cost ownership increases; environmental impact; forces continued vendor dependency. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What design choices are invisible to users; what affordances and constraints are not obvious; what assumptions about users are hidden from designers? |
| II (Systemic) | How does this technology couple with social systems, institutions, and processes in ways not fully modelled; what emergent effects might arise from interactions? |
| III (Informational) | What does this technology measure and what does it hide; are training data and algorithms biased; are metrics being gamed or drifting from original intent? |
| IV (Temporal) | What lock-in is increasing over time and becoming less visible; are design assumptions becoming violated; what technical debt is accumulating; is reversibility decreasing? |
| V (Relational) | Who actually controls this technology and who is excluded from governance; is power asymmetry masked as technical necessity; are vendor dependencies growing? |
| VI (Ontological) | What categories and representations foreclose possibilities; who cannot be represented in the technology's schema; what alternatives are not even conceivable? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Design Determinism Invisibility (design choices treated as inevitable rather than contingent)
- Black-Box Opacity (algorithmic systems whose logic is not legible to users, operators, or creators)
- Affordance Blindspots (what technology makes impossible disappears from consideration as alternative choices)
- Infrastructure Invisibility (assumed reliability and neutrality of ubiquitous infrastructure)
- Embedding Assumptions Invisibility (values and worldviews baked into design but treated as technical)
- Power Distribution Concealment (who controls technology hidden behind technical necessity claims)
- Lock-in Concealment (switching costs and path dependency treated as inevitable rather than chosen)
- Measurement & Bias Blindness (algorithms opaque; training data biased; metrics gamed; outcomes invisible)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Algorithmic bias in training/outcomes | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Hiddens (Tier 2) | User outcome analysis; third-party audit; disparate impact testing | Rebalance data; add fairness constraints; establish appeals process and independent oversight |
| Black-box opacity in ML decisions | Framework of Governance; Framework of Hiddens (Tier 2) | Decision explanation inspection (SHAP/LIME); edge case testing; forensic analysis | Redesign for interpretability; require human-in-loop for high-stakes; establish appeals; governance oversight |
| Infrastructure dependency lock-in | Framework of Resources; Framework of Governance | Switching cost assessment; platform health monitoring; alternative capacity testing | Reduce dependency; invest in switching capacity; negotiate portability; diversify vendors |
| User affordance mismatch | Framework of Processes; Framework of Personas | User behaviour observation; support ticket analysis; user testing with diverse populations | Redesign affordances; add configurability; improve feedback; co-design with users |
| Measurement gaming and metric drift | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Metrics | Metric/outcome divergence analysis; metric definition audit; gaming vulnerability analysis | Multi-metric dashboards; include leading indicators; audit metric definitions; link accountability to outcomes |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. List focal technologies in the scenario; identify artefacts (platforms, algorithms, data systems, infrastructure, governance tech).
2. Classify each technology into one or more of the six meta-categories (I–VI) and assign a core type (1–30); note if type is uncertain.
3. For each focal technology, assess two critical dimensions: User Agency & Affordance Shape (what is easy/hard/possible/impossible for users?) and Technical Opacity (is the logic legible or opaque?).
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six Hiddens meta-categories lightly; flag obvious perceptual blindspots (design assumptions invisible to users), systemic couplings, or informational gaps (what is measured/unmeasured?).
5. Shortlist the 2–3 highest-consequence Hiddens from Tier 1 scan; note detection/remediation interfaces; flag for escalation if high-consequence and high-uncertainty.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. List focal technologies; create technology inventory with versions, deployment scale, user populations, dependencies.
2. Classify each technology (meta-category + core type 1–30); note taxonomy deviations and domain-specific refinements if needed.
3. Assess all five cross-cutting dimensions for each focal technology: User Agency, Technical Opacity, Power Distribution & Control, Embedded Values & Assumptions, Lock-in & Path Dependency; record dimension profiles.
4. Analyse all ten dynamics patterns; for each focal technology, assess which patterns are active (nascent, present, high-risk) and why; identify cascades and feedback loops.
5. Map three interface types: User-Facing Affordances (what users can/cannot do), Systemic Effects & Coupling (where tech couples with other systems, where effects ripple), Governance & Control Levers (who controls, who is accountable, governance gaps).
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan (structured deepening): for each high-relevance Hidden (e.g., algorithmic bias, black-box opacity, design assumptions invisibility), identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; assign detection and remediation interfaces.
7. Document unresolved Unknowns; apply Escalation Rule if any Unknown could change decisions or accountability; generate Residual Unknowns register and closure artefacts.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add shortlist of technology-specific Hiddens with meta-category tag (I–VI), mechanism, detectability, consequence. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate any high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hidden to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record technology type classifications, dimension assessments, dynamic pattern presence/absence, evidence quality, provenance. | After technology classification and dimensional profiling; note gaps (e.g., affordance analysis, black-box explainability testing). |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about technology effects, design assumptions, lock-in trajectory; propose tests (affordance analysis, user testing, data audit, algorithm audit). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request design documentation, user interaction data, outcome data (disaggregated by demographic), algorithm explainability, training data audit results. | To reduce Unknowns and validate Hiddens detection; coordinate with Evidence and Governance frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified the focal technologies, classified them into the six meta-categories, assessed critical dimensions (User Agency, Technical Opacity, Power Distribution), run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-consequence Unknowns to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Evidence, Governance)?

## Section 8: Cross-Framework Routing Pointers

### 8a. Upstream Inputs (frameworks commonly feeding into this one)

- Situations Context Classification (what situation type makes which technologies relevant?)
- Boundaries (what governance/jurisdictional constraints affect technology use?)
- Scale & Time (technology scalability and deployment timelines must match scenario dimensions)
- Agents & Personas (who are the users; what are their contexts, goals, mental models?)
- Evidence (what evidence exists about technology effects, quality, gaps, contested claims?)
- Power & Institutions (who controls technology; how does it enforce/subvert rules?)

### 8b. Downstream Handoffs (frameworks commonly consuming this framework's outputs)

- Framework of Hiddens (technology-specific Hiddens detection and remediation design)
- Framework of Evidence (evidence quality audit; technology performance measurement)
- Framework of Governance (technology governance structure, decision authority, accountability design)
- Framework of Risk (risk register from technology dynamics and failure patterns)
- Framework of Interventions (technology redesign, replacement, harm remediation options)
- Framework of Responsibility (accountability mapping for technology control and effects)

### 8c. Targeted Scans (OG §7.5 scans that invoke this framework)

| Targeted Scan | Role of this framework |
|---|---|
| Scan 1: Design-embedded values and assumption audit | Identify what values are baked into technology design and treated as neutral; surface whose assumptions are embedded and whose are excluded. |
| Scan 3: Black-box opacity and algorithmic bias detection | Surface algorithmic opacity, training data bias, metric gaming, lack of explainability; trigger remediation design (governance oversight, interpretability requirements). |
| Scan 5: Infrastructure invisibility and assumed neutrality check | Make infrastructure assumptions explicit; audit reliability claims; identify what would happen if assumptions are violated; design monitoring and graceful degradation. |
| Scan 6: Affordance blindspot and user mismatch discovery | Identify what users cannot do or must work around; surface design assumptions about users that may not hold across diverse populations; trigger redesign. |
| Scan 9: Measurement gaming and metric drift detection | Compare metrics to actual outcomes; audit metric definitions; identify gaming incentives; trigger metric redesign and outcome accountability. |
