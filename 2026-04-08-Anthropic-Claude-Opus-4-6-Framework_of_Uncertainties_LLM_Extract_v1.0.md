## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Uncertainties framework classifies all forms of epistemic gaps and unknowns bearing on decisions through six meta-categories and thirty core types, profiles uncertainty across five dimensions (type, knowability, consequence, reducibility, value-of-information), maps ten dynamics patterns of uncertainty evolution, and surfaces uncertainty-driven visibility failures via tiered Hiddens scans. It enables explicit uncertainty accounting, prioritises information gathering by decision impact, calibrates confidence levels to actual knowledge, and distinguishes between Unknowns (what we don't know we don't know), Known Unknowns (gaps acknowledged), and epistemic gaps (where knowledge is impossible or prohibitively costly).

## Section 4: Condensed Core Taxonomy

| Meta-category | Core types (names only) | Diagnostic question (1 sentence) |
|---|---|---|
| I. Unknowns About State | Known State, Partially Known State, Unknown State, Ontological Unknown, Unknowable State | What is unknown about the current or past state and could it be known? |
| II. Unknowns About Mechanism | Known Mechanism, Mechanism Disagreement, Unknown Mechanism, Mechanism Novelty, Emergent Mechanism | What is unknown about how things work and how consequential is that gap? |
| III. Unknowns About Prediction & Futures | Point-Prediction Uncertainty, Distribution Uncertainty, Threshold Uncertainty, Black-Swan Unknown, Long-Tail Uncertainty | What is unknown about future states and what is the tail-risk profile? |
| IV. Unknowns About Context & Boundary | Local Applicability Unknown, Boundary-Condition Unknown, Transferability Unknown, Interaction Unknown, Novel-Context Unknown | What is unknown about how this understanding applies across contexts and boundaries? |
| V. Unknowns About Value & Goal | Goal Ambiguity, Value Trade-off Unknown, Preference Uncertainty, Long-Horizon Value Unknown, Incommensurable-Value Unknown | What is unknown about what we actually care about and how to trade off competing goals? |
| VI. Meta-Uncertainties About Knowledge Itself | Ignorance About Ignorance, Framework Uncertainty, Assumption Uncertainty, Model-Form Uncertainty, Paradigm Uncertainty | What is unknown about the limits and reliability of our knowledge systems themselves? |

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unknown discovery | Unknown surfaces through observation, testing, or challenge; becomes Known Unknown; surprise and adaptation required | New evidence or perspective surfaces gap; challenger or external event makes ignorance visible; formal scanning discovers Unknowns | Opportunity to address before crisis; but also disruption and sunk-cost loss on prior decisions based on false certainty |
| Premature certainty | Confidence asserted despite substantive Unknowns; Unknowns remain Hidden; decisions made with false precision | Pressures to decide; sunk costs in prior narrative; motivated reasoning suppressing doubt; incentives to project certainty | High-consequence failure when Unknown manifests; post-hoc accountability confusion; brittle systems unprepared for actual variability |
| Reducibility misjudgement | Unknown classified as reducible when actually knowable only at prohibitive cost; or classified as unknowable when actually could be reduced by feasible test | Optimism bias in feasibility assessment; incentives to believe Unknowns are manageable; overestimating value-of-information | Over-investment in impossible tests; under-investment in feasible ones; resources spent on wrong priorities |
| Confidence calibration gap | Stated confidence mismatches actual confidence warranted by evidence and Unknowns; overconfidence common | Pressure to project confidence; underestimation of Unknown scope; cognitive biases in probability assessment; incentive to appear certain | Decisions over-weighted by apparently settled beliefs; fragility when assumptions break; post-hoc surprise |
| Unknown coupling and cascade | Multiple Unknowns interact; addressing one amplifies another; compounding uncertainties not tracked | Unknowns are interdependent; solutions for one create new Unknowns; systemic coupling ignored | Interventions backfire; attempts to reduce uncertainty increase it; surprises cascade; resilience lower than expected |
| Assumption invisibility | Assumptions supporting knowledge claims become background, treated as facts rather than contingencies; Unknowns about assumptions remain Hidden | Assumption propagates until challenged; habituation makes assumptions invisible; alternative assumptions not considered; foundational beliefs protected from scrutiny | Framework boundaries become invisible; paradigm inhabitants unaware of frame dependence; shock events reveal assumption brittleness |
| Tail-risk blindness | Low-probability high-consequence Unknowns are neglected in planning; focus on expected case; tail events treated as impossible rather than unlikely | Base-rate bias; salience bias; optimism bias; incentive to plan for expected case; formal risk models omit or underweight tails | Rare but catastrophic events not prepared for; surprise asymmetry (bad surprises more than good); resilience focused on wrong outcomes |
| Ontological Unknown persistence | Unknown is not just lack of information but lack of framework within which information could count; even full evidence would not resolve it | Fundamental paradigm gap; incommensurable worldviews; mutually exclusive ontological commitments | Cannot be reduced by ordinary information gathering; requires paradigm shift or perspective-taking; often misidentified as reducible Unknown |
| Meta-uncertainty denial | Uncertainty about the reliability of knowledge itself (model-form uncertainty, assumption dependence, paradigm boundaries) is not acknowledged; knowledge claims treated as grounded rather than contingent | Cognitive preference for certainty; institutional pressure to project confidence; incentives to treat models as reality; intellectual humility costs | Confidence in faulty models; over-reliance on frameworks known to be incomplete; institutional brittleness when paradigm shifts |
| Value-of-information miscalculation | Investment in information gathering is not prioritised by actual decision sensitivity; effort goes to low-impact Unknowns; high-impact ones remain unexamined | Availability bias (nearby Unknowns studied); salience bias (visible rather than consequential); institutional path dependency in research funding | Resources misallocated; decision margins smallest where uncertainty highest; opportunity cost of foregone high-impact tests |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What Unknowns are outside the frame of perception; what don't we notice we don't know; what is treated as fact when actually Unknown? |
| II (Systemic) | How do institutional routines embed assumptions about what is known; what Unknowns are structurally suppressed or normalised as irrelevant? |
| III (Informational) | What Unknowns exist because information is not collected or is gated; what measurement blindspots mask Unknowns; what is untestable within current frameworks? |
| IV (Temporal) | What Unknowns about futures are discounted because they are temporally distant; what assumption changes might occur over planning horizon? |
| V (Relational) | What Unknowns are suppressed because acknowledging them threatens status, role, or authority; what Unknown-discovery is punished? |
| VI (Ontological) | What Unknowns are not resolvable within current paradigm; what alternative paradigms would reframe what is Unknown; what is framework-bound rather than fact-bound? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms

- Unknowns treated as facts (false certainty masking Unknown)
- Meta-uncertainty denial (lack of awareness of knowledge system limits)
- Tail-risk blindness (low-probability high-consequence Unknowns neglected)
- Unknown coupling invisibility (interdependencies between Unknowns not mapped)
- Ontological Unknown disguise (paradigm-bound Unknowns mistaken for reducible Unknowns)
- Value-of-information invisibility (decision-critical Unknowns not identified as priorities)
- Assumption invisibility (foundational Unknowns about assumptions treated as settled facts)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tail-risk Unknown neglected | Framework of Risk; Framework of Failures | Scenario testing; low-probability-high-consequence event design; resilience planning | Widen planning scenarios; design tail-risk preparedness; stress-test assumptions |
| Assumption invisibility or paradigm uncertainty | Framework of Perspectives; Framework of Realities; Framework of Hiddens | Assumption audit; alternative paradigm exposure; "what if our frame is wrong?" probing | Surface assumptions; expose alternative frames; design paradigm-challenge sessions |
| Unknown coupling not recognised | Framework of Systems; Framework of Risk | System modelling; sensitivity analysis; cascade simulation | Model interdependencies; test cascade pathways; design for decoupling or early detection |
| Meta-uncertainty denial | Framework of Failures; Framework of Hiddens (Tier 3) | Epistemological audit; model-reliability testing; assumption-dependence analysis | Require explicit model-form justification; test across models; calibrate confidence to model uncertainty |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Identify focal decisions and list Known Unknowns explicitly; categorise by type (state, mechanism, prediction, context, value, meta-uncertainty).
2. For 2–3 highest-consequence Unknowns, assess: Is this reducible (could more information resolve it) or irreducible (unknowable or untestable)?
3. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: check for false certainty, meta-uncertainty denial, tail-risk blindness, assumption invisibility.
4. Identify 2–3 highest-consequence Unknowns and recommend information gathering priorities.
5. Flag any meta-uncertainties (paradigm dependence, model-form uncertainty, ontological Unknowns) for escalation.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Map all Unknowns (both Known Unknowns and discovered Hidden Unknowns) across six meta-categories; profile each across five dimensions (type, knowability, consequence, reducibility, value-of-information).
2. Trace assumption chains; identify foundational Unknowns about assumptions; assess assumption dependence of key claims.
3. Analyse tail-risk and low-probability-high-consequence Unknowns; map event scenarios with low prior but high impact.
4. Assess meta-uncertainties: model-form uncertainty (are alternative models plausible?), paradigm uncertainty (could paradigm shift change what is Unknown?), framework uncertainty (are frame boundaries appropriate?).
5. Prioritise Unknowns by decision sensitivity; estimate value-of-information for top candidates; identify most diagnostic tests.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: identify Hidden Unknowns (Unknowns not yet recognised); assess consequence and detectability of each.
7. Document residual Unknowns with mechanism, consequence, and reducibility assessment; recommend information gathering plan and escalation decisions.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add Unknown-related Hiddens (false certainty, meta-uncertainty denial, tail-risk blindness, assumption invisibility); mechanism; detectability | After Tier 1 scan; escalate to Tier 2 for high-consequence Unknowns |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Propose diagnostic tests to reduce top Unknowns; prioritise by decision sensitivity and reducibility; note Unknowns that are irreducible | For all Known and Hidden Unknowns |
| Information Requests | Request studies, tests, or monitoring to reduce Unknown; note cost-benefit and lead time | For highest-value-of-information Unknowns |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we inventoried Known Unknowns, discovered Hidden Unknowns (especially meta-uncertainties and tail-risks), assessed reducibility and decision sensitivity for each, run Tier 1 Hiddens scan across six categories, and escalated high-consequence irreducible or meta-level Unknowns to appropriate frameworks?
