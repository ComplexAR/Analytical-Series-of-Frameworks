# Introduction

## What is Institutions?

Institutions are the formal and informal rules, norms, conventions, and enforcement mechanisms that structure behaviour over time. Institutions include written policies, laws, and regulations, but they also include unwritten rules—the conventions that "everyone knows," the practices that are standard without being codified, the expectations about who can say what in which forums. Institutions are distinct from Culture (shared values and identity) and from Governance (formal decision-making authority). A person may follow an institution because they believe in the rule, but they may also follow it because violation carries consequence, or because they have never imagined an alternative, or because the rule is so embedded in the environment that they cannot see it as a rule at all.

Institutions matter because they are powerful, often invisible, and extremely sticky. Once an institutional rule is established—a way of doing things, a convention about appropriate behaviour, a form that must be filed before permission is granted—it shapes behaviour across many actors simultaneously without requiring repeated enforcement. Institutions are economical: instead of policing every decision, a well-designed institution makes certain actions impossible or costly and other actions routine. But institutions are also a primary source of Hiddens. A rule that is never written down cannot be challenged. A rule that is so embedded in the environment that no one questions it becomes a constraint that is invisible. A rule that is followed in formal compliance but broken in practice creates a gap between what is supposed to happen and what actually happens, and that gap is systematically hidden. Institutions can lock organisations into paths that seemed sensible decades ago but are now counterproductive; actors cannot leave those paths because the rules—formal and informal—that established them are still in place, still enforced, still legitimate in the narratives people tell about "how things work here."

This framework provides an operational definition: institutions are the structured rules, norms, conventions, and enforcement mechanisms that make certain behaviours possible or impossible, costly or routine, legitimate or shameful. The framework's central question is: **What formal and informal rules, norms, conventions, and enforcement mechanisms structure behaviour in this scenario, and what do they make invisible?**

## Why does Institutions matter for Hiddens work?

Institutions are a primary source and concealer of Hiddens. Informal institutions that are never written down but powerfully constrain behaviour are invisible to external auditors and even to some internal actors. Institutional logics—the deeper narratives about what kinds of action are appropriate in this context—become so embedded that they are experienced as the natural order rather than as choices. Rule-practice gaps hide the true operating system: organisations file compliant reports that describe formal governance, while actual decisions are made through shadow channels and informal arrangements that the rules never imagined. Institutional lock-in prevents change because the rules that established the path, the enforcement mechanisms that maintain it, and the narratives that legitimise it all reinforce each other. Actors may recognise that the current institution is counterproductive, but they cannot imagine a world in which it has changed because the institution is, literally, the structure of how they understand possibility. Cross-institutional conflicts are hidden: organisations may comply perfectly with each individual institution they face (policy A, regulation B, professional standard C) while those three institutions contradict each other, creating impossible choice situations that are systematically hidden by formal compliance.

Conversely, this framework surfaces six families of Hiddens:

- **Invisible Informal Institutions (Hidden-1)**: Rules that are never written, learned through informal socialisation, and enforced through subtle social pressure rather than explicit sanctions. These rules constrain behaviour powerfully while remaining invisible to external analysis.

- **Embedded Institutional Logics (Hidden-2)**: The deeper narratives about what kinds of action are appropriate, what constitutes legitimate authority, what outcomes are possible. These logics are so embedded that they function as assumptions rather than rules, making alternatives invisible.

- **Rule-Practice Gaps (Hidden-3)**: The systematic divergence between what formal institutions prescribe and what actually happens in practice. This gap is hidden by formal compliance reporting that describes the formal institution rather than the lived institution.

- **Institutional Lock-In (Hidden-4)**: Path dependencies created by rules that were sensible when adopted but are now counterproductive. Actors cannot leave these paths because the rules, enforcement, and narratives all maintain the lock-in, making the foreclosure of options invisible.

- **Informal Institutional Capture (Hidden-5)**: Informal institutions can be captured by particular interests just as formal institutions can be captured. This capture is hidden because informal institutions are not transparent, lack formal safeguards, and are legitimised through cultural narratives rather than accountability structures.

- **Cross-Institutional Conflicts (Hidden-6)**: When an organisation faces multiple institutional environments (compliance regimes, professional standards, internal policies), those environments may conflict. The conflicts are hidden by formal compliance with each institutional rule in isolation.

Without this framework, institutions are treated as background landscape—"that's how things work here"—and their role in producing Hiddens is invisible. With the framework, institutional analysis becomes a repeatable discipline that surfaces informal rules, tests rule-practice fidelity, diagnoses lock-in, and designs institution-aware interventions that are robust to institutional resistance.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises institutional diagnosis and design through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 institutional types** organised into six meta-categories (Formal Legislative Rules, Formal Operational Rules, Enforcement & Exception Management, Informal Normative Institutions, Informal Cognitive Institutions, Institutional Lock-in & Dynamics) that recur across contexts and allow rapid classification of the rule environment.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (Scope & Formality Gradient, Enforcement Mechanisms, Rule-Practice Fidelity, Institutional Logics Visibility, Change & Adaptability) that profile any institutional instance and explain variation across similar institutional environments.

3. **Twelve institutional dynamics patterns** (Drift & Decay, Institutionalisation of Workarounds, Lock-In Deepening, Norm Cascades, Legitimacy Erosion, Formal Rigidity Driving Informality, Informal Capture, Cross-Institutional Conflict Suppression, Exception Creep, Norm Inversion, Institutional Forgetting, Innovation Suppression) that describe how institutions evolve, degrade, and interact over time.

4. **Three interface types** (I-A Rule Setting & Maintenance, I-B Rule Practice & Compliance, I-C Detection & Remediation) that make visible where and how institutional mechanisms are maintained, where gaps emerge between intention and practice, and where institutions can be altered.

5. **Institutional-specific Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** (Tier 1 six-category audit, Tier 2 structured deepening, Tier 3 full-spectrum escalation) that surface the six systematic sources of institutional Hiddens and the six categories of visibility failure that permit Hiddens to persist.

6. **A 6-step application protocol** that moves from situation clarification through institutional scan, formal-informal mapping, dynamics analysis, Hiddens diagnosis, and institution-aware intervention design.

The framework populates standard registers (Institutional Map & Catalogue, Rule-Practice Gap Register, Institutional Lock-In Inventory, Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Institutional Reform Backlog) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow. It is designed for repeated use: institutions are mapped, Hiddens are surfaced, reforms are attempted, and the institution is re-audited to measure effectiveness and identify institutional resistance.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework early when diagnosing repeated failures, when formalising rules in new contexts, whenever an organisation or system has been operating for more than a few years, and periodically to audit for lock-in and decay. The framework is most valuable when used together with the Governance, Culture & Norms, Systems, Power, and Incentives frameworks to understand how institutions interact with formal authority, shared values, and incentive structures. The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch type identification, formal-informal mapping, Tier 1 Hiddens scan) and Investigative Run Mode (full dimensional profiling, deep dynamics analysis, Tier 2–3 Hiddens scanning, rule-practice audits, informal institution testing).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: identify active institutional types (formal and informal), map one or two dimensions, spot obvious rule-practice gaps and lock-in risks, conduct a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, evidence about actual practice is weak or contested, informal institutional capture is suspected, or lock-in effects are material. The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all institutional claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording disagreements, uncertainties, and residual unknowns rather than collapsing them into false consensus about "how things work."

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario and the Operating Guide, producing a disciplined institutional assessment with explicit Hiddens, F/I/A/U tagging, and bounded closure artefacts.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Institutions (operational)**: Formal and informal rules, norms, conventions, and enforcement mechanisms that structure behaviour over time by making certain actions possible/impossible, costly/routine, or legitimate/shameful.

**In scope**:
- Formal institutions (written laws, policies, standards, contracts, procedures)
- Informal institutions (conventions, unwritten rules, norms, expectations learned through socialisation)
- Institutional logics (deeper narratives about what kinds of action are appropriate and possible in this context)
- Enforcement mechanisms (legal consequences, social pressure, shame, routinisation)
- Rule-practice gaps (divergence between prescribed and actual behaviour)
- Institutional lock-in and path dependencies (rules that establish irreversible paths)
- Institutional dynamics (how institutions evolve, interact, decay, get captured, get reformed)
- Informal institutional capture (systematic shaping of informal rules by particular interests)
- Cross-institutional conflicts (contradictions between institutions that organisations face simultaneously)
- Institutional interfaces (where rules are set, where practice occurs, where detection and remediation happen)
- Hiddens sources specific to institutions (invisible informal rules, embedded logics, gaps, lock-in, captures, conflicts)

**Out of scope**:
- Detailed policy design and drafting (treated as downstream application)
- Domain-specific technical standards (treated as constraint and input)
- Purely normative institutional theory (use Legitimacy, Culture & Norms frameworks jointly)
- Legal interpretation and enforcement (treated as constraints and inputs)

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Formal Legislative Institutions

Formal legislative institutions are explicitly written rules: laws, regulations, policies, standards, and contracts that prescribe what must or must not happen. These are the visible institutions—the ones that can be audited against formal policy, the ones that external regulators check, the ones that are codified in repositories and version-controlled. Formal institutions are transparent in principle (available to be read, audited, challenged) but may be inaccessible in practice (too long, too technical, hidden in repositories, not in the language most people speak). Formal institutions are enforced through explicit mechanisms: violation has stated consequences. Formal institutions are powerful because they can be enforced across large numbers of people without requiring individual relationships or deep socialisation.

### II. Formal Operational Institutions

Formal operational institutions are the procedures and workflows through which formal rules are implemented. A formal rule says "all expenditures above X must be approved"; the operational institution is the approval workflow, the form that must be completed, the sign-off authority, the system that prevents the payment from being issued without approval. Operational institutions are where formal rules become real: the place where rules constrain actual behaviour. A rule that is beautifully written but implemented through a broken workflow will be ignored; a rule that is vague but implemented through a tight operational workflow will be enforced precisely. Operational institutions reveal where formal rules matter and where they are performative.

### III. Enforcement & Exception Management

Every institutional framework requires enforcement—mechanisms for detecting violation, mechanisms for administering consequence—and exception management—mechanisms for approving deviations, criteria for when exceptions are permitted. This meta-category captures the mechanisms through which institutions are maintained in practice. Enforcement mechanisms may be hard (automated, no human judgment) or soft (monitored, but contextual judgment applied). Exception management may be transparent (clear criteria, open to audit) or opaque (granted through informal channels). The patterns of enforcement and exception-granting reveal whether institutions are applied consistently or selectively—and selective enforcement is a primary source of institutional capture and shadow governance.

### IV. Informal Normative Institutions

Informal normative institutions are the unwritten rules and conventions that everyone knows and follows. In a meeting, everyone knows that the boss speaks first and others defer. In this professional context, everyone knows that you must cite the research literature in a certain way. These norms are not written anywhere; they are learned through socialisation, maintained through social pressure (if you violate the norm, you are viewed as ignorant or rude), and enforced without explicit rules or stated consequences. Informal normative institutions are powerful because they require no explicit enforcement apparatus; the social pressure is the enforcement. They are invisible to external auditors because they are not written. They are particularly stable because they are rarely questioned—"that's just how people do things here."

### V. Informal Cognitive Institutions

Informal cognitive institutions are deeper logics, mental models, and narratives about what is possible and appropriate in this context. These are the "institutional logics"—the implicit frameworks through which actors make sense of their world. In a cost-driven organisation, the institutional logic says that all problems have cost-reduction solutions; in a risk-driven organisation, the logic says that all problems have risk-mitigation solutions. These logics are not written rules, but they are powerful constraints on what actions are visible and imaginable. An actor in a cost-driven organisation who sees a problem and thinks "we need to invest more to build safety" is proposing something that is barely visible in that institutional context—the logic makes cost reduction visible and additional investment invisible.

### VI. Institutional Lock-In & Dynamics

This meta-category captures the mechanisms through which institutions become locked in—the rules, incentives, and narratives that make change difficult and prevent alternatives from being imagined. Lock-in occurs when: rules establish an irreversible path (sunk costs in a particular technology); multiple rules reinforce each other (you cannot change rule A without breaking rule B, which depends on rule C); the narratives about why the current path is necessary become embedded in organisational identity. Lock-in is not the same as rules; it is the mechanism through which rules become irreversible and foreclose alternatives.

| 5 | Procedural rules & workflows | Formal rules that define the sequence of steps that must be followed to accomplish a result; defined in procedures, protocols, and workflows; create the structure through which decisions are made and actions are taken. | Procedure documentation; workflow diagrams; step-by-step protocols; decision trees; approval chains. | Procedures are unclear or unmaintained; procedures conflict with operational reality (steps cannot be done in stated sequence); procedures require hard stops that create blockage (nothing can proceed until approval is obtained, but approval is slow). ## 3.2 Types (Category II: Formal Operational Institutions)

| # | Type | Description (1–3 sentences) | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 6 | Operationalised approval workflows | Formal rules implemented through actual workflows and systems that require approvals; hard stops are built in (cannot proceed without approval); approval authority is encoded in system rules or delegation authorities. | Workflow system configurations; system access controls; delegation authorities; approval requirements in systems; logs of approval denials. | Approvals are routinely overridden or waived; approval logic is unclear to staff; approvals create bottlenecks but are inconsistently enforced. | 9 | Delegation & authority structures | Formal rules operationalised through explicit delegation of authority; who can decide what is encoded in role description or delegation authority document; decisions are tracked to the authority holder. | Delegation authority documentation; role descriptions; sign-off authorities at each level; spending limits and authority matrices; audit of authorities granted vs exercised. | Authority is unclear (staff do not know whether they have decision-making authority); authority diverges from formal roles (decisions are made by informal influencers); authority is exceeded (people make decisions beyond their delegated authority). | 10 | Monitoring, reporting & dashboards | Rules operationalised through monitoring systems that track performance against formal standards; reports are generated; dashboards display metrics; evidence is recorded in systems. | Monitoring system specifications; metric definitions; reporting schedules; dashboard configurations; data collection methods; evidence of metric integrity. | Metrics are not meaningful (measure activity rather than outcome); metrics are gamed (behaviour adjusts to meet metric targets rather than substantive objectives); metrics hide variability (average hides range). ## 3.3 Types (Category III: Enforcement & Exception Management)

| 11 | Monitoring & detection mechanisms | Rules require mechanisms for detecting violations; detection is embedded in processes, systems, audits, or escalation channels; without detection mechanisms, rules have no real enforcement. | Detection protocol documentation; monitoring system configurations; audit plan; escalation triggers; testing of detection effectiveness. | Detection is absent or weak (violations are not discovered); detection is biased (certain violations are more likely to be detected than others); detection is not acted upon (violations are discovered but not responded to). | 13 | Escalation & appeal processes | Rules require mechanisms for escalating violations or appealing consequences; without escalation, enforcement can be arbitrary; escalation creates a check on enforcement discretion. | Escalation protocols; appeal procedures; channels for raising concerns about enforcement; documentation of escalations and appeals; time limits for escalation response. | Escalation is unsafe (people fear retaliation if they escalate); escalation is ignored (escalations are received but not responded to); escalation routes are blocked (people do not know how to escalate). | 15 | Remediation & corrective action | Rules specify what happens after violation to correct the breach; remediation may be mandated action, additional monitoring, or re-training; effectiveness of remediation determines whether violation recurs. | Corrective action plans; remediation requirements; re-training protocols; additional monitoring requirements; follow-up audit of remediation completion. | Remediation is not completed (corrective actions are not finished); remediation does not prevent recurrence (violations happen again); remediation is excessive and creates undue burden. Hidden-risk: Remediation theatre (corrective actions are documented as complete but are not) and remediation avoidance (organisations resist remediation requirements and comply only on paper). |

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)

| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | 6 meta-categories × 5 types = 30 core institutional types. This baseline applies across all contexts. |
| Domain-specific refinement | Extensions should be additions to the 30 types, not replacements. Examples: "Regulatory capture in healthcare" (extension of type 1), "Labour union institutions" (extension of type 4), "Technology-platform network effects lock-in" (extension of type 27). Domain refinements should map back to the canonical baseline. |
| Preserving mappability | When extending, preserve the ability to map extensions back to the canonical 30. Do not create parallel taxonomies. |
| Structural invariants | Do not alter the six meta-categories without justification and re-versioning (v1.1 or v2.0). Do not exceed 40–50 types without revisiting the baseline structure. |
| Periodic reassessment | Reassess the taxonomy every 2–3 years or when extensions exceed 40 total types. Consider consolidation, reorganisation, or creation of specialist sub-frameworks. |
| Versioning & governance | Extensions are recorded in Section 12 Version History with rationale and domain-specific justifications. Coordinate extensions across frameworks to maintain overall consistency in the Analytical Series. |

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)

| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Formality: fully informal (0% written) to fully formal (100% codified in policies). | Count types of behaviour covered by institutions; measure ratio of written to unwritten rules; assess coverage of decision domains. | Identifies whether behaviour is heavily institutionalised or loosely governed; identifies gaps where institutions are needed; reveals informal dominance if formality is low. |
| 2. | 5. # 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)

| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Drift & Decay | Institutions gradually erode in practice over time; compliance declines; enforcement weakens; the rule environment becomes less structured and more ambiguous. | Initial non-compliance is tolerated; enforcement is not consistently applied; staff gradually stop following rules; decay becomes normal practice. | Drift creates ambient uncertainty (staff do not know what is actually required); enables creeping violations (small breaches accumulate into systemic non-compliance); creates coordination failure (different groups follow different rules). | Periodic compliance audits; enforcement of written rules; refresher training; clarity about which rules are core vs flexible; consequences for sustained non-compliance. |
| 2 | Institutionalisation of Workarounds | When formal institutions prevent necessary action, staff create workarounds; workarounds initially are informal and shadow; over time, workarounds become institutionalised as the real rules. | Formal rule creates problem; staff find informal solution; informal solution works and becomes standard practice; informal solution becomes as binding as formal rule; formal rule becomes performative. | Hides the true rule environment (formal rules are theatre; informal rules are real); creates governance failure (written policies no longer govern behaviour); blocks future change because informal rules are now entrenched. | Treat workarounds as signal that formal rule is broken; redesign formal rule or formal processes; surface workarounds through non-punitive inquiry; make workarounds transparent and then legitimise or block them. |
| 3 | Lock-In Deepening | Path dependencies created by initial institutions become tighter and more irreversible over time; sunk costs increase; complementarities strengthen; role structures become more dependent on current institutions. | Initial rule creates path; investments are made that depend on path; role structures evolve to depend on path; alternatives become more costly; constituents develop vested interests in path. | Makes change progressively harder (early change windows close as lock-in deepens); institutional inertia increases (momentum favours continuation); reform becomes impossible without major disruption. | Periodically revisit lock-in assumptions (are they still true?); invest in reducing switching costs; maintain alternative capabilities and expertise; build in deliberate change windows before lock-in becomes total. |
| 4 | Norm Cascades | Social norms spread rapidly through networks through observation and imitation; what one group does becomes what the next group does; norm spread can be much faster than formal rule change. | One group or influencer adopts new norm; observation by others creates imitation; norm spreads through network; norm becomes standard practice faster than formal rules change. | Can be positive (good practices spread quickly) or negative (bad practices spread quickly); creates network effects that either reinforce or undermine formal institutions; shows that norms may matter more than formal rules for behaviour change. | Identify norm influencers and work with them to establish desired norms; make norm spread visible (measure norm adoption rate); intervene early in cascade before norm becomes entrenched; use norm spread to amplify institutional change. |
| 5 | Legitimacy Erosion | Narratives and justifications that made institutions seem legitimate gradually lose power; people begin to question whether the institution is necessary or good; support for institution weakens. | Institution is established with clear justification; justification is repeated in training and communication; over time, justification is questioned or contradicted by evidence; people stop believing the narratives; support declines. | Weakened legitimacy creates vulnerability (institutions with weak legitimacy are easier to change but also more fragile); may trigger unexpected reform (if legitimacy erodes suddenly, institutions can collapse quickly); creates opening for competing narratives. | Refresh legitimacy narratives with updated evidence; address criticisms of institutions directly rather than ignoring them; measure legitimacy support periodically; have plausible reform pathways ready when legitimacy weakens. |
| 6 | Formal Rigidity Driving Informality | When formal institutions are too rigid, create unintended consequences, or conflict with operational reality, staff create informal institutions that parallel or replace the formal ones. | Formal rule is perceived as impossible to follow or counterproductive; staff find informal way to accomplish needed result; informal practice becomes standard; formal rule is ignored; shadow governance emerges. | Creates dual rule systems (formal rules exist but are not followed); hides true operating procedures (formal documentation does not match reality); enables capture (informal systems are less transparent and more easily manipulated). | Test formal institutions for unintended consequences; involve operational staff in rule design; build in adaptive capacity so rules can evolve; monitor for workaround emergence; distinguish between rules that must be strict and rules that should be flexible. |
| 7 | Informal Capture | Informal institutions can be captured by particular interests just as formal institutions can be; informal norms are adjusted to favour particular groups; informal power is exercised to shape what rules apply to whom. | Informal norm is established; over time, norm is applied selectively (benefits particular groups); interpretation of norm drifts to favour powerful interests; informal institution becomes mechanism for maintaining power differentials. | Capture is less visible in informal institutions because they lack transparency; harder to detect and remediate; creates systemic bias that is not attributed to rules but to "how people are." | Periodically audit informal norms for selective application; interview multiple hierarchical levels about whether norms apply to all equally; create formal appeals mechanisms for informal enforcement; increase transparency of informal norms. |
| 8 | Cross-Institutional Conflict Suppression | When institutions conflict, rather than resolving conflicts, organisations manage conflicts by suppressing visibility of the conflict; formal compliance with each institution separately hides the conflicts. | Institution A requires outcome X; Institution B requires outcome not-X; organisation manages by having different groups follow different institutions; conflicts are not surfaced as contradictions; formal compliance is reported separately. | Suppresses signal about institutional incoherence; allows organisations to report compliance that is not real (compliant with each rule separately but not coherent overall); enables systematic choice-point hiding (people face impossible choices but they are not visible). | Map all institutions an organisation faces and test for contradictions; require explicit conflict identification when institutions contradict; escalate conflicts rather than suppressing them; design explicit conflict-resolution mechanisms. |
| 9 | Exception Creep | Exceptions to formal rules are granted to handle special cases; over time, exceptions become more frequent; rate of exceptions increases; exceptions gradually undermine the rule. | Rule is established with clear criteria for exceptions; exceptions are approved in legitimate cases; exception rate increases over time; exceptions stop being exceptional; rule is followed in minority of cases. | Creates ambient uncertainty (unclear which rule applies in which situation); weakens rule by showing it is not essential; creates precedent for future exceptions (if X was approved before, why not now?). | Track exception frequency over time; set explicit limits on exception rate (if exceeding X%, revisit the rule); require escalation for exceptions; redesign rule if exception rate is high; distinguish between legitimate flexibility and rule erosion. |
| 10 | Norm Inversion | When institutions fail or become counterproductive, they may be inverted; what was previously punished becomes rewarded; what was previously rewarded becomes shameful. | Institution is established as constraint on behaviour; under pressure or in extreme circumstances, institution is violated; violation becomes survival behaviour; over time, inverted norm becomes standard; previous institution becomes viewed as foolish. | Represents fundamental institutional failure and shift; can be rapid and destabilising; creates confusion about what is actually expected. | Monitor for norm inversion signals (are people rewarding what used to be punished?); investigate root causes (why is inversion happening?); either recommit to original institution with enforcement, or formally update institution to match new reality. |
| 11 | Institutional Forgetting | Rules and institutions are forgotten over time; institutional memory is lost; reasons why rules were established are lost; rules are followed without understanding why. | Institution is established with clear purpose and justification; personnel turn over; institutional knowledge is not documented; new staff do not understand why rule exists; rule is followed habitually or dropped without understanding consequences. | Makes rules fragile (if forgotten, they lose legitimacy); enables rule erosion (rules that are not remembered cannot be defended); creates risk when circumstances change (old rules may have guarded against forgotten risks). | Document institutional history and rationale; refresh training periodically; maintain institutional memory explicitly; periodically revisit rules to check whether original rationale still applies; use institutional audits to surface forgotten institutions. |
| 12 | Innovation Suppression | Institutions can suppress innovation by making it difficult or impossible to try new approaches; rules enforce adherence to established ways; alternatives are not permitted or are costly to explore. | Institutions establish standard way of doing things; rules enforce adherence; innovation is not permitted because it violates rules; when rule is the constraint rather than the constraint being the rule, innovation becomes impossible. | Slows adaptation; prevents beneficial change; makes organisations brittle (unable to adapt when circumstances change); locks in obsolete practices. | Distinguish between rules that must be rigid (safety-critical) and rules that should have flex for experimentation; create formal innovation pathways within rule frameworks; use Controlled Variation protocols to permit testing of alternatives; measure innovation rate and loss of innovation as warning sign. |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Invisible informal institutions | Analysis focuses on formal policies and misses informal rules that actually govern behaviour. Informal institutions are not written; they are learned through socialisation; they are invisible to external auditors. Behaviour appears to violate formal policy but actually complies with informal rules. | Conduct ethnographic study and interviews to surface informal rules; observe practice to identify what people actually do; ask "why do people follow this rule when it is not written anywhere?"; map the informal rule environment alongside formal rules; surface hidden social pressures and shame mechanisms. |
| 2 | Embedded institutional logics | Deeper narratives and mental models about what is possible and appropriate become so embedded that they are experienced as facts rather than as rules. Institutions appear immutable because actors cannot imagine alternatives. Analysis that focuses on rules while missing institutional logics will fail to explain why certain alternatives are never attempted. | Interview for counterfactual thinking ("what if we changed X?"); explore what is taken for granted rather than questioned; examine metaphors and framings used to describe the organisation; test whether actors can articulate the logics or whether they experience them as reality; challenge assumptions explicitly. |
| 3 | Rule-practice gaps | Formal institutional descriptions diverge from observed practice. Analysis that compares formal rules to written procedures will miss the actual rule environment. Compliance reporting describes formal institutions while actual behaviour follows informal institutions or workarounds. | Compare formal policies to observed practice in detail (trace actual decisions, observe workflows); measure compliance rates; identify systematic gaps; interview staff about workarounds and why they exist; trace decisions from formal initiation to actual execution; audit whether process steps in formal procedures are actually executed. |
| 4 | Institutional lock-in | Institutions that made sense when adopted become locked in through sunk costs, complementarities, role structures, and narrative justification. Analysis that does not interrogate whether institutions are still necessary will miss the foreclosure of options. Institutions persist not because they are good but because they are locked in, creating "path dependence" that is invisible unless explicitly examined. | Map historical institutional evolution and identify decision points where the path could have diverged; calculate switching costs and identify sunk costs; map institutional complementarities and identify what breaks if one institution changes; examine role structures that depend on current institutions; test whether actors recognise current institutions as changeable or fixed. |
| 5 | Informal institutional capture | Informal institutions can be shaped by particular interests just as formal institutions can be captured. Informal institutions lack transparency and formal safeguards, making capture harder to detect. Analysis that treats informal institutions as neutral conventions will miss systematic bias. | Audit informal institutions for selective application; interview multiple hierarchical levels about whether informal rules apply to all equally; test whether informal enforcement is consistent or varies by actor; look for patterns of who benefits from informal institutions; examine whether informal norms are applied more strictly to some groups than others. |
| 6 | Cross-institutional conflicts | When an organisation faces multiple institutional environments, those environments may conflict. Conflicts are hidden by formal compliance with each individual institution in isolation. Analysis that assesses each institution separately will miss contradictions that actors face. | Map all institutions an organisation faces (regulatory, professional, internal policy); test for contradictions explicitly ("does compliance with A require non-compliance with B?"); interview staff about impossible choice situations they face; identify choice points where institutions conflict; surface these conflicts rather than treating them as private problems of individual actors. |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)

| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I. Perceptual | Institutional logics and narratives make certain possibilities invisible. Actors cannot imagine alternatives because institutional logics embed assumptions about what is possible. Institutional analysis may be invisible to analysts who are embedded in the same institutions. | Behaviour appears irrational when it is actually institutionally rational. When asked why they do something, people say "that's just how things are done." Counterfactual thinking is difficult (people cannot imagine alternatives). | Ask what is taken for granted rather than questioned. Explore what would happen if we changed each institution (do people find alternatives unimaginable?). Interview people from outside the organisation for their view on institutions. Test whether institutional logics are stated as fact or subject to debate. |
| II. Systemic | Institutions are interdependent; changing one institution breaks others. The interconnections are not visible unless explicitly mapped. Actors may believe that individual institutions could be changed when systemic analysis would reveal that change requires comprehensive reform. | Attempted reforms fail because they did not account for interconnected institutions. People say "we cannot change that because it would break X." System appears brittle because interdependencies are not visible. | Map institutional interdependencies explicitly. When reform is proposed, trace downstream effects to identify what else would break. Test whether tight interdependencies are perceived or recognised. Identify whether organisation is aware of systemic lock-in or treats lock-in as inevitable. |
| III. Informational | Evidence about institution problems or non-compliance is hidden or suppressed. Information flows are shaped by institutions that prevent certain information reaching decision-makers. Compliance reporting describes formal institutions; evidence of non-compliance is not recorded or not reported. | Formal compliance reports show everything is fine, but observed practice shows significant non-compliance. Audit findings are not acted upon. Problems are reported to management but recommendations are not implemented. Information that contradicts official narratives does not circulate. | Audit information flows (what information reaches decision-makers and what does not). Track what happens to audit findings and escalations (are they acted upon?). Compare compliance reports to observed practice. Interview staff about whether they report problems and where. Measure escalation closure rate (do escalations result in action?). |
| IV. Temporal | Institutional history is forgotten; rationale for institutions becomes unclear; decisions about whether institutions should continue are made without understanding why they were established. Lock-in may be temporal—institutions persist because time has made the original decision path irreversible. Institutional memory is concentrated in individuals who may leave. | Institutions are followed without people understanding why. When asked why a rule exists, people do not know the original rationale. Institution persists despite being counterproductive, indicating lock-in more than necessity. Personnel turnover causes institutional knowledge loss. | Document institutional history and rationale. When institutions are challenged, retrieve the original justification and test whether it still holds. Measure institutional knowledge retention through personnel transitions. Ask people why institutions exist (if they cannot answer, institutional memory is weak). Track which institutions are followed habitually vs understood. |
| V. Relational | Power asymmetries are embedded in institutions without being named. Informal institutions concentrate power in ways that are not visible to those not in the power network. Relationships between formal governance and informal institutional power are hidden. Accountability relationships may diverge from formal role structures. | Actual authority diverges from formal roles. Important decisions are made in informal forums rather than formal governance bodies. Power is visible to those in it but invisible to those outside it. Some actors have impunity to violate rules while others are strictly enforced. | Map formal vs informal authority. Trace actual decisions to their real decision-makers (are they formal roles or informal influencers?). Interview across hierarchical levels to understand where real authority lies. Audit enforcement patterns for selective application. Map informal networks and their influence on decisions. Test whether formal governance bodies actually govern or are ceremonial. |
| VI. Ontological | What counts as an institution is itself institutionally determined. The categories used to classify institutions may be institutional themselves. Alternative institutional frameworks are not imagined. Institutions that are institutionally invisible (so embedded they are not seen as institutions at all) are not questioned. | Institutional analysis focuses on formal policies and misses informal rules entirely. Alternative institutional frameworks are not proposed or considered. Some institutional mechanisms (e.g., shame, reputational pressure, narrative legitimacy) are not recognised as institutional mechanisms. Institutions that are culturally or cognitively embedded are not named as institutions. | Conduct ethnographic analysis to identify institutions not named in official language. Ask what mechanisms enforce behaviour (formal consequences, social pressure, shame, routinisation, cognitive embedding—all are institutional). Test whether all six meta-categories of institutions are visible in the analysis or whether some are invisible. Seek alternative frameworks for understanding institutions. Invite external perspectives to identify institutions that are invisible from inside. |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

1) Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (early pass; assume Hiddens exist) (OG v2.5 §7.1). Use the table in §8.2 to test each category.
2) Shortlist candidate Hiddens; for each, rate: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence (OG v2.5 §7.2).
3) Assign at least one detection interface (I-A/I-B/I-C) and one remediation interface per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (OG v2.5 §7.2).
4) Run the Hiddens source analysis (§8.1) and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens in the Hiddens Register.
5) If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism (e.g., informal capture, lock-in), invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG v2.5 §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3).
6) After executing the framework protocol and any available tests/probes (rule-practice audits, informal-institution mapping, lock-in analysis), re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas.
7) Produce Residual Unknowns (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or institutional reform priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG v2.5 §7.4.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- High consequence (safety, regulatory, existential, severe harm potential) and institutional mechanism suspected
- Adversarial context / strategic concealment likely through institutional design
- Persistent disagreement or inconsistent evidence about institutions
- Repeat institutional failures despite prior institutional reforms
- Strong suspicion of cascades across hidden types (e.g., lock-in deepens capture; capture strengthens logics; logics justify lock-in)
- Decision-critical residual unknowns about institutions

Tier 3 execution (per OG v2.5 §D.6.10):
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into full institutional taxonomy (institutional variants, micro-dynamics, cross-framework institutional effects).
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements on institutional claims, more explicit competing institutional hypotheses, more rigorous disconfirming tests of institutional assumptions.
- When adversarial institutional design is plausible: adds concealment vectors (deliberate institutional design to hide facts) and independent corroboration paths.
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when institutional failures threaten multiple groups or when institutional reform affects system-wide outcomes.

## 9.2 Standard Deliverables (recommended)

- **Minimum deliverable (1–2 pages)**: Institutional type classification (which of the 30 types are active); rule-practice gap register (key formal-informal divergences); institutional Hiddens identified with mechanism and consequence; one or two immediate institution-aware interventions.
- **Full deliverable pack**: All outputs from Steps 1–7 plus institutional map (visual representation of formal + informal institutions), dimensional profile, dynamics assessment, interface failure diagnosis, full Hiddens crosswalk (Tier 2 or Tier 3), institutional reform backlog with sequencing, closure artefacts.
- **Monitoring indicators**: Rule-practice compliance rate (% of formal rules actually followed in practice); institutional capture indicators (selective enforcement patterns, benefit distribution to particular interests); lock-in risk trajectory (are switching costs increasing?); institutional decay signals (compliance declining, exceptions increasing); change/adaptability index (how quickly are institutions updated?).

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table

| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Institutions are powerful and often invisible | Formal policies are visible; informal institutions, embedded logics, and rule-practice gaps are invisible without deliberate analysis. Powerful constraints that determine behaviour are often not named as constraints. Institutional analysis must surface the invisible alongside the visible. |
| 2. Institutions are neither purely voluntary nor purely coercive | Institutions work through multiple mechanisms (formal enforcement, social pressure, shame, routinisation, cognitive embedding). Analysis must account for all mechanisms. Compliance comes from fear, belief, habituation, and logic collapse simultaneously. Single-mechanism interventions will be incomplete. |
| 3. Rule-practice gaps are systematic, not accidental | When formal rules diverge from practice, the divergence is often systematic (formal rule creates unintended consequence; informal institution emerged as workaround). Gaps are maintained by actors who find informal practice necessary or preferable. Institutional reform that ignores gaps will fail. |
| 4. Informal institutions can be captured like formal institutions | Informal institutions lack transparency and formal safeguards, making them vulnerable to capture by particular interests. Informal capture is harder to detect but equally consequential. Institutional analysis must audit informal institutions for selective application and asymmetric benefit. |
| 5. Lock-in is self-reinforcing and forecloses alternatives | Institutions create path dependencies through sunk costs, complementarities, role structures, and narrative justification. Lock-in makes alternatives invisible and change costly. Breaking lock-in requires addressing multiple elements simultaneously; changing rules without changing narratives, role structures, or incentives will fail. |
| 6. Institutional change requires institution-aware intervention design | Interventions that ignore institutions are likely to fail. Institutional resistance is not irrationality; it is rational response to real switching costs, genuine role threats, and legitimate uncertainty about alternatives. Effective institutional reform works with rather than against institutional logic; reduces switching costs; updates narratives; redesigns role structures; creates new coalitions that benefit from change. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms; recommended; mandatory for "Stable" status)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Formal institution | Explicitly written rules, policies, laws, standards, or contracts that define what must or must not happen. Codified, accessible, auditable; enforced through explicit mechanisms. |
| Informal institution | Unwritten rules, conventions, and norms learned through socialisation and maintained through social pressure, reputation, or shame. Not codified; invisible to external auditors; enforced through subtle mechanisms. |
| Institutional logic | Deeper narrative frameworks or mental models that shape what kinds of action are visible and appropriate in a context. What organisations "know" to be true without questioning. Determines what alternatives are imagined. |
| Rule-practice gap | Systematic divergence between what formal institutions prescribe and what actually happens in practice. Usually hidden by formal compliance reporting. Indicates either that formal rule is counterproductive or that informal institution has replaced formal rule. |
| Institutional lock-in | Path dependency created by rules, investments, role structures, and narratives that make change difficult and alternatives invisible. Current institutions are maintained not because they are best but because change is more costly than continuation. |
| Institutional capture | Systematic shaping of institutional rules (formal or informal) toward particular interests; rules are applied selectively or interpreted in ways that favour particular groups. Capture is often invisible in informal institutions. |
| Informal institutional capture | Selective application of informal norms or enforcement of informal rules in ways that benefit particular interests. E.g., "the rule about showing deference is enforced strictly against junior staff but not against senior leaders." |
| Cross-institutional conflict | Contradictions between multiple institutions that an organisation faces simultaneously. E.g., policy A requires outcome X; regulation B requires outcome not-X; professional standards C require different outcome. Conflicts are hidden by formal compliance with each institution separately. |
| Institutional rigidity | Formal institutions that are too strict, create unintended consequences, or conflict with operational reality. Rigidity drives the creation of workarounds and shadow governance. |
| Shadow governance | Informal institutions that emerge to replace or supplement formal institutions; typically where formal institutions are absent, rigid, or counterproductive. Actual authority, decisions, and accountability migrate into informal channels. |
| Sunk costs | Investments and decisions that cannot be recovered if institutions change; create incentive to persist with current institutions regardless of whether they remain optimal. Lock-in mechanism. |
| Complementarity | Structural interdependence between institutions; cannot change one rule without breaking others that depend on it. Creates systemic lock-in; makes individual rule change appear to require catastrophic overhaul. |
| Exception creep | Frequency of exceptions to formal rules increases over time; exceptions become standard practice; rule is followed in minority of cases. Indicates rule erosion; signal to redesign rule. |
| Institutional forgetting | Loss of institutional knowledge and memory of why institutions were established; reasons for institutions become unknown; rules are followed habitually without understanding rationale. Creates vulnerability when circumstances change. |
| Norm inversion | When institutions fail or become counterproductive, norms may invert; what was previously punished becomes rewarded; what was previously rewarded becomes shameful. Represents institutional failure and regime shift. |
| Institutional dynamics | Patterns over time in how institutions evolve, degrade, interact, and respond to pressure. The 12 patterns in §5.1. |

## 12.2 Integration Notes (optional)

This framework complements the Governance framework (which focuses on formal decision-making authority and oversight) by providing visibility into the broader rule environment (formal and informal) that shapes behaviour independent of formal authority structures. It complements the Culture & Norms framework (which focuses on shared values and identity practices) by examining rules and constraints that work regardless of belief or value alignment. It complements the Systems framework (which focuses on functional interdependencies) by examining the rule-based constraints that structure system operation and determine who can change system design. It should be used together with Power and Incentives frameworks to understand how institutions distribute power and incentives, and to diagnose why institutional change is resisted.

The framework is particularly valuable in contexts where: repeated failures occur despite reforms (suggesting institutional lock-in); rule-practice gaps are suspected (indicating shadow governance); informal institutions are powerful (visible only through ethnography and observation); institutional change is being attempted (requires institution-aware intervention design); or institutional capture is suspected (requires audit of selective rule application).
