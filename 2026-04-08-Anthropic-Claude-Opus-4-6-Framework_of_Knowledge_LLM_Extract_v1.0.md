## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Knowledge framework classifies forms of human and institutional knowing through six meta-categories and thirty core types, profiles knowledge across five dimensions (form, codifiability, distribution, retrievability, obsolescence-risk), maps ten dynamics of how knowledge is created, transferred, and lost, and surfaces knowledge-specific visibility failures via tiered Hiddens scans. It enables knowledge-holding pattern audits, identifies fragile or siloed knowledge, designs knowledge-preservation and transfer mechanisms, and surfaces the Hiddens created by knowledge gaps, loss, and distribution asymmetries. It populates the Evidence Ledger and Information Requests with knowledge-location and knowledge-transfer requirements.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tacit-knowledge loss through turnover | Expert leaves; knowledge walks out the door; no documented record; successor must relearn painfully | Knowledge concentrated in person; poor transfer mechanisms; incentive misalignment (expert benefit from knowledge retention); cultural undervaluing of documentation | Loss of operational capability; recovery time and learning curve extended; productivity dips; recurrent failures from knowledge loss |
| Knowledge silo formation | Knowledge concentrates in team or department; access restricted; integration with other knowledge difficult | Specialisation and expertise clustering; incentive to protect specialist status; information asymmetry advantage; poor cross-team communication | Knowledge fragmentation; duplicated learning across silos; missed integration opportunities; resilience decreased by knowledge fragmentation |
| Knowledge archive invisibility | Knowledge exists in archive but nobody knows; retrieval requires extensive search; institutional memory of knowledge location lost | Archive created without indexing or discovery mechanism; knowledge about knowledge is lost; growth makes archive unmaintainable | Knowledge exists but unavailable; duplicate learning occurs; lost opportunity to use existing knowledge; archive becomes liability |
| Knowledge staleness and decay | Knowledge documented but not revisited; assumptions valid at creation now violated; no regular refresh cycles | Knowledge maintenance cost deferred; assumption drift not monitored; absence of review triggers; institutional inertia | Reliance on stale knowledge produces errors; false confidence in outdated understanding; intervention based on expired model; drift undetected |
| Cross-domain knowledge translation failure | Knowledge from one domain misapplied to another; boundary conditions not recognised; context sensitivity ignored | Confidence in knowledge generalisability; failed recognition of domain differences; incentive to leverage existing knowledge without adaptation; insufficient boundary testing | Interventions fail due to domain-specific knowledge requirements; learning doesn't transfer as expected; false positive from successful domain masks boundary failure |
| Expert monopoly and bottleneck | Knowledge concentrated in expert; systems and processes dependent on expert presence; expert availability becomes bottleneck | Knowledge tacit and difficult to transfer; insufficient investment in transfer; expert reluctance to transfer (job security); incentive misalignment | Bottleneck limits scalability; expert loss is crisis event; organisational vulnerability to expert departure; learning and adaptation constrained |
| Knowledge-assumption invisibility | Knowledge carries embedded assumptions valid at time of creation; assumptions become invisible; context changes but knowledge carries old assumptions | Assumption checking occurs once at creation; assumption validity not revisited; institutional inertia and path dependency; low cost of assumption persistence | Knowledge applied with outdated assumptions; decisions rest on violated preconditions; failures surprises; assumptions treated as facts rather than contingencies |
| Knowledge integration failure | Multiple knowledge domains needed but poorly integrated; specialists talk past each other; systems knowledge misses human knowledge and vice versa | Specialisation and silo thinking; poor collaborative mechanisms; translation failures between disciplines; incentive misalignment | System designs miss human factors; human factors analysis misses system effects; interventions fail due to integration gaps; capability ceiling lower than potential |
| Knowledge retrieval when needed failure | Knowledge exists and is accessible but retrieval mechanism fails when needed; time pressure or cognitive load prevents activation; knowledge remains dormant | Knowledge codification insufficient for time-constrained retrieval; cognitive load prevents search; activation mechanisms not pre-designed | Crisis occurs while knowledge remains dormant; post-hoc discovery that knowledge could have prevented failure; learning opportunity missed; knowledge credibility questioned |
| Knowledge deprecation and devaluation | Knowledge becomes old-fashioned; newer knowledge paradigm emerges; old knowledge dismissed as outdated even if still valid in domain | Paradigm shift and Kuhn-cycle dynamics; incentive to adopt new paradigm; sunk costs in old knowledge; authority shifts to new paradigm advocates | Valuable knowledge lost; re-discovery required if new paradigm inadequate; opportunity cost of knowledge loss; vulnerability to paradigm reversal |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What knowledge is held but not active (dormant or poorly retrieved); what knowledge-retrieval blindness exists? |
| II (Systemic) | How do institutional structures embed knowledge dependencies; what knowledge is institutional rather than individual; what is structural knowledge loss? |
| III (Informational) | What knowledge is restricted, sequestered, or not widely accessible; what archive knowledge is invisible due to poor indexing? |
| IV (Temporal) | What knowledge is stale, untested, or carries outdated assumptions; what knowledge maintenance is deferred; what decay is unmonitored? |
| V (Relational) | What expert knowledge is concentrated in individuals; what knowledge asymmetries create power imbalances; what knowledge is withheld due to incentive misalignment? |
| VI (Ontological) | What knowledge paradigm makes other knowledge types unthinkable or invalid; what boundary conditions make knowledge non-generalisable? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms

- Tacit-knowledge invisibility (knowledge in people's heads, not codified)
- Knowledge-silo formation (fragmentation, poor cross-team accessibility)
- Archive invisibility (knowledge exists but discovery mechanisms fail)
- Knowledge-staleness invisibility (outdated knowledge applied as current)
- Expert-monopoly vulnerability (key knowledge concentrated in individuals)
- Cross-domain translation failure (boundary conditions unrecognised)
- Knowledge-assumption invisibility (embedded assumptions become invisible)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tacit-knowledge loss risk | Framework of Knowledge (transfer design); Framework of Learning & Adaptation | Expert interview and documentation; knowledge-transfer mechanism design | Document tacit knowledge; design transfer mechanisms; reduce knowledge concentration |
| Knowledge-silo formation | Framework of Knowledge (integration work); Framework of Systems | Cross-team knowledge audit; translation mechanism design | Create integration touchpoints; cross-training; reduce silo incentives |
| Archive invisibility | Framework of Knowledge (discovery design) | Archive audit; indexing and discovery redesign | Implement search and indexing; create knowledge-location services; maintain archive metadata |
| Knowledge obsolescence | Framework of Knowledge (maintenance); Framework of Learning & Adaptation | Assumption audit; knowledge-refresh scheduling; validity testing | Schedule knowledge reviews; test assumptions; update outdated knowledge; retire superseded knowledge |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Identify critical knowledge domains (3–5) for focal decisions; assess form of knowledge (tacit, explicit, procedural, semantic, episodic).
2. Map knowledge distribution: who holds knowledge; is it concentrated or distributed; is access restricted or open?
3. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: check for tacit-knowledge invisibility, silo formation, archive inaccessibility, knowledge staleness, expert bottlenecks.
4. Identify 2–3 highest-risk knowledge gaps or loss points (likely turnover, outdated assumptions, poor retrieval mechanisms).
5. Flag any expert monopolies or critical tacit-knowledge dependencies for escalation to Learning & Adaptation framework.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Audit all critical knowledge holdings: identify form (tacit/explicit), source, distribution, retrievability, scope, obsolescence-risk; classify by meta-category and core type.
2. Map knowledge dependencies: which critical outcomes depend on which knowledge; what is knowledge-critical path?
3. For each critical knowledge domain, assess: Is it codified or tacit? Concentrated or distributed? Current or stale? Accessible when needed?
4. Identify knowledge gaps, knowledge loss risks (turnover, non-maintenance), knowledge silos, archive accessibility; test knowledge-retrieval mechanisms.
5. Assess cross-domain knowledge integration; identify translation failures or boundary-condition blindness.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: for each critical knowledge domain, identify high-consequence Hiddens; design detection (how to surface gaps) and remediation (how to preserve/transfer/integrate).
7. Produce knowledge-preservation and knowledge-transfer plan; schedule knowledge refreshes; redesign knowledge-access and knowledge-discovery mechanisms.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Evidence Ledger | Record knowledge holdings, form, source, distribution, current status, obsolescence risk for critical knowledge domains | After knowledge audit |
| Information Requests | Request knowledge-transfer engagement; document tacit knowledge; audit knowledge-maintenance schedules | For critical knowledge at high-loss risk |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Propose knowledge-retrieval tests; design knowledge-obsolescence checks; test cross-domain translation | For knowledge with retrieval or staleness concerns |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we audited critical knowledge holdings (form, distribution, retrievability, currency), identified knowledge gaps and loss risks, assessed knowledge-silo formation and cross-domain integration, run Tier 1 Hiddens scan for tacit-knowledge invisibility and expert-monopoly risk, and escalated knowledge-preservation requirements to Learning & Adaptation?
