## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Coordination framework identifies how multiple actors manage or fail to manage the alignment of their actions through explicit protocols, hierarchical direction, market signals, shared conventions, information systems, and trust networks. It classifies coordination mechanisms into six meta-categories and thirty core types; maps how coordination mechanisms fail through scope gaps, reliability degradation, and cost invisibility; and surfaces visibility failures created by assumed coordination, silent misalignment, and orphan risks. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with coordination-failure analysis, enabling downstream frameworks to design coordination interventions and detect where formal authority assumes coordination that does not occur.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emergence & Self-Organisation | Coordination occurs without explicit mechanisms; patterns discovered through trial and error; actors report "it just worked out." | Coordination emerges from interaction; patterns are discovered and repeated; efficient in stable, small groups. | Efficient and adaptive; but invisible and fragile when conditions change or scale increases. |
| Learning & Adaptation | Mechanisms improve through feedback, reflection, redesign; failures trigger root-cause investigation and systematic improvement. | Deliberate review; causes understood; improvements tested; learning documented and shared. | Mechanisms become increasingly effective; requires time and psychological safety; knowledge is preserved. |
| Degradation & Drift | Mechanisms work initially but gradually degrade as actors adapt to overcome friction or as context changes without mechanism updating. | Mechanisms become outdated; actors develop workarounds; degradation invisible until significant failure. | Invisible degradation increases risk; point of no return reached before problems visible. |
| Cascade & Contagion | Coordination failures propagate through interdependent systems; misalignment in one place creates cascading failures in others. | Tight coupling creates high risk; failure effects amplified as they propagate; visibility of cascade risk is low. | Single failure affects multiple systems; recovery requires coordinated action across systems. |
| Oscillation & Overcorrection | Mechanisms create feedback loops that oscillate or overcorrect in response to error signals; can create instability. | Corrections overshoot target; feedback loops create swings between states; dampening mechanisms needed. | Stability difficult to achieve; system repeatedly swings between states. |
| Lock-In & Path Dependence | Early coordination choices become locked in as actors build dependencies; changing mechanisms becomes progressively more difficult. | Switching costs increase; actors build around existing mechanisms; inefficient mechanisms persist despite better alternatives. | Small early decisions have outsized long-term effects; alternatives become difficult to implement. |
| Centralisation & Decentralisation | Coordination evolves between centralised (single authority) and decentralised (distributed authority) models. | Centralisation prioritises consistency and control; decentralisation prioritises local adaptation and responsiveness. | Tension exists between objectives; oscillation between models is common; optimal level varies by context. |
| Crisis Mode & Exceptionalism | Under crisis or time pressure, formal mechanisms break down; actors shift to informal, rapid-response modes. | Formal procedures bypassed; informal coordination becomes primary; speed and flexibility increase at cost of visibility. | Crisis mode more effective than normal mode but creates risks if exceptionalism becomes permanent. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What coordination mechanisms do actors assume exist but do not actually function as expected? |
| II (Systemic) | How do coordination mechanisms at one level create misalignment, coordination gaps, or cascade risks at other levels? |
| III (Informational) | What coordination-critical information is not flowing between actors due to information asymmetries or filtering? |
| IV (Temporal) | How are coordination mechanisms degrading over time, becoming increasingly misaligned with context, or becoming locked in through path dependence? |
| V (Relational) | How are asymmetric coordination relationships creating hidden dependencies or enabling some actors to control information while others are blind? |
| VI (Ontological) | What coordination assumptions treat coordination mechanisms as inevitable or neutral while actually reflecting specific power choices? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Assumed Coordination (actors assume alignment occurs when it does not)
- Silent Misalignment (actors work at cross-purposes without visible conflict)
- Coordination Cost Invisibility (effort absorbed by specific actors who do not report burden)
- Scope Gaps (interdependencies lack explicit coordination; orphan risks remain uncovered)
- Degradation Invisibility (coordination mechanism degrades gradually; sudden failure arrives as surprise)
- Informal Coordination Shadow Governance (informal mechanisms undermine formal authority without visibility)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Assumed coordination masking silent misalignment | Framework of Governance; Framework of Communications | Alignment visibility audit; misalignment detection and surfacing | Make assumptions explicit; test coordination actually occurring; establish information flows enabling detection |
| Scope gaps leaving orphan risks and uncoordinated dependencies | Framework of Risk; Framework of Systems | Dependency mapping; coverage analysis; gap identification | Extend coordination mechanisms to cover gaps; assign responsibility for uncovered interdependencies |
| Coordination cost invisibility and unequal burden distribution | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Resources | Cost accounting and visibility; burden distribution audit | Make coordination costs visible; redistribute unsustainably-concentrated burden; account for invisible labour |
| Degradation and lock-in from path dependence | Framework of Evolution; Framework of Governance | Mechanism effectiveness audit; alternative-design exploration; adaptive-capacity assessment | Refresh mechanisms regularly; reduce switching costs; maintain adaptive capacity for mechanism change |
| Informal mechanisms undermining formal coordination | Framework of Governance; Framework of Power | Formal vs informal mechanism comparison; power-structure analysis | Align informal and formal mechanisms; make informal mechanisms explicit; assess power underlying informality |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Identify focal interdependencies in the scenario: What actions must be aligned? Which actors are interdependent? Where could misalignment cause material harm?
2. For each focal interdependency, identify what coordination mechanisms are in place: explicit (protocols, contracts, standards) or informal (norms, trust, signals)?
3. Assess whether coordination mechanisms are functioning: Do actors assume coordination is occurring? Is there evidence coordination actually happens? Are information flows adequate?
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six Hiddens meta-categories lightly; flag assumed coordination that may not be functioning, scope gaps where no coordination exists, and cost invisibility where some actors absorb unrecognised burden.
5. Shortlist 2–3 highest-consequence coordination failures or risks; note whether failures would be visible to affected actors or hidden until crisis; escalate if high-consequence and high-uncertainty.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Inventory focal interdependencies; map which coordination mechanisms address each (I–VI core types); assess coverage and redundancy for each interdependency.
2. Classify coordination mechanisms for each interdependency: assign to one or more core types (I–VI); assess which types are high-consequence and which are fragile or degraded.
3. For each coordination mechanism, assess five cross-cutting dimensions: Explicitness (formal vs implicit), Scope (full vs partial coverage), Reliability (robust vs brittle under stress), Cost (visible vs invisible burden), Adaptive Capacity (can mechanism evolve?); record dimension profiles.
4. Analyse all eight dynamic patterns; assess which are active or nascent (emergence, learning, degradation, cascade risk, oscillation, lock-in, centralisation/decentralisation, crisis mode); identify which patterns compound effects.
5. Map coordination gaps and scope failures: Which interdependencies lack explicit coordination? Where would failure cascade through networks? What orphan risks are uncovered?
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan (structured deepening): for each high-relevance Hidden (assumed coordination, silent misalignment, scope gaps, cost invisibility, degradation, lock-in, informal-formal misalignment), identify mechanism, detectability, consequence; assign remediation interfaces.
7. Document unresolved Unknowns about coordination effectiveness, cascade risks, and adaptive capacity; apply Escalation Rule; generate Residual Unknowns register.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add coordination-failure Hiddens: assumed coordination, silent misalignment, scope gaps, cost invisibility, degradation, lock-in; tag by meta-category and interdependency. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate cascade risks and high-consequence scope gaps to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record coordination-mechanism classification, dimension assessments, mechanism effectiveness (is coordination actually occurring?), dynamic pattern presence, evidence of misalignment. | After mechanism inventory and dimensional profiling; note gaps requiring misalignment testing and effectiveness validation. |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about coordination failures, cascade risks, and mechanism effectiveness; propose tests (misalignment detection, mechanism functioning validation, stress-testing). | Identified Unknowns; residual Hiddens from Tier 1 scan; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request coordination-mechanism documentation; request evidence of mechanism functioning (alignment data, misalignment reports); request actor perception data. | To reduce Unknowns and validate coordination diagnosis; coordinate with Governance and Communications frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified focal interdependencies, mapped coordination mechanisms addressing each, assessed coverage and functioning, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan across all six meta-categories, and escalated high-consequence coordination risks and scope gaps to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Governance, Risk, Systems)?

## Five Dimensions of Coordination (Profiling Template)

For any coordination mechanism or instance, profile across five dimensions:

| Dimension | Low | High | Questions to ask |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Explicitness & Codification** | Informal, implicit, relational | Written, formalised, unambiguous | Are rules documented or understood implicitly? Can new actors learn from documentation? |
| **Scope & Coverage** | Limited, covering few interdependencies | Comprehensive, covering most dependencies | Which interdependencies are covered? What gaps exist? |
| **Reliability & Robustness** | Brittle, fails under stress or change | Robust, functions under stress and adaptation | Does mechanism fail suddenly or degrade gracefully? Works under pressure/scale change? |
| **Cost & Overhead** | Lightweight, minimal overhead | High effort, significant time/resources | What effort required to maintain? Who bears costs? Are costs visible? |
| **Adaptive Capacity** | Fixed, slow to improve | High feedback loops, rapid learning and improvement | Can mechanism adapt to new conditions? How quickly? |

## Eight Dynamic Patterns (Detection Indicators)

**Emergence & Self-Organisation**: Coordination occurs without design; actors discover patterns; trial-and-error learning; efficient in stable groups but brittle under change.

**Learning & Adaptation**: Failures trigger review; root causes investigated; mechanisms adjusted; improvements implemented; learning documented.

**Degradation & Drift**: Works initially, degrades gradually; actors develop workarounds; misaligned with changed context; invisible until failure; point of no return reached.

**Cascade & Contagion**: Single failure affects downstream systems; effects amplified through interdependencies; tight coupling creates risk; visibility low until imminent failure.

**Oscillation & Overcorrection**: Feedback loops create swings between states; corrections overshoot; oscillation frequency/amplitude increase; stability difficult.

**Lock-In & Path Dependence**: Early choices become locked in; switching costs accumulate; alternatives difficult to implement; inefficient mechanisms persist.

**Centralisation & Decentralisation**: Evolution between centralised (consistency/control) and decentralised (adaptation/responsiveness); tension between objectives; oscillation common.

**Crisis Mode & Exceptionalism**: Formal procedures bypassed under pressure; informal coordination becomes primary; speed increases at cost of visibility/accountability; risk if exceptionalism becomes permanent.

## Three Interface Types (Failure Points)

### C-A: Mechanism-to-Actor
**How coordination rules reach actors and how they understand/commit to following them.**
- **Key risks**: Rules not communicated; actors don't commit; multiple conflicting rules; rules seen as illegitimate; new actors can't learn
- **Design**: Clarity, legitimacy, accessibility, participation, enforcement

### C-B: Actor-to-Actor
**How actors communicate, negotiate, and align actions; often informal and relational.**
- **Key risks**: Inadequate communication; actors don't understand each other's constraints; conflicts unresolved; information asymmetries; relationships break
- **Design**: Transparency, channels, feedback, resolution mechanisms, trust

### C-C: Mechanism-to-System
**How coordination mechanisms affect system properties, performance, and risks.**
- **Key risks**: Bottlenecks created; mechanisms misaligned with system properties; unintended side effects; cascade risks invisible; scalability failures
- **Design**: Alignment with system properties, scalability, visibility, adaptability, efficiency

## Six Hiddens Categories (Tier 1 Scan)

Rate each for visibility risk (Green/Yellow/Red); escalate Yellow/Red to Tier 2 investigation.

### Hidden-A1: Assumed Coordination
Are actors assuming coordination occurs when it does not? Brittle dependencies on invisible/failed handoffs.
- **High risk signals**: Informal handoffs, unstated assumptions, implicit dependencies, no historical testing
- **Test method**: Trace handoff, verify functioning, test under stress

### Hidden-A2: Silent Misalignment
Actors work at cross-purposes without visible conflict. Unmanaged interdependencies, different assumptions.
- **High risk signals**: Incomplete coordination mechanisms, limited actor awareness, no alignment verification
- **Test method**: Test actor understanding of each other's actions/constraints, compare assumptions

### Hidden-A3: Coordination Cost Invisibility
Effort to maintain coordination absorbed by specific actors without visibility to system leadership.
- **High risk signals**: No coordination cost tracking, overhead in specific teams, unclear who pays for coordination
- **Test method**: Inventory coordination activities, estimate effort, track who absorbs costs

### Hidden-A4: Failed Coordination Reframed as Individual Failure
System coordination failure attributed to individual incompetence rather than mechanism failure.
- **High risk signals**: Culture attributing failures to people not systems, no root-cause investigation, blame-driven responses
- **Test method**: Review failure investigations, check for systemic vs individual framing

### Hidden-A5: Information Asymmetry Coordination Failures
Selective/asymmetric information creates systematic misalignment without actors realising it.
- **High risk signals**: Information access disparities, strategic withholding, different understandings of shared state
- **Test method**: Map information access, compare understandings, check for selective alignment patterns

### Hidden-A6: Dynamic Coordination Drift
Mechanisms work initially but degrade as conditions change; drift invisible until failure.
- **High risk signals**: No historical tracking of mechanism status, no early-warning signals, degradation invisible
- **Test method**: Track mechanism status over time, compare to baseline, identify trend indicators

## Seven-Step Coordination Audit Protocol (Execution Flow)

### Step 1: Situation Clarification
Define the coordination problem (which interdependencies matter?), establish scope/boundaries, identify actors and dependencies, clarify success criteria.

**Prompt**: "Describe the system boundary, key actors, critical interdependencies, and the coordination problem or question in scope."

### Step 2: Coordination Mechanism Inventory
Map all formal (procedures, standards, contracts, reporting lines) and informal (relationships, norms, conventions) mechanisms.

**Prompt**: "For each critical interdependency, identify what coordination mechanisms (if any) are supposed to manage it. Distinguish formal from informal."

### Step 3: Dependency Mapping & Scope Assessment
Identify all interdependencies, classify each (critical vs non, tight vs loose, sync vs async), assess mechanism-dependency alignment.

**Prompt**: "Map which interdependencies have coordination mechanisms and which lack any. Where is mechanism scope misaligned with dependency scope?"

### Step 4: Alignment Audit
For key interdependencies, test whether coordination actually occurs. Test assumed coordination. Probe for silent misalignment. Compare stated to actual practice.

**Prompt**: "For 3–5 critical interdependencies, test: (1) Is the coordination mechanism actually working? (2) Are actors assuming it works when it doesn't? (3) Are actors working at cross-purposes? (4) Does actual practice match stated procedures?"

### Step 5: Coordination Failure Diagnosis
For observed/suspected misalignment, diagnose root cause. Is it mechanism-failure or assumption-failure? Mechanism-design flaw or implementation gap?

**Prompt**: "For each observed misalignment, diagnose: What coordination mechanism was supposed to prevent it? Why did it fail? Is this isolated or systematic?"

### Step 6: Hiddens Scan (Tier 1 minimum)
Conduct Tier 1 scan across six Hiddens categories. Escalate Yellow/Red to Tier 2.

**Prompt**: "For each of the six Hiddens categories (A1–A6), assess: Is this type of Hidden likely to be present in this scenario? Evidence? Risk level (Green/Yellow/Red)?"

### Step 7: Coordination Reform & Implementation Planning
Design reform options, assess trade-offs, prioritise by impact/feasibility, build implementation roadmap.

**Prompt**: "For each identified failure or Hidden, design 2–3 coordination reform options. What are the trade-offs? What is the implementation roadmap?"

## Disambiguation: Nearest Neighbours

**Coordination vs Governance**: Use Coordination when multiple actors' actions must align but authority is unclear or informal; want to understand informal coordination bypassing formal authority. Use Governance for formal authority, decision rights, oversight.

**Coordination vs Systems**: Use Coordination when interdependencies create coordination challenges; mapping how actors interact and depend on each other. Use Systems for system properties, feedback loops, emergent behaviour.

**Coordination vs Processes**: Use Coordination to audit whether process design enables coordination; coordination handoffs embedded in workflows. Use Processes to design workflow steps and task sequences.

## Routing Triggers (When to Invoke)

- Multiple independent actors must coordinate but integration is poor or failing
- Actors report surprise about what others are doing; misalignment is suspected
- A coordination mechanism is failing; root cause is unclear
- Handoff failures or interface failures occurring; coordination burden is invisible
- Information asymmetries or selective alignment are suspected
- Coordination costs are high, hidden, or borne unevenly
- System outcomes poor despite good intentions; coordination failure suspected
- Cascade failures or contagion occurring; coordination interdependencies creating risk

## F/I/A/U Tagging Discipline (Mandatory for LLM)

Tag all coordination claims:
- **F (Fact)**: Directly observed or recorded (e.g., "Mechanism X does not have written documentation")
- **I (Interpretation)**: Analysis or inference from evidence (e.g., "The lack of documentation suggests actors rely on implicit understanding")
- **A (Assumption)**: Accepted as true to proceed (e.g., "We assume actors have met to discuss alignment")
- **U (Unknown)**: Explicitly unknown; uncertainty acknowledged (e.g., "Whether mechanism X has ever failed is unknown; no incident records available")

**Rule**: Explicitly flag all A and U tags. Do not collapse disagreements into false consensus. Escalate unresolved disagreements.
