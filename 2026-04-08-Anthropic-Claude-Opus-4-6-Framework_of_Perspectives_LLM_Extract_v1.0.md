## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Perspectives framework identifies how different agents interpret the same scenario through structured vantage points shaped by position, knowledge, time, scale, orientation, and method; classifies perspectives into six meta-categories and 32 core types; maps how perspectives reveal and conceal different realities; and surfaces positional blindness, perspective suppression, and false consensus that prevent robust understanding. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with perspective-specific visibility failures and enables downstream frameworks to design multi-perspectival decision-making and governance.

## Section 4: Condensed Core Taxonomy

| Meta-category | Core types (names only) | Diagnostic question (1 sentence) |
|---|---|---|
| I. Positional Perspectives | First-person, Second-person, Third-person, Participant, Observer, Insider, Outsider | Where is the observer positioned relative to the phenomenon, and what does that structural location reveal and conceal? |
| II. Epistemic Perspectives | Expert, Novice, Disciplinary, Transdisciplinary, Tacit | What domain knowledge, methods, and evidence standards shape what this perspective can know and how it judges validity? |
| III. Temporal Perspectives | Present-oriented, Retrospective, Prospective, Developmental, Cyclical | What time orientation and trajectory-framing shapes interpretation of what is happening and why? |
| IV. Scalar Perspectives | Local, Global, Micro, Macro, Multi-scalar | What level-of-analysis and scale of observation reveals or conceals different system dynamics and interactions? |
| V. Orientational Perspectives | Interested, Disinterested, Sympathetic, Critical, Appreciative | What disposition toward the phenomenon (stakes, sympathy, criticism) shapes priority-setting and outcome evaluation? |
| VI. Methodological Perspectives | Synthetic, Analytic, Theoretical, Practical, Hermeneutic | What approach to analysis—synthesis vs decomposition, theory vs practice, interpretation vs explanation—governs reasoning and standards of proof? |

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Perspective Lock-In | Analysis trapped in single lens without recognising partiality; claims appear certain because alternatives are invisible. | Default perspective is framed as neutral/objective; competing perspectives are dismissed as bias or opinion; no systematic perspective-shifting practice. | Blind spots unexamined; alternatives not tested; brittleness to shocks; false consensus masking disagreement; missed learning opportunities. |
| False Neutrality & Hidden Position | Analysis claims to occupy no perspective while operating from a particular vantage point; position is invisible to analyst. | Analyst assumes analysis itself is position-independent; unnamed assumptions treated as facts; evidence selection appears objective when perspective-guided. | Policy driven by unstated perspective; resistance misattributed to irrationality rather than different valid perspective; decisions fail when stakeholder realities differ. |
| Positional Invisibility | Different positions literally see different realities; gap is not recognised; analyses proceed as if single reality is obvious. | Insider/outsider, participant/observer, leader/operator gaps create structural asymmetries of visibility; each assumes their view is "how things are." | Miscommunication and talked-past dialogue; policy failures when designer's perspective doesn't match operator's reality; surprise at stakeholder resistance. |
| Epistemic Mismatch & Incommensurability | Evidence standards, knowledge admissibility rules, and methods differ across perspectives; claimed disagreement is unresolvable by more data. | Different disciplines, expertise levels, and methods embody different definitions of valid evidence; attempting data solution to meaning problem. | Conflict treated as factual when it is epistemic; evidence piling up without resolution; dismissal of valid perspectives as unscientific/biased/naive. |
| Perspective Suppression & Silencing | Some perspectives are systematically excluded from dialogue or governance; safe-to-say differs materially from true. | Power asymmetries, incentive misalignments, or psychological safety deficits enable institutional capture of framing and perspective privilege. | Low-power perspectives remain hidden until crisis; decision-making unaware of critical constraints or risks; implementation failure when suppressed realities re-emerge. |
| Orientation & Value Capture | A particular orientation (interested vs disinterested; critical vs appreciative; short-term vs long-term) is privileged as though it were neutral or more valid. | Governance, incentives, or cultural norms favour certain orientations; other orientations are delegitimised or treated as bias. | Single-dimensional optimisation misses trade-offs; long-term risks hidden by short-termism or long-term vision dismissed as impractical; asymmetric risk coverage. |
| Scalar Collapse & Aggregation Illusion | Local variation and constraints are flattened by macro models; or macro dynamics are missed by local optimisation. | Macro view treats local as noise; local view misses portfolio effects; no cross-scalar integration discipline. | Local reality contradicts macro model; macro policy fails in implementation; cascade failures when local coupling is invisible to centre; scalability surprises. |
| Methodological Boundary Closure | Different methodologies (quantitative vs qualitative; analysis vs synthesis; hermeneutic vs reductionist) are treated as mutually exclusive rather than complementary. | Disciplinary boundaries are enforced as gatekeeping; legitimacy attaches to one method; other methods are categorised as non-rigorous or non-evidence-based. | Important insights available through non-privileged methods are dismissed; blind spots where methodology cannot see; false precision in chosen method; missed integration. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What perspectives are structurally excluded from visibility or access; what is invisible from certain positions that others take for granted; who cannot easily be heard? |
| II (Systemic) | What incentives or governance structures systematically privilege some perspectives and suppress others; whose voice is amplified and whose is muted by the system? |
| III (Informational) | What information asymmetries exist across perspectives; what do some perspectives know that others cannot access; where is critical information withheld, fragmented, or distorted? |
| IV (Temporal) | What perspectives are being sidelined because they operate on different time horizons; whose urgency is being prioritised and whose timeline is being overridden; what temporal lag or myopia is driving perspective conflict? |
| V (Relational) | What power asymmetries and relationship structures shape which perspectives are safe to hold and voice; where is safe-to-say materially different from true? |
| VI (Ontological) | What categories and ontologies do different perspectives take for granted; are fundamental definitions of reality, causation, or value shared or radically different; where are perspectives talking past each other rather than disagreeing on facts? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Perspectival Lock-In (analysis trapped in single lens; partiality invisible)
- False Neutrality (claiming view from nowhere while operating from particular vantage point)
- Category Neglect (ignoring whole meta-categories of perspectives)
- Incommensurability Assumption (treating perspectives as fundamentally incomparable rather than translatable)
- Positional Obscuring (insider/outsider, operator/leader, participant/observer gaps render certain realities structurally invisible)
- Perspective Suppression (low-power perspectives silenced by governance or incentives)
- Epistemic Filtering (knowledge standards act as gatekeepers; certain evidence types dismissed as non-rigorous)
- Orientation & Framing Capture (particular value stances or methodological approaches privileged as universal or objective)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Perspective lock-in; false neutrality | Framework of Hiddens; Framework of Evidence | Type A (Dialogue): explicit six-category perspective scan; perspective rotation exercises; bring in contrasting views | Implement mandatory perspective-shifting in analysis; require F/I/A/U tagging per perspective; document position and assumptions |
| Positional invisibility (insider/outsider, operator/leader gaps) | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Governance | Type B (Translation): paired analysis; translation guides between roles; mediation protocols; escalation rules | Design dialogue protocols forcing each position to articulate what the other cannot see; embed translation in governance |
| Epistemic mismatch and incommensurability | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Diagnosis | Type A/B (Dialogue + Translation): evidence standards workshops; bridging documents translating between disciplines; multi-method evidence requirements | Create evidence-standard bridge documents; require plural evidence for high-stakes claims; establish translation mediators |
| Perspective suppression and silencing | Framework of Governance; Framework of Hiddens (Tier 2) | Type A (Dialogue): psychological safety audits; anonymous surveys; protected escalation channels; confidential interviews | Redesign governance to require plural perspectives; create dissent channels; protect minority perspectives; assign perspective-holders authority |
| Value and orientation conflicts | Framework of Decisions; Framework of Governance | Type C (Conflict): explicit value mapping; trade-off documentation; arbitration rules; governance requiring complementary orientations | Surface value differences as legitimate, not factual; document trade-offs; design decisions that require multiple orientations; prevent single orientation dominance |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. List focal stakeholder groups and roles in the scenario; identify 4–6 key perspectives distributed across roles.
2. Assign each perspective to meta-categories (I–VI) and note which categories are represented and which are missing; flag obvious category neglect.
3. For each focal perspective, identify what it reveals (visibility gain) and what it conceals (blind spot); note key assumptions and evidence standards.
4. Map obvious perspective conflicts and dialogue/translation/conflict interface needs; identify 1–2 highest-consequence interface risks.
5. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six Hiddens meta-categories lightly; flag obvious positional invisibility, perspective suppression, or false neutrality.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Create comprehensive perspective register: identify all relevant perspectives held by stakeholder cohort; classify by role, position, knowledge, time orientation, scale, orientation, methodology.
2. Profile each of 6–10 key perspectives across five dimensions (Accessibility, Stability, Partiality, Reflexivity, Shareability); assess compatibility and interface requirements.
3. Analyse all five perspective operations (Shift, Bracket, Triangulate, Embed, Fuse); determine which operations are needed to reduce lock-in and improve integration.
4. Map all three interface types (Type A: Dialogue for mutual learning; Type B: Translation for lossy meaning transfer; Type C: Conflict for incompatibility/arbitration); identify risks and remediation strategies.
5. Analyse eight dynamic patterns; for each key perspective, determine which patterns are active (nascent, present, high-risk) and mechanisms driving them.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan (structured deepening): for each high-relevance Hidden (e.g., positional invisibility, epistemic filtering, framing capture), identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; assign detection/remediation interfaces.
7. Design multi-perspectival synthesis: document what perspectives agree on, where they diverge and why, which disagreements are factual/value/positional, how complementary views will be integrated in governance.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add shortlist of perspective-specific Hiddens (positional/epistemic/framing obscuring; suppression; false neutrality) with meta-category tag (I–VI), mechanism, detectability, consequence. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate any high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hidden to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record which perspectives hold key claims; evidence standards per perspective; confidence per perspective; competing claims across perspectives; evidence admissibility rules. | After perspective classification and dimension profiling; note gaps and incommensurabilities. |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about perspective differences (rooted in fact vs value vs position vs method); propose tests (dialogue prompts, evidence standards audits, cross-perspective interviews, replication with contrasting perspectives). | Identified perspective conflicts and Tier 1 Hiddens triggers; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request stakeholder interviews (structured prompts keyed to six categories); governance documents (decision authority, evidence standards, escalation rules); evidence by perspective; artefacts from each perspective-holder. | To reduce Unknowns and validate perspective inventory; coordinate with Evidence and Governance frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified focal stakeholder perspectives, classified them across six meta-categories, assessed critical dimensions (Accessibility, Stability, Partiality, Reflexivity, Shareability), mapped dialogue/translation/conflict interfaces, run Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-consequence Unknowns to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Evidence, Governance)?

## Section 8: Cross-Framework Routing Pointers

### 8a. Upstream Inputs (frameworks commonly feeding into this one)

- Situations (situation type and classification determine which perspectives are relevant)
- Boundaries (geographic, jurisdictional, organisational boundaries create perspective divides)
- Scale & Time (scenario scale and timeline shape which perspectives matter and their time horizons)
- Agents & Personas (who the stakeholders are determines which perspective types are represented)
- Evidence (what is known and unknown; evidence base available to each perspective)
- Power & Institutions (who has authority and incentive power; shapes which perspectives are privileged)

### 8b. Downstream Handoffs (frameworks commonly consuming this framework's outputs)

- Framework of Evidence (evidence standards vary by perspective; multi-perspective evidence audit)
- Framework of Hiddens (perspective-driven visibility failures; detection/remediation via dialogue/translation/conflict)
- Framework of Diagnosis (multi-perspectival root-cause analysis; perspective-dependent causal attribution)
- Framework of Decisions (perspective requirements for robust decision-making; plural governance design)
- Framework of Governance (perspective-inclusive decision processes; dissent channels; plural evidence requirements)
- Framework of Interventions (perspective-driven implementation risk; translation requirements for uptake)
- Framework of Learning & Adaptation (perspective-aware post-mortems; multi-perspectival feedback loops)
