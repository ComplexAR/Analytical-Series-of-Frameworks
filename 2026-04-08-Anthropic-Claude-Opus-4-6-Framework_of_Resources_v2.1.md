# Introduction

## What is Resources?

In any situation, agents (individuals, teams, organisations) can influence outcomes only through resources they can draw upon. Resources are the practical substrate of action: what can be mobilised to change states, build capacity, meet constraints, or seize opportunities. In narrow usage, "resources" means budget and headcount. In this framework, resources extend far beyond. They include:

- Tangible assets (facilities, equipment, materials, cash)
- Capabilities and expertise (skills, experience, tacit knowledge, human attention)
- Information and analytical tools (data, models, decision frameworks, scanning capacity)
- Relationships and social capital (networks, trust, reputation, alliances)
- Structural and institutional levers (authority, legal powers, market position, mandates, organisational design)
- Temporal and optionality assets (time windows, schedule slack, contingency capacity, flexibility, options, adaptive capacity)

The reason resource mapping is analytically essential is that feasibility and constraint both live here. An organisation with unclear resource baselines will misidentify where constraints truly bind, allocate capital to non-bottlenecks, discover infeasibility late, and miss optionality. Intangible resources (trust, slack, morale, learning capacity) often erode silently—invisible until crisis. And distributed knowledge means no single actor sees the full resource picture, creating coordination failures and vulnerability to hidden exhaustion.

## Why does Resources matter for Hiddens work?

Resource analysis is uniquely vulnerable to visibility failures. Constraints hide in multiple ways:
- **Systemic** — bottlenecks are buried in aggregated dashboards; fragmented ownership means no one sees the full constraint.
- **Informational** — intangible resources (trust, slack, morale) are poorly measured, so depletion is invisible until acute.
- **Temporal** — resource erosion is slow; momentum masks approaching exhaustion; decision windows close before risks surface.
- **Relational** — power shapes what resources are visible and valued; politically inconvenient constraints are suppressed; informal networks that actually enable work are ignored in formal reporting.
- **Ontological** — dominant accounting frames hide certain resource classes entirely (e.g., "reputational debt," "time poverty," "option death").

This framework surfaces these hiding mechanisms by forcing explicit inventory across all six categories, by profiling resources along five dimensions (including visibility), by tracking dynamics (trajectories that signal hidden depletion), and by applying a mandatory tiered Hiddens scan to catch what analysis naturally misses.

## What does this framework produce?

This framework produces a complete resource map: a 6×5 taxonomy populated with the specific resources available to focal agents; dimension profiles showing which resources are fungible, controllable, visible, and durable; dynamic patterns showing accumulation, depletion, conversion, and lock-in trajectories; and interfaces showing how resources are accessed, allocated, converted, and constrained.

These artefacts integrate into the broader investigation via:
- **Upstream inputs** from Situations, Evidence, Causation, and Systems frameworks (what exists, what changed, what drives change).
- **Integration** with Decisions, Interventions, Governance, and Responsibility (translating resource maps into feasibility judgments and governance moves).
- **Hiddens work** — the mandatory Tier 1 scan catches visibility failures specific to resources; Tier 2 deepening prioritises high-impact hidden depletions; Tier 3 escalation applies full-spectrum taxonomy if concealment or cascades are suspected.
- **Standard registers** — Resource Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests, and Residual Unknowns feed the shared investigation discipline.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework when resource feasibility, constraint identification, capacity planning, or intangible depletion is decision-critical. Typical triggers include:
- Pre-intervention feasibility assessment
- Transformation or change programme capacity planning
- Bottleneck and constraint diagnostics
- Resilience and optionality assessment (how robust? how reversible?)
- Post-failure root cause (often hidden resource exhaustion)
- Governance and authority alignment (responsibility without resources)

**Default execution depth**: Light Depth by default (quick resource inventory, top constraints, baseline dimension scan). Escalate to Full Depth when bottleneck location changes decisions, when feasibility is contested, when intangible depletion is suspected, or when cascading constraint failures are possible.

**Integration with the iterative loop** (OG §5.0): Resources analysis occupies the Middle stage, consuming Upstream inputs (Situations, Evidence, Systems, Causation) and feeding Downstream decisions, interventions, and governance. When routed by the Operating Guide's decision tree (§4.3), run with minimum G2+G6 coverage; include G1 for boundary/scope; include G5 for evidence/hiddens checks.

**Tier 1 Hiddens scan** is mandatory at least twice—early (after initial framing) and pre-closure (after tests and table population)—to catch visibility failures before closure. If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism (e.g., Suppression, Temporal Drift, Uncertainty Masking), invoke the appropriate Targeted Scan (OG §7.5.3 Triage Matrix).

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Resources are the practical substrate through which agents influence outcomes. No action is possible without resources; no feasibility assessment is valid without resource mapping. This framework provides a repeatable method to:

1. **Inventory resources comprehensively** across six meta-categories and thirty core types (Material & Financial; Human & Capability; Informational & Analytical; Relational & Social Capital; Structural & Institutional; Temporal, Optionality & Resilience).

2. **Characterise critical resources along five dimensions**: what kind of resource it is (fungibility); how controllable and renewable (control/regeneration); how it scales and transfers (scalability); how visible and measurable it is (visibility); and how durable or reversible (durability).

3. **Understand resource dynamics** — patterns of accumulation, depletion, conversion, complementarity, and lock-in that shape trajectories and reveal hidden exhaustion.

4. **Map interfaces** (who accesses resources? how are they allocated? what conversion frictions exist? where do bottlenecks and constraints bind?).

5. **Identify feasibility constraints** and design **resource moves** (protect, grow, acquire, reallocate, convert, wind down, buffer) with explicit governance, monitoring, and renewal strategies.

6. **Surface hidden resource failures** through mandatory tiered Hiddens analysis (Tier 1 six-category scan, Tier 2 deepening of top Hiddens, Tier 3 escalation for high-stakes or concealment-suspected cases).

Because resource analysis is uniquely vulnerable to invisibility (intangibles unmeasured, dashboards gamed, fragmented knowledge, delayed depletion), this framework includes an embedded Hiddens discipline. Resource moves that appear feasible often fail because hidden constraints were missed or intangible depletion was undetected. The framework embeds defences against these failures through structured hiddens sources analysis, tiered Hiddens scanning, and mandatory disclosure of residual unknowns.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definition of Resources**: Anything that can be drawn upon by an agent in a given situation to influence realities, processes, and outcomes. Resources include physical assets, capabilities, information, relationships, institutional levers, time, slack, options, and resilience. Resources are agent-relative (resources for whom? to do what?) and situation-dependent (a resource in one context may not be in another).

**In scope**:
- Six meta-categories and thirty core resource types (canonical 6×5 taxonomy)
- Five cross-cutting resource dimensions (fungibility, control/regeneration, scalability, visibility, durability)
- Resource dynamics patterns (accumulation, depletion, conversion, complementarity, lock-in, mutation)
- Resource interfaces (access, allocation, conversion, constraint/bottleneck mechanisms)
- Hiddens sources, tiered Hiddens scans (Tier 1–3), and Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scans (OG §7.5)
- Resource moves and governance discipline
- Monitoring, review cadence, and renewal strategy

**Out of scope**:
- Full intervention and change management catalogue (see Interventions framework)
- Detailed governance and accountability allocation (see Governance and Responsibility frameworks)
- Domain-specific financial/accounting standards and compliance (use finance and assurance standards as inputs)
- Technology selection and implementation (see Systems framework)

**Common confusions ("Resources are not …")**:
- Not only budget or headcount; intangibles and soft constraints matter equally
- Not static snapshots; resources are trajectories with dynamics
- Not synonymous with capability (capability is one resource type; resources are broader)
- Not necessarily owned (some are shared, borrowed, navigable, or environment-provided)
- Not infinitely fungible (constraints and conversion frictions are real)

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions

### I. Material & Financial Resources
Tangible assets, money, and physical/environmental inputs that directly enable activity and create hard constraints. This category encompasses everything with material form: buildings, equipment, technology, consumables, and financial capital. It also includes access—to suppliers, financing, and natural resources. Material constraints are often most visible (you can count them), but their capacity is often overestimated (peak utilisation rates, duty cycles, maintenance requirements). Financial resources are both enabling (capital to acquire other resources) and constraining (cash flow limits, cost-of-carry, sunk costs that lock in prior commitments).

**Diagnostic cues**: Facility utilisation reports; equipment schedules; inventory levels; cash position; budget spend; supplier lead times; procurement cycles.

**Typical failure mode**: Paper budget exceeds real spending power (committed costs, inflation, friction); equipment rated capacity exceeds sustained throughput (peak vs duty cycle); financial constraints discovered late (cash flow crisis despite positive P&L).

### II. Human & Capability Resources
Skills, expertise, attention, motivation, and health that determine what humans can do, at what quality, and for how long. This category includes both codified expertise (certifications, experience, knowledge of methods) and tacit know-how that lives only in people. It includes psychological and physical capacity—energy, focus, motivation, health, resilience to stress—that shape whether people can sustain effort. Availability (full-time equivalent, turnover, absences) is a critical constraint. So is the quality of execution and innovation that emerges from engaged, capable teams.

**Diagnostic cues**: Skill inventories; training and development pipelines; utilisation rates and backlog; turnover and absence; employee engagement surveys; quality metrics and rework; innovation velocity.

**Typical failure mode**: Key person dependencies hidden; tacit knowledge lost to turnover; capability gaps masked by heroic effort; burnout latent until sudden departures; team knowledge fragmented across sites or shifts.

### III. Informational & Analytical Resources
Data, knowledge artefacts, analytical tools, and scanning capacity that determine sensemaking and decision quality. This category includes raw data (numbers, evidence), codified knowledge (playbooks, best practices, lessons learned), analytical capacity (teams, software, methodologies), and the scanning and learning systems that keep understanding current. It is often underestimated as a constraint—decisions are only as good as the information that informs them, yet information gathering and analysis are chronically under-resourced.

**Diagnostic cues**: Data availability and latency; analytical backlog; decision support tool coverage; metadata and documentation; knowledge management system maturity; research and analysis team capacity.

**Typical failure mode**: Dashboards measure what is easy, not what is important (metrics game); analysis lags behind decisions (too little, too late); key data is unavailable or proprietary; institutional knowledge is scattered and hard to find; analytical capacity is consumed by firefighting.

### IV. Relational & Social Capital Resources
Relationships, trust, reputation, and the collaborative and influence channels that enable coordination and power. This category includes formal networks (governance, supply chains, partnerships) and informal networks (friendships, communities of practice, trusted advisors). Trust is the critical intangible—it enables fast decision-making without heavy oversight; it signals credibility; it allows requests for help without detailed justification. Reputation compounds trust; it creates opportunity. Diversity of connections reduces groupthink and increases resilience.

**Diagnostic cues**: Organisational network analysis; relationship strength and diversity; trust surveys; influence mapping; collaboration tools and patterns; community health metrics; cross-boundary connector identification.

**Typical failure mode**: Trust eroded silently (no one notices until crisis); key connectors burn out or leave; information silos form (insularity); informal power networks bypass formal governance; diversity declines (homophily); network fragmentation during stress.

### V. Structural & Institutional Resources
Authority, legal powers, mandates, governance structures, and positional advantage that enable formal action and change. This category includes the rules and institutions that shape what is permitted and what has standing—regulatory authority, property rights, decision rights, board-level sponsorship, platform control, market position. These resources are often taken for granted until they are missing (no sponsorship, no legal authority, market position eroded). They are also often contested (whose authority trumps whose? where do mandates overlap or conflict?).

**Diagnostic cues**: Governance structure and decision rights; regulatory approval paths; stakeholder mandates and constraints; organisational design and clarity; contractual rights and obligations; market position and competitive dynamics.

**Typical failure mode**: Authority fragmented or unclear (responsibility without power); sponsorship withdrawn (political cover lost); mandate narrow (constrained scope); positional advantage eroded (market dynamics, regulation); institutional rules work against desired outcomes (perverse incentives, siloed accountability).

### VI. Temporal, Optionality & Resilience Resources
Time windows, flexibility, slack, options, and adaptive capacity that shape how robust and reversible action is. This category includes schedule flexibility (can we move a deadline?), contingency budgets (can we spend more if needed?), backup systems (what happens if this fails?), and pivot options (what if our assumption is wrong?). It includes the learning and adaptation capacity that allows course correction. Slack is the critical enabler—without slack, there is no resilience; every constraint is binding; failure cascades.

**Diagnostic cues**: Schedule criticality and float analysis; contingency reserves and burn rate; redundancy and backup capacity; capability to pivot or experiment; learning velocity and feedback cycles; stress-test results and shock resilience.

**Typical failure mode**: Slack eliminated for efficiency (all constraints binding); schedule critical path tightens over time (no float for course correction); contingency budgets consumed early (none left for surprises); backup systems under-invested (failure of primary system cascades); learning infrastructure neglected (organisations repeat mistakes).

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy
| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | The 6×5 baseline is stable and exhaustive for most contexts. Extensions are domain-specific refinements, not fundamental additions. |
| Domain-specific refinement | Add subtypes within a meta-category (e.g., "AI/ML expertise" within Technical Expertise) if domain-important and distinct. Maintain explicit mapping back to canonical type. |
| Preserving mappability | Every extended type must map to exactly one canonical type. Do not create types that span multiple categories. |
| Structural invariants | Do not add meta-categories without major version bump. Do not reduce meta-categories. Preserve 5:1 ratio of types to categories where feasible. |
| Periodic reassessment | Reassess extensions every 12–24 months or after major organisational change (merger, disruption, etc.). Consolidate extensions that prove sticky; deprecate those not used. |
| Versioning & governance | Extensions are v[X].[y] updates (minor versions). Major rewrites (v[X+1].0) require review of canonical mapping and framework integration. |

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Resource

## 4.1 Dimensions Table
| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. **Fungibility (replaceability & interchangeability)** | How easily can this resource be substituted or reallocated to different uses or agents? | Low (specialist expertise, key relationships, specific authority): hard to substitute. Medium (generalised skills, budget, generic capabilities): some substitution. High (money, time, generic labour, standard equipment): readily fungible. | Interview agents: "If we lose this resource, what alternatives exist?" Check substitutability and cost/quality of alternatives. | Fungible resources can be reallocated; non-fungible resources create bottlenecks and dependencies. Guides constraint-relief strategy. |
| 2. **Control & Regeneration (locus of control; renewal rate)** | How much control does the focal agent have over the resource? How does it renew or degrade? | Fully controlled (owned; can invest to grow); Partially controlled (influence but not ownership; renewal depends on others); Uncontrolled (environment-provided or outside control; depletes on use). | Interview: "Do we control this? Can we grow it? What determines renewal?" Check ownership, investment authority, regeneration rate. | Controlled resources can be grown; uncontrolled ones must be protected or conserved. Guides investment and optionality strategy. |
| 3. **Scalability (how does it respond to volume)** | How much can the resource expand under growth? Are there economies or diseconomies of scale? | Scales linearly (double investment → double output: money, labour). Scales with friction (doubling requires >2x investment or cost: expertise, relationships, authority). Sub-linear or threshold effects (hard scaling limits: key person, fixed facility capacity). | Scenario test: "If we double output, can this resource double?" Identify friction and hard limits. | Resources with scaling friction become bottlenecks under growth. Guides capacity planning and constraint relief. |
| 4. **Visibility & Measurability (how observable and quantifiable)** | How easy is it to see the state and changes in this resource? How well can it be measured? | Highly visible & measurable (cash, headcount, equipment): objective, real-time measurement. Partially visible (skills, trust, slack): proxy measurement, subjective. Low visibility (intangibles, informal networks, slack consumption): hard to observe until acute. | Collect metrics or proxies for top resources. Ask: "How do we know we have enough of this? What signals depletion?" | Invisible resources are vulnerable to silent depletion. Guides monitoring and early-warning instrumentation. |
| 5. **Durability & Reversibility (longevity; reversibility of depletion)** | How long-lasting is the resource? If depleted, how quickly can it recover? | Durable (physical assets, financial capital, authority): long-lasting, slow to degrade, recovery takes time. Moderately durable (skills, relationships, reputation): sustains effort but erodes with neglect, recovery months–years. Fragile (slack, morale, trust, contingency): erodes rapidly, recovery slow (months–years). | Historical analysis: "How long does this resource last? How quickly does it degrade?" Reference restoration projects and burnout recovery. | Fragile resources require active protection and renewal. Guides resilience and monitoring strategy. |

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table
| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Accumulation** (resource grows over time, compounding) | Resource stock increases; positive feedback (growth begets growth); examples: financial capital earning interest, relationships deepening with time, expertise accumulating through repetition, brand building through consistency. | Positive feedback loops; reinvestment of returns; experience curve improvement; network effects. | Enables optionality and resilience; allows compound growth and capabilities. Identifies where reinvestment is critical. | Reinvest returns; maintain compound growth investment; protect accumulation infrastructure (brand, relationships, learning systems). |
| 2 | **Depletion** (resource decreases over time, often invisibly) | Resource stock decreases; negative feedback loops; examples: trust eroded by broken commitments, slack consumed by firefighting, expertise lost to turnover, contingencies exhausted in crises. | Consumption rate > renewal rate; unrenewed draw-downs; opportunity cost of use; entropy and decay. | Explains latent fragility; signals need for renewal investment. Hidden depletion is the highest-risk pattern. | Renewal investment; reduce consumption rate; restore balance between use and regeneration; early-warning instrumentation. |
| 3 | **Conversion** (resource transforms into another type, often lossy) | One resource type is used to create another; examples: money buys expertise, expertise creates authority, time spent on relationships builds trust. Conversions often involve friction and loss. | Exchange and trade-offs; leveraging; opportunity cost; conversion friction (taxes, spoilage, waste). | Explains constraint shifts and enables intervention (what leverage points exist?). Also reveals where conversion is expensive (friction). | Identify low-friction conversions; invest in conversion mechanisms; reduce friction where possible; accept high-friction conversions only when necessary. |
| 4 | **Complementarity & Lock-In** (resources that require each other; mutual dependencies that limit optionality) | Resources become tightly coupled; one cannot be deployed without the other; examples: specialised expertise requires specific tools, market position requires continuous brand investment, infrastructure investment locks in strategy. | Coupling and dependency; sunk costs; strategic alignment; ecosystem lock-in. | Reveals constraints and limits optionality; explains stickiness and path dependence. High-stakes resource decisions create lock-in. | Understand lock-in implications before decisions; maintain flexibility and optionality; diversify and de-couple where feasible; plan for lock-in reversal cost. |
| 5 | **Mutation & Obsolescence** (resource changes in character or becomes irrelevant) | The resource is fundamentally altered by external change; examples: technological disruption makes expertise obsolete, market shift reduces brand value, regulation changes structural advantage. Mutations are often sudden and hard to predict. | Technological change; market shift; regulatory change; paradigm shift; competitor action. | Explains strategic vulnerability; signals need for adaptation and renewal. Mutation risk is highest for lock-in resources. | Monitor external change for mutation risks; invest in adaptation capability; maintain multiple pathways; periodically reassess resource value. |

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Intangible Invisibility** | Intangible resources (trust, morale, slack, options, resilience) are poorly measured and tracked; depletion is silent until acute. Dashboards measure what is easy (budget, headcount) and miss what is important (trust, fatigue, adaptive capacity). | Collect proxies for intangibles (pulse surveys, utilisation audits, morale assessments, sentinel incident review). Design early-warning indicators. Build Tier 2 deepening around top intangible Hiddens. |
| 2 | **Aggregation Blindness** | Bottlenecks are buried in aggregated metrics and dashboards; system utilisation at 70% hides that a critical subsystem is at 95%. Fragmented knowledge (silos) means no one sees the full constraint picture. | Conduct constraint walk-through with operations SMEs. Build disaggregated constraint map. Use discrete event simulation to identify bottlenecks. Cross-check dashboard against ground truth. |
| 3 | **Paper vs Real Capacity Gap** | Paper budget/FTE/capacity exceeds real delivery capacity due to measurement gaming, hidden friction, and underestimated conversion losses. Plans built on nominal capacity hit real constraints in execution. | Measure actual utilisation vs nominal (time logs, utilisation surveys, friction audits). Compare historical velocity to nominal. Test assumptions with pilot runs. Document friction explicitly. |
| 4 | **Temporal Lag & Depletion Invisibility** | Resource erosion is slow; momentum masks approaching exhaustion; decision windows close before risks surface. Trust erodes gradually; slack is consumed imperceptibly; turnover in critical roles compounds undetected. | Implement leading indicators for intangible depletion (turnover, absence, error rates, decision backlog). Set review cadence matching depletion pace, not crisis response. Escalate pre-crisis. |
| 5 | **Power-Shaped Visibility** | Politically inconvenient constraints are suppressed or downplayed; informal power networks that enable work are invisible in formal reporting; resources controlled by powerful actors are overvalued; resources of marginal actors are ignored. | Conduct informal network analysis and power mapping. Run independent resource audits outside normal power channels. Create psychological safety for constraint reporting. Cross-check formal and informal resource pictures. |
| 6 | **Ontological Blindness** | Dominant accounting and measurement frames exclude entire resource classes (reputational debt, relationship depth, option value, time poverty, psychological safety, learning capacity). These become invisible until crisis. | Expand resource ontology beyond financials and headcount. Define proxies for excluded resources. Use Tier 2 deepening to surface hidden resource classes. Challenge measurement assumptions. |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)
| Hiddens meta-category | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **I. Perceptual** | Actors may not perceive resource constraints (gap between what leaders think is available vs what is real; illusions about capacity, slack, or optionality). | Leaders claim we have "flexibility" that doesn't exist; heroic narratives ("we can do more with less"); surprise when plans fail; disbelief when constraints surface. | Reality check: what does ground truth say about capacity? Interview frontline (they know real constraints). Compare forecast to historical velocity. Check for heroic culture. |
| **II. Systemic** | Constraints are buried in system structure (bottlenecks in coupling, fragmentation hiding interdependencies, silos hiding shared constraints, aggregation masking binding constraints). | Bottleneck emerges at subsystem level but is invisible in system metrics; silos create hand-off delays; constraint surprises emerge during integration/execution. | Map value streams and hand-offs. Identify critical paths and coupled constraints. Walk through constraint discovery with ops teams. Check for siloed knowledge. |
| **III. Informational** | Resource facts are poorly measured, unknown, or inaccessible (data gaps, metrics game, intangibles unmeasured, fragmented knowledge). | Metrics say we have slack but operations says we don't; experts can't find data they need; intangible resources (trust, morale) are assumed to be stable but are eroding; decision makers lack resource visibility. | Audit data availability and quality. Interview decision-makers about information gaps. Measure intangibles. Check for metrics gaming. Validate dashboards against ground truth. |
| **IV. Temporal** | Depletion is delayed and hidden; momentum masks approaching exhaustion; pace of erosion is underestimated; decision windows close before risks surface. | Burnout is latent until sudden departures; trust erodes gradually until crisis; contingency consumed early but risk not appreciated until late; slack consumption invisible until project fails. | Track leading indicators of depletion (turnover, absence, error rates, near-misses). Establish early-warning review cadence. Compare erosion pace to decision lag. Check for repeat failure patterns (depletion ignored). |
| **V. Relational** | Power shapes visibility (politically inconvenient constraints suppressed, informal enablers ignored, powerful actors' resource needs overvalued, marginal actors' resources undervalued). | Constraint owners avoid raising issues; informal networks enable work but are invisible; powerful actors' resource requests get fast-tracked; resource allocation doesn't match actual need. | Map informal networks and power. Run independent resource audits. Create psychological safety for constraint raising. Interview across power lines. Cross-check formal vs informal resource pictures. |
| **VI. Ontological** | Dominant resource ontology misses resource classes (intangibles like trust/morale/options, psychological safety, time poverty, reputational debt, learning capacity, option death). | Accounting frames exclude certain resources; measurement is silent on intangibles; crises surprise because excluded resources have collapsed (trust, morale, options); interventions fail because resource class not recognised. | Expand resource ontology. Define proxies for intangibles. Check whether conventional measures miss key resources. Analyse past crises for unrecognised resource failures. Design inclusive measurement. |

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **HID_R_A** Hidden intangible depletion (trust in change programme) | H | Latent erosion via broken commitments; no early indicators measured | M (emerging signs in feedback, retention, discretionary effort) | Leadership accountability (who oversees trust renewal?); cultural stewards | High (trust collapse blocks change; full re-earn required) | Pulse surveys; skip-level interviews; flight risk analysis; informal feedback channels | Explicit renewal investment; transparency and apology; small wins; psychological safety restoration | Interacts with all downstream initiatives (hard to succeed without trust). ## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts per OG v2.5 §7.2)

1. **Run the Tier 1 scan** across all six Hiddens meta-categories early (assume Hiddens exist; do not pre-filter). (OG §7.1)
2. **Shortlist candidate Hiddens**; for each, rate: mechanism (what creates the hiding?), reducibility (how much can we learn?), detectability (how hard to surface?), agency (who can fix?), consequence (what is the impact?). (OG §7.2)
3. **Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface** per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (OG §7.2). (For Resources: detection = measurement/visibility mechanism; remediation = resource move or governance redesign.)
4. **Run the Hiddens source analysis** (§8.1) and record unresolved Unknowns and Hiddens.
5. **If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism**, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG §7.5; use Triage Matrix at §7.5.3). Example scans relevant to Resources: Temporal Drift (how does depletion hide?), Uncertainty Masking (how do measurement gaps hide constraints?), Positional Blindness (what do sideline actors see that leaders don't?), Suppression (what constraints are politically inconvenient?), Cascade Discovery (what resource failures cascade?).
6. **After executing the framework protocol and tests**, re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas (new Hiddens discovered, prior risks eliminated, priority shifts).
7. **Produce Residual Unknowns** (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or remediation priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation per OG v2.5 §7.3 and §D.6.10)

Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:

- **High consequence** (decision-critical feasibility, safety, regulatory, existential, severe operational harm)
- **Adversarial context / strategic concealment likely** (contested allocations, power disputes, reputational stakes)
- **Persistent disagreement or inconsistent evidence** (different actors claim different constraints; dashboards conflict with ground truth)
- **Repeat failures despite prior interventions** (same constraint surprises again; prior learning not applied; pattern suggests hidden mechanism)
- **Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement** (resource depletion in one area triggers failures in others; interdependencies create amplification)
- **Decision-critical residual unknowns** (remaining Unknowns could change feasibility judgment, governance, or remediation priorities)

**Tier 3 execution** (per OG §D.6.10):
- Expands beyond Tier 1 six categories into the full Hiddens taxonomy (full-spectrum exploration of all hiding mechanisms across all domains).
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements, more explicit competing hypotheses, more rigorous disconfirming tests.
- When adversarial intent is plausible (contested resources, power disputes): adds concealment vectors (what would actors do to hide this?) and independent corroboration paths (who else can verify?).
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree branch A6 (OG §4.3), particularly if governance or power misalignment is suspected.

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| **1. Agent-Relative Clarity** | Resources are always relative to an agent and a goal ("resources for whom, to do what?"). Avoid abstract or decontextualised resource claims. Always specify the agent, goal, and time window. |
| **2. Intangible Reality** | Intangible resources (trust, morale, slack, options, resilience, learning capacity) are real and consequential, not "soft" or secondary. They require measurement, monitoring, and explicit renewal investment. |
| **3. Constraint Precedence** | Identify binding constraints first; focus resource moves on constraint relief. Do not waste effort on non-bottleneck resources. Remember that removing one constraint often exposes the next. |
| **4. Conversion Friction Awareness** | Resources do not convert without friction; conversion often involves waste, delay, or loss. Make conversion costs and frictions explicit; do not assume resource fungibility. |
| **5. Hidden Depletion Vigilance** | Silent resource erosion is the most dangerous pattern. Invest in early-warning instrumentation for intangibles, especially trust, morale, slack, and options. Escalate pre-crisis, not in crisis. |
| **6. Governance-Authority Alignment** | Responsibility for resource stewardship must align with authority to act. Misalignment creates corruption, avoidance, and failure. Check alignment explicitly before assigning stewardship. |
| **7. Optionality & Reversibility** | Preserve flexibility and options; avoid irreversible lock-in unless forced. Understand lock-in costs before major resource commitments. Build adaptation capacity and learning investment. |
| **8. Visible Monitoring** | Design transparent monitoring and early-warning systems for critical resources, especially intangibles. Avoid metrics gaming; cross-check dashboards against ground truth. |
| **9. Renewal & Reinvestment** | Resource accumulation requires active renewal investment; depletion of intangibles requires explicit rebuilding. Do not assume resources self-maintain; programme renewal cycles. |
| **10. Integration with Governance & Decisions** | Resource maps feed directly into decisions, interventions, governance, and accountability. Do not treat resource analysis as separate from action; integrate explicitly. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Bottleneck** | A resource constraint that limits throughput or constrains the broader system. Typically at <100% capacity, other elements queue or wait. Identified by discrete event simulation, constraint walk-throughs, or historical velocity analysis. |
| **Constraint** | A resource availability or characteristic that limits what is possible. Binding constraints shape feasibility; non-binding constraints are irrelevant to current decisions. |
| **Contingency** | Reserved capacity or budget held in reserve for surprises or escalation. Often called "contingency reserve" or "buffer." Critical for resilience. |
| **Fungibility** | The degree to which a resource can be substituted or reallocated to different uses. Money is highly fungible; specialised expertise is not. |
| **Intangible Resource** | A resource without material form (trust, morale, slack, options, relationships, knowledge, psychological safety). Often poorly measured but highly consequential. |
| **Slack** | Unused capacity, schedule float, or buffer time that provides flexibility and absorption capacity for surprises. Essential for resilience; often eliminated for efficiency gains. |
| **Resource Move** | A deliberate action to change resource status: protect (preserve/defend), grow (invest/accumulate), acquire (obtain from outside), reallocate (shift), convert (transform), wind down (divest), or buffer (add resilience). |
| **Tangible Resource** | A resource with material form (money, equipment, facilities, materials, people). Often more visible and measurable than intangibles. |
| **Trajectory** | The time path of a resource (accumulation, depletion, conversion, lock-in, mutation). Used to forecast future state and identify early-warning signals. |
