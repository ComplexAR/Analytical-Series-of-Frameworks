# Introduction

## What is Causation?

Causation is not a simple, linear phenomenon. The question "Why did this happen?" admits multiple valid answers depending on how the causal question is framed. A plane crash can be caused by pilot error (agentive), by inadequate maintenance procedures (structural), by fatigue (systemic), by inadequate training investment (developmental), or by undetected metal fatigue in a particular component (generative). Each answer is true; each points to different interventions. The same underlying event can give rise to entirely different causal narratives depending on the boundary of analysis, the temporal horizon, the decision context, and the stakes of the investigation.

This framework provides a standardised taxonomy of thirty recurring causal types, organised into six meta-categories, that enables practitioners to:
- move beyond single-cause or proximate-cause explanation to **mechanistic completeness**,
- identify hidden causal chains that operate across system boundaries and time horizons,
- distinguish causal types that require different forms of governance and intervention,
- surface counterfactual reasoning about what would have happened differently,
- integrate causal analysis across multiple stakeholder perspectives, and
- tie causal models to monitoring, governance, and learning systems.

Without this discipline, causal analysis drifts toward the convenient, the visible, or the politically safe explanation, leaving drivers of future failure unaddressed.

## Why does Causation matter for Hiddens work?

Causation is the domain where "unknowns" concentrate. Causal Hiddens emerge from several families:

**Mechanism invisibility** (Hidden-3, Hidden-4, Hidden-5) arises because intermediate steps between trigger and outcome often remain opaque. We observe A and C but not B. The causal chain becomes a black box, and interventions target the wrong node. A safety failure is blamed on operator error when the true driver is a systematic design flaw that creates impossible choices.

**Causal ambiguity** (Hidden-11, Hidden-19) occurs because multiple causal mechanisms often operate simultaneously, and their relative contribution is contested or unobservable. Disentangling which causes matter most requires controlled comparison, counterfactual analysis, and explicit evidence provenance—disciplines that investigations often skip.

**Structural/systemic blindness** (Hidden-20, Hidden-23) means that structural and systemic causes remain hidden until they generate repeated failures across multiple actors. A single incident is attributed to an individual's error; a pattern of similar incidents reveals an underlying systemic driver.

**Counterfactual erasure** (part of Temporal Hiddens) obscures the causal contribution of **prevention**—what did not happen because controls worked. These causes (the hazards that were stopped, the risks that were mitigated) vanish from narratives, leaving governance and investment decisions systematically biased toward failure modes that actually occurred.

Without this framework, causal analysis remains shallow: visible, proximate, convenient. With the framework, causation becomes a disciplined, tiered, multi-mechanism investigation that surfaces what was hidden and integrates causal models into governance.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises causal analysis through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 causal types** organised into six meta-categories (Generative, Structural, Agentive, Systemic, Developmental, Counterfactual) that recur across contexts and cases, enabling rapid causal coding and comparative analysis.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (potency, visibility, reversibility, scope, temporal dynamics) that profile any causal factor and explain why the same type of cause demands different interventions in different contexts.

3. **Four interface types** (coupling, cascade, reversion, emergence) that map how causes interact, propagate, compound, and sometimes reverse or transform during execution.

4. **Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** that surface the systematic sources of causal blindness (mechanism invisibility, causal ambiguity, structural myopia, counterfactual erasure, agency underestimation) and the six categories of visibility failure.

5. **A canonical process chain model** that relates causation to Evidence, Systems, Processes, Time, and Responsibility frameworks, ensuring causal models feed into governance and accountability.

6. **A 7-step application protocol** that moves from causal question framing through mechanism mapping, dimensional profiling, interface detection, counterfactual testing, Hiddens scanning, and closure.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Causal Factor Register) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework after situation classification (Framework of Situations) and evidence synthesis (Framework of Evidence) but early in the investigation lifecycle. Causation is most valuable for post-event analysis (incident investigation, audit, post-mortem), diagnostics (why performance is degrading), and forward-looking work (risk analysis, governance design, prevention planning). The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (rapid causal coding using meta-categories; light mechanistic depth) and Investigative Run Mode (full-spectrum type mapping, deep dimensional analysis, mechanism tracing, counterfactual protocols, multi-stakeholder causal narrative synthesis).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: identify the primary meta-categories involved, code 3–5 key causal factors using the taxonomy, profile 1–2 dimensions, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, evidence is contested, coupling/cascade potential exists, or mechanistic understanding is critical for intervention design. The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all causal claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording mechanistic uncertainty and alternative causal narratives.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Causation is not a simple, linear phenomenon; it is a structured problem of explanation. The question "Why did this happen?" does not have a single correct answer. Multiple causal mechanisms typically operate simultaneously; their visibility and importance vary by stakeholder perspective, by scale, and by the decision context. An effective causal analysis must therefore:

- Distinguish among six fundamental types of causal mechanism (generative triggers, structural preconditions, agentive choices and actions, systemic relationships, developmental histories, and counterfactual comparisons),
- map intermediate causal chains that connect distal causes to proximate triggers to observable outcomes,
- profile each causal factor along consistent dimensions (potency, visibility, reversibility, scope, temporal dynamics),
- surface counterfactual reasoning about what would have occurred under different conditions,
- integrate causal analysis across multiple stakeholder models, and
- tie causal understanding to governance, monitoring, and intervention design.

This framework provides a stable, repeatable, cross-domain taxonomy that enables practitioners to move beyond convenient or visible explanations to mechanistic completeness.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Causation** (in this framework): A systematic relationship in which one or more factors (generative, structural, agentive, systemic, developmental, or counterfactual) contribute to the occurrence of an outcome. Causation is not deterministic; it is probabilistic, multi-mechanistic, and path-dependent. Attribution of causation requires integrating evidence, logic, and disciplined counterfactual reasoning.

**Causal mechanism**: The intermediate chain of events and relationships through which a cause produces an outcome. "Mechanism" is not an assumption; it is an empirical question about how the causal process operates.

**Causal factor**: A specific element coded as belonging to one of the six meta-categories (Generative, Structural, Agentive, Systemic, Developmental, Counterfactual) that contributes to explaining why the outcome occurred.

**Mechanistic explanation**: An account of causation that traces the causal chain from antecedent conditions through intermediate steps to observable outcomes, making visible the "how" and not merely the "that."

**Counterfactual reasoning**: The disciplined practice of asking "What would have happened if [this factor] were different?" as a way of testing causal claims and distinguishing essential from incidental causes.

**Coupling, cascade, reversion, emergence**: Four interface types that describe how multiple causal mechanisms interact (coupling: mutual dependence; cascade: sequential propagation with amplification; reversion: causal reversal under different conditions; emergence: novel causation arising from interaction).

**Scope of this framework**: Incident/post-event causal analysis; diagnostic analysis (why performance is degrading); forward-looking risk and governance analysis (what are the causal drivers of future failure?); intervention design (which causal levers matter most?); accountability and responsibility analysis (who/what caused the failure?).

**Non-goals**: This framework does not attempt to assign moral or legal culpability (see Framework of Responsibility); it does not require proof beyond reasonable doubt (see Framework of Evidence); it does not predict future outcomes with precision (see Framework of Risk); it is not a root-cause analysis template, though it can be used as part of one.

## 1.4 Position in the Series (Upstream / Midstream / Downstream)

**Stage: Midstream** — Causation is positioned after Situations (boundary-setting), Evidence (provenance & confidence), and Systems (structural mapping), but before Diagnosis (causal model testing), Interventions (causal lever selection), Governance (causal structure in controls), and Responsibility (causal assignment in accountability).

**Upstream dependencies**: Framework of Situations (situation framing); Framework of Evidence (what do we know?); Framework of Systems (structural boundaries); Framework of Time (timeline and temporal relationships); Framework of Scale (analysis scale and aggregation level); Framework of Power (stakeholder influence on causal narratives).

**Downstream outputs to**: Framework of Diagnosis (does the causal model explain the evidence?); Framework of Interventions (what causal factors should interventions target?); Framework of Governance (what causal structures require ongoing control?); Framework of Responsibility (which causal factors map to agent roles and accountability?); Framework of Learning & Adaptation (how do causal insights feed into system improvement?).

**Cross-cutting integration**: Framework of Hiddens (causal Hiddens as a specific domain); Framework of Processes (process causation as distinct from outcome causation); Framework of Risk (causal mechanisms as risk drivers); Framework of Knowledge (epistemology of causation).

### 1.6.3 Routing triggers
- Outcome explanation requires tracing causal chains across multiple levels (generative, structural, agentive, systemic, developmental)
- Causal factors are contested or multiple stakeholders assign causation differently
- Investigation needs to distinguish proximate triggers from structural preconditions or developmental histories
- Coupling, cascade, or feedback effects are suspected; causation is not linear or independent

# 2. Meta-Categories and Types

## 2.1 Meta-Categories Table (mandatory)

| Meta-category | Symbol | Definition | Core question | Typical scope |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I. Generative | GN | Direct, triggering causes that initiate or ignite an event or sequence | What triggered the event? What was the spark? | Immediate, event-level |
| II. Structural / Dispositional | ST | Pre-existing conditions, arrangements, vulnerabilities, or capacities that enable or constrain outcomes | What preconditions made this outcome possible or likely? | System architecture, design |
| III. Agentive / Practical | AG | Actions, choices, decisions, or deliberations by actors that contribute to the outcome | What did the key actors choose to do or not do? | Individual/collective decisions and their consequences |
| IV. Systemic / Relational | SY | Interconnections, feedback loops, alignment failures, or relational dynamics that amplify, dampen, or redirect causation | How did system interactions shape the outcome? | Cross-component dynamics, interfaces |
| V. Developmental / Temporal | DV | Histories, trajectories, path dependencies, or slow-moving processes that accumulated to create conditions for the outcome | What history or process led here? What was building? | Long-term trends, cumulative effects, regime changes |
| VI. Counterfactual / Attributional | CF | Comparative or "what-if" reasoning about alternative scenarios, prevention, or causal credit under uncertainty | What would have happened under different conditions? Who/what prevented worse? | Inference, attribution, prevention analysis |

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Generative Causation

Generative causes are direct triggers or initiators: the spark plug igniting fuel, the person pressing the alarm button, the system entering a cascade condition. They are often proximate in time to the outcome, usually observable, and are the focus of immediate incident narratives. However, generative causes are insufficient for explanation—a spark without fuel does not cause fire. Generative causation is necessary but not complete causal explanation. Investigations that stop at generative causes often miss the structural, agentive, and systemic factors that made the outcome possible.

### II. Structural / Dispositional Causation

Structural causes are enabling preconditions: the physical arrangements, incentive structures, information asymmetries, role definitions, control gaps, and design choices that make certain outcomes more or less likely. A badly designed process creates conditions for error; a knowledge gap creates conditions for miscommunication; inadequate investment creates conditions for degradation. Structural causes are often invisible because they are "how things are"—background conditions that only become salient when failure occurs. Yet they are often the highest-leverage targets for intervention and governance.

### III. Agentive / Practical Causation

Agentive causes are actions, choices, and decisions by actors that contribute to the outcome. This includes not only active interventions (what actors chose to do) but also non-actions (what actors chose not to do, what they failed to do, what was deprioritised). Agentive causation is complicated by the fact that actors operate within constraints (structural, informational, temporal, incentive-based), so the distinction between "agentive choice" and "structural constraint" is often blurred. Nevertheless, the question "What did the key actors choose to do?" is essential for accountability and intervention analysis.

### IV. Systemic / Relational Causation

Systemic causes arise from interactions, feedback loops, cascades, and misalignments among system components. A single component functions as designed, yet the interaction of components produces an emergent, unintended outcome. Silos cause communication failures; feedback loops amplify small errors into large crises; temporal misalignment causes cascades. Systemic causation is often the most difficult to see because it is distributed across multiple actors and components, and the causal pathway crosses organisational boundaries.

### V. Developmental / Temporal Causation

Developmental causes are histories, trajectories, and path dependencies. An outcome that appears sudden often has a long history of build-up: minor incidents consolidating into patterns, slow erosion of capability, accumulation of deferred maintenance, gradual shift in incentive structures. Developmental causation is obscured by the tendency to treat outcomes as events rather than as endpoints of processes. Yet understanding developmental causation is critical for prevention and early warning: interventions targeted only at proximate triggers will fail if the developmental driver is not addressed.

### VI. Counterfactual / Attributional Causation

Counterfactual causation is reasoning about what would have happened under different conditions, and it serves three purposes: (1) testing causal claims (if causation is real, removing the cause should prevent the outcome), (2) understanding causal credit (who or what prevented worse?), and (3) assigning responsibility and accountability (what was the actor's causal contribution given constraints?). Counterfactual reasoning is the most formally tractable form of causal explanation, but it is often the most neglected in investigation practice. It is also the domain where causation connects to decision and learning: understanding what would have happened differently creates the basis for prevention and governance design.

# 3. Causal Types (Taxonomy: 30 Types Across 6 Meta-Categories)

## 3.1 Types (Category I: Generative Cause Types 1–5)

| Type | Name | Definition | Example | Hidden risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I.1 | Ignition / triggering event | A discrete event that initiates a sequence | System overload triggers cascade; equipment failure initiates emergency | The actual initiator is obscured by a proximate proxy; true trigger remains hidden |
| I.2 | Activation / state change | Transition of a system or component into an active, hazardous, or critical state | Switching to unsafe mode; enabling hazardous condition | State change occurs without detection; passivity masks latent activation |
| I.3 | Boundary crossing / threshold breach | Violation of a defined operational or safety boundary | Exceeding safe operating parameters; breaching confidentiality threshold | Threshold is ambiguous or not communicated; crossing is undetected |
| I.4 | Collision / interference | Unintended interaction of elements that should be separate | Conflicting instructions from multiple sources; competing processes on same resource | Separation is assumed but not verified; hidden points of intersection |
| I.5 | Release / rupture | Breach of a containment boundary allowing escape of energy, information, or hazard | Pressure vessel failure; data breach; epidemic escape | Containment robustness is unknown; early escape is silent |

## 3.2 Types (Category II: Structural / Dispositional Cause Types 6–10)

| Type | Name | Definition | Example | Hidden risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| II.6 | Design flaw / asymmetry | Inherent property of a system's architecture that creates risk or enables failure | Information asymmetry that enables adverse selection; process design that creates impossible choices | Design flaws are "background conditions" and invisible until failure; alternative designs are not explored |
| II.7 | Capacity / resource gap | Shortage of material, personnel, knowledge, or capability required to execute safely | Understaffing creates bottlenecks; inadequate training; insufficient capital investment | Gaps are chronic and normalised; threshold for failure is crossed silently |
| II.8 | Incentive misalignment | Reward or penalty structure that motivates actors toward behaviour contrary to system objectives | Bonuses for throughput without safety penalty; perverse incentives in controls | Misalignment is often invisible to leadership; actors adjust behaviour quietly |
| II.9 | Control gap / design failure | Absence of a control, check, or safeguard that should be present | Missing approval step; no independent verification; control designed but not deployed | Control is assumed to exist; compliance is not verified; failure mode is latent |
| II.10 | Interface / boundary weakness | Structural vulnerability at the interface between components, systems, or stakeholder groups | Communication gap between shift changes; handoff failure; cross-functional misalignment | Interface failures are diffuse; responsibility is unclear; redesign is complex |

## 3.3 Types (Category III: Agentive / Practical Cause Types 11–15)

| Type | Name | Definition | Example | Hidden risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| III.11 | Omission / inaction | Failure to act when action was required or expected | Not escalating a concern; delaying decision; failing to follow procedure | Omission is silent; inaction leaves no trace; actor may not realise action was needed |
| III.12 | Erroneous action / mistake | Action taken with false belief about the situation or the consequences | Operator selects wrong mode due to interface confusion; misdiagnosis due to incomplete information | Mistake-as-inevitable is normalised; root cause (information gap, design) is not addressed |
| III.13 | Deliberate violation / intentional risk-taking | Conscious choice to deviate from procedure or accepted practice, with awareness of risk | Shortcut to meet deadline; bypassing control to save time; deliberate non-compliance | Violation is rationalised as "everyone does it"; disciplinary focus masks systemic drivers |
| III.14 | Misjudgement / poor decision-making | Cognitive limitation, bias, or insufficient analysis leading to a decision that, in hindsight, was suboptimal | Underestimation of risk; overconfidence in prediction; inadequate consideration of alternatives | Misjudgement feels like "reasonable given information available"; hindsight bias obscures ex-ante rationality |
| III.15 | Structural inability / constrained choice | Actor is placed in a situation where all available choices lead to undesirable outcomes (no-win scenario) | Pressure to meet conflicting targets; deadline/quality trade-off with no resolution path | Constrained choice is often invisible to leadership; actor bears blame for choosing between bad options |

## 3.4 Types (Category IV: Systemic / Relational Cause Types 16–20)

| Type | Name | Definition | Example | Hidden risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| IV.16 | Coupling / interdependence | Tight connection between components such that failure of one cascades to others | Failure of critical supplier cascades to production; data system failure cascades to decision-making | Coupling is invisible until failure; magnitude of cascade is underestimated |
| IV.17 | Feedback loop / amplification | Reinforcing cycle that amplifies small perturbations into large crises | Panic buying creates scarcity which drives panic buying; loss of trust triggers risk-seeking behaviour | Feedback enters regime where linear assumptions fail; early intervention is not visible as necessary |
| IV.18 | Alignment failure / coordination breakdown | Failure of multiple actors or systems to coordinate, align, or synchronise | Left hand doesn't know what right hand is doing; conflicting priorities between functions; misaligned incentives | Misalignment is distributed and no single actor is accountable; system-level fix is complex |
| IV.19 | Emergence / unintended consequence | Outcome that arises from system interaction and is not reducible to component properties | Interaction of multiple risk factors creates tail risk; good intentions compound into bad outcomes | Emergence is counterintuitive; actors did not foresee interaction; blame-focused investigation misses system analysis |
| IV.20 | Cascade / propagation | Sequential failure of components in a chain where each failure increases vulnerability of the next | Domino effect in supply chain; failure of backup systems; sequential deterioration of resilience | Cascade dynamics are nonlinear; early intervention opportunity is not visible in data |

## 3.5 Types (Category V: Developmental / Temporal Cause Types 21–25)

| Type | Name | Definition | Example | Hidden risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| V.21 | Path dependency / history lock-in | An outcome that is the result of a sequence of prior choices that constrained future options | Technical debt that makes system change costly; organisational culture that is resistant to change; past investment in legacy system | Path dependency is invisible as a "cause" because the past is not now observable; change alternatives seem economically impossible |
| V.22 | Accumulation / slow build-up | Gradual increase in risk, burden, or preconditions over time | Deferred maintenance accumulating; compliance risk accumulating; capability degradation over years | Accumulation is silent; threshold crossing is not detected; prevention opportunity is invisible until failure |
| V.23 | Erosion / gradual deterioration | Slow loss of capability, resource, or protection | Institutional knowledge loss through retirement; gradually increasing system fragility; slow erosion of safety culture | Erosion is normalised as "how things are"; actor adaptation masks loss of protection; failure occurs when buffer is exhausted |
| V.24 | Regime change / critical transition | Crossing of a tipping point or threshold into a qualitatively different regime with different dynamics | Shift from stable to chaotic; shift from growth to decline; shift from preventive to reactive mode | Regime change is not detected until after it occurs; return to prior regime may be impossible |
| V.25 | Deferral / downstream consequence | Postponement or shifting of costs/problems to a future time or another actor, creating latent risk | Deferring maintenance creates future risk; postponing a difficult decision compounds the problem; cost-cutting that creates latent liabilities | Deferral is invisible to decision-maker who reaps benefits; downstream actor bears the consequences |

## 3.6 Types (Category VI: Counterfactual / Attributional Cause Types 26–30)

| Type | Name | Definition | Example | Hidden risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| VI.26 | Prevention / safeguard effectiveness | The causal contribution of a control, safeguard, or preventive measure that functioned and prevented worse | Backup system that activated and limited damage; control that caught the error before propagation | Prevention is invisible in narratives (what did not happen doesn't generate incident reports); investment in prevention is undervalued |
| VI.27 | Counterfactual alternative | A plausible alternative causal path that would have produced different outcome under different conditions | "If the shift change had occurred 10 minutes earlier, the error would have been caught"; "If budget had been available, training would have prevented the mistake" | Counterfactuals are speculative and hard to defend; hindsight bias inflates or deflates their significance |
| VI.28 | Causal insufficiency under uncertainty | Causation that is real but cannot be conclusively demonstrated with available evidence, due to complexity, time lapse, or observability limits | Causal factor is inferred from pattern rather than directly observed; mechanism is theoretically sound but not empirically tested | Uncertainties are often collapsed into false confidence; alternatively, causal factor is dismissed as "unproven" |
| VI.29 | Suppressed alternative | A counterfactual outcome that would have resulted had a particular factor been different, but is suppressed in official narratives by power, politics, or incentive alignment | Alternative narrative where incident is blamed on systemic factors is suppressed in favour of individual blame | Suppressed alternatives create narrative Hiddens; post-incident culture shift may later reveal what was previously hidden |
| VI.30 | Responsibility under constraint | Attribution of causal responsibility to an actor even though actor operated under severe structural constraint | Operator held responsible for error even though system design made error likely; manager blamed for non-compliance when incentives made compliance irrational | Responsibility without full causal control creates injustice and undermines learning; system-level causation is obscured |

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)

The 30-type taxonomy is a starting point, not an exhaustive list. Domain-specific expansions and refinements are expected. When new causal types are discovered:

1. Assign a code (e.g., I.1a for a variant of I.1)
2. Define the type clearly with reference to the parent meta-category
3. Document the domain(s) where it recurs
4. Add to local Hiddens analysis and testing protocols
5. Propose integration into the next series update if the type is cross-domain

Do not expand the taxonomy without explicit causal necessity; the goal is standardisation, not proliferation.

# 4. Dimensions: Five Cross-Cutting Properties

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)

| Dimension | Definition | High | Low | Medium | Diagnostic value |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Potency** | Magnitude of causal contribution to the outcome; how much did this cause matter? | Strong influence on outcome; outcome unlikely without this cause | Contributory but minor; outcome would have been similar without this cause | Moderate influence; outcome contingent on presence of this cause | Potency determines governance priority and intervention target value |
| **Visibility** | Observable or discoverable without expert investigation; how easy is this cause to detect? | Easily observable; stakeholders aware of this cause; documented in records | Deeply hidden; requires expert analysis, reconstruction, or inference; invisible to routine monitoring | Partially visible; known to some stakeholders but not others; detectability varies by role | Visibility determines whether causal factor will be spontaneously reported vs requires active discovery |
| **Reversibility** | Can the causal factor be undone, modified, or prevented once identified? | Easily reversed or modified; control levers exist; mitigation is straightforward | Irreversible or extremely difficult to undo; historical or structural factors; path dependency; past cannot be recovered | Partially reversible; some levers exist but limited, costly, or slow | Reversibility determines whether causation can be managed prospectively vs only acknowledged retrospectively |
| **Scope** | Does the cause affect a singular incident, local system, or propagate systemically? | Systemic/cross-functional; affects multiple independent incidents; pattern evidence; design flaw affecting all instances | Singular/local; affects only this case; incident-specific; random or unique circumstance | Organisational level; affects a department, programme, or process; local pattern | Scope determines whether intervention should be case-specific vs systematic (redesign) |
| **Temporal dynamics** | Pattern of causation over time—is it sudden, gradual, chronic, cyclical? | Sudden/acute; sharp change; event-driven; time-localised; clear trigger point | Chronic/gradual; slow accumulation; path dependent; regime change; long build-up | Episodic or cyclical; recurring pattern; seasonal or event-triggered repetition | Temporal pattern determines whether prevention strategy is emergency response vs long-term process change |

# 5. Dynamic Patterns and Process Causation

## 5.1 Seven-Step Causal Analysis Process (mandatory)

The Causation framework integrates into the broader investigative workflow via a seven-step protocol:

**Step 1: Frame the causal question**
- Define the focal outcome to be explained
- Establish causal boundary (what is "cause" vs external context or condition)
- Identify stakeholder perspectives and disagreements on causation
- Tag question framing as F/I/A/U per PD-02

**Step 2: Map the temporal/mechanistic sequence**
- Construct timeline with key events and decision points
- Identify proximate triggers and distal preconditions
- Trace intermediate steps between trigger and outcome
- Make visible any "black box" steps where causation is obscure

**Step 3: Scan all six meta-categories**
- For each meta-category (I–VI), determine if causation is relevant to this case
- Identify 1–3 key causal factors per meta-category
- Code each factor using the 30-type taxonomy (§3.1–3.6)
- Tag each factor as F/I/A/U; note evidence provenance

**Step 4: Profile causal factors on five dimensions**
- For each factor, rate potency, visibility, reversibility, scope, temporal dynamics
- Note which factors are hidden, obvious, or contested
- Identify where dimensional profiles create governance or intervention challenges

**Step 5: Map causal interfaces and interactions**
- Identify where causal factors couple (mutual dependence)
- Trace cascades (sequential failure amplification)
- Note reversions (causal factor change depending on conditions)
- Detect emergences (novel causation from interaction)

**Step 6: Run mandatory tiered Hiddens scans**
- Tier 1 (six-category scan): §8.2
- Tier 2 (structured deepening): §8.3 if consequence is high or evidence is weak
- Tier 3 (full-spectrum escalation): §8.5 if Tier 2 finds material gaps or disputes

**Step 7: Synthesise causal model and test against evidence**
- Integrate causal factors into coherent mechanistic narrative
- Test causal model against full evidence base (Diagnosis framework)
- Identify residual uncertainties and next-best tests
- Populate all standard registers (Causal Factor Register, Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog)
- Declare investigation closure with explicit residual unknowns (PD-09)

## 5.2 Causal Process Phases (recommended)

Causation often unfolds in distinct phases. Mapping these phases makes visible where intervention leverage exists:

| Phase | Characteristics | Causal types active | Governance focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Precondition phase** | Structural and developmental causes accumulate; system drifts into vulnerability | II (Structural), V (Developmental), VII.26 (Prevention failures) | Design review; control audit; early warning indicators |
| **Activation phase** | Triggers, boundary crossings, or state changes occur; initiating events | I (Generative), III (Agentive choices), IV (Systemic feedback) | Real-time detection; escalation protocols; emergency response readiness |
| **Propagation phase** | Cascades, amplification, and coupling cause spread and escalation | IV (Coupling, feedback, cascade), VI (Counterfactual prevention) | Containment; damage control; backup system activation; isolation |
| **Consequence phase** | Outcome is realised; harm, loss, or impact is recorded | VI (Counterfactual—what mitigation prevented additional harm?) | Stabilisation; remediation; recovery planning |
| **Learning phase** | Root causes are investigated; causal models are built; prevention is designed | All categories, integrated across Diagnosis/Evidence/Learning frameworks | Systematic improvement; cultural change; capability redesign |

# 6. Interface Types (Causation Interactions)

## 6.1 Interface Types Table (mandatory)

| Interface type | Definition | Mechanism | Governance implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Coupling** | Two or more causal factors that are tightly linked; modification of one affects the other | Mutual dependence; structural reinforcement; incentive alignment | Cannot solve one cause in isolation; system redesign often required; targeted intervention may be ineffective |
| **Cascade** | Sequential failure of causal factors where each failure increases the vulnerability of the next | Trigger → failure of component A → exposure of component B to hazard → failure of B → ... | Amplification of impact; identification of first failure point is critical for prevention; cascade dynamics are nonlinear and difficult to predict |
| **Reversion** | Causal relationship that changes direction or polarity depending on context or scale | Same factor is protective at one level and harmful at another; same intervention has opposite effects in different regimes | Context dependency is critical; universal solutions are risky; monitoring must detect regime boundaries |
| **Emergence** | Novel causation arising from interaction of components; causal factor at system level that does not exist at component level | Intentional actions by rational agents compound into unintended system-level consequences | Emergence is counterintuitive; blame-focused investigation misses system analysis; prevention requires system-level thinking |

# 7. Integration: The 36-Framework Causation Interface

## 7.1 Primary Integration Links (recommended)

Causation is a midstream framework that draws input from upstream frameworks and feeds output to downstream frameworks. Key integration points:

**Upstream integration (inputs to Causation):**

- **Framework of Situations**: Situation type determines which causal patterns are expected (crisis causation differs from routine causation; transformational change has different drivers than project delivery)

- **Framework of Power**: Stakeholder conflicts over causation are often driven by power asymmetries; some causal narratives are suppressed

- **Framework of Diagnosis**: Causal model is tested against evidence; diagnostic question is "Does this causal model explain the evidence?"
- **Framework of Interventions**: Causal factors are mapped to intervention levers; potency and reversibility dimensions identify which causes can be targeted
- **Framework of Governance**: Causal structure determines control model (who monitors which causal factors? At what frequency?)
- **Framework of Responsibility**: Causal factors are linked to agent roles and accountability; responsibility assignment must account for causal contribution
- **Framework of Learning & Adaptation**: Causal lessons feed into organisational learning and capability redesign; prevention is built from causal understanding

# 8. Hiddens Work: Tiered Scans and Escalation

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

Causal Hiddens arise from six systematic sources of visibility failure:

**Perceptual Hiddens**: Causal factors that are invisible because they are not directly observable. Example: mechanism invisibility in a black-box process; structural preconditions that are "background" and therefore not salient.

**Systemic Hiddens**: Causal factors that are distributed across multiple actors or system components and therefore not visible to any single participant. Example: emergent causation from multi-component interaction; cascade dynamics that only become visible when multiple failures compound.

**Informational Hiddens**: Causal factors that are not observed because information is not collected, shared, or analysed. Example: near-misses that are not reported; anomalies that are not correlated into patterns; early warning signals that are not connected to causal factors.

**Temporal Hiddens**: Causal factors that are hidden by time lag between cause and consequence. Example: path dependency that only becomes visible retrospectively; accumulation that is silent until threshold is breached; regime change that is only detected after crossing.

**Relational Hiddens**: Causal factors that are hidden by asymmetric power or information. Example: actors downplay their causal contribution; alternative narratives are suppressed; accountability is assigned to scapegoats rather than structural drivers.

**Ontological Hiddens**: Causal factors that are hidden because they are not recognised as "real" in the dominant causal paradigm. Example: systemic causes are dismissed as "too complex"; cultural drivers are dismissed as "soft"; counterfactual prevention is invisible as causation.

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)

### Tier 1 Hiddens Scan (mandatory minimum)

| Visibility category | Scan question | Probing for | Typical Hidden |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Perceptual** | What causal factors are invisible because they are not directly observable? | Black-box mechanisms; structural preconditions; "background" conditions; design flaws; system architecture | A control is assumed to work but has never been tested; design flaw enables failure but is "how we do business" |
| **Systemic** | What causal factors arise from system interaction that no single actor can see? | Emergent causation; distributed failures; feedback loops; cascade dynamics; cross-functional misalignment | Three departments independently make rational decisions that compound into system failure; cascade amplifies small perturbations |
| **Informational** | What causal factors are not observed because information is not collected or connected? | Gaps in monitoring; blind spots in data collection; anomalies not correlated; early warning signals not recognised | Near-miss pattern is not detected because each incident is investigated in isolation; metric is collected but not analysed |
| **Temporal** | What causal factors are hidden by time lag between cause and consequence? | Path dependency; accumulation; slow build-up; regime change; deferred consequences | Maintenance deferral is invisible for years; erosion is so gradual it is normalised; threshold crossing is not detected |
| **Relational** | What causal factors are hidden by power asymmetry, information asymmetry, or incentive misalignment? | Actor downplays own causal contribution; alternative narratives suppressed; accountability assigned to convenient scapegoats; organisational culture silences dissent | Manager blames operator for fatigue-driven error, not the understaffing policy that created fatigue; whistleblower warning is ignored |
| **Ontological** | What causal factors are hidden because they fall outside the dominant causal paradigm? | Systemic causes dismissed as "too complex"; cultural drivers dismissed as "soft"; preventive causation (what did not happen) invisible in narratives; counterfactual reasoning not attempted | Investment in prevention is treated as waste (no incident means no ROI); systemic driver is dismissed because it is not "someone's fault" |

## 8.3 Hiddens Crosswalk (Tier 2 – structured deepening layer)

When Tier 1 identifies material Hiddens, Tier 2 deepening applies framework-specific scan patterns. See Operating Guide §7.5 (Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scans) for thirteen cross-framework scan families. Key scans relevant to Causation:

| Scan family | Scan name | Applicable to causation types | Tier 2 depth questions |
|---|---|---|---|
| A. Mechanism Blindness | Mechanism Invisibility (A1) | I, II, IV, V (all mechanism-based causes) | Is the intermediate causal chain observable? What steps are unverified? Which assumptions about mechanism are untested? |
| B. Causation-Specific | Causal Ambiguity (not yet named in OG) | VI (counterfactual / attribution) | When multiple causal factors operate simultaneously, can they be disentangled? What controlled comparison would isolate each factor's contribution? |
| D. Structural Blindness | Structural Myopia (D3) | II, IV, V (structural, systemic, developmental) | Are structural/systemic drivers being replaced with individual-blame narratives? What system-level causation is being obscured? |
| E. Temporal Blindness | Temporal Erasure (E2) | V (developmental causation especially) | Is the long-term history of accumulation visible? What regime changes have occurred? Are deferred consequences recognised as future causation? |
| F. Narrative Blindness | Narrative Capture / Framing Dominance (F1) | VI (suppressed alternatives, counterfactual) | Whose causal narrative is dominant? What alternative causal models are suppressed? What power dynamics shape the narrative? |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

For each causal factor identified in Causal Factor Register, run the following micro-protocol:

```
QUESTION 1: Is this factor's mechanism fully visible?
  IF NO → Tier 2 scan: mechanism invisibility; propose test to make mechanism observable

QUESTION 2: Is this factor's contribution measurable and testable?
  IF NO → Tier 2 scan: causal ambiguity; propose controlled comparison to isolate contribution

QUESTION 3: Is this factor adequately addressed in governance/controls?
  IF NO → Escalate to Tier 2 or Tier 3; design control or test to address gap

QUESTION 4: Is this factor's causal narrative being suppressed or contested?
  IF YES → Tier 2 scan: narrative capture; document alternative models explicitly; preserve disagreement

QUESTION 5: Could this factor be a proxy for deeper causal driver?
  IF YES → Tier 2 deepening: trace mechanism to underlying cause; distinguish proximate from distal causation

QUESTION 6: Does counterfactual reasoning change the significance of this factor?
  IF YES → Run counterfactual protocol (§5.1, Step 5); test "what if this factor were absent/different"
```

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum causation run)

Escalate to Tier 3 Hiddens if any of the following conditions are met:

1. **High consequence**: Outcome has material impact on safety, reputation, financial health, or system integrity
2. **Contested causation**: Stakeholders significantly disagree on causal attribution (power-driven narratives)
3. **Weak evidence**: Evidence base for key causal factors is limited to inference or assumption (F/I/A/U includes U or A for critical factors)
4. **Coupling/cascade suspected**: Causal factors interact in ways that amplify risk or create non-linear dynamics
5. **Prevention critical**: Governance or intervention design depends on understanding causal mechanisms
6. **Pattern across incidents**: Similar causal factors appear in multiple incidents (suggesting systemic driver)

**Tier 3 execution**: Run full Targeted Hiddens Discovery scans (OG §7.5) on mechanism families A, B, D, E, F; perform in-depth counterfactual reasoning; execute multi-stakeholder narrative elicitation with explicit disagreement mapping; commission empirical tests where feasible (controlled comparisons, interrupted time series, natural experiments); perform cross-domain causal pattern matching to identify analogies and lessons.

# 10. Principles and Constraints

## 10.1 Principles Table

| Principle | Rationale | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanistic completeness** | A single cause is almost never sufficient to explain complex outcomes. Explanation must map across all six meta-categories. | Scan all six meta-categories even if some are marked "minimal"; do not stop at convenient explanation |
| **Multi-mechanism integration** | Different causal types require different governance and intervention. Treating structural causes as individual problems creates perpetual failure. | Distinguish meta-categories in register; design interventions by meta-category, not by outcome |
| **Visibility discipline** | Invisible causes remain hidden until failure occurs. Assume hidden until proven otherwise. | Flag visibility of each factor; design monitoring to detect hidden factors; Tier 2 Hiddens scans when visibility is low |
| **Counterfactual reasoning** | Causation is verified by asking "What would have happened if this factor were different?" | **Evidence provenance** | Causal claims without evidence provenance are speculation. F/I/A/U tagging is non-negotiable. | Every causal factor has provenance row in Evidence Ledger; weak evidence is acknowledged and flagged for testing; alternative hypotheses are documented |
| **Stakeholder pluralism** | Different stakeholders have different causal models. Power asymmetries suppress some models. Integrating analysis without collapsing disagreement is essential. | Elicit causal models from multiple stakeholders; document disagreement explicitly; note power dynamics; create integrated narrative that preserves alternatives |
| **Prevention-as-causation** | What did not happen—the hazards that were stopped, the risks mitigated—represents causal work that is invisible in incident narratives. | **Reversibility assessment** | Some causes can be undone; others cannot. Governance and intervention strategy depend on this distinction. | Profile each factor on reversibility; distinguish recoverable from path-dependent causation; plan prevention for non-reversible factors |
| **Temporal accountability** | The timing of causation matters. Sudden triggers require emergency response; chronic accumulation requires long-term redesign. | Profile temporal dynamics; align governance to temporal pattern (sudden causation → detection/response; chronic → prevention/redesign) |

# 11. Glossary

## 11.1 Local domain glossary (Causation-specific terms)

**Agentive causation**: Actions, choices, decisions, or omissions by an actor that contribute to an outcome. Agentive causation is complicated by the distinction between choice and constraint: an actor may be blamed for a choice that was the only available option given structural constraints.

**Causal ambiguity**: A situation where multiple causal mechanisms operate simultaneously and their relative contribution cannot be disentangled without controlled comparison or explicit counterfactual reasoning.

**Causal chain**: The sequence of events and relationships connecting an initial cause through intermediate steps to a final outcome. Making causal chains explicit—rather than jumping from cause to outcome—reveals opportunities for intervention at each node.

**Causal completeness**: An explanation is causally complete when it addresses all six meta-categories and has ruled out or explained away material Hiddens. Causally incomplete explanations typically stop at convenient or visible causes.

**Causal factor**: A specific element (generative, structural, agentive, systemic, developmental, or counterfactual) that contributes to explaining why an outcome occurred. Causal factors are coded using the 30-type taxonomy.

**Causal mechanism**: The intermediate process through which a cause produces an outcome. "Mechanism" is an empirical question, not an assumption. Transparency about mechanism enables testing, prediction, and intervention.

**Causal narrative**: A coherent story connecting cause(s) to outcome. Causal narratives integrate factual evidence, inferences, assumptions, and counterfactual reasoning into an explanation. Quality causal narratives make mechanism visible and flag uncertainties.

**Causal sufficiency**: A question of whether a causal factor, alone or in combination with others, is sufficient to produce the outcome. Sufficiency is assessed via counterfactual reasoning: remove the cause, and the outcome no longer occurs.

**Cascade / cascading failure**: Sequential failure of causal factors where each failure increases vulnerability of the next, amplifying impact. Cascade dynamics are nonlinear and difficult to predict without system modelling.

**Complexity of causation**: The observation that complex outcomes typically result from interaction of multiple causal factors across multiple meta-categories, not from a single root cause. Effective investigation must address this complexity rather than reduce it to a single blame target.

**Confounding**: A situation where two causal factors are correlated but only one is actually causal. Disentangling confounding requires controlled comparison or causal reasoning beyond correlation.

**Counterfactual reasoning**: Disciplined practice of asking "What would have happened if [factor] were different?" Counterfactuals are verified through control-group logic, process tracing, or comparable case analysis. Counterfactuals are the most rigorous form of causal verification.

**Coupling**: Tight interdependence between causal factors such that modification of one affects the other. Coupling complicates targeted intervention and often requires system-level redesign.

**Developmental causation**: The historical and process-level causes that accumulate over time. Developmental causation often explains outcomes that appear sudden but have long precursors (path dependency, accumulation, regime change).

**Dimensioning**: The practice of profiling a causal factor on multiple cross-cutting dimensions (potency, visibility, reversibility, scope, temporal dynamics) to enable more nuanced analysis and governance design.

**Distal causation**: Causes that are distant in time or causal chain from outcome. Distal causes are often more important than proximate causes for prevention, but are less visible in narratives.

**Emergence / emergent causation**: Novel causation arising from interaction of system components; a causal factor at system level that does not exist at component level. Emergence is counterintuitive and often missed by investigations focused on blame of individual actors.

**Generative causation**: Direct triggers or initiators that spark a sequence. Generative causes are often proximate and visible, but are insufficient for explanation without addressing preconditions.

**Governance-causation alignment**: The principle that control and monitoring structures should be aligned to causal drivers, not to convenient metrics. Governance-causation misalignment leads to controls that do not address root causes.

**Hidden causation**: Causal factors that remain invisible to decision-makers and investigators until late consequences reveal them. Hiddens arise from six systematic sources: perceptual, systemic, informational, temporal, relational, ontological.

**Latent causation**: Causal factors that are present but not yet activated. Example: design flaws that only cause failure under certain conditions.

**Mechanism invisibility**: A primary source of causal Hiddens where the intermediate steps between trigger and outcome are not observable or not traced.

**Meta-category**: One of six foundational types of causation (Generative, Structural, Agentive, Systemic, Developmental, Counterfactual) that organise the 30-type taxonomy.

**Multi-mechanism explanation**: An explanation that integrates causal factors from multiple meta-categories rather than reducing to a single cause.

**Path dependency**: Causation where prior choices constrain future options, and the outcome depends on the sequence of choices, not just the final state. Path dependency explains why "going back" to a prior state is often impossible.

**Potency**: Dimension assessing magnitude of a causal factor's contribution to outcome. High potency factors are critical for prevention; low potency factors may be ignorable.

**Proximate causation**: Causes that are immediate in time or causal chain to outcome. Proximate causes are visible but often insufficient for explanation.

**Reversibility**: Dimension assessing whether a causal factor can be undone or prevented once identified. Reversible causes can be managed prospectively; irreversible causes require prevention or acceptance.

**Root cause / root causation**: A cause that, if addressed, would prevent recurrence of the outcome. "Root cause" is often contested because multiple root causes often exist across different meta-categories.

**Scope**: Dimension assessing whether a cause is singular (this incident only), local (affects a department), or systemic (affects many independent incidents). Scope determines whether intervention should be case-specific or systematic redesign.

**Structural causation**: Pre-existing conditions, arrangements, or constraints that enable or constrain outcomes. Structural causes are often invisible as causes because they are "background conditions."

**Systemic causation**: Causation arising from interactions, feedback loops, and relational dynamics across system components. Systemic causation is distributed and difficult to assign to a single actor.

**Temporal dynamics**: Dimension assessing the timing pattern of causation (sudden, gradual, chronic, cyclical). Temporal dynamics determine appropriate governance mode (emergency response vs long-term redesign).

**Type**: One of 30 specific causal mechanisms within the six meta-categories. Each type is a recurring pattern that can be identified, measured, and managed.

**Visibility**: Dimension assessing whether a causal factor is easily observable or remains hidden. Hidden factors require active discovery and monitoring.

## Appendix A: Problem-Focused Causal Question Set (optional but recommended)

Use this structured question set to focus causation analysis on the decision problem at hand:

| Decision/Problem | Causal questions |
|---|---|
| **Post-incident investigation** | What caused this incident? What were the triggering events? What preconditions made it possible? What controls failed? What would have prevented it? |
| **Recurring failure pattern** | Why does this keep happening? Is the cause individual/agentive (who is making the mistake?) or systemic (what design enables the mistake)? What would prevent recurrence? |
| **Performance degradation** | Why is performance declining? Is it a sudden drop (trigger) or gradual erosion (accumulation)? Is it cyclical? What is the trajectory? |
| **Intervention failure** | Why didn't the intervention work? Was the problem diagnosed correctly? Was the causal mechanism misunderstood? Did the intervention address the right lever? |
| **Responsibility assignment** | What is the causal contribution of each actor? What choices did they make? What constraints did they face? What was under their control? |
| **Prevention design** | What are the leading causal indicators of failure? What controls would address causal drivers? What should be monitored? |
| **System redesign** | What causal factors recur across cases? What structural or systemic patterns emerge? What design changes would address root causes? |
