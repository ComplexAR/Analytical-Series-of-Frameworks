# Introduction

## What is Power?

Power is the structured capacity of an agent or configuration to shape **what is possible** for oneself and for others. It is not merely force or money: it includes authority, rule-setting, gatekeeping, network leverage, meaning-making, protection, and the ability to redesign institutions and option spaces. Power is inherently relational and asymmetric: it exists in who can constrain whom, who can compel compliance, who can set agendas, and who can create or remove alternatives. In complex socio-technical systems, power is typically **multi-base** and **multi-level**: formal roles may not track real leverage; quiet chokepoints (platforms, procurement, data access, standards, informal coalitions) often matter more than visible hierarchy. Power also converts across bases: resources can buy narratives; narratives can create legitimacy; legitimacy can reduce enforcement costs and stabilise control.

## Why does Power matter for Hiddens work?

Power is a primary source of systematic visibility failure. When power relations are mis-analysed, hidden, or treated as inevitable constraints, key decision-drivers remain invisible until they generate failure or cascade. This framework surfaces several families of hiding mechanisms: **Power Denial** (treating situations as purely technical while ignoring who can constrain whom); **Surface Power Focus** (attending only to visible roles and laws while neglecting structural, network, and discursive power); **Positional Invisibility** (different roles see radically different power distributions; elites often misperceive constraints felt at frontline); **Shadow Systems** (informal decision rights and off-books enforcement); **Narrative Capture** (dominant frames hide how power is actually distributed and exercised). Without this framework, these Hiddens remain latent until they generate failure. With it, power becomes an explicit diagnostic dimension that supports realistic feasibility assessment and governance design.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises power analysis through six core elements: (1) a taxonomy of 30 power types organised into six meta-categories (Structural/Positional, Resource/Capability, Relational/Network, Discursive/Epistemic, Coercive/Protective, Generative/Transformational); (2) five cross-cutting dimensions (scope, intensity, visibility, legitimacy, mode) that profile any power relation; (3) five dynamic patterns (accumulation, circulation, conversion, counter-power, erosion) that describe how power shifts over time; (4) three interface types (alignment, contestation, translation) that make visible where power bases meet and interact; (5) hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans that surface characteristic visibility failures; and (6) a six-step application protocol that moves from agent mapping through power profiling, dynamics analysis, interface mapping, Hiddens checking, and governance design. The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader investigation workflow.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework whenever formal authority and resource maps alone fail to explain why outcomes persist, why "reasonable" proposals fail, or why interventions don't work as designed. It works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch power mapping, type classification, and Tier 1 Hiddens scan) and Investigative Run Mode (deep dimensional profiling, multi-stakeholder mapping, full Hiddens scanning). Default execution is Light Depth: map agents, classify 5–10 dominant relations into power types, profile one or two dimensions, spot obvious interfaces, and run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, power asymmetry is extreme, capture risks are plausible, or contestation is active. The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all power claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown and explicitly recording disagreements and residual unknowns rather than collapsing them into false consensus. For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Power is the structured capacity to shape what is possible for oneself and for others. It manifests through six meta-categories—Structural & Positional, Resource & Capability, Relational & Network, Discursive & Epistemic, Coercive & Protective, and Generative & Transformational—and operates through 30 identifiable types that can be mapped, measured, and contested. Power is inherently relational and asymmetric: A has power over B to the extent that B's options or outcomes depend on A's choices or capacity. Critically, power is multi-base and multi-level, meaning a single agent may hold multiple bases that reinforce or substitute for one another, and power may be concentrated or distributed across multiple levels and boundaries. The framework produces operational artefacts: a power map (actors × relations), classification into power types, five-dimension profiles (scope, intensity, visibility, legitimacy, mode) that quantify feasibility constraints, and interface/dynamics analyses that identify where configurations are stable, contested, or convertible. These outputs support realistic decisions, intervention portfolios, and governance design grounded in what is actually feasible under the power constraints present.

Because power is frequently concealed, misreported, and position-dependent, Section 8 treats hiddens detection and a tiered Hiddens scan as mandatory for non-trivial or high-stakes analysis. The framework also explicitly links to governance and legitimacy: power that lacks legitimacy requires higher maintenance costs and generates sustained resistance; power that lacks corresponding responsibility and accountability creates accountability gaps and cascading failure risks.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals
- **Definition of power (operational)**: the structured capacity of an agent or configuration to shape options, constraints, and outcomes for itself and others.
- **In scope**:
  - Six meta-categories and thirty core power types (baseline vocabulary)
  - Five dimensions for profiling power relations (scope, intensity, visibility, legitimacy, mode)
  - Power dynamics (how power shifts over time) and interface types (where power bases meet)
  - Hiddens source analysis + tiered Hiddens cross-check
  - Application protocol and deliverables for power-aware analysis and design
- **Out of scope**:
  - A full ethical theory of power (use Values/Legitimacy for normative analysis)
  - Full intervention catalogue (use Interventions; this framework informs feasibility and leverage selection)
  - Detailed causal mechanism decomposition (use Causation/Systems; this framework grounds what is realistically manipulable)
- **Common confusions**: "Power is not…": (1) Power is not merely coercion or force; quiet structural and network power often dominates. (2) Power is not synonymous with authority; many powerful actors operate informally or in shadow. (3) Power is not static; configurations accumulate, circulate, convert, and erode over time.

### 1.6.3 Routing triggers
- Formal authority/org chart diverges from actual influence and decision outcomes
- "Reasonable" proposals repeatedly fail to gain traction despite rational merit
- Interventions or reforms succeed with some stakeholders but fail with others
- Gatekeepers, brokers, or informal leaders have disproportionate leverage vs formal role
- Stakeholders disagree on who decides or who has authority (contested power)
- Hidden coalitions or shadow governance mechanisms are suspected or evidenced
- Narratives or "technical" framing appear designed to hide or legitimise power asymmetry
- Resource scarcity creates chokepoints; control of those chokepoints drives outcomes
- Capture risk is plausible (formal rules subverted; enforcement selective or absent)
- Feasibility question cannot be answered without understanding who can constrain whom
- Multi-stakeholder alignment is fragile; counter-power or exit threats are active
- Legitimacy of power distribution is contested; compliance is conditional or resisted

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Structural & Positional Power
Formal roles, rule systems, ownership, and structural positions in hierarchies, markets, and networks. Determines who has authority, who controls access, and whose decisions are binding. Typically the most visible and defensible power base, but often masks more decisive network and discursive power beneath.

### II. Resource & Capability Power
Control over material resources (assets, facilities, supply chains), financial resources (budgets, capital, liquidity), human resources (expertise, labour, credentialing), information and analytical capability, and technological systems. Higher resource endowment typically increases options and flexibility; unequal resource distribution creates dependence.

### III. Relational & Network Power
Emerges from position in networks of connection and trust. Brokerage (bridging disconnected groups), centrality (being hub-like), coalition capacity (assembling joint leverage), and social capital (reputation and reciprocity) all function as power bases. Often operates quietly and converts readily into structural and discursive power.

### IV. Discursive & Epistemic Power
The ability to shape how reality is framed, what counts as knowledge, whose expertise is granted deference, and what issues dominate agendas. Operates through narratives, standards, categories, and legitimacy claims. Often the most difficult power to contest because it shapes what appears possible or natural.

### V. Coercive & Protective Power
Capacity to credibly threaten harm (force, legal sanction, economic punishment, cyber attack) or to protect others from harm (security, legal cover, buffering). Produces compliance through fear of harm or dependence on protection. Can be visible (obvious enforcement) or subtle (routine surveillance creating discipline).

### VI. Generative & Transformational Power
The ability to create new options, redesign institutions, convene multiple actors, shift narratives and imaginaries, and adapt under shocks. Often exercised by innovators, institutional architects, conveners, and resilience-builders. Represents power to expand the possibility space rather than to control within existing constraints.

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Power Denial & Neutrality Illusion | Treating situations as purely technical, economic, or procedural while ignoring underlying power relations ("we are just following the process"). Underestimates how power shapes options, incentives, and voices. | Explicitly ask: Who gains or loses from this configuration? Who can say "no"? Who sets the terms? Make power an explicit analytic dimension. |
| 2 | Surface Power Focus | Attending only to formal, visible, or coercive power (roles, org charts, laws) while neglecting structural, network, discursive, and resource-based power that may be more decisive. | Map both formal and informal power; look at networks, narratives, resources, and boundaries, not just roles and laws. |
| 3 | Static Snapshot Bias | Analysing power at a single moment and ignoring dynamics of accumulation, erosion, conversion, and resistance. Leads to underestimation of emerging counter-power and overestimation of stability. | Analyse trajectories: where did this power configuration come from, where is it trending, and what events could reconfigure it? |
| 4 | Unidimensional Reduction | Reducing power to a single basis (for example, "money", "force") and neglecting how multiple bases combine, compensate, or substitute for each other in specific situations. | Systematically scan all six meta-categories and 30 power types; look for substitution, complementarity, and hidden compensations between power bases. |
| 5 | Hiddens Omission and Strategic Concealment | Assuming power relations are fully visible and faithfully reported. Hidden principals, shadow structures, distorted accounts, and positional silencing produce systematically wrong maps. | Run the tiered Hiddens scan; treat neutrality claims as hypotheses; triangulate across positions and artefacts; assign detection and remediation moves to the interface model. |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)
| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| I  Perceptual | Power is often subtle, normalised, and cognitively backgrounded; analysts default to visible authority and miss quiet constraints. | Surprise vetoes; 'inexplicable' non-decisions; missing gatekeepers; overconfidence in organisational charts. | Fresh-eyes power map; dependency/chokepoint walkthrough; counterfactual "who could stop this?" prompts. |
| II Systemic | Institutions and incentives create systematic misrepresentation of power (compliance theatre, scapegoating, capture). | Formal vs actual authority divergence; repeating failure despite 'correct' policies; measurement Goodharting. | Principal–agent audit; incentive tracing; compare written rules vs lived practice; capture indicators. |
| III Informational | Information about power is politically controlled: secrecy, propaganda, and selective disclosure are common. | Conflicting accounts; missing logs/records; sudden narrative shifts; taboo topics. | Access and provenance audit; triangulate independent sources; shadow-channel mapping; forced transparency points. |
| IV Temporal | Power configurations are path-dependent and evolve; legacy lock-in and institutional forgetting distort current maps. | Rules justified by history no one can explain; 'we tried that' without evidence; drift in mandates and enforcement. | Timeline of mandates and reforms; institutional memory retrieval; review of past crises and settlements; successor mapping. |
| V  Relational | Role and position govern what is sayable and visible; low-power actors often cannot report truthfully without risk. | Frontline silence; fear of retaliation; disproportionate confidence from high-status accounts; 'everyone agrees' illusions. | Positional sampling; protected disclosure; stakeholder power mapping; dissent and whistleblowing channel review. |
| VI Ontological | Some 'power' is emergent (markets, culture, infrastructure) and is misclassified as personal intent or 'just the way things are'. | Reification ('the market decided'); inability to name hybrid platform power; persistent anomalies in accountability. | Emergent-system tests; category review; avoid personification without safeguards; create new subtype labels if needed. |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) Run Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (assume hiddens exist).
2) Shortlist Tier 2 hidden types (particularly Blindspot, Distortion, Secret, Shadow, Positional, Emergent Invisibility) and rate detectability/agency/consequence.
3) Assign at least one detection and one remediation move per high-consequence hidden; attach to the interface model (Alignment/Contestation/Translation).
4) Re-run hiddens sources; define escalation triggers and monitoring indicators.
5) If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG v2.5 §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3).

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)
Escalate to a full-spectrum Hiddens scan + detection/remediation matrix mapping if any apply:
- High consequence (safety, regulatory, major irreversibility, systemic harm, reputational exposure)
- Strong incentives for concealment (capture, corruption, sensitive operations, adversarial contexts)
- Persistent disputes over "who really decides" or "why we can't do X"
- Evidence of shadow decision rights or propaganda/echo loops
- Repeat failures of interventions despite formal authority or resources
- Governance changes or power reconfiguration is being contemplated

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| Explicit, Not Assumed, Power Analysis | Treat power as a distinct analytic dimension in any situation, rather than assuming it is already "baked into" other categories such as resources, roles, or culture. Make power relations, bases, and dynamics visible and discussable. |
| Multi-Base and Multi-Level Perspective | Always scan across all six meta-categories and across relevant system levels (from local to systemic) when analysing power. Avoid explanations that reduce power to a single basis (for example, money or hierarchy) or a single level (for example, "the organisation"). |
| Dynamic and Scenario-Oriented Mapping | Analyse how power is likely to accumulate, circulate, convert, or erode over time under different scenarios. Use the dynamics and interface types to identify tipping points, vulnerabilities, and opportunities for constructive reconfiguration. |
| Normative and Legitimacy-Aware Use | Combine the Framework of Power with the Framework of Values to examine how power is justified, contested, or abused. Distinguish between capacity and legitimate use; design interventions that both work and are ethically defensible. |
| Empowerment and Safeguard Orientation | Use power analysis not only to detect threats or dominance, but also to design empowerment pathways, protections, and counter-power for vulnerable agents. Prioritise configurations that enhance resilience, pluralism, and fair voice. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms; recommended; mandatory for "Stable" status)

## 11.1 Core Glossary Table
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Power | The structured capacity of an agent or configuration of agents to shape what is possible for themselves and others, by enabling, constraining, or transforming actions, options, and outcomes. |
| Power Configuration | The overall pattern of power relations among agents in a situation, including who has what kinds of power, over whom, and in which domains. |
| Power Relation | A specific asymmetric relation between agents (or between an agent and a system) in which one has some capacity to shape the options or outcomes of the other. |
| Power-Over | Power exercised to constrain or direct others' actions against or irrespective of their preferences (domination, coercion, control). |
| Power-To | An agent's capacity to act and achieve outcomes (capability, competence, agency), regardless of whether this is exercised over others. |
| Power-With | Joint power arising from cooperation and collective action (coalitions, alliances, collaborative capacity). |
| Power-Within | Inner resources of confidence, identity, and self-understanding that enable agents to recognise and pursue their own interests and values. |
| Structural & Positional Power | Power arising from formal roles, rules, ownership, and structural positions in systems or markets. |
| Resource & Capability Power | Power arising from control over material, financial, human, informational, or technological resources and capabilities. |
| Relational & Network Power | Power arising from patterns of connection, dependence, and trust that confer brokerage, centrality, or coalition leverage. |
| Discursive & Epistemic Power | Power arising from the ability to shape meanings, narratives, agendas, categories, and what counts as knowledge. |
| Coercive & Protective Power | Power arising from capacities to threaten or apply harm, impose sanctions, enforce compliance, or shield from harm. |
| Generative & Transformational Power | Power arising from the ability to create new options, institutions, narratives, and resilient configurations that change the underlying space of possibilities. |
| Gatekeeping | Exercising power by controlling access to resources, arenas, or audiences; a central mechanism of structural & positional power. |
| Agenda-Setting | Exercising power by influencing which issues are considered, prioritised, and resourced, and which are ignored or excluded. |
| Framing | Selecting and highlighting certain aspects of reality and linking them into a coherent story or problem definition, thereby shaping perceived options and responsibilities. |
| Brokerage | Occupying a position that connects otherwise disconnected actors or groups, enabling control or shaping of information and resource flows. |
| Legitimacy | The degree to which the exercise of power is perceived as rightful, appropriate, or justified by those affected and by relevant audiences. |
| Domination | A persistent configuration in which some agents systematically have power over others in ways that the latter cannot reasonably escape or contest. |
| Empowerment | Processes that increase an agent's power-to and power-with, often by building capabilities, resources, and legitimacy, and by changing structures and narratives. |
| Counter-Power | Power developed and exercised to resist, constrain, or transform dominant power configurations (for example, social movements, investigative journalism, open-source ecosystems). |
| Capture | A situation in which institutions, regulators, or decision processes come under the disproportionate influence of particular interests, distorting their purposes and outputs. |
| Shadow Power | Influence exercised informally or off the official record (for example, back-channel negotiations, informal networks, hidden sponsors). |
| Platform Power | Power arising from controlling a technical or organisational platform on which others depend, combining structural, resource, network, and often discursive power. |
| Soft Power | The capacity to influence others through attraction, persuasion, and perceived legitimacy rather than coercion or payments. |
| Hard Power | The capacity to influence others through coercion or explicit material incentives, including threats of force or sanctions. |
