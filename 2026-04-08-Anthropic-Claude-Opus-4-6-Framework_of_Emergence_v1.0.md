# Introduction

## What is Emergence?

Emergence is the phenomenon by which complex systems produce properties, behaviours, and outcomes at the system level that cannot be predicted from or reduced to the properties of individual components. A flock of starlings produces coordinated geometric patterns with no central choreographer; traffic congestion arises from individual driver decisions that no single driver intends; a financial market crash emerges from many rational actors responding to incentives; a species forms and becomes distinct not because any organism consciously drove speciation but because accumulated small variations, environmental pressures, and reproductive isolation created qualitatively new organisation. In each case, the system-level phenomenon is unexpected, non-linear, threshold-dependent, or qualitatively different from what component analysis alone would predict.

Emergence matters for complex scenario analysis because it is a systematic blindspot. Reductionist analysis—breaking the system into parts, studying each part, predicting overall behaviour from part-level properties—misses emergence entirely. A supply chain optimised for efficiency at every node can become fragile because parts cannot buffer shocks; a distributed organisation designed for autonomy can produce cascade failures that no individual decision created; a population of perfectly rational actors can produce collective outcomes that harm everyone (tragedy of the commons). The gap between component-level analysis and system-level outcome is emergence, and it is where many real-world surprises hide.

## Why does Emergence matter for Hiddens work?

Emergence generates hiddens through multiple mechanisms. Non-linearity blindspot (Hidden-2 in the Hiddens taxonomy) is the systematic failure to anticipate threshold-crossing and tipping-point behaviour; analysis that works under normal conditions (linear models, component averaging) becomes catastrophically wrong at transition points. Reductionist invisibility (Hidden-3) makes system-level emergent properties invisible because they only exist at the aggregate level—individual components have no properties that sum to the emergent whole. Temporal mismatch (Hidden-5) is the gap between the timescale of emergence (e.g., ecological succession, financial market herding, cascade propagation) and the timescale of investigation—the process is invisible because it is too slow or too fast to be observed in available windows.

Cascade invisibility (Hidden-4) occurs when initial small changes that will cascade to system-level disruption are not identifiable as significant from local observation at the time. Ontological novelty (Hidden-6) means that genuinely emergent phenomena may lack existing analytical categories, leaving them unnameable and therefore harder to detect and govern. The Framework of Emergence is designed specifically to surface these hiding mechanisms and convert emergence from "surprise" into "foreseeable phenomenon requiring specific governance."

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises emergence analysis through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 emergence types** organised into six meta-categories (Non-Linear Dynamics, Threshold & Tipping Points, Cascade & Propagation, Self-Organisation & Adaptation, Lock-In & Path Dependence, Ontological Novelty) that recur across domains and enable classification of emergent phenomena.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (Predictability & Observability, Reversibility & Robustness, Temporal Scope, Spatial Extent, Consequence & Stakeholder Impact) that profile any emergent phenomenon and determine its analytic and governance priority.

3. **Twelve dynamic patterns** (Bifurcation, Herding, Contagion, Punctuated Equilibrium, Lock-In Amplification, Hybrid Emergence) that describe how emergent phenomena develop over time and signal where interventions may succeed or backfire.

4. **Three interface types** (Detection & Sensing, Intervention & Control, Adaptation & Learning) that make visible how emergence can be detected early, influenced or constrained, and learned-from in real-time.

5. **Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** that surface five systematic sources of emergence-analysis blindness (Aggregation Reduction, Temporal Horizon Mismatch, Nonlinearity Incompleteness, Category Absence, Intervention Paradox) and map them to the six Hiddens meta-categories (I–VI).

6. **A six-step application protocol** that moves from emergence identification through classification, dimensional profiling, dynamic pattern analysis, interface design, and governance, producing standard registers (Hiddens, Evidence, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests).

The framework populates shared registers (Group Coverage Matrix, Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series workflow and enable hand-off to downstream frameworks (Decisions, Interventions, Governance, Risk, Learning & Adaptation).

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework when system-level properties are plausible and component-level or reductionist analysis appears incomplete. Typical triggers include: unexpected system behaviour despite component-level optimisation, non-linear or threshold phenomena, cascade risk, self-organisation or phase-change candidates, or interventions that produce counterintuitive effects. The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (quick emergence classification and risk scan) and Investigative Run Mode (full dimensional profiling, mechanism chains, Tier 2+ Hiddens scanning, scenario stress tests).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: classify the focal emergent phenomenon(a) into the 30-type taxonomy, assess against the five key dimensions, identify obvious interface risks (detection, intervention, adaptation failures), run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan (all six meta-categories touched lightly). Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, multiple emergent phenomena interact, past interventions have failed to account for emergence, or ontological novelty is suspected. The framework's companion Prompt Discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all emergence type claims and mechanism assertions as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly preserving uncertainties rather than collapsing them into false consensus.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, canonical Prompt Discipline Rules pointers, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario, the Operating Guide, and the six core elements, producing disciplined emergence analysis with explicit mechanisms, F/I/A/U tagging, dimensional profiles, and bounded closure.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Emergence is not rare or exotic. It is the default in complex adaptive systems—systems with many agents, strong feedback, non-linear interactions, and boundaries that are permeable, contested, or evolutionarily shifting. Where emergence occurs, reductionist analysis systematically fails because it assumes: that system behaviour is the sum of component properties; that relationships are linear (doubling input doubles output); that time evolution follows from initial conditions in obvious ways; that categories of phenomena are stable and pre-existing. When all four assumptions hold, reductionism works. When any breaks, emergence appears and reductionism becomes dangerously blind.

Emergence expresses itself through several recurrent patterns. **Non-linear dynamics** means that proportional changes in inputs do not produce proportional changes in outputs; thresholds exist where behaviour qualitatively shifts. **Tipping points** are unstable equilibria where small perturbations produce large cascades. **Cascades** are propagation chains where local interactions amplify across boundaries. **Self-organisation** occurs when distributed components spontaneously coordinate without centralised control or explicit direction. **Path dependence** means historical contingency locks systems into particular trajectories; alternative outcomes become inaccessible not because they are impossible but because the path to them is blocked. **Ontological novelty** means genuinely new phenomena emerge that were not present at the component level.

This framework provides a structured way to:
- recognise and classify emergent phenomena against a 30-type taxonomy,
- assess their temporal, spatial, and consequence characteristics,
- identify detection and intervention interfaces,
- run a mandatory tiered Hiddens cross-check for non-trivial emergence work, and
- convert emergent phenomena from "surprise" into "foreseeable and governable reality."

Because emergence analysis is vulnerable to visibility failures (aggregation reduction, temporal mismatch, category absence, intervention paradox), the framework explicitly pairs with **Framework of Hiddens v4.5** as the series-standard visibility audit layer. Tier 1 and Tier 2 checks provide fast coverage for routine emergence work; Tier 3 escalation means running full emergence taxonomy analysis plus detection/remediation mapping and scenario stress-testing.

## 1.2 Assumptions & Preconditions

Emergence analysis often fails because implicit dependency claims are left untested: the system boundary is assumed rather than argued; the timescale of emergence is assumed visible when it may be too slow or fast; components are assumed independent when they are coupled; categories are assumed stable when they are shifting. The tables below surface the most consequential assumptions and preconditions that sit beneath emergence mapping and intervention design. If a high-impact assumption is weak or contested, treat it as an **uncertainty** and decide whether to reduce, hedge, robustify, or preserve options. If an assumption may be strategically suppressed or distorted, run an explicit **Hiddens** scan and consider escalation.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals
- **Emergence**: a system-level property, behaviour, or outcome that arises from interactions between components and cannot be predicted from or reduced to the components individually.
- **Non-linear dynamics**: behaviour where proportional changes in input do not produce proportional changes in output; often exhibits thresholds and bifurcations.
- **Tipping point**: an unstable equilibrium or threshold beyond which a system rapidly transitions to a qualitatively different state.
- **Cascade**: propagation of local interactions across boundaries that amplifies in scope or consequence.
- **Self-organisation**: spontaneous coordination of distributed components without centralised control.
- **Path dependence**: historical contingency that locks a system into particular trajectories; alternative futures become unreachable.
- **Ontological novelty**: emergence of genuinely new properties or categories that did not exist at any lower level.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Non-Linear Dynamics
- **Definition**: System behaviour where inputs and outputs do not maintain proportional relationships; behaviour depends on current state and history; feedback creates amplification or damping that varies with conditions.
- **Diagnostic cues**: "Doubling resources didn't double output"; "small changes had big effects and big changes had small effects"; "performance jumped suddenly"; "we don't see proportional response to inputs."
- **Typical failure mode**: Linear models are applied to non-linear systems; threshold behaviour is not anticipated; feedback amplification is mistaken for component failure; state-dependent behaviour is missed.

### II. Threshold & Tipping Points
- **Definition**: Unstable or critical equilibria at which systems rapidly transition from one state to another; small perturbations near the threshold produce large jumps; behaviour is reversible only at cost or not at all.
- **Diagnostic cues**: "The system seemed stable then suddenly failed"; "we have early-warning signals but no mid-course correction point"; "recovery is much harder than collapse"; "the transition was too fast to intervene."
- **Typical failure mode**: Tipping points are not identified until after transition; early-warning signals are missed or misinterpreted; interventions assume linear improvement path instead of cliff or irreversibility; critical thresholds are treated as moving targets rather than structural invariants.

### III. Cascade & Propagation
- **Definition**: Local interactions amplify across boundaries, producing system-wide effects; failures or changes propagate through networks or feedback loops faster than governance can respond.
- **Diagnostic cues**: "It started small but spread across the whole system"; "no single point of failure but multiple local failures cascaded"; "the system had no firebreaks for local containment"; "contagion jumped across compartments we thought were isolated."
- **Typical failure mode**: Cascade risk is not modelled; local controls are assumed sufficient; cross-boundary interactions are missed; amplification loops are not mapped; speed of propagation is underestimated.

### IV. Self-Organisation & Adaptation
- **Definition**: Distributed components spontaneously coordinate and adapt to produce emergent collective behaviour without centralised direction, explicit planning, or even awareness that coordination is occurring.
- **Diagnostic cues**: "The system organised itself in ways we didn't design"; "we tried to centrally control it but distributed logic won out"; "local rules produced global patterns"; "the system adapted faster than we could formally re-plan."
- **Typical failure mode**: Attempts to control or centralise self-organising systems backfire; the adaptive capacity is overridden; local incentives produce unintended collective outcomes; distinction between designed and emerged structure is blurred.

### V. Lock-In & Path Dependence
- **Definition**: Historical contingencies and reinforcing feedback create irreversible trajectories; the system becomes locked into a particular state or behaviour pattern; alternative outcomes become unreachable even if preferable.
- **Diagnostic cues**: "We're stuck in this pattern despite knowing it's suboptimal"; "switching costs are too high"; "past decisions foreclose present options"; "the system rewards staying the course even when direction is wrong."
- **Typical failure mode**: Sunk cost and lock-in are conflated with rational preference; the possibility of escape is assumed when exit is actually blocked; interventions ignore that lock-in is self-reinforcing; alternatives are treated as still available when they have been closed off.

### VI. Ontological Novelty
- **Definition**: Genuinely new phenomena emerge that were not present or observable at any component level; existing conceptual frameworks and categories do not accommodate the phenomenon; new language or framing is required.
- **Diagnostic cues**: "We don't have a name for this"; "it doesn't fit our standard categories"; "we're making up the category as we observe"; "past frameworks don't capture what's happening"; "the phenomenon crosses category boundaries."
- **Typical failure mode**: New phenomena are forced into existing categories, distorting both phenomenon and category; absence of category is misinterpreted as absence of reality; governance fails because it assumes stable categories; learning is incomplete because phenomenon is unnamed or miscategorised.

## 3.1 Types (Category I: Non-Linear Dynamics)
| # | Type | Description (1–3 sentences) | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | State-Dependent Amplification | System response to an input depends on current state; small input in one state produces large output, same input in another state produces small output. Feedback loops have direction and magnitude that vary with state. | "Why did that intervention work last time but not now?"; behaviour changes unpredictably as state changes; same stimulus produces different responses. | Reductionist analysis assumes state-independent properties; model failures blamed on external factors or incompetence when state-dependence is the cause. |
| 2 | Positive Feedback Amplification | Feedback mechanisms amplify change in one direction, creating runaway or explosive growth; the system lacks equilibrating negative feedback; disturbance accelerates rather than damps. | Exponential or accelerating growth; "we lose control of it once it starts"; small disturbances trigger large excursions; system reaches extremes unexpectedly. | Positive feedback is mistaken for linear growth; governance assumes correction mechanisms exist when they don't; intervention timing is too late once amplification is visible. |
| 3 | Oscillation & Cycling | System behaviour oscillates between states; feedback mechanisms create time-lagged responses that overshoot and reverse, producing regular or irregular cycles. | Cyclical patterns in metrics; "we swing from one extreme to another"; oscillation frequency is constant even though causes change. | Oscillation is blamed on external variation; lag times are not identified; interventions target amplitude instead of frequency, failing to break the cycle. |
| 4 | Hysteresis & Path Dependency | System response depends on trajectory, not just current state; the path to a state matters; transitioning in opposite direction does not retrace the path; transitions are not reversible. | "It's harder to come back than to go away"; recovery curve differs from collapse curve; system behaviour is different after a disturbance even at the same input level. | One-direction causation is assumed; reversibility of change is taken for granted; recovery is assumed to be mirror image of collapse. |
| 5 | Bifurcation & State Transition | Small change in a parameter or condition causes qualitative shift in system behaviour; new behaviour modes or stability regions emerge; system splits into multiple possible states. | "Behaviour changed suddenly when condition crossed a threshold"; multiple possible stable states coexist; system "chooses" between them based on small perturbations. | Linear extrapolation misses bifurcation; behaviour change is attributed to external shock rather than internal instability; transition direction is unpredictable. |

|---|---|---|---|---|

| # | Type | Description (1–3 sentences) | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | Network Contagion & Diffusion | Change or failure propagates through network connections; spread rate depends on network structure and connection density; nodes far from initial failure are still reached. | Rapid spread through connected populations; "it spread like wildfire through the network"; infection/adoption follows network structure; isolated nodes are spared. | Network structure is not mapped; spread is modelled as well-mixed when structure matters; boundary effects and network clustering are missed; containment assumes random distribution. |

## 3.5 Types (Category V: Lock-In & Path Dependence)

|---|---|---|---|---|
| 21 | Increasing Returns & Self-Reinforcement | Early adoption creates feedback that raises cost of alternatives; installed base and network effects create runaway advantage for one option. | "Once enough people adopted it, everyone had to"; switching cost grows over time; first-mover advantage compounds; minority option becomes lock-in. | Lock-in is attributed to superior features rather than self-reinforcement; cost of exit is underestimated; alternatives are assumed still available when critical mass has been crossed. |

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)
| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | 30 types across 6 meta-categories (5 per category) provide coverage of emergence phenomena across diverse domains. |
| Domain-specific refinement | Within each meta-category, domain expertise may identify sub-types or context-specific variants (e.g., financial emergence vs ecological emergence). Document refinement with rationale and map back to canonical parent. |
| Preserving mappability | Any extension must map clearly back to one of the six meta-categories. A new type should refine or split an existing canonical type, not add a new meta-category (unless structural change is deliberate and versioned). |
| Structural invariants | The six meta-categories should remain stable; extensions should operate at the type level. If a meta-category is added or removed, increment major version and justify in §12. |
| Periodic reassessment | As domains change and new emergence phenomena are observed, schedule periodic reassessment of whether canonical types adequately cover the landscape. Use "none-of-the-above" frequency as a trigger. |
| Versioning & governance | Document all extensions in version history (§12.1); tag domain-specific additions clearly; maintain mappings backward to canonical v1.0. Control changes through formal review process. |

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Emergence

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)
| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Predictability & Observability | How clearly can the emergent phenomenon be anticipated or observed before it fully manifests? Can early-warning signals be identified? Is the phenomenon visible only in retrospect? | High (observable early warnings; predictable within margin), Medium (observable but only partially; some warning possible), Low (invisible until manifestation; no early warning or only post-hoc observation) | Look for baseline data, historical incidence, model-ability, whether surprise was inevitable or avoidable. | Determines whether prevention, mitigation, or adaptation is the viable strategy; shapes monitoring and governance design. |
| 2. Reversibility & Robustness | Can the emergent phenomenon be reversed or contained? Is the system fragile to perturbation once emergence occurs? How much cost/time/energy is required to reverse? | High (reversible at low cost; system robust to perturbation), Medium (reversible but at significant cost; limited robustness), Low (irreversible or reversal cost is prohibitive; system fragile to perturbation) | Examine past reversal attempts; estimate switching/recovery costs; assess alternative stable states. | Shapes intervention strategy: prevent emergence (if reversible cost is high) or adapt/live-with-emergence (if cost is prohibitive). |
| 3. Temporal Scope | What is the timescale of emergence: onset, full manifestation, and potential reversal? Is emergence fast (hours/days) or slow (years/decades)? Are there inflection points? | Fast (hours to weeks; visible within investigation timeframe), Medium (weeks to months; observable across a programme), Slow (years to decades; may exceed investigation and governance horizons) | Examine historical emergence of similar phenomena; use case-study analogues; model latency and accumulation. | Determines whether emergence is visible in governance cycles; informs whether adaptation can keep pace; shapes information requests for timeline. |
| 4. Spatial Extent & Boundary Transcendence | Does the emergent phenomenon operate only within a local or defined region, or does it propagate across boundaries? How many scales or domains does it span? | Localised (affects specific node/domain; limited boundary crossing), Regional (affects multiple connected nodes/domains; crosses some boundaries), System-wide (affects all connected nodes/domains; pervasive boundary crossing) | Map cascade pathways and network structure; test in scenarios; assess isolation or buffering mechanisms. | Determines containment vs system-wide governance requirements; shapes intervention boundary; informs information requests on scale. |
| 5. Consequence & Stakeholder Impact | What is the magnitude of harm, benefit, or disruption if emergence occurs? Whose interests are affected? Is impact concentrated or distributed? | High (major harm/benefit to key stakeholders; concentrated impact), Medium (moderate harm/benefit; distributed impact), Low (minor or threshold-effect impact; limited stakeholder concern) | Stakeholder impact assessment; cost-benefit analysis; comparative risk; use consequence scenarios. | Determines governance urgency and escalation triggers; shapes resource allocation; prioritises monitoring and intervention. |

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)
| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Bifurcation Cascade | System exhibits multiple bifurcations in sequence; each bifurcation opens new possibilities and closes others; trajectory through bifurcation space is path-dependent. | Parameter change → bifurcation → new baseline → parameter change → bifurcation in new baseline → trajectory. | Late discovery of bifurcation point means intervention is infeasible; understanding bifurcation sequence allows strategic parameter control. | Monitor proximity to bifurcation points; design control parameters that avoid bifurcation regions; maintain linearly-accessible states when possible. |
| 2 | Herding & Information Cascade | Initial uncertain information or early-adopter actions trigger information cascade; later agents weight observed actions more than independent information; cascade accelerates and creates momentum. | Ambiguity → early actor decision → observation by others → decision to follow observed → cascade → commitment → lock-in. | Cascade velocity is invisible early; late intervention is too late; initial decision makers underestimate influence; cascade is blamed on information quality not cascade mechanism. | Diversify early-adopter pool; inject countervailing information; slow cascade through friction or delay; identify and interrupt feedback loops. |
| 3 | Contagion & Epidemic Spread | Emergence spreads through contact (infection, information, belief); spread rate depends on contact probability and infectiousness; threshold exists above which spread is exponential. | Infected individual × contact rate × susceptible population → new infections → exponential growth until threshold or threshold. | Spread is modelled as linear or well-mixed when network structure matters; threshold is not identified; containment assumes random distribution. | Map contact network; identify bottlenecks; increase distance between infected and susceptible; reduce contact rate or infectiousness; vaccinate/inoculate before threshold. |
| 4 | Punctuated Equilibrium & Jump Dynamics | System appears stable for extended period (stasis) then rapidly transitions to new stability region; transition appears sudden but is result of slow accumulation of unseen stress. | Slow accumulation (hidden) + trigger event → rapid transition → new equilibrium → slow accumulation. | Stasis is misinterpreted as stability; accumulation is invisible; trigger event is blamed when accumulated stress is the cause; trigger is not necessary, just coincidental. | Monitor accumulation metrics; identify hidden stressors; reduce trigger sensitivity; design buffer capacity; prepare for rapid transition; plan for new equilibrium. |
| 5 | Lock-In Amplification | Initial commitment creates path dependence; path dependence increases cost of alternatives; increasing cost reinforces initial choice; lock-in deepens with time. | Initial choice → increasing returns → switching cost rises → alternative options foreclose → lock-in deepens. | Lock-in is attributed to choice quality not to mechanism; alternatives are assumed available until too late; escape windows close undetected. | Identify lock-in mechanism early; maintain option value during window; invest in reversibility while it is cheap; diversify options before lock-in deepens. |
| 6 | Emergence Maturation & Crystallisation | New emergent phenomenon begins as surprise/anomaly; observers develop explanatory frameworks; frameworks become categories; categories become institutionalised; institutional inertia sets in. | Surprise → ad-hoc explanation → category formation → institutional routine → inertia → resistance to change. | Early emergence is misinterpreted as anomaly; once named/categorised, phenomenon is treated as stable; adaptive potential of emergent phenomenon is lost. | Preserve ambiguity during emergence phase; maintain multiple explanatory frames; delay crystallisation; keep phenomenon fluid longer before institutionalising. |
| 7 | Cascading Failure & Systemic Breakdown | System exhibits increasing fragility as components specialise or optimise locally; single failure propagates due to tight coupling and lack of buffers; system-wide breakdown becomes likely. | Local optimisation → reduced coupling slackness → increased fragility → single failure → cascade → system breakdown. | Fragility is invisible until breakdown; coupling is mapped only after failure; local optimisation is credited with success until cascade occurs. | Maintain diversity and redundancy; decouple critical components; maintain slack for shock absorption; design for graceful degradation not cascade. |
| 8 | Self-Organisation & Spontaneous Reorganisation | System exhibits crisis response through spontaneous coordination and reorganisation; new structures emerge without design or planning; emerge initially out of disorder then crystallise. | Disruption → local adaptation → emergence of coordination → spontaneous structure → stabilisation → normalisation. | Spontaneous reorganisation is credited to leadership; assumed to be temporary but becomes permanent; adaptive potential is captured by formal systems. | Recognize and protect spontaneous coordination; document emergence before formalising; support diversity and local autonomy; avoid premature crystallisation. |
| 9 | Dual-Process Competition & Switching | System governed by two qualitatively different processes (e.g., rule-based and habit-based; engineered and evolved); processes compete for control; dominance switches based on conditions. | Condition A → Process 1 dominates → stable behaviour → Condition B → Process 2 emerges → new behaviour → switching oscillation. | Switching is blamed on inconsistent execution; dual process is not recognised; intervention targets Process 1 without addressing Process 2. | Identify dual processes; map switching conditions; design interventions addressing both processes; avoid attempting to eliminate one process. |
| 10 | Adaptive Trap & Moving Target | System adapts in response to intervention; adaptation changes the problem landscape; simple moving-target effect means intervention must continually increase to maintain effect. | Intervention → adaptation → reduced effect → increased intervention → faster adaptation → escalation trap. | Adaptation is invisible; intervention failure is blamed on insufficient intensity; trap deepens with escalation; exit is increasingly difficult. | Monitor for adaptation; change intervention strategy not intensity; identify adaptation mechanism; reduce incentive for adaptation; design for robustness not reactive adjustment. |
| 11 | Threshold Wandering & Bifurcation Migration | Tipping point or bifurcation threshold drifts or moves due to system evolution, learning, or feedback; threshold that was stable becomes unstable; new thresholds emerge. | System learns from near-miss → tolerance increases → threshold shifts → prior interventions become less effective → new thresholds appear. | Threshold is treated as fixed; drift is not monitored; interventions are calibrated to past threshold not current threshold; surprise when threshold is crossed at unexpected condition. | Monitor threshold location; re-calibrate interventions as threshold drifts; design adaptive control not fixed setpoints; maintain sensitivity to threshold movement. |
| 12 | Hybrid Emergence & Boundary Dissolution | Genuine hybrids emerge that do not fit existing categories; categorical boundaries become fuzzy; system operates across multiple domains simultaneously. | Categories separate → interaction at boundaries → hybridisation → boundary dissolution → new ontology required. | Hybrid phenomena are forced into existing categories; boundary is treated as stable; new governance is not developed because hybrid was not anticipated. | Recognise and name hybrid phenomena early; develop new categories for hybrids; update governance to accommodate boundary dissolution; maintain flexibility in ontology. |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Aggregation & Averaging Blindness | System-level emergent property is invisible in component-level or averaged data; aggregation reduces variance and obscures non-linearity; emergent phenomenon exists only at system level. | Disaggregate data by multiple dimensions; look for bimodal or multimodal distributions; examine local hotspots rather than averages; use high-resolution temporal and spatial data. |
| 2 | Temporal Mismatch & Observability Gap | Emergence unfolds on timescale that does not match investigation window; slow emergence is invisible (climate shift, institutional inertia, evolutionary adaptation); fast emergence is invisible (cascade, cascade collapse, herding dynamics). | Extend historical data window; use analogues and comparative cases; model emergence at multiple timescales; identify leading indicators on faster timescale; schedule re-scans aligned with emergence tempo. |
| 3 | Reductionist Model Assumption | Linear or component-based models are assumed sufficient; models assume additive effects and state-independence; failure to model feedback, thresholds, or non-linearity. | Test linearity assumption with data; identify feedback mechanisms; look for threshold behaviour; examine state-dependence; build emergence scenarios; stress-test models against extreme conditions. |
| 4 | Category Absence & Ontological Blindness | Emergent phenomenon lacks existing analytical category or name; phenomenon is forced into ill-fitting existing categories; disagreement on categorisation signals ontological novelty. | Recognise and name novel phenomena; suspend existing categories; examine multiple frameworks; develop hybrid or novel categorisations; track "unclassifiable" frequency. |
| 5 | Intervention Paradox & Unanticipated Feedback | Interventions designed to control or prevent emergence trigger different emergence; intervention feedback amplifies rather than dampens; control measures create emergent side effects. | Model intervention-system feedback before acting; design for robustness not optimisation; pilot with monitoring; expect and plan for emergence as response to control; build adaptive intervention logic. |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)
| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| I  Perceptual | Non-linearity and emergent behaviour are counterintuitive; observers expect proportional response and linear growth; tipping points are invisible until after transition; cascade onset appears sudden because ramp-up was slow and unobserved. | "We were shocked when X happened"; "it seemed stable then suddenly failed"; "the problem appeared out of nowhere"; "small changes had big effects"; surprise at outcomes that data could have predicted. | Collect historical data on behaviour; look for acceleration, variance increase, or regime change; ask whether early-warning signals were observable; examine whether observers were watching the right metrics; test whether perception lags reality. |
| II Systemic | Emergence arises from system structure that is not visible or not mapped; feedback loops and interactions are not explicit; boundaries are assumed rather than tested; multi-scale effects are not modelled. | "The system organised itself in unexpected ways"; "no single person could see the whole picture"; "structure prevented warning signals from reaching decision-makers"; "components worked fine individually but failed together." | Map system structure including feedback loops and delays; identify what information flows to what roles; trace cascade pathways; examine whether any role has full-system visibility; test whether hidden dependencies explain outcomes. |
| III Informational | Evidence is aggregated or filtered at points that hide emergence; signals are attenuated in communication chains; local knowledge that would contradict central narrative is not surfaced; information cascade suppresses countervailing data. | "The data was there but no one saw it"; "individual sites reported warning signs but centre didn't know"; "information was reported but not acted on"; "early warnings were there in the data"; dissent or disagreement on whether emergence occurred. | Examine raw data vs reported summaries; interview observers at different levels; track information routing; look for signals that should have reached decision-makers but didn't; check whether dissent was recorded. |
| IV Temporal | Emergence unfolds on timescale that does not match investigation or governance cycle; slow emergence is invisible within short timeframes; fast cascade is invisible because decision-making is slower; re-scan windows miss inflection points; institutional memory fades before learning occurs. | "By the time anyone noticed, it was too late"; "we were looking at quarterly data but it was a monthly phenomenon"; "we learned too late to avoid the next occurrence"; "the pattern only visible over decades"; governance cycles miss emergence onset. | Identify emergence timescale; compare with investigation and governance cycle; look for accelerating change pre-tipping; examine whether monitoring frequency matches emergence speed; check whether past learning was retained; schedule re-scans aligned with emergence tempo. |
| V  Relational | Emergence involves distributed agents or components whose interactions are not visible to any single role or authority; positional blindness means some roles see emergence and others don't; network structure determines who perceives what; dissent and disagreement signal emergence recognition gaps. | "Some groups saw it coming, others didn't"; "the groups most affected couldn't convince decision-makers"; "only people embedded in the network understood"; "central authority was blind to what edge actors could see"; hidden relational patterns structure emergence. | Map relationships and network structure; interview multiple roles; examine whether dissent is recorded and taken seriously; look for misalignment between role-based perception and system-level reality; trace how emergence is visible/invisible across relationship structure. |
| VI Ontological | Emerging phenomenon is novel and lacks existing categories; disagreement on terminology signals ontological gap; inability to name phenomenon hinders governance; existing frameworks do not accommodate hybrid or unprecedented forms. | "We don't have a word for this"; "it's not quite X or Y"; "everyone describes it differently"; "the category doesn't fit"; "past playbooks don't apply"; governance struggles because phenomenon is unnamed; rules assume stable categories. | Recognise and name novel phenomenon; examine whether existing categories fit; check whether different observers use different labels; look for frameworks that might accommodate novelty; design new categories if needed; track whether naming change enables action. |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (early pass; assume Hiddens exist in emergence analysis) (OG §7.1).
2) Shortlist candidate emergence-related Hiddens; for each, rate: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence (OG §7.2).
3) Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (OG §7.2).
4) Run the emergence-specific hiddens source analysis and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens.
5) If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific emergence-hiding mechanism (temporal mismatch, category absence, intervention paradox), invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3).
6) After executing the emergence protocol and any available tests/probes (scenario stress-testing, model validation, network analysis), re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas.
7) Produce Residual Unknowns (with mechanism, impact, and next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, intervention design, or governance priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)
Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- High consequence (potential for major cascade, regime shift, or system-level harm)
- Emergent phenomena are novel or poorly understood (ontological novelty suspected)
- Evidence on emergence is contested or weak
- Multiple emergence types interact (bifurcation cascades, emergence-triggered emergence)
- Prior interventions have failed or backfired due to emergence
- Temporal mismatch means emergence is unfolding outside governance horizon or visibility window
- Irreversibility or path-dependence is suspected (lock-in analysis required)

Tier 3 execution (per OG §D.6.10):
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into full emergence taxonomy × Hiddens interaction analysis.
- Tightens controls: stricter evidence standards for emergence claims; explicit alternative hypotheses; disconfirming tests designed for each alternative.
- When strategic concealment or adversarial context: adds investigation of whether emergence is being deliberately obscured or leveraged.
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree (OG §4.3) because emergence implications cut across framing, mechanism, agency, meaning, epistemics, and action.

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Assume Non-Linearity | In complex systems, non-linear behaviour is the default. Assume thresholds, feedback amplification, and state-dependence unless evidence clearly shows linearity. Treat emergence as a working assumption, not a special case. |
| 2. Map Feedback and Coupling | Emergent phenomena arise from interactions. Explicitly map feedback loops, delays, and interdependencies. Hidden coupling is a primary source of emergence hiding. Do not treat components as independent without evidence. |
| 3. Honour Temporal Scope | Emergence operates on multiple timescales. Investigate at multiple timescales; do not assume observation window captures emergence. Extend historical data; use analogues; identify acceleration and slowdown. Align governance cycles with emergence tempo. |
| 4. Prioritise Early Detection | Detection interface design is critical. Develop leading indicators and early-warning systems. Recognise that emergence is easiest to influence at onset; cascade grows faster than capacity to respond. Invest in sensing and monitoring infrastructure. |
| 5. Expect Intervention Paradox | Interventions designed to prevent emergence may trigger different emergence. Anticipate feedback and design for robustness. Pilot with monitoring. Build adaptive management approach: expect surprises, monitor response, adjust. Preserve options and slack. |
| 6. Embrace Ontological Humility | Novel emergent phenomena may not fit existing categories. Preserve ambiguity and multiple hypotheses. Suspend premature categorisation. Resist forcing emergence into ill-fitting frames. Recognise and name genuinely new phenomena. Update ontology as learning accumulates. |

## 11.1 Local domain glossary
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Bistability** | Condition where a system has two stable equilibria; perturbation can flip system between states; recovery to prior state is slow or impossible. |
| **Criticality** | State of system near a tipping point or phase transition; small perturbation produces large effects; system is sensitive to initial conditions. |
| **Heterogeneity** | Diversity of components, agents, or properties; lack of homogeneity; heterogeneous systems can exhibit emergent coordination; homogenisation reduces emergence. |
| **Hysteresis loop** | Non-reversible response where return path differs from forward path; system retains "memory" of trajectory; recovery requires larger driving force than caused transition. |
| **Infective closure** | Moment at which tipping-point transition is inevitable; even if perturbation is removed, system continues to new state. Differ from threshold (reversal still possible). |
| **Lag** | Delay between cause and effect; time delay in feedback loops; short lag allows responsive control; long lag creates overshoot and oscillation. |
| **Lattice effects** | Emergence that depends on spatial structure; local interactions on network or spatial grid produce large-scale patterns. |
| **Network motifs** | Recurring local patterns in networks; motifs determine cascade dynamics and emergence behaviour. |
| **Order parameter** | A collective variable that characterises the state of a system; useful for tracking phase transitions and emergence. |
| **Phase transition** | Qualitative change in system behaviour at critical point; transition is discontinuous in order parameter; small change in control parameter produces large change in state. |
| **Quasistability** | Apparent stability that is brittle; system is stable only under narrow conditions; outside conditions, rapid transition to new state. |
| **Stochastic resonance** | Phenomenon where noise or randomness enhances detection of weak signals or facilitates transition; counter-intuitive benefit of noise/variability. |
| **Tipping point** | See Phase transition; commonly used term for critical threshold where system behaviour qualitatively shifts. |

### B. Worked Example (micro-case): Emergence in Market Dynamics

**Scenario**: A supply-demand market for critical commodity exhibits sudden price spike and shortage despite no change in underlying supply or demand fundamentals. Reductionist analysis of buyers and sellers individually does not explain the spike.

**Emergence Type**: Type 12 (Information Cascade & Herding) coupled with Type 13 (Amplification through Feedback Coupling).

**Mechanism**: Early-stage traders observe small price movement → interpret as signal of future price rise → buy → purchase pressure raises price → other traders observe rising price → interpret as vindication of expectation → buy in herd → feedback coupling amplifies initial movement → cascade occurs despite no change in fundamentals.

**Dimensions**:
- Predictability: Low early (small movement is not predictive), High late (cascade becomes obvious).
- Reversibility: Medium (market can reverse but at cost; path-dependent).
- Temporal Scope: Fast (hours to days; cascade is visible in intra-day data).
- Spatial Extent: System-wide (affects all market participants through price signal).
- Consequence: High (shortage, price volatility, economic disruption).

**Dynamic Pattern**: Pattern 2 (Herding & Information Cascade) and Pattern 13 (Cascading Failure & Systemic Breakdown in supply-demand coupling).

**Hiddens**: 
- Reductionist invisibility: individual trader rationality does not predict cascade.
- Temporal mismatch: cascade unfolds faster than information processing by slower traders.
- Information cascade hiding: traders interpret herding action as information rather than recognising cascade.

**Interventions**: Circuit breakers (pause cascade); information intervention (release countervailing signal); structural change (increase transparency, diversify sources, reduce information coupling).

**Monitoring**: Track order flow and price divergence from fundamentals; flag information cascade signatures; monitor herding metrics.
