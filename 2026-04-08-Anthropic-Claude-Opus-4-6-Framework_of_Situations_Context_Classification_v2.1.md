# Introduction

## What is Situations and Context Classification?

A "situation" is not merely a collection of facts or events—it is a structured context in which agents perceive, decide, and act. The same underlying reality can constitute different situations for different agents, at different scales, and across different timeframes. This framework addresses a fundamental analytical problem: without clarity on **what kind of situation we are in**, we tend to apply inappropriate tools, governance, and interventions. An organisation responding to a crisis with routine procedures fails catastrophically. A team attempting exploratory discovery using project-delivery discipline wastes resources and locks in premature closure. A governance structure designed for stable operations breaks under transformational change. Situation classification is therefore not a luxury; it is a foundational discipline that determines whether subsequent analysis and action succeed.

This framework provides a standardised taxonomy of thirty recurring situation types organised into six meta-categories (Routine, Project/Design, Exploratory, Crisis/Disruption, Conflict/Plurality, Transformational). It operationalises situation typing through five cross-cutting dimensions (structural order, valence, uncertainty, agency, temporality) that distinguish superficially similar contexts. It makes visible the three principal interfaces where situations interact (transitions between types, overlaps of nested situations, and framing conflicts). And it surfaces the characteristic sources of misclassification and visibility failure that plague situational analysis in complex contexts.

## Why does Situations and Context Classification matter for Hiddens work?

Misclassification is itself a Hidden—a reality-relevant factor that remains invisible to decision-makers until late consequences reveal the error. This framework surfaces several families of hiding mechanisms:

**Framing capture** (Hidden-8) operates when situation labels foreground certain levers and hide others. One party frames a context as "routine management"; another sees "existential crisis." Each frame enables different actions and suppresses different concerns. Declaring a change programme "strategic transformation" rather than "operational efficiency project" alters what gets spent, who gets consulted, and what timeline is credible.

**Contextual blindness** (Hidden-24) arises because situation types vary by context—by scale, by agent role, by temporal horizon. A situation that is "crisis" at the frontline may appear "routine" to distant leadership. The same event is "opportunity" for a competitor and "threat" for an incumbent. Assuming a single situation type across layers masks these asymmetries.

**Temporal drift** (part of Temporal Hiddens, Hidden-15 Latency, Hidden-7 Mirage) allows situations to evolve without reclassification. A manageable risk becomes chronic overload. A promising exploration ossifies into stagnation. A stable regime tips into crisis. Yet outdated playbooks persist, governance mechanisms become misaligned, and decisions are made on the basis of a situation that no longer exists.

Without this framework, these Hiddens remain latent: invisible until they generate failure, regret, or cascade effects. With the framework, situation typing becomes a repeatable, testable discipline that flags misclassification risks early and enables deliberate framing management.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises situation classification through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 situation types** organised into six meta-categories that recur across contexts, allowing rapid initial classification and comparison.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (structural order, valence, uncertainty, agency, temporality) that profile any situation and explain why different agents classify the same reality differently.

3. **Dynamics patterns** (escalation, crystallisation, pluralisation, drift, transformation) that describe how situations evolve, enabling detection of transitions and early warning of regime change.

4. **Three interface types** (transition, overlap, framing) that make visible where and how situations interact, clash, or nest—zones of high risk and opportunity.

5. **Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** that surface the four systematic sources of situational misreading (misclassification, valence myopia, scope error, temporal drift) and the six categories of visibility failure (perceptual, systemic, informational, temporal, relational, ontological).

6. **A six-step application protocol** that moves from situation naming through typing, dimensional profiling, interface mapping, Hiddens checking, and integration handoff to other frameworks.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow. It is designed for repeated use: situations are classified, decisions are made, and the situation is re-typed at scheduled intervals or triggered by threshold breaches, allowing the analysis to track evolution in real time.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework early and repeatedly. It is most valuable at scenario intake (to establish what kind of problem we face), during major decision gates (to validate whether our situation model still holds), and when interventions fail to produce expected effects (often a signal of misclassification). The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch classification and interface spotting) and Investigative Run Mode (deep dimensional profiling, full Hiddens scanning, multi-stakeholder narrative synthesis).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: assign situation types, profile one or two dimensions, spot obvious interfaces, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, evidence is contested, framing conflicts are active, or you are approaching a transition threshold. The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all situation type claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording disagreements, uncertainties, and residual unknowns rather than collapsing them into false consensus.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario and the Operating Guide, producing a disciplined situation classification with explicit Hiddens, F/I/A/U tagging, and bounded closure.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

A "situation" is not merely a set of facts; it is a structured context in which agents perceive, decide, and act. The same underlying reality can constitute different situations for different agents, at different scales, and at different times. Because the type of situation determines which forms of analysis, governance, and intervention are appropriate, **situation classification is a foundational step**.

This framework provides a standardised approach to:
- elicit stakeholder narratives and define the focal situation boundary,
- classify the situation using a stable taxonomy (6 meta-categories × 5 types = 30),
- profile the situation along five cross-cutting dimensions,
- map interfaces where situations transition, overlap, or are contested by framing,
- scan for characteristic hiddens (and run a mandatory tiered Hiddens cross-check), and
- hand off an actionable situational profile to downstream frameworks and decision processes.

### 1.6.3 Routing triggers
- Multiple stakeholders assign fundamentally different situation types to the same facts (framing conflict signal)
- Classification has shifted without explicit re-typing; playbooks no longer fit observed dynamics (drift signal)
- Intervention is failing despite alignment on facts; question whether situation type is incorrect (misclassification signal)
- Transition thresholds are approaching or have been breached; regime change may be imminent (transition signal)
- High-stakes decisions will be constrained by situation type assumptions; weak evidence base exists (consequence + evidence weakness signal)
- Scope boundary is contested; agents operate at different scales or temporal horizons with incompatible classifications (scope error signal)
- Valence is asymmetric across agents; one party's crisis is another's opportunity; suppressed dissent on situation framing (valence myopia signal)
- Periodic governance rhythm or re-typing trigger point has arrived; situational drift has accumulated (temporal signal)
- Post-incident analysis reveals earlier misclassification drove cascade effects or wrong response mode (learning/accountability signal)
- Organisational capability to operate under the current situation type is in doubt; governance mismatch with situation is evident (governance signal)

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)
### I. Routine Situations
Stable, recurring contexts with known goals, known methods, and largely predictable outcomes. The dominant task is maintaining performance, reliability, and incremental improvement. Deviations are treated as anomalies to be corrected rather than signals of structural change.

### II. Project/Design Situations
Situations in which goals are reasonably clear but the path to achieving them requires design, planning, and coordinated execution. Cause–effect relationships are knowable with appropriate expertise, but effort is needed to specify, engineer, and integrate solutions.

### III. Exploratory/Discovery Situations
Situations in which goals, constraints, or problem definitions are unclear, contested, or evolving. The dominant task is sensemaking, exploration, hypothesis generation, and learning rather than execution against a fixed plan.

### IV. Crisis/Disruption Situations
Situations characterised by volatility, time pressure, high stakes, and partial loss of control. Existing structures no longer behave as expected; harm and/or opportunity is concentrated in short windows where action or inaction has disproportionate consequences.

### V. Conflict/Plurality Situations
Situations in which multiple agents, interests, values, or realities interact in ways that are mutually constraining or antagonistic. The dominant challenge is not primarily technical but social, political, or normative: managing disagreement, aligning or balancing interests, and structuring cooperation or competition.

### VI. Transformational/Liminal Situations
Situations in which identities, boundaries, narratives, and structures themselves are in motion. The basic "rules of the game" are being questioned or rewritten. These are threshold conditions in which systems can tip into qualitatively new regimes.

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Situation

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)
| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Structural Order | How ordered or chaotic are the causal relationships and behavioural patterns in this situation? | **Simple-Ordered** – Clear, stable cause–effect relationships; one or few obvious "right" approaches. Patterns repeat reliably; best practice is known and broadly agreed. **Complicated-Ordered** – Cause–effect relationships are knowable but require analysis or expertise. Multiple viable approaches exist; solutions can be engineered and optimised. **Complex** – Cause–effect relationships are only visible in retrospect. Patterns emerge from many interactions; interventions produce non-linear and sometimes surprising effects. **Chaotic** – No perceivable order in the relevant timeframe. Events are highly volatile; actions aim to create temporary order or escape from chaos rather than optimise within it. **Meta-Reflexive** – The situation is explicitly about choosing, constructing, or changing the structural regime itself (e.g., re-architecting systems, altering coupling, changing rules of interaction). | Classify whether cause–effect is stable, knowable, or emergent; look for repeatability vs novelty; identify feedback loops. | Select analysis method and governance tempo; avoid using routine playbooks in complex/chaotic contexts. |
| Valence Profile | What is the balance of perceived threat and opportunity, and for whom? | **Predominantly Harmful** – Threats, losses, or harms dominate; potential upside is negligible or irrelevant for most key agents. **Threat-Weighted Mixed** – Both upside and downside are present, but losses or risks are more salient or likely given current trajectories. **Ambivalent/Unclear** – Valence is contested, distributed asymmetrically, or genuinely unclear. Different agents experience the same situation as opportunity or threat. **Opportunity-Weighted Mixed** – Both upside and downside are present, but potential gains are more salient or likely given competent handling. **Predominantly Beneficial** – The situation is largely favourable; downside is limited or manageable relative to expected gains. | Elicit threat and opportunity perceptions per agent; check asymmetry; distinguish objective risk from subjective valence. | Prevents one-sided framing; supports coalition design, communication, and risk/opportunity trade-offs. |
| Uncertainty Profile | What kind of not-knowing predominates in this situation? | **Low** – Key variables, ranges, and relationships are well understood. Residual uncertainty is small and largely reducible with available tools. **Moderate-Estimable** – Uncertainty is material but can be approximated with data, models, or scenarios. Probabilities or ranges can be meaningfully estimated. **High-Model** – We have models, but parameters, structures, or boundary conditions are contested or highly unstable. Different models give materially different answers. **Deep/Knightian** – We cannot reliably assign probabilities to key outcomes or even fully enumerate relevant possibilities. Unknown unknowns are expected. **Unknown-Unknown-Rich** – There is strong reason to suspect that important factors are currently outside our conceptualisation. Exploration and learning dominate over prediction. | Separate known-knowns, known-unknowns, unknown-unknowns; test evidence quality and disagreement structure. | Determines whether to execute, explore, or redesign; guides evidence collection and decision rules. |
| Agency & Power Configuration | How is effective agency and power distributed in this situation? | **Centralised** – One agent (person, institution, or tightly aligned coalition) holds decisive power and can meaningfully steer outcomes. **Hierarchical** – Power is structured in a clear chain-of-command; decisions cascade from higher to lower levels with some discretion at each level. **Polycentric** – Several significant centres of agency exist, each with partial power and interdependence. No single actor can unilaterally determine outcomes. **Fragmented** – Agency is dispersed across many weak or loosely connected actors. Coordination problems dominate; no one is clearly in charge. **Externalised** – Key drivers of the situation lie outside the control of directly involved agents (e.g., global markets, natural forces, distant regulators). | Map who can act, who can veto, and who can enforce; include informal power; assess alignment and coercion capacity. | Explains why 'obvious' actions fail; guides governance, responsibility, and intervention feasibility. |
| Temporal Character | How does the situation unfold in time? | **Flash** – Very brief events or moments (seconds to days) where critical decisions or outcomes are locked in quickly. **Acute Episode** – Intense but time-bounded episodes (days to months) with clear before/after phases. **Chronic Condition** – Long-running background situations that persist for extended periods (months to years) and define the context for other situations. **Cyclical Pattern** – Recurrent situations that return with some regularity (seasons, business cycles, electoral cycles), allowing anticipation and pattern-based response. **Transformational Trajectory** – One-way, path-dependent developments where the situation is moving toward a qualitatively different regime with limited reversibility. | Identify horizon, pace, windows, and irreversibility; distinguish acute, episodic, chronic, cyclical, and transformational trajectories. | Prevents tempo mismatch; informs sequencing, buffering, and monitoring cadence; highlights option erosion and drift. |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Situation Misclassification | Handling complex or transformational situations with routine tools; treating crises as routine issues until too late; forcing conflict/plurality situations into purely technical frames. | **Deliberate situation-typing** as an explicit step; testing alternative classifications; asking "If we are wrong about what kind of situation this is, how would we know?" |
| 2 | Valence Myopia | Risk-only framing that paralyses initiative; opportunity-only framing that ignores downside risk; assuming valence is homogeneous across agents. | **Explicit valence profiling** across agents; asking "Who experiences this as threat? Who as opportunity? What are we not seeing?" |
| 3 | Scope & Nesting Error | Treating local manifestations as isolated when they are nodes in a larger situation; over-expanding scope such that action becomes impossible; ignoring nested or adjacent situations. | **Systematic scan for nested and neighbouring situations**; mapping how the focal situation sits within wider contexts and contains sub-situations. |
| 4 | Temporal Myopia & Drift | Using short-term crisis tactics for long-term chronic conditions; treating transformational trajectories as if they were reversible; failing to update classification as the situation changes. | **Explicit temporal characterisation** and periodic re-typing; asking "Has the situation type changed since we last looked? What time horizons are actually relevant?" |
| 5 | Hiddens omission | Skipping systematic Hiddens checks leads to persistent misclassification, framing capture, and invisible constraints. | Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan for every non-trivial case; shortlist Tier 2 hidden types and assign detection/remediation moves. |

|---|---|---|---|

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) Run Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (assume hiddens exist) (OG v2.5 §7.1).
2) Shortlist candidate Hiddens; for each, rate: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence (OG v2.5 §7.2).
3) Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (OG v2.5 §7.2).
4) Run the hiddens source analysis and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens.
5) If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan (OG v2.5 §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3).
6) After executing the framework protocol and any available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1 (pre-closure pass) and note deltas.
7) Produce Residual Unknowns (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or remediation priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG v2.5 §7.4.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)
Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- High consequence (safety, regulatory, existential, severe harm potential)
- Adversarial context / strategic concealment likely
- Persistent disagreement or inconsistent evidence
- Repeat failures despite prior interventions
- Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement across hidden types
- Decision-critical residual unknowns

Tier 3 execution (per OG v2.5 §D.6.10):
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into the full Hiddens taxonomy (full-spectrum exploration).
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements, more explicit competing hypotheses, more rigorous disconfirming tests.
- When adversarial intent is plausible: adds concealment vectors and independent corroboration paths.
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree branch A6 (OG v2.5 §4.3).

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Situational Pluralism | Multiple valid situation-types can coexist for the same underlying reality at different scales, for different agents, or across time. The goal is not to find a single "correct" label but to understand how different classifications illuminate different action possibilities. |
| 2. Valence Duality | Every situation contains both threat and opportunity, though asymmetrically distributed across agents and time. Treating a situation as purely harmful or purely beneficial is almost always a distortion. |
| 3. Nestedness of Situations | Situations are nested within one another across levels (local, organisational, sectoral, societal) and across time (flash events within acute episodes within chronic conditions within transformational trajectories). Effective analysis attends to these nested structures. |
| 4. Dynamism of Situations | Situations evolve. Classification is a moving picture, not a static label. What begins as exploratory can become project/design; what begins as crisis can become transformational; what begins as routine can become chronic risk if left unattended. |
| 5. Reflexive Construction | How agents *name* and *frame* a situation changes what it becomes. Declaring a crisis, announcing an opportunity, or redefining a context as transformational alters incentives, expectations, and behaviour. Situational analysis is therefore both descriptive and constitutive, and should be conducted with reflexive awareness. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms; recommended; mandatory for "Stable" status)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Situation | A structured context in which agents perceive, decide, and act; a configuration of realities, relations, agents, values, uncertainties, boundaries, perspectives, and processes at a particular time and scale. |
| Situational Context | The specific combination of environmental conditions, histories, constraints, and active processes that make a situation distinct from other situations. |
| Meta-Category of Situation | One of the six highest-level classes of situation (Routine, Project/Design, Exploratory/Discovery, Crisis/Disruption, Conflict/Plurality, Transformational/Liminal) defined by its structural pattern of goals, information, causality, time, and agency. |
| Situation Type | One of the thirty more granular patterns within the meta-categories (e.g., Operational Steady State, Strategic Inflection Point) used to describe recurring forms of situational structure. |
| Structural Classification | Classification of a situation based on its underlying structure (e.g., stability vs disruption, clarity vs ambiguity, single vs multiple agents) rather than on whether it is "good" or "bad" for any particular actor. |
| Valence | The perceived beneficial or harmful character of a situation for a given agent or group (threat vs opportunity, loss vs gain, problem vs opening). |
| Valence Classification | Assessment of a situation in terms of its valence profile (how beneficial or harmful it is) for specific agents, overlaid on top of structural classification. |
| Valence Profile | The pattern of perceived threats and opportunities across agents and over time within a situation (e.g., threat-weighted for incumbents, opportunity-weighted for challengers). |
| Problem Situation | A situation that is experienced primarily as harmful, risky, or loss-making for one or more focal agents, given their values and objectives. |
| Opportunity Situation | A situation that is experienced primarily as beneficial, promising, or gain-making for one or more focal agents, given their values and objectives. |
| Structural Order | The degree to which causal relationships and behavioural patterns are stable, knowable, or emergent (simple-ordered, complicated-ordered, complex, chaotic, meta-reflexive). |
| Uncertainty Profile | The dominant form and level of not-knowing in a situation (from low and estimable to deep, Knightian, or unknown-unknown rich). |
| Agency & Power Configuration | The way effective agency and power are distributed in a situation (centralised, hierarchical, polycentric, fragmented, externalised). |
| Temporal Character | The way a situation unfolds in time (flash event, acute episode, chronic condition, cyclical pattern, transformational trajectory). |
| Interface (Situational) | A boundary or zone of interaction between situations or between different framings of the same underlying reality (transition, overlap, and framing interfaces). |
| Transition Interface | A point or phase where one situation type transforms into another (e.g., routine into crisis, crisis into transformational). |
| Overlap Interface | A zone where multiple situation types coexist at different scales or for different agents (e.g., an acute incident nested inside a chronic condition and a strategic inflection point). |
| Framing Interface | A context in which different agents classify the same underlying reality as different situation types (e.g., routine issue vs existential crisis), often linked to power and interest. |
| Hiddens Source | One of the four systematic sources of situational misreading in this framework: situation misclassification, valence myopia, scope & nesting error, temporal myopia & drift. |
| Situation Typing | The deliberate act of assigning one or more structural situation types to a focal context, using the six meta-categories and thirty core types. |
| Dimensional Profiling | Characterising a situation along the five dimensions (structural order, valence profile, uncertainty profile, agency & power configuration, temporal character) to distinguish superficially similar contexts. |
| Nested Situation | A situation that exists within another situation at a different scale or temporal level (e.g., a flash event within an acute episode within a chronic condition). |
| Liminal | Referring to threshold or in-between conditions where identities, rules, or structures are in flux and systems may tip into new regimes. |
| Strategic Inflection Point | A transformational/liminal situation type in which the viability or payoff structure of existing strategies or business models is changing sign; continuing as before is no longer neutral. |
| Meta-Situation | A higher-order situation in which the situation itself becomes the object of design or redesign (e.g., situation redesign, changing stakeholders, goals, or constraints). |
| Application Protocol | The six-step procedure for using this framework (situation naming & narrative, situation typing, dimensional profiling, interface mapping, hiddens source analysis, integration & action design). |
| Situational Pluralism | Recognition that multiple valid situation-types can coexist for the same underlying reality at different scales, for different agents, or across time. |
| Reflexive Construction | The idea that how agents name and frame a situation partly creates what it becomes, by changing expectations, incentives, and behaviours. |
