# Introduction

## What is Realities and Multi-Layer Mapping?

Reality is plural and irreducible. The same phenomenon can show up in multiple ways: as physical constraints, as lived experience, as representations in dashboards and models, as institutional rules and roles, as competing values, as feasibility boundaries, and as historical path dependencies. Most analyses operate on a single layer—often metrics or narratives or institutional directives—and miss the others. When the layers diverge (substrate is changing but representations are stale; institutions enforce rules disconnected from experience), diagnosis fails and interventions target the wrong layer. This framework provides a disciplined method to map all relevant reality-types, characterise their relationships, and identify where misalignment causes harm.

The seven meta-categories (substrate, experience, representation, institution, normativity, modality, process) are not metaphysical positions but practical analytical distinctions. Treating a dashboard as the reality, ignoring the substrate constraints it depends on, leads to false confidence. Prescribing policies detached from lived experience breeds resistance. Assuming feasibility when modal constraints forbid it causes failures and regrets. This framework surfaces these category errors early, makes implicit assumptions explicit, and enables coordinated action across reality-layers.

## Why does Realities and Multi-Layer Mapping matter for Hiddens work?

Realities mapping surfaces multiple classes of hiding mechanisms. **Hidden-11 (Theory/Model)** operates when implicit reality assumptions in a model are not exposed: the analyst believes the metrics represent the system, unaware of translation loss. **Hidden-6 (Distortion)** occurs when a single representation (e.g., a KPI) distorts by emphasising one layer while suppressing others. **Hidden-19 (Reification)** manifests when one layer is treated as the whole reality: policy becomes law becomes nature. **Hidden-4 (Inference)** hides when layer relationships are assumed rather than tested. **Hidden-24 (Contextual)** emerges because reality salience varies by context—what is "real" in headquarters may be invisible on the frontline.

Without this framework, these Hiddens remain latent, generating confident-but-wrong decisions. With it, category errors become visible, interface risks are flagged, and layer-specific monitoring and governance can be designed.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises multi-layer reality mapping through seven core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of seven meta-categories** (substrate, experience, representation, institution, normativity, modality, process) that recur across contexts and domains.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (clarity, coherence, scope, authority, contestation) that profile any mapped reality and explain variation within and across layers.

3. **Dynamics patterns** (layer divergence, emergence, translation loss, reification, pluralisation) that describe how realities interact and evolve.

4. **Three interface types** (translation, emergence, boundary) that make visible where realities meet, clash, or depend on each other—zones of high risk and opportunity.

5. **Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** that surface four systematic sources of layer misreading (category error, reification, translation loss, perspectival capture) and the six categories of visibility failure (perceptual, systemic, informational, temporal, relational, ontological).

6. **A six-step application protocol** that moves from boundary definition through reality mapping, dimensional profiling, interface mapping, Hiddens checking, and integration handoff.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow. It is designed for repeated use: realities are mapped, decisions are made, and the map is revisited when interventions fail or contexts change, allowing the analysis to track layer divergence and emergence in real time.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework when stakeholders disagree on "what is real," when metrics seem disconnected from lived experience, when policies fail despite good intentions, or when feasibility appears uncertain. The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch reality inventory and obvious interface spotting) and Investigative Run Mode (deep dimensional profiling, full Hiddens scanning, multi-stakeholder narrative synthesis).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: map seven realities at the level of 1–3 key elements per layer, profile one or two dimensions per reality, spot obvious interfaces, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when intervention targets are unclear, stakeholder narratives diverge sharply on which layer is "real," or you suspect material layer divergence (e.g., substrate changing while institutional rules do not). The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all reality claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording disagreements and residual unknowns rather than collapsing them into false consensus.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario and the Operating Guide, producing a disciplined reality map with explicit Hiddens, F/I/A/U tagging, and bounded closure.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Reality is plural: situations are composed of multiple, distinct reality-types that are not reducible to each other. Material constraints, lived experience, representations, institutions, values, possibilities, and processes each have their own causal significance. Misunderstanding arises when one layer is treated as the whole (for example, treating a dashboard as the system, or treating a policy as the reality it attempts to govern).

This framework provides a disciplined approach to mapping and integrating reality-types and their interfaces so that analysis and action target the correct objects, at the correct layer, with explicit recognition of translation loss, emergence thresholds, and boundary disputes. It operationalises this through a repeatable six-step protocol: boundary definition, reality mapping across seven meta-categories, dimensional characterisation, interface identification, Hiddens source analysis with tiered scanning, and integration handoff to downstream frameworks.

The output is a multi-reality map that prevents category errors, exposes layer divergence, identifies where interventions must focus, and enables coordinated action. It transforms "we all disagree on what's real" into "here is how these realities interact, where they diverge, and what that means for action."

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Substrate Realities
Material and physical facts: resources, assets, constraints, and causal powers that exist independent of our beliefs about them. Includes infrastructure, natural systems, technical limits, thermodynamic constraints, and time delays inherent in physical processes. These realities are typically slow-changing and difficult to override.

### II. Experiential Realities
The lived world as experienced by agents: what it feels like to be in a situation, what agents perceive as salient, what they fear or desire, what they find legible or opaque. Includes embodied knowledge, emotional responses, trust and distrust, morale, and felt workload. These realities are subjective but causally real—they shape effort, cooperation, and resistance.

### III. Representational Realities
How situations are modeled, narrated, and made intelligible to agents. Includes dashboards and KPIs, strategic narratives, conceptual maps, metaphors, categories, and the language used to talk about a domain. These realities mediate understanding but are always partial—every representation highlights some aspects while suppressing others.

### IV. Social-Institutional Realities
Rules, roles, and structures that exist through collective recognition and enforcement: org charts, contracts, laws, money, formal procedures, hierarchies, and cultural norms that carry social force. These realities constrain what is permissible and what can be compelled. They are causally powerful but also malleable—they change when enough actors decide to treat them differently.

### V. Normative Realities
The landscape of values, ethics, and obligations: what is deemed right or wrong, fair or unjust, necessary or permissible. Includes sacred values, legitimacy criteria, principles of justice, and risk appetite. These realities shape what actors are willing to do and what they believe should be done, even when material incentives point elsewhere.

### VI. Modal Realities
The space of possible futures and the constraints that limit it: hard physical and institutional constraints that make certain futures infeasible; soft options that are technically possible but not yet chosen; reversibilities and irreversibilities. These realities answer "what else could we do?" and "what would happen if?"

### VII. Process Realities
The historical and causal trajectory that brought the situation into being and continues to unfold. Includes path dependencies, sedimented decisions, learning curves, feedback loops, and institutional inertia. These realities explain how the current situation emerged and constrain what is feasible in the near term.

# 3. Five Cross-Cutting Dimensions

## 3.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)
| Dimension | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Clarity | How unambiguous, well-defined, and agreed-upon is this reality? | **Transparent** – All relevant agents perceive and understand this reality in roughly the same way; little dispute about its existence or properties. **Legible but contested** – The reality is visible to most agents, but interpretations differ; some disagreement about meaning or implications. **Opaque** – This reality is invisible or barely visible to some or all agents; it is hidden, tacit, or not yet conceptualised. **Unknowable** – This reality cannot be directly observed or inferred with available tools; it must be posited theoretically. | Test whether agents have common language for the reality; ask if disagreement is factual or interpretive; check evidence availability. | Clarity gaps signal education/communication opportunities; opacity signals Hiddens work needed. |
| Coherence | How internally consistent and mutually reinforcing are the elements within this reality? | **Highly coherent** – Elements align, reinforce each other, and tell a consistent story; no obvious contradictions. **Mostly coherent** – Some internal tensions, but overall consistent narrative. **Fragmented** – Multiple incompatible versions or narratives coexist; internal contradictions are salient. **Incoherent** – The reality lacks any unifying logic; elements appear random or chaotic. | Identify inconsistencies within single-layer claims; look for narratives that support vs contradict each other. | Low coherence suggests instability or misunderstanding; high coherence may mask hidden assumptions. |
| Scope | What is the extent or applicability of this reality? | **Universal** – This reality applies across all contexts, actors, and timescales without exception. **Domain-bound** – This reality applies consistently within a particular domain or context (e.g., "in manufacturing plants" or "for this population"). **Contextual** – This reality is highly sensitive to local context, scale, or agent role; applicability varies significantly. **Singular** – This reality is unique to one specific case or instance; it does not generalise. | Test whether the reality claim holds in different contexts; ask which populations or timeframes it covers. | Scope mismatch (assuming universal when contextual) is a major source of failure. |
| Authority | Who can legitimately claim, define, or challenge this reality? | **Delegated** – Authority is formally distributed (e.g., regulatory bodies, expertise gates, institutional roles). **Distributed** – Authority is shared across multiple actors with recognised legitimacy. **Centralised** – Authority rests with one agent (person, institution, or coalition) who can set definitions and constraints. **Contested** – No single authority is accepted; claims to define the reality are disputed. **Absent** – No recognised authority exists for this reality; it is ungoverned or self-governing. | Map who is recognised as having the right to speak for this reality; test whether claims are accepted or resisted. | Authority disputes signal governance risks; absent authority signals either emergence or legitimacy failure. |
| Contestation | How much disagreement is there about this reality, and is it acknowledged or suppressed? | **Settled** – This reality is accepted across stakeholders; disagreement is minimal or absent. **Acknowledged dispute** – Disagreement is visible, discussed, and managed through explicit processes. **Hidden/suppressed dispute** – Stakeholders disagree but do not acknowledge it openly; conflict is latent. **Fundamental clash** – The disagreement goes to the core; no shared ground; different realities are claimed. **Unresolvable** – The disagreement cannot be resolved within available tools; escalation or governance redesign is needed. | Elicit independent stakeholder accounts; test whether all parties acknowledge alternative views; listen for what is not said. | Suppressed disputes are major Hiddens sources; acknowledged disputes are manageable through deliberate processes. |

# 5. Interface Types

## 5.1 Interface Types Table (mandatory)
| Type | Name | Description | Key questions | Typical examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Translation Interfaces | Points where information or meaning moves from one reality to another. Always lossy—something is selected, something is suppressed, something changes meaning. | **What gets lost in translation from substrate to policy? From experience to metrics? What is amplified?** | Dashboard translates experiential reality (suffering, workload) into KPI reality (efficiency, utilisation). Policy translates normative reality (justice, dignity) into institutional reality (procedures, enforcement). Loss: nuance, contingency, context. Gain: aggregation, clarity, comparability. |
| B | Emergence Interfaces | Thresholds where aggregate patterns in one reality produce novel phenomena at another level or reality-type. Micro-interactions generate macro-order; individual choices aggregate to collective outcomes. | **What thresholds exist between realities? Where does bottom-up emergence meet top-down institution? Where are phase transitions?** | Individual preference (experience) aggregates into market demand (modal/economic reality). Informal norms (experience, social interaction) crystallise into formal institutions (institutional reality). Micro-delays accumulate into systemic lag (process reality). |
| C | Boundary Interfaces | Disputes about category membership, layer classification, or the demarcation between realities. What counts as "factual" vs "normative"? What is "real" vs "constructed"? Where do authorities' claims conflict? | **What are the contested boundaries here? Who is claiming which reality is 'really' in charge? Is a feeling of injustice an experiential fact or a political narrative? Is a delay in response time a substrate constraint or a process problem? |

# 7. Hiddens Source Analysis & Hiddens Cross-Check

## 7.1 Hiddens Sources (Four Core Mechanisms in Realities Work)

Reality-mapping surfaces Hiddens through four systematic sources:

1. **Category Error & Reification** (Hidden-19): Treating one layer as the whole reality. A dashboard treated as "what's really happening." A policy treated as a constraint. An ideal treated as a fact. These confusions generate confident-but-wrong maps.

2. **Translation Loss & Interface Opaqueness** (Hidden-6): Information lossy in moving between layers. Experience translated into KPIs loses meaning. Substrate translated into narrative becomes fiction. Gaps at interfaces are invisible.

3. **Layer Capture & Perspectival Lock-In** (Hidden-11): Being trapped within one reality's native perspective. Engineer sees only substrate; accountant sees only representation; ethicist sees only normativity. Partial truths become universal claims.

4. **Contestation & Authority Suppression** (Hidden-8): Disagreement about which realities are "real" is visible but suppressed, hidden, or resolved via power rather than evidence. The map that gets drawn is the map the powerful want.

## 7.2 Tier 1 Hiddens Scan (Mandatory Six-Category Sweep)

Apply these six questions at least twice (early and pre-closure):

1. **Is one reality layer being treated as the whole reality?** (Reification check)
2. **What is lost when moving information between layers?** (Translation loss check)
3. **Is one agent's perspective being generalised as universal?** (Perspectival capture check)
4. **What disagreement about "what is real" is being suppressed?** (Contestation check)
5. **Are there reality-layers that are completely invisible in current narratives?** (Invisibility check)
6. **Which reality-maps would predict better outcomes if implemented?** (Predictive adequacy check)

## 7.3 Tier 2 Hiddens Crosswalk (Structured Deepening)

When Tier 1 flags a concern, use the Tier 2 crosswalk to map into specific Hiddens types:

| Tier 1 symptom | Possible Hiddens (by ID and name) | Tier 2 probe questions | Remediation levers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reification (one layer as whole) | Hidden-19 Reification; Hidden-6 Distortion | Which layer is most salient? Why? What would change if we privileged a different layer? | Explicit reality mapping; multi-layer review gates; reduce salience of dominant layer. |
| Translation loss at interface | Hidden-6 Distortion; Hidden-4 Inference | Where does meaning change in translation? What is assumed about how layers connect? What gets amplified/suppressed? | Translation protocols; bilingual roles; test critical translations before decision. |
| Perspectival capture | Hidden-11 Theory/Model; Hidden-21 Positional | Whose perspective is built into the map? What would a different expert see? What does this perspective hide? | Diversify experts by reality-layer; adversarial review; explicitly state perspective limits. |
| Suppressed contestation | Hidden-8 Framing; Hidden-25 Perspectival | Who disagrees about what is real? Why is disagreement suppressed? What would change if alternatives were visible? | Protected truth channels; stakeholder reality mapping; escalate to negotiation/Governance frameworks. |
| Invisible reality-layer | Hidden-24 Contextual; Hidden-9 Institutional | What realities are invisible from the focal perspective? What would a different scale/role reveal? | Multi-stakeholder sensing; cross-layer audit; strengthen sensing for suppressed realities. |
| Weak predictive adequacy | Hidden-23 Assumption; Hidden-11 Theory/Model | Which reality-map generates better predictions? What does the poor-predicting map assume? | Test competing reality-maps; invest in leading indicators; escalate high-uncertainty maps. |

## 7.4 Tier 3 Escalation (Full Spectrum)

Escalate to full Hiddens framework (Tier 3) if:
- Residual unknowns about reality-layers could change intervention priorities or accountability
- Disagreement about what is real is not resolvable within this framework
- Layer divergence is material and involves high-stakes decisions
- Authority disputes over who can claim each reality are contested or unresolved

# 9. Principles & Ground Rules

Five foundational principles guide framework application:

1. **Ontological Pluralism**: Multiple reality-types genuinely exist; none is reducible to another. Physicalism, phenomenalism, institutionalism, and normativity are all real. Accept the plurality.

2. **Practical Orientation**: The framework exists to improve understanding and action, not to achieve metaphysical completeness. If a reality-layer distinction helps diagnosis or intervention, use it. If not, abandon it.

3. **Interface Awareness**: Realities meet at interfaces (translation, emergence, boundary). Interface failures are major Hiddens sources. Attend to interfaces, not just content.

4. **Reflexive Application**: The framework applies to itself. Our understanding of the situation is itself a representational reality with particular properties (clarity, authority, contestation). Be aware of your own map's limits.

5. **Cumulative Contestation**: Acknowledge that "what is real" is often genuinely disputed. Multiple reality-maps may co-exist. Design governance and decision-making to manage plural realities deliberately, rather than imposing false consensus.

# 10. Glossary (Core Terms)

- **Boundary interface**: A dispute over category membership or reality-layer classification; e.g., "is this a substrate constraint or a policy choice?"
- **Category error**: Treating one reality-layer as the whole; e.g., "the dashboard is what's really happening."
- **Clarity dimension**: How unambiguous and agreed-upon a reality-layer is; ranges from transparent to unknowable.
- **Coherence dimension**: How internally consistent and mutually reinforcing a reality-layer is; ranges from highly coherent to incoherent.
- **Emergence interface**: A threshold or phase transition where micro-level dynamics in one reality generate macro-level phenomena in another; e.g., "individual preferences aggregate into market demand."
- **Interface (A/B/C)**: One of three types of reality-layer interactions: translation (Type A), emergence (Type B), or boundary (Type C).
- **Layer divergence**: A situation where different reality-layers drift out of alignment; e.g., "institutional rules have not changed but substrate conditions have."
- **Modal reality**: Possibility space; what could/must/might be; constraints and options.
- **Normativity**: Values, ethics, and obligations; what should be done and why.
- **Operationalisation**: Making a reality-layer concrete and testable through specification of objects, evidence sources, and measurement methods.
- **Perspectival lock-in**: Being trapped in one reality-layer's native perspective without recognising its partiality.
- **Reification**: Treating an abstract or constructed reality-layer (institution, representation, norm) as a hard physical fact.
- **Representation reality**: How situations are modeled, narrated, and made intelligible; dashboards, narratives, categories, metaphors.
- **Scope dimension**: The extent or applicability of a reality-layer; ranges from universal to singular.
- **Substrate reality**: Material and physical facts; resources, constraints, and causal powers independent of belief.
- **Translation interface**: A point where information or meaning must convert from one reality-layer to another; always lossy.
- **Translation loss**: The meaning, nuance, or context that is lost when information moves between reality-layers.
