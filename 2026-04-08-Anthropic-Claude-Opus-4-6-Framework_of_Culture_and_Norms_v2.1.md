## What is Culture & Norms?
Culture is the **shared meaning-and-norm system** that coordinates behaviour and interpretation across an organisation or system. It governs what people *notice*, what they *believe counts as evidence*, what is *allowed to be said*, who is *credible*, and which actions are *safe* versus *punishable*. In practice, culture is not slogans or mission statements. It is a working system of **categories, metaphors, default explanations, propriety rules, status orderings, and routines**, reinforced continuously through rewards, sanctions, legitimacy cues, and role-identity pressures.

This framework treats culture as an **analytic object** that can be mapped with the same discipline as processes, systems, or controls. It provides (i) a structured taxonomy of cultural components (six meta-categories, thirty core types), (ii) five cross-cutting dimensions to characterise and compare cultural elements consistently, (iii) typical cultural dynamics and failure modes, (iv) five interface types where cultural breakdowns predictably occur, and (v) a systematic application protocol for discovery and intervention.

Culture shapes behaviour so powerfully that violations of unstated norms can trigger severe social and professional consequences even in the absence of formal policy enforcement. This makes culture a critical variable in scenarios involving policy–practice divergence, compliance failure, repeated incidents, resistance to change, cross-functional friction, and evidence that is available but not acted upon.

## Why does Culture & Norms matter for Hiddens work?
Culture is a major generator of "unknown knowns" and other structured invisibilities. Taboo topics, euphemisms, silence spirals, compliance theatre, legitimacy scripts, and defensive routines routinely prevent accurate diagnosis. Cultural rules about who can speak to whom, what evidence is admissible, and which problems are "unspeakable" create systematic visibility gaps that persist despite intelligence, effort, and good faith.

For this reason, the framework is designed to integrate with the **Framework of Hiddens** as a mandatory cross-check in any non-trivial analysis. Section 8 (Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check) operationalises this integration, including a framework-specific Hiddens source analysis, a systematic six-category Tier 1 scan, a Tier 2 crosswalk for the most relevant Hiddens types, and escalation logic for high-stakes contexts.

Conversely, culture provides the "hiding infrastructure" for many other failures—not through malice, but through the logic of roles, incentives, legitimacy rules, and meaning systems that are perfectly rational within their local context.

## What does this framework produce?
**Primary outputs:**
- A culture map showing the operative meaning system, norms, sanctions, identity/status structures, and legitimacy rules
- A norm/sanction register (distinguishing formal policy from actual reward/penalty ecology)
- A boundary/interface risk map (translation losses, governance misalignments, evidence admissibility clashes, legitimacy contests, drift hotspots)
- A set of drift indicators and monitoring mechanisms to track norm erosion
- A ranked set of intervention options, annotated with feasibility, cultural resistance, and governance constraints
- A Hiddens shortlist (8–12 high-relevance types per case) with detection and remediation interfaces mapped

**Secondary outputs:**
- Subculture catalogue and comparison (where it matters)
- Voice allocation and deference analysis (who is heard; who is protected)
- Policy–practice gap register (where shadow workflows matter)
- Escalation path test results and retaliation risk assessment
- Monitoring governance model and review cadence

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)
Culture is the **shared meaning-and-norm system** that coordinates behaviour and interpretation. It operates as an informal second governance system, often more powerful than formal policy. Culture governs what people *notice*, what they *believe counts as evidence*, what is *allowed to be said* without risk, who is *credible*, and which actions are *safe* versus *socially costly*.

In operational terms, culture manifests as (i) a system of categories and metaphors that shape interpretation; (ii) unarticulated rules about permission, obligation, and prohibition; (iii) identity and status structures that allocate voice and protection; (iv) repeated practices and habits that persist despite official policy; (v) legitimacy scripts that make authority acceptable or contested; and (vi) dynamics of drift, subculture formation, and boundary clashes.

The core insight is that culture is **patterned and reinforced structurally**, not a collection of individual attitudes. It can be mapped, measured, and intervened on with the same rigour as any other organisational system. However, culture is also a major generator of "unknown knowns"—taboos, euphemisms, silence spirals, and legitimacy rules that prevent accurate diagnosis. For this reason, rigorous culture analysis must always integrate a mandatory Hiddens cross-check (§8) to surface the invisibilities that culture creates.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Definitions:**
- **Culture:** Shared meaning-and-norm system that shapes behaviour and interpretation, including tacit assumptions, enforcement patterns, identity structures, and legitimacy rules.
- **Norm:** Socially enforced rule of permission/obligation/prohibition, often informal and culturally enforced rather than formally codified.
- **Tacit assumption:** Unstated premise treated as obvious or self-evident; rarely examined until contested or consequential.
- **Sanction:** Reward or penalty (formal or informal, material or social) that enforces adherence to norms.
- **Status order:** Hierarchy of prestige that shapes voice, credibility, influence, and protection from consequences.
- **Subculture:** Local cultural configuration within a broader system; distinct meaning system, norms, and enforcement patterns.

**In scope:**
- Meaning systems (categories, metaphors, interpretive defaults, reality tests, admissibility rules)
- Norms and enforcement regimes (propriety, performance, exception norms, taboos, sanction regimes, protection patterns)
- Identity, belonging, and status structures (in-/out-groups, prestige, voice allocation, credibility)
- Practices, routines, and habits (both formal and shadow workflows)
- Authority, legitimacy, and power-in-culture (deference norms, legitimacy scripts, voice allocation, power signalling)
- Dynamics (drift, subculture formation, counter-cultures, tipping, boundary clashes)
- Interfaces (translation, governance, evidence, legitimacy, drift/time boundaries)

**Out of scope (by design):**
- Complete ethical theory for what norms *should* be (Values; Governance frameworks)
- Complete causal model of incidents or failures (Diagnosis; Causation frameworks)
- Full decision method for culture change (Decisions; Interventions frameworks)
- Comprehensive history of organisational identity or strategy (unless directly relevant to norms)

**Common confusions:**
- Culture ≠ stated values (values are often aspirational; culture is enacted and enforced)
- Culture ≠ "people problems" (culture is patterned and structurally reinforced, not personality-based)
- Norms ≠ written policy (informal sanctions frequently dominate formal rules)
- "Strong culture" ≠ "healthy culture" (strength can amplify failure modes; unity can suppress dissent)
- Culture change ≠ messaging (messaging without incentive/role/power alignment produces theatre)

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)
### I. Meaning & Sensemaking
- **Definition:** The interpretive layer that determines categories, metaphors, defaults, and admissibility rules. How people understand "what kind of thing" something is and "what counts as real."
- **Diagnostic cues:** Same facts yield different conclusions across cohorts; persistent "obvious" disagreements; evidence discounting; different "reality tests."
- **Typical failure mode:** Treating culture as communication style or personality variation rather than as a reality-definition system that constrains diagnosis.

### II. Norms, Rules & Sanctions
- **Definition:** The local rule system and its enforcement (formal and informal). What is socially required, permitted, or prohibited, and what happens if you violate it.
- **Diagnostic cues:** People know what not to do/say even without explicit policy; sanctions appear "informal" but consistent; exceptions are unwritten.
- **Typical failure mode:** Assuming policy governs behaviour more than informal penalties, rewards, and protection patterns actually do.

### III. Identity, Belonging & Status
- **Definition:** Membership boundaries and prestige hierarchies that allocate credibility, voice authority, and protection from consequences.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Who is listened to; who is promoted; who is forgiven; who is blamed; who is protected; who is expendable.
- **Typical failure mode:** Mistaking competence signals for status signals (or vice versa); conflating role authority with cultural credibility.

### IV. Practices, Habits & Routines
- **Definition:** Repeated behaviours and processes, including shadow workflows that exist to survive mismatches between policy and reality.
- **Diagnostic cues:** "This is how it really works"; unofficial checklists; side applications and spreadsheets; standing workarounds; accepted deviations.
- **Typical failure mode:** Measuring compliance by documents and stated process rather than by observing what is actually done.

### V. Authority, Legitimacy & Power-in-Culture
- **Definition:** The cultural mechanisms that make authority acceptable (or contested) and distribute voice, credibility, and protection.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Deference patterns; who can speak; who is overridden; who is protected; who is blamed; legitimacy disputes.
- **Typical failure mode:** Conflating formal role authority with cultural credibility; ignoring legitimacy contestation until it escalates.

### VI. Dynamics, Drift & Boundary Clashes
- **Definition:** How culture changes, fragments, and fails across boundaries. Slow erosion, sudden tipping, integration breakdowns.
- **Diagnostic cues:** Exception frequency increasing; norm violations normalised; subcultures diverging; sudden reversals; post-merger culture wars; regulator clash.
- **Typical failure mode:** Treating culture as static; missing path dependencies and tipping risks; underestimating boundary translation losses.

# 3. Core Types (30 types across 6 meta-categories)

## 3.1 Meta-Category I: Meaning & Sensemaking (5 core types)

### I.1 Interpretive Categories
**Definition:** Shared classifications and conceptual vocabulary that shape what people notice and how they interpret it.
**Key questions:** What categories do people use? Are they named or tacit? Do they align or diverge across cohorts? Are there sacred vs profane categories?
**Typical risk:** Category absence (what cannot be named cannot be addressed); misalignment across boundaries.
**Intervention lever:** Audit categories; surface hidden taxonomies; map contested categories; design naming conventions for previously invisible problems.

### I.2 Metaphors & Analogies (dominant frames)
**Definition:** Dominant metaphors that shape interpretation ("this is a war" vs "this is a family"; "this is a machine" vs "this is an organism").
**Key questions:** What metaphors dominate? What do they afford and constrain? Are metaphors shared?
**Typical risk:** Tacit metaphors lock in assumptions; metaphor clash across boundaries; misalignment with reality.
**Intervention lever:** Elicit and surface metaphors; test fitness to reality; introduce competing frames; reframe problems.

### I.3 Reality Tests & Evidence Admissibility Rules
**Definition:** Unstated criteria for what counts as "real," "true," or "credible" in this setting.
**Key questions:** What evidence is trusted? Who is a credible source? What is dismissed? What counts as proof?
**Typical risk:** Evidence admissibility rules exclude inconvenient truths; reality tests diverge across cohorts; denial systems become institutionalized.
**Intervention lever:** Audit admissibility rules; test reality hypotheses against ground truth; introduce alternative evidence formats; establish triangulation discipline.

### I.4 Salience & Attention Defaults
**Definition:** What gets noticed, what is backgrounded, what is invisible. Patterns of selective attention.
**Key questions:** What is salient to whom? What is habitually overlooked? Do attention patterns align or diverge?
**Typical risk:** Systematic blindness to certain types of problems; inattention to weak signals; "we didn't see it coming" despite visible evidence.
**Intervention lever:** Implement scanning discipline; rotate perspectives; establish monitoring against attention defaults; surface habitually overlooked signals.

### I.5 Causal Attribution Defaults
**Definition:** Automatic explanations for why things happen (luck, merit, conspiracy, individual incompetence, systemic failure, external forces).
**Key questions:** What is the default attribution? Are failures attributed to systems or individuals? Are successes attributed to luck or skill?
**Typical risk:** Systematic scapegoating; defensive routines that prevent learning; attributions that align with status/power rather than evidence.
**Intervention lever:** Surface attribution rules; test against evidence; introduce competing explanations; redesign accountability systems.

## 3.2 Meta-Category II: Norms, Rules & Sanctions (5 core types)

### II.1 Propriety Norms (decorum; what is "appropriate")
**Definition:** Rules about appropriate demeanour, language, dress, formality, and social behaviour.
**Key questions:** What is "professional"? What is "disrespectful"? What is acceptable to say or do?
**Typical risk:** Propriety norms enforced harshly; status-dependent (different rules for different people); taboo zones enforced through propriety rules.
**Intervention lever:** Audit propriety rules; test consistency; identify status dependency; redesign propriety norms to enable upward truth flow.

### II.2 Performance Norms (what work looks like; standards of excellence)
**Definition:** Unstated standards for what counts as "good work," effort, thoroughness, and speed.
**Key questions:** What does "good" look like? What is the effort expectation? How is thoroughness valued?
**Typical risk:** Performance norms misaligned with stated goals; norms enforced inconsistently (heroes excused from rules); exception norms become the rule.
**Intervention lever:** Surface performance norms; test against capability and evidence; establish explicit standards; identify and address exception norms.

### II.3 Taboos (forbidden topics, questions, actions)
**Definition:** Things that must not be said, questioned, or done. Enforced through shame, retaliation, or exclusion.
**Key questions:** What cannot be discussed? Who is protected by taboo? What questions are off-limits?
**Typical risk:** Taboos prevent diagnosis; silence spirals; problems remain unaddressed; taboo zones overlap with high-risk domains (safety, ethics, compliance).
**Intervention lever:** Map taboos; identify protected categories; establish safe channels for taboo topics; test and challenge taboo assumptions.

### II.4 Sanction Regimes (what actually happens if you violate norms)
**Definition:** The real reward and penalty ecology: what gets rewarded, what gets punished, who is protected, who is scapegoated.
**Key questions:** What are the actual consequences? Are they formal or informal? Are they consistent? Who is protected?
**Typical risk:** Informal sanctions dominate; sanctions inconsistent (status-dependent); retaliation spirals; scapegoating patterns.
**Intervention lever:** Map actual sanctions (not policy); identify protection patterns; trace retaliation risk; redesign accountability systems.

### II.5 Exception Norms (what is routinely "bent" or made exceptions to)
**Definition:** Rules that are systematically violated or bent, with tacit permission.
**Key questions:** Which rules are routinely bent? Who can bend them? Who cannot? How is this managed?
**Typical risk:** Exception norms become rule; policy–practice gap; equity problems (different rules for different people); compliance theatre.
**Intervention lever:** Audit exception patterns; understand adaptive logic; test necessity; redesign policy or processes to align with practice.

## 3.3 Meta-Category III: Identity, Belonging & Status (5 core types)

### III.1 In-/Out-Group Boundaries (insider vs outsider identity)
**Definition:** Who is "one of us" and who is "other"? Markers of belonging.
**Key questions:** Who is inside? How do you become inside? Who is permanently outside? What are the markers?
**Typical risk:** Rigid boundaries; outsiders discounted or mistrusted; diversity/integration initiatives fail due to boundary strength.
**Intervention lever:** Map boundary markers; test permeability; identify exclusion mechanisms; redesign onboarding and integration.

### III.2 Prestige & Status Hierarchies
**Definition:** Who is respected, who is dismissed, who is powerful, who is powerless.
**Key questions:** What determines status? Is it transparent? Does status align with competence? What are the status signals?
**Typical risk:** Status misaligned with competence (deference to incompetent authority); status used to enforce silence; hierarchy brittle and contested.
**Intervention lever:** Map status signals; test alignment with competence; identify status-based voice suppression; redesign role clarity.

### III.3 Voice Authority & Credibility
**Definition:** Who is listened to? Whose voice counts? Who is dismissed?
**Key questions:** Who can speak? Who is heard? Who is disbelieved? What determines voice authority?
**Typical risk:** Voice authority misaligned with expertise; systematic suppression (gender, race, tenure, function); credibility gaps.
**Intervention lever:** Audit voice allocation; test against expertise; identify suppression patterns; redesign listening and escalation systems.

### III.4 Protection Patterns (who is shielded from consequences)
**Definition:** Who is protected from consequences and who is scapegoated?
**Key questions:** Who makes mistakes and is forgiven? Who makes mistakes and is blamed? Who is protected by role or status?
**Typical risk:** Powerful people protected, powerless blamed; scapegoating; innocent people punished; accountability vacuums.
**Intervention lever:** Map protection patterns; audit fairness; identify and challenge scapegoating; redesign accountability.

### III.5 Credentialing & Legitimacy Markers
**Definition:** What makes someone "legitimate" or "credible" (credentials, tenure, background, network, access)?
**Key questions:** What credentials matter? What background is required? Can outsiders ever be credible?
**Typical risk:** Credentialing reproduces homogeneity; outsider perspectives discounted; expertise unrecognized; gatekeeping.
**Intervention lever:** Audit credentialing criteria; test against competence; identify gatekeeping; redesign recognition systems.

---

## 3.4 Meta-Category IV: Practices, Habits & Routines (5 core types)

### IV.1 Shadow Workflows (the real process vs the stated process)
**Definition:** How work actually gets done (often different from stated policy or process).
**Key questions:** What is the real process? Why does it differ from policy? Who knows the real process?
**Typical risk:** Shadow workflows reveal policy–practice gaps; new people trapped by learning wrong process; workarounds become invisible sources of error.
**Intervention lever:** Ground-truth observation; map shadow workflows; understand adaptive logic; redesign policy or process; formalize workarounds if necessary.

### IV.2 Escalation Habits (how decisions actually get made; who decides; what triggers escalation)
**Definition:** The real decision-making process: who has voice, who decides, what actually triggers escalation vs suppression.
**Key questions:** How do decisions really get made? Who decides? What stops escalation? Who escalates and who suppresses?
**Typical risk:** Escalation suppressed by culture; decisions made by the wrong person; escalation latency masks problems.
**Intervention lever:** Trace escalation patterns; test against policy; identify suppression; redesign escalation channels and triggers.

### IV.3 Handoff & Translation Routines
**Definition:** How information and work move across boundaries (teams, functions, systems).
**Key questions:** How is information translated? What is lost in translation? Who controls handoffs?
**Typical risk:** Information loss at boundaries; translation failures; interface gaps; work gets duplicated or lost.
**Intervention lever:** Map handoff points; trace information loss; identify translation rules; redesign interfaces; establish translation discipline.

### IV.4 Standard Exceptions & Workarounds
**Definition:** Accepted shortcuts, workarounds, and deviations from stated process.
**Key questions:** What shortcuts are standard? What workarounds are routine? Are they known to leadership?
**Typical risk:** Workarounds hide real constraints; deviations create equity problems; compliance audit may fail despite safe practice.
**Intervention lever:** Map workarounds; understand constraints; formalize or redesign; establish exception governance.

### IV.5 Habituation & Attention Drift
**Definition:** How risks become normalised through habituation; how attention drifts from important but routine signals.
**Key questions:** What risks have become "normal"? What warning signals are routinely ignored? What is the "I've always been like this" effect?
**Typical risk:** Normalisation of deviance; risks become invisible; warning signals unheeded; "that's just how it is here."
**Intervention lever:** Audit normalised risks; test against external benchmarks; establish monitoring; reset attention discipline.

---

## 3.5 Meta-Category V: Authority, Legitimacy & Power-in-Culture (5 core types)

### V.1 Legitimacy Scripts (why authority is accepted or resisted)
**Definition:** The narrative or cultural logic that makes authority acceptable (or explains why it is contested).
**Key questions:** Why is authority accepted? What makes a leader legitimate? What delegitimizes authority?
**Typical risk:** Legitimacy contested; authority brittle; leadership changes trigger culture clashes; tacit legitimacy rules invisible until violated.
**Intervention lever:** Surface legitimacy rules; test against reality; identify and resolve contests; redesign leadership signals and accountability.

### V.2 Deference & Voice Allocation (to whom do people defer; who gets heard)
**Definition:** Patterns of deference, respect, and who gets heard in meetings, decisions, and crises.
**Key questions:** To whom do people defer? When is deference withdrawn? How is voice allocated?
**Typical risk:** Deference to incompetence; deference withdrawal surprises authority; upward truth flow blocked.
**Intervention lever:** Audit deference patterns; test against competence; establish voice allocation discipline; redesign listening.

### V.3 Power Signalling & Status Display
**Definition:** How power and status are communicated and recognised (dress, language, office, access, visibility).
**Key questions:** What signals power? What signals powerlessness? Is power visible or hidden?
**Typical risk:** Power signals misaligned with actual authority; signals reproduce inequality; status display enforces compliance.
**Intervention lever:** Audit power signals; test alignment with authority; identify and challenge status enforcement; redesign symbols.

### V.4 Protection Norms & Retaliation Risk
**Definition:** Tacit rules about who is safe from retaliation and who is at risk.
**Key questions:** Who can speak without risk? Who is at risk of retaliation? How does retaliation operate?
**Typical risk:** Retaliation spirals; silence enforced by fear; truth withheld due to retaliation risk; escalation suppressed.
**Intervention lever:** Assess retaliation risk; build safe channels; establish psychological safety; redesign accountability.

### V.5 Override Norms (who can override whom; what authority trumps what)
**Definition:** Rules about who can override decisions, authority, or rules.
**Key questions:** Who can override whom? What creates override authority? Are overrides explained?
**Typical risk:** Override authority unclear; powerful people bypass process; equity problems; accountability gaps.
**Intervention lever:** Clarify override authority; test necessity; establish transparency; redesign authority structure.

---

## 3.6 Meta-Category VI: Dynamics, Drift & Boundary Clashes (5 core types)

### VI.1 Norm Drift (gradual, incremental norm erosion under pressure)
**Definition:** How norms change gradually through repeated trade-offs, exceptions, and pressures.
**Key questions:** Which norms are drifting? Why are they drifting? How fast? What are the tipping points?
**Typical risk:** Drift normalised as incremental change; tipping points pass unnoticed; brittle systems fail suddenly; ethics erode.
**Intervention lever:** Establish drift indicators; monitor exception frequency and escalation latency; set tipping-point triggers; reset norms before failure.

### VI.2 Subculture Formation & Divergence
**Definition:** How distinct meaning systems and norms form within different functions, sites, or leadership groups.
**Key questions:** What subcultures exist? How do their norms diverge? Where do they clash?
**Typical risk:** Monolithic culture assumption misses divergence; integration failures; cross-functional friction; hidden conflicts.
**Intervention lever:** Map subcultures explicitly; compare norms and meaning systems; identify interface risks; design integration.

### VI.3 Counter-Culture & Dissent Norms
**Definition:** How dissent and counter-narratives are treated; whether challenge is safe or punished.
**Key questions:** Can people disagree? Are dissenters protected or expelled? What does healthy dissent look like?
**Typical risk:** Dissent suppressed; counter-culture driven underground; groupthink; blind spots accumulate.
**Intervention lever:** Establish safe dissent; create counter-narrative space; protect dissenters; rotate perspectives.

### VI.4 Boundary Clashes & Culture Misalignment
**Definition:** Conflicts that arise at boundaries (M&A, regulation, cross-functional work) when meaning systems and norms diverge.
**Key questions:** Where do boundaries create friction? What meaning systems conflict? How are clashes resolved?
**Typical risk:** Integration failures; regulatory contestation; cross-functional breakdown; persistent misalignment.
**Intervention lever:** Map boundary misalignments; understand each culture; design translation; establish bridge roles; create joint norms.

### VI.5 Tipping Points & Path Dependency
**Definition:** Critical thresholds where culture shifts suddenly; lock-in from past decisions.
**Key questions:** Are there tipping points? What triggers sudden shifts? Are we locked into past decisions?
**Typical risk:** Sudden culture collapse; inability to change course; path dependency traps; regime shifts surprise leadership.
**Intervention lever:** Identify tipping-point thresholds; monitor approaching limits; establish intervention before tipping; unwind path dependency.

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions (profiling cultural elements)

## 4.1 Five Cross-Cutting Dimensions
All 30 core types can be profiled along five dimensions. Use these for comparison, hypothesis generation, and intervention targeting.

| Dimension | Definition | Spectrum | Diagnostic question |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Explicitness** | Is the cultural element stated or tacit? | Explicit (written, stated, rule-like) ←→ Tacit (unspoken, assumed, enacted) | Do people know and can they state this rule, or is it "just how we do things"? |
| **Consistency** | Is the element uniformly enforced or is it variable? | Consistent (applied to all) ←→ Variable (status-dependent, context-dependent) | Is this rule applied the same way to everyone, or does it depend on who you are? |
| **Fit** | Does the element align with stated values, policy, and external norms? | Aligned (consistent with values/policy/regulation) ←→ Misaligned (contradicts values/policy/regulation) | Does this norm contradict stated policy or espoused values? Is it defensible externally? |
| **Visibility** | Is the element easy or hard to surface? | Visible (open discussion; artefacts available) ←→ Hidden (taboo; defensive; denied) | Can we ask about this directly, or is it protected by silence, shame, or denial? |
| **Contestation** | Is the element shared or disputed? | Shared (consensus; majority agreement) ←→ Contested (disagreement; subculture divergence) | Does everyone agree, or is there significant disagreement about this norm? |

# 5. Typical Dynamics & Failure Modes (5 primary patterns)

## 5.1 The Silence Spiral
**Mechanism:** Taboo prevents speaking → absence of voice increases sense of taboo → shame increases → silence deepens.
**Trigger:** High-stakes topic (safety, ethics, power); status asymmetry; retaliation risk.
**Failure signature:** Problems remain unaddressed; evidence suppressed; diagnosis delayed; sudden crises.

## 5.2 Normalisation of Deviance
**Mechanism:** Exception becomes routine → routine becomes normal → risk becomes invisible → tipping point approached undetected.
**Trigger:** Chronic pressure; repeated exception tolerance; weak monitoring.
**Failure signature:** Gradual drift unnoticed; sudden system failure; "we didn't see it coming."

## 5.3 Scapegoating & Blame Spirals
**Mechanism:** Powerful people protected → powerless people blamed → accountability vacuums → systemic problems invisible → scapegoat cycle repeats.
**Trigger:** Failure or crisis; status asymmetry; absence of systemic accountability.
**Failure signature:** Repeated blame of same person/group; protection of powerful; systemic problems unaddressed.

## 5.4 Meaning Misalignment
**Mechanism:** Different cohorts interpret same facts differently → decisions fail → misalignment attributed to "people problems" → no structural change.
**Trigger:** Crossing boundaries; change initiatives; new evidence.
**Failure signature:** "They just don't understand"; persistent misalignment despite effort; cross-functional friction.

## 5.5 Integration Failure (post-M&A, regulation, organisational change)
**Mechanism:** Two cultures with different meaning systems, norms, and identities forced together → clash → one culture dominates → misfits exit or suppress themselves → integration fails.
**Trigger:** M&A; regulatory change; organisational restructuring; geographic expansion.
**Failure signature:** "Two companies in one"; culture wars; high turnover; performance decline; governance breakdown.

# 6. Five Interface Types: Where Culture Breaks Down

## 6.1 Interface Type A: Translation Failures (meaning doesn't cross)
**Definition:** What counts as "obvious" or "real" or "evidence" differs across boundaries.
**Risk:** Information loss; talking past each other; evidence discounted.
**Detection:** Do people agree on basic facts? Is there persistent "obvious" disagreement?
**Remediation:** Establish shared meaning; translation discipline; triangulation across cohorts.

## 6.2 Interface Type B: Governance Misalignment
**Definition:** Decision rights, authority, and escalation rules differ or clash.
**Risk:** Decisions go to the wrong person; escalation blocked; authority contested.
**Detection:** Who actually decides? Does it match the formal chart? Are there override conflicts?
**Remediation:** Clarify decision rights; establish escalation clarity; test against incidents.

## 6.3 Interface Type C: Evidence Admissibility Clash
**Definition:** One side trusts evidence the other side dismisses (data vs intuition; external vs internal; quantitative vs qualitative).
**Risk:** Dialogue impossible; evidence discounted; diagnosis fails.
**Detection:** What evidence moves decisions? What is dismissed? Why?
**Remediation:** Establish evidence triangulation; test alternative formats; build shared evidence standards.

## 6.4 Interface Type D: Legitimacy Contest
**Definition:** Authority is accepted on one side of boundary but not the other.
**Risk:** Commands resisted; authority brittle; integration fails.
**Detection:** Is authority accepted across boundaries? What legitimacy scripts apply?
**Remediation:** Establish shared legitimacy; build bridge roles; redesign authority signals.

## 6.5 Interface Type E: Drift/Time Boundary
**Definition:** Older and newer members have different norms and meaning systems; historical memory differs.
**Risk:** Generational culture clash; past decisions lock in path dependency; new people misaligned.
**Detection:** How do newer and longer-tenure members differ? What histories are remembered?
**Remediation:** Establish historical awareness; design onboarding; reset norms consciously.

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Framework-Specific Hiddens Source Analysis
Culture is a major generator of invisibilities. This section operationalises the mandatory integration with the Framework of Hiddens.

**Why Culture hides:**
- Taboos prevent naming; silence spirals enforce avoidance
- Propriety norms restrict what can be said; shame silences
- Status hierarchies suppress upward truth flow; lower-status people self-censor
- Meaning systems discount inconvenient evidence; reality tests block information
- Protection patterns shield powerful people from consequences; blame falls elsewhere
- Role identity pressures create "not my job" thinking; diffuse responsibility
- Legitimacy scripts explain away contradictions; defensiveness prevents diagnosis

## 8.2 Tier 1 Scan: Six-Category Visibility Audit (mandatory early and pre-closure)

Run this scan at Start-of-Run and Pre-Closure. Tag H/M/L relevance per category.

**Informational Hiddens (what is not said or is euphemized)**
- Are there taboo topics? What questions are off-limits? What is euphemized?
- Are there silence spirals? What triggers them? Who speaks and who is silent?
- Does upward truth flow exist? Do junior people report problems to senior? Do they expect to be heard?
- Evidence: interview upward truth flow directly; sample across levels; observe who speaks in meetings.

**Systemic Hiddens (how structures obscure reality)**
- Do meaning systems block diagnoses? Are inconvenient realities reframed?
- Are evidence admissibility rules excluding inconvenient evidence?
- Do decision rights obscure accountability? Is responsibility diffuse?
- Evidence: trace how decisions are made; test evidence admissibility; audit meaning systems.

**Relational Hiddens (who is excluded or invisible)**
- Is voice allocated fairly? Who is heard and who is dismissed?
- Are lower-status people invisible in decisions? Are they protected or exposed?
- Are there scapegoated groups? Are powerful people protected?
- Evidence: audit voice allocation; trace protection patterns; observe who is blamed.

**Ontological Hiddens (what cannot be seen because it has no name or category)**
- Are there category absences? Can't name it = can't address it?
- Are sacred cows unnamable? Are they protected by taboo?
- Are problems legitimised as "just how we are"? Are they reframed as unsolvable?
- Evidence: test what can be discussed; identify protected categories; look for "that's just us" closure.

**Existential Hiddens (denial; things that are "too big" or "too scary" to acknowledge)**
- What cannot be admitted? What would be too damaging if acknowledged?
- What is protected by collective denial? What historical truths are denied?
- Are role failures hidden by diffuse blame? Is individual responsibility obscured?
- Evidence: look for defensive routines; test closure questions; interview on vulnerable topics.

**Epistemological Hiddens (what is known but not trusted; what knowledge is blocked)**
- Is relevant knowledge blocked from decision-makers? Is information bottlenecked?
- Are expertise and intelligence misaligned? Do experts lack credibility?
- Are there credentialing gatekeepers? Is outside knowledge discounted?
- Evidence: audit information flow; test expert credibility; trace gatekeeping.

# 9. Application Protocol (7 steps)

## 9.1 Seven-Step Application Framework (Light and Full Depth)

### STEP 1: Scope, Boundaries & Subculture Mapping
**Objective:** Define the scope of the culture analysis; identify subcultures and boundaries.

**Light Depth (1–2h):**
1. Scope Charter: Which part of the organisation? Which functions? Which time horizon (last 12/24 months)? Which incident or event is the anchor?
2. Identify 3–5 most relevant subcultures (by function, site, tenure, leadership microclimate).
3. Map key boundaries (function, geography, hierarchy, role, profession, customer-facing vs internal).
4. Select 1–2 boundaries for detailed interface risk analysis.

**Full Depth (3–4h):**
1. Complete Scope Charter with stakeholder agreement.
2. Identify all subcultures; map meaning-system divergence across each.
3. Map all boundary types and rank by consequence (using Interface Types A–E, §6).
4. Identify 2–4 high-risk boundaries for detailed interface analysis.
5. Establish stakeholder interviews: frontline, middle, senior; diverse functions; include protected and scapegoated classes.

**Outputs:**
- Scope Charter (signed; decision rights, sponsor, governance model confirmed)
- Subculture List (with brief characterization)
- Boundary Risk Map (interfaces ranked by consequence)
- Interview Schedule (cohort composition; confidentiality agreements)

---

### STEP 2: Meaning Systems Elicitation
**Objective:** Surface the interpretive layer: categories, metaphors, reality tests, evidence admissibility rules, sensemaking defaults.

**Light Depth (2–3h):**
1. **Interpretive Categories:** Interview (5–10 people across levels): "What categories matter in this culture? How do people describe 'normal' vs 'abnormal' vs 'impossible'?"
2. **Dominant Metaphors:** "What metaphors shape how people talk about work, problems, relationships?" (War? Family? Machine? Journey?)
3. **Reality Tests:** "How do people know something is true? What counts as proof here? What evidence moves decisions?"
4. **Sensemaking Defaults:** "What explanations do people reach for first? How do people explain failures, successes, surprises?"
5. Document in table format; identify consensus vs contestation across cohorts.

**Full Depth (4–6h):**
1. Extend interview cohort to 20–30 across all levels, functions, and subcultures.
2. Add document analysis: policies, internal communications, dashboards, review formats, ritual language.
3. Conduct observation (meetings, escalations, decision points): what is actually discussed vs what is absent?
4. Identify meaning-system divergences across cohorts; use Perspectives framework for subculture comparison.
5. Test reality tests against ground truth: Do people behave as their stated reality tests predict? Where do they diverge?

**Outputs:**
- Meaning System Map (categories, metaphors, reality tests, sensemaking defaults; tagged by consensus/contestation)
- Cohort Comparison (frontline vs middle vs senior; function vs function)
- Evidence Admissibility Register (what moves decisions; what is dismissed; by whom)
- Reality-Test Hypothesis Backlog (for Step 5 validation)

---

### STEP 3: Norms, Sanctions & Status Mapping
**Objective:** Build an explicit map of the sanction ecology, status hierarchies, and identity structures.

**Light Depth (2–3h):**
1. **Sanction Ecology:** Interview (5–10 across levels): "What are the real consequences if you break a rule? Who gets punished? Who gets protected? What actually happens—not the policy, the reality?"
2. **Propriety Norms:** "What is considered 'professional'? What is 'disrespectful'? What language/dress/behaviour is appropriate?"
3. **Performance Norms:** "What does 'good work' look like? How much effort is expected? What counts as thorough?"
4. **Status Hierarchies:** "Who is respected? Who is dismissed? Who is promoted? Who is forgiven?"
5. **Voice Authority:** "Whose opinions move decisions? Who is listened to? Who is disbelieved?"
6. Document sanction patterns (by status); identify consistency and protection patterns.

**Full Depth (4–6h):**
1. Extend interviews to trace sanctions across 10–15 recent incidents or decisions.
2. Observation: Who is praised, blamed, protected, scapegoated in meetings, reviews, escalations?
3. Document analysis: promotion patterns; performance reviews; who is fired vs reassigned; protection patterns.
4. Identify status markers and signals; test alignment with competence.
5. Map scapegoating patterns and retaliation risk.
6. Audit voice allocation: does voice match expertise? Is voice status-dependent?

**Outputs:**
- Sanction Register (actual consequences; by status; protection patterns; retaliation risk assessment)
- Status Hierarchy Map (prestige signals; credentialing criteria; alignment with competence)
- Voice Allocation Map (who is heard; who is dismissed; by role/status/function)
- Protected vs Scapegoated Register (who is protected; who bears consequences; evidence-based)

---

### STEP 4: Practices, Routines & Shadow Workflows
**Objective:** Map what is actually done; compare to stated policy; identify adaptive logic and policy–practice gaps.

**Light Depth (2–3h):**
1. **Shadow Workflows:** "How does work actually get done? What is the real process vs the stated process?" Trace 2–3 key workflows.
2. **Escalation Habits:** "How do decisions really get made? Who decides? What stops escalation?"
3. **Handoff Patterns:** "How does information move across teams/functions? What gets lost? What gets translated?"
4. **Exception Norms:** "What rules are routinely bent? With whose permission? Who cannot bend them?"
5. **Workarounds:** "What shortcuts are standard? What spreadsheets parallel the system? What is done 'the real way'?"
6. Identify adaptive logic: Why do practices differ from policy?

**Full Depth (4–6h):**
1. Observation: Shadow 2–3 workflows in detail (meetings, decisions, handoffs, escalations).
2. Trace 5–10 recent incidents/decisions through the real process (not policy).
3. Document all shadow workflows; build workflow register with policy comparison.
4. Identify cascade effects: where do workarounds create problems downstream?
5. Test impact: where does policy–practice divergence create risk, equity problems, or compliance failure?

**Outputs:**
- Shadow Workflow Register (real process vs stated; with policy comparison)
- Escalation Mapping (how decisions are really made; who decides; what triggers suppression)
- Handoff & Translation Register (where information is lost; who translates; impact on downstream)
- Exception Norm Register (what is bent; by whom; with whose permission; equity implications)
- Workaround Register (shortcuts; technical debt; systemic constraints they reveal)

---

### STEP 5: Hiddens Tier 1 & 2 Scans (mandatory early and pre-closure)
**Objective:** Run mandatory Hiddens integration. Scan for all six Hiddens meta-categories; escalate if threshold met.

**Light Depth (1–2h):**
1. Run Tier 1 scan (§8.2): tag H/M/L per category; identify highest-relevance types.
2. If 3+ H ratings: escalate to Tier 2 (§8.3); run focused scan on top categories.
3. Populate Hiddens Register (framework view, §8.5).
4. Assess escalation threshold (§8.4): does retaliation risk, safety, compliance, or taboo involvement trigger Tier 3?

**Full Depth (2–4h):**
1. Complete Tier 1 scan as above.
2. Run full Tier 2 scan if triggered (§8.3); detailed mechanism analysis per category.
3. If Tier 3 escalation triggered: invoke full Hiddens protocol (Framework of Hiddens, OG §7.2–7.3); establish joint mapping.
4. Integrate Hiddens findings into culture map; update risk register.

**Outputs:**
- Hiddens Register (Tier 1 + 2; mechanism analysis; detection + remediation interfaces)
- Escalation Assessment (Tier 3 triggered? Y/N; evidence; recommendation)
- Hiddens-Culture Integration Map (where culture creates invisibilities; remediation via culture + Hiddens levers)

---

### STEP 6: Boundary Risk & Interface Scanning (Light and Full)
**Objective:** Scan high-risk boundaries using Interface Types A–E; identify translation failures, governance misalignment, evidence clashes, legitimacy contests, drift risks.

**Light Depth (1–2h):**
1. Select 1–2 high-risk boundaries (from Step 1 Boundary Risk Map).
2. Scan each for Interface Types A–E (§6.1–6.5): is meaning different? Decision rights unclear? Evidence admissibility rules clash? Legitimacy contested? Time/drift divergence?
3. Document risk per interface type; rank by consequence.
4. Identify 1–2 high-consequence interface risks for intervention.

**Full Depth (3–4h):**
1. Scan all high-risk boundaries (2–4) for Interface Types A–E.
2. Deep dive on top 2–3 risks: trace specific incidents or decisions that failed due to interface risk.
3. Test each interface for root cause: is it meaning misalignment, decision-rights ambiguity, evidence clash, legitimacy contest, or time drift?
4. Document interface risk register; link to culture elements that drive each risk.

**Outputs:**
- Interface Risk Register (boundary × interface type × consequence)
- Interface Root-Cause Analysis (what culture elements drive each interface risk)
- High-Risk Interface Shortlist (top 2–3 for intervention)

---

### STEP 7: Intervention Design, Drift Indicators & Monitoring (Full Depth only; Light Depth abbreviate)
**Objective:** Design interventions; establish drift indicators and monitoring governance.

**Light Depth (1–2h):**
1. Shortlist top 3 culture elements (types) to intervene on (based on consequence and feasibility).
2. Identify 1 change lever per element (comms, process, incentive, role design, boundary reform).
3. Identify sponsor and governance owner; note cultural resistance risk.
4. Establish 2–3 drift indicators (exception rate, escalation latency, voice suppression signals).
5. Commit to review cadence (quarterly or post-incident).

**Full Depth (4–6h):**
1. Design detailed interventions for top 3–5 culture elements:
   - **Change lever:** What is the mechanism? (Comms, process change, incentive redesign, role redesign, boundary reform, symbol/narrative change)
   - **Sponsor + governance:** Who owns change? Who decides success?
   - **Resistance assessment:** Where will backlash come from? How will it manifest?
   - **Constraint mapping:** What governance, power, or resource constraints block change?
   - **Rollout plan:** Pilot or full? Sequence? Dependencies?
2. For high-risk boundaries: design integration and bridge mechanisms (joint norms, translation roles, cross-boundary governance).
3. For Hiddens escalations: design complementary Hiddens remediation (safe channels, retaliation risk mitigation, upward truth flow design).
4. Build drift indicators:
   - Exception frequency (rate of policy violations; by status)
   - Escalation latency (time from problem identification to escalation)
   - Sanction consistency (are consequences applied evenly or status-dependently?)
   - Voice suppression signals (do junior people speak in meetings? Do they report problems?)
   - Norm violation frequency (are people increasingly breaking rules?)
5. Establish monitoring governance:
   - Review owner (who tracks drift indicators?)
   - Review cadence (monthly, quarterly, post-incident)
   - Escalation trigger (e.g., "if exception rate exceeds 20% in any cohort, escalate to sponsor")
   - Feedback loop (how are findings fed back to leadership and into learning?)
6. Link to Risk framework for tipping-point detection; link to Learning & Adaptation for reflection.

**Outputs:**
- Intervention Register (element, change lever, sponsor, resistance risk, resource needs, rollout plan)
- Boundary Integration Plan (for high-risk interfaces)
- Hiddens Remediation Links (how Hiddens and Culture interventions integrate)
- Drift Indicator Dashboard (metrics, owners, cadence, escalation thresholds)
- Monitoring Governance Charter (owner, review cadence, escalation, learning feedback)

# 10. Framework Principles (5 core commitments)

1. **Rigorous Distinction of Stated vs Enacted Culture:** Do not assume policy governs practice. Map both; compare; explain divergence. Honour the logic of shadow workflows.

2. **Structural over Individual Attribution:** Culture is patterned and reinforced structurally. Do not blame personality; trace incentives, role pressures, meaning systems, and enforcement ecologies.

3. **Mandatory Hiddens Cross-Check:** Culture hides. Assume invisibilities exist. Run Tier 1 scan early and pre-closure. Escalate to Tier 2 or 3 if threshold met. Do not close analysis without Hiddens integration.

4. **Triangulation across Cohorts:** Frontline, middle, and senior have different reality tests and norms. Interview across levels. Surface consensus and contestation. Do not accept single narratives.

5. **Provenance and Precision:** Record who said it, in what context, with what confidence. Distinguish Fact/Inference/Assumption/Unknown. Preserve evidence; archive interviews. Avoid anecdotal diagnosis.

# 11. Extension Guidance (adapting the framework)

## 11.1 Subculture-Specific Application
Run the full framework (Steps 1–7) separately for each major subculture (e.g., by function, site, leadership group). Then:
1. Compare meaning systems, norms, and practices across subcultures.
2. Identify areas of convergence and divergence.
3. Scan boundaries between subcultures using Interface Types A–E.
4. Design integration or managed separation (if subcultures serve distinct purposes).

## 11.2 Dyadic Culture Analysis (two-party conflict, merger, etc.)
1. Run Steps 1–4 separately for each party; produce independent culture maps.
2. Compare maps directly: where do meaning systems clash? Where do norms diverge? Where are legitimacy scripts contested?
3. Scan all five Interface Types (A–E) on the boundary between parties.
4. Design integration (if goal is merger) or coexistence governance (if goal is managed separation).

## 11.3 Quick Scan for Targeted Scoping
If time is extremely limited (< 4h):
1. Conduct 5–10 focused interviews on a single theme (e.g., "Why does escalation get suppressed?" or "Why do people breach X norm?").
2. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan on 2–3 highest-relevance categories.
3. Deliver shortlist of top 3 cultural mechanisms driving the problem and 2–3 ranked interventions.
4. Flag decision-critical unknowns and monitoring needs.

## 11.4 Culture + Power Integration
Culture & Norms + Power framework:
- Culture maps the informal authority system (legitimacy scripts, deference, voice allocation).
- Power maps formal authority, resource control, and dependency relationships.
- Together: understand both the formal and informal governance system; identify where formal and informal authority align or clash.
- Use Power framework findings to understand **why** certain culture elements persist (powerful people protect them).

## 11.5 Culture + Incentives Integration
Culture & Norms + Incentives framework:
- Culture maps the informal reward/penalty system (sanction ecology, protection patterns).
- Incentives maps formal incentive structures (metrics, rewards, penalties by role).
- Together: understand why shadow workflows exist (adaptive response to incentive misalignment); design incentives aligned with cultural realities; trace unintended consequences of incentive changes on culture.

## 11.6 Culture + Hiddens Deep Integration
Culture & Norms + Hiddens framework (mandatory for high-stakes scenarios):
- Run both frameworks in parallel.
- Use Culture findings to understand the **mechanism** of hiding (what cultural forces conceal what Hiddens types?).
- Use Hiddens findings to identify **what** is hidden and why it matters.
- Co-design remediation that addresses both the Hiddens (surface it) and the Cultural mechanism (change the hiding infrastructure).

# 12. Glossary & Key Terms

**Cadence:** Review rhythm or iteration cycle (e.g., "quarterly cadence" = quarterly review).

**Causal attribution:** Automatic explanation for why something happened (individual incompetence, luck, conspiracy, systemic failure, external forces).

**Cohort:** Group of people sharing a characteristic (e.g., "frontline cohort," "senior cohort," "new hire cohort").

**Consensus:** Shared agreement across majority of cohort.

**Constraint:** Limiting factor (governance, resource, power, capability) that bounds possible interventions.

**Contestation:** Disagreement or dispute about a norm, meaning, or decision.

**Defensive routine:** Automatic pattern that prevents awareness of threat or failure (explanation, reframing, denial, blame).

**Deference:** Show of respect, submission to another's authority or opinion.

**Divergence:** Difference in meaning systems, norms, or practices across subcultures or boundaries.

**Drift:** Gradual change in norms or practices over time, often unnoticed until tipping point.

**Enacted vs Espoused:** What is actually done (enacted) vs what is said or intended (espoused).

**Evidence admissibility:** Rules about what counts as "real" or "credible" evidence in a setting.

**Exception norm:** Rule that is routinely violated, with tacit permission.

**Governance:** System of decision rights, authority, and accountability.

**Hiding infrastructure:** Structural and social mechanisms that make things invisible (silence, shame, taboo, suppression, meaning systems, frame distortion).

**Interface:** Boundary where different cultures, meaning systems, or authorities meet.

**Legitimacy:** Quality that makes authority accepted or rightful.

**Legitimacy script:** Narrative explaining why authority is acceptable.

**Meaningful difference:** Difference that matters for diagnosis, decision, or intervention.

**Meaning system:** Set of categories, metaphors, defaults, and interpretive rules that shape sensemaking.

**Meta-category:** Highest-level grouping of cultural elements (6 meta-categories in this framework).

**Monolithic:** Treating as unified when actually diverse (e.g., assuming one culture when multiple subcultures exist).

**Normalisation of deviance:** Process by which violations of norms become routine and unremarkable.

**Norm:** Socially enforced rule of permission, obligation, or prohibition.

**Ontological:** Relating to what exists or can be named; what is real.

**Path dependency:** Lock-in from past decisions; cannot easily change course because previous decisions constrain future options.

**Propriety norm:** Rule about decorum, appropriate behaviour, language, and respect.

**Reality test:** Criterion for determining what is true or real.

**Retaliation risk:** Danger of negative consequences for speaking up or dissenting.

**Sanction:** Reward or penalty that enforces adherence to norms.

**Scapegoating:** Pattern where powerless person/group bears blame while powerful people are protected.

**Shadow workflow:** How work actually gets done (often different from stated policy or process).

**Silence spiral:** Pattern where taboo/shame prevent speaking, preventing awareness, deepening taboo, deepening silence.

**Status:** Position in a hierarchy of prestige and influence.

**Status order:** Hierarchy that shapes voice, credibility, protection, and influence.

**Subcult:** Cultural variation within a larger system; distinct meaning systems and norms.

**Tacit:** Unspoken, assumed, enacted (not explicitly stated).

**Taboo:** Forbidden topic or action; enforced through shame or fear.

**Tipping point:** Critical threshold beyond which change becomes sudden and dramatic.

**Translation:** Process of converting meaning or information across cultural boundaries; often involves loss or reinterpretation.

**Triangulation:** Testing claims against multiple sources (interviews, documents, observation) to surface consensus and disagreement.

**Voice:** Ability to speak and be heard; influence.

**Workaround:** Informal shortcut or adaptation to survive misalignment between policy and reality.

## 13.2 Known Issues & Limitations

1. **Subculture Assumption:** Framework assumes multiple subcultures exist. In very small, homogeneous organisations, may need to abbreviate Step 1.

2. **Time Constraints:** Full Depth (24–36h) is ideal; Light Depth (10–17h) acceptable for lower-consequence scenarios. Rapid mode (< 4h) is not recommended except for very scoped, single-theme quick scans.

3. **Access Constraints:** Framework depends on access to multiple levels (frontline, middle, senior). If access is limited, escalate to Full Depth only if stakes warrant, and use alternative evidence sources (documents, observation, retrospective interviews).

4. **Emotional Intensity:** Culture work surfaces sensitive topics (retaliation risk, scapegoating, taboos). Facilitate carefully; protect psychological safety; consider independent facilitator for high-stakes scenarios.

5. **Change Resistance:** Culture is slow to change. Interventions designed per §9 Step 7 may take 6–12 months to embed. Set realistic expectations; establish monitoring to prevent backsliding.
