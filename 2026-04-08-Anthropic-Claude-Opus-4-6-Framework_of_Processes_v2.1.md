# Introduction

## What is Processes and Process Analysis?

Entities come into being, persist through deliberate maintenance, develop and mature, transform under pressure or opportunity, and sometimes degrade, collapse, or disappear. Yet much analytical practice treats change as incidental—focusing on snapshots rather than trajectories, on events rather than the patterns that connect them over time. Process analysis inverts this bias: it asks what kind of change is happening, what keeps it going, what slows or accelerates it, where non-linear shifts become likely, and how long-horizon dynamics shape near-term decisions. A process is not an event; it is a structured pattern of change over time that produces, maintains, transforms, or terminates entities, properties, or system states. Understanding processes is essential for strategy, intervention design, risk management, and governance in complex, changing environments.

## Why does Process Analysis matter for Hiddens work?

Process dynamics create multiple families of visibility failures. **Temporal hiddens** arise when the time-structure of a process is invisible: early-warning signals are dismissed as noise; crises appear sudden but were telegraphed months before; late interventions fail because tipping points have already been crossed. **Systemic hiddens** emerge when non-linear feedback and thresholds are mismodelled as linear trends: forecasts fail; interventions trigger unexpected backlash; cascade failures appear to come from nowhere. **Informational hiddens** cluster when evidence is fragmented across time or silos: practitioners "see" different phases because they sample at different cadences or access different evidence streams. **Relational hiddens** hide process dynamics that operate through network structures: spread of contagion; synchronisation across apparently independent domains; distributed coupling that no single observer can perceive. This framework surfaces these hiddens by forcing explicit process typing, dynamic diagnosis, timeline reconstruction, and tipping-point identification.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises process analysis through six core elements: (1) a **taxonomy of 30 process types** across six meta-categories (origination, persistence, development, transformation, termination, coupling/propagation) that recur across contexts; (2) **five cross-cutting dimensions** (temporality, continuity, directionality, agency, scale) that profile any process and enable discrimination between superficially similar dynamics; (3) **five dynamic patterns** (amplification, dampening, cascade, oscillation, perturbation) that describe how processes evolve and enable anticipation of tipping points; (4) **three interface types** (transition, synchronisation, competition) that map where processes interact with high consequence; (5) **a tiered Hiddens analysis protocol** that surfaces characteristic blindspots (static bias, category neglect, timescale mismatch, linear assumption) and connects to the full Hiddens framework; and (6) **a six-step application protocol** that moves from process mapping through dimensional characterisation, dynamic analysis, interface identification, Hiddens checking, and integration into decision/intervention workflows. The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests, and Residual Unknowns) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation loop.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework whenever a scenario involves change over extended time, non-linear dynamics, or questions about timing, tipping points, and irreversibility. It is most valuable in three contexts: **(1) strategy and foresight** (what trajectory is the system on, and what would alter it?), **(2) intervention design** (when is the window open/closed, and what phase should we target?), and **(3) risk and resilience** (where are the tipping points, what are the cascade channels, and what early warnings exist?). The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch process typing and interface spotting) and Investigative Run Mode (deep dimensional profiling, dynamic stress testing, full Hiddens scanning). Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: assign process types, profile one or two dimensions, spot obvious interfaces, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, process dynamics are contested, you are approaching a transition threshold, or interventions are expected to reorient the trajectory. For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Reality is dynamic: entities come into being, persist by doing work, develop capabilities and structure, transform under pressure and opportunity, and sometimes degrade, collapse, or disappear. Analytical error is often a temporal error: treating processes as if they were states, treating trajectories as if they were points, or extrapolating linearly where the underlying dynamics are non-linear.

The Framework of Processes operationalises "change over time" as a structured object of analysis. It provides: a **taxonomy** for naming what kind of change you are looking at (origination, persistence, development, transformation, termination, and coupling/propagation); **dimensions** for describing how that change operates (temporal structure, continuity, directionality, agency, and scale); **dynamics** for recognising recurrent time-patterns (amplification, dampening, cascade, oscillation, perturbation); and **interfaces** for analysing where processes meet and interact (transition, synchronisation, competition).

Used properly, the framework shifts analysis from "What is happening?" to "What process is producing this, what will it do next, and what would need to change for the trajectory to change?" It enables practitioners to anticipate tipping points, detect early warnings, design interventions at the right phase, and avoid the cascade and timing errors that derail otherwise sound strategies.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals
- **Definition of Process**: A structured pattern of change over time that produces, maintains, transforms, or terminates entities, properties, relations, or system states.
- **In scope**:
  - Entity lifecycles (birth → development → transformation/decline → termination)
  - Stability-maintaining activity (maintenance, regulation, reproduction, cycles)
  - Non-linear change (tipping points, cascades, collapse)
  - Multi-scale processes (micro/meso/macro; cross-scale propagation)
  - Coupling between processes (synchronisation, diffusion/contagion, feedback loops)
- **Out of scope**:
  - Purely static classification (use Framework of Realities when time is not material)
  - Purely normative decision rules (use Framework of Decisions for action design)
  - Purely evidentiary disputes about "what happened" (use Framework of Evidence to adjudicate sources)
- **Common confusions ("Process is not …")**:
  - "a single event" (events can be snapshots or punctuation within a process)
  - "mere motion" (process implies an intelligible dynamic, not arbitrary change)
  - "always gradual" (many processes are discontinuous, punctuated, or thresholded)
  - "always agent-driven" (many processes are emergent or structurally constrained)

# 2. Meta-Categories of Processes

## 2.1 Meta-Categories Table (mandatory)
| Meta-category | Core question | Primary focus | Typical artefacts/examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| I. Origination Processes | How something comes into existence | Founding events; genesis stories; emergence analysis; branching trees; formation thresholds |
| II. Persistence Processes | How something remains and reproduces through time | Maintenance budgets; stability controls; homeostatic loops; renewal cycles; replication pathways |
| III. Development Processes | How capabilities/structure grow and mature | Growth curves; capability roadmaps; differentiation maps; integration architectures; learning loops |
| IV. Transformation Processes | How something reconfigures into a different regime | Phase-change indicators; adaptation plans; evolutionary dynamics; metamorphosis narratives; revolutionary breaks |
| V. Termination Processes | How something degrades, dissolves, collapses, or ends | Wear/decay models; dissolution pathways; extinction thresholds; collapse cascades; absorption/assimilation plans |
| VI. Coupling & Propagation Processes | How processes spread, coordinate, and mutually shape each other | Synchronisation maps; diffusion/contagion models; feedback diagrams; coupling matrices; co-evolution timelines |

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Origination Processes
Processes that generate new entities, properties, roles, institutions, or regimes. These are characterised by novelty with identifiable boundary/identity, formation thresholds, first-instance conditions, and branching of trajectories. A common failure mode is back-projecting today's structure onto the origin, missing formative constraints and lock-ins.

### II. Persistence Processes
Processes that keep entities/regimes in existence, including renewal and replication. These are characterised by steady-state variables, maintenance work, regulation against perturbations, recurring cycles, and reproduction mechanisms. A common failure mode is treating persistence as passive ("it continues by default"), missing the work/resources required for continuity.

### III. Development Processes
Processes that increase capacity, complexity, specialisation, coordination, and learning. These are characterised by capability accumulation, functional differentiation, integration across subsystems, maturation plateaus, and experience-driven improvement. A common failure mode is equating "more" with "better" or assuming indefinite growth, ignoring developmental ceilings and trade-offs.

### IV. Transformation Processes
Processes that change regime, structure, or form—sometimes abruptly—while producing a new mode of operation. These are characterised by discontinuities, thresholds, reconfiguration of feedback loops, adaptation to new environments, and selection pressures. A common failure mode is linear extrapolation through a regime change, misdiagnosing a phase shift as incremental variation.

### V. Termination Processes
Processes that reduce function, dissolve structure, eliminate lineages, or abruptly end regimes. These are characterised by increasing fragility, declining returns on maintenance, loss of coherence, cascade vulnerability, and irreversibility. A common failure mode is denial and late recognition, confusing reversible degradation with irreversible collapse/extinction.

### VI. Coupling & Propagation Processes
Processes that connect trajectories across entities/systems, enabling spread, alignment, amplification, and mutual adaptation. These are characterised by correlated timing, network-mediated spread, feedback-driven acceleration, coupled constraints, and arms-race dynamics. A common failure mode is analysing entities in isolation, overlooking that "the process is in the network," not only in the focal object.

# 3. Core Types Taxonomy

## 3.0 Canonical Rule & Deviations
**Canonical baseline (series standard):** 6 meta-categories × 5 types = **30 core process types**.

**Source crosswalk:** The originating version of this framework used 5 meta-categories and 25 types (I–V). This rewrite preserves those 25 types as Types **1–25** and adds a sixth, series-standard category (VI) with Types **26–30** to capture process coupling/propagation explicitly and to align with the broader Analytical Series invariants.

**Mappability:** The mapping back to the 25-type baseline is direct: Baseline Categories I–V map one-to-one to Categories I–V below; Baseline types map one-to-one to Types 1–25; Category VI (Types 26–30) is additive (no conflicts with baseline types); it formalises dynamics already referenced via synchronisation/competition interfaces and diffusion-like behaviour.

|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | Preserve the 6×5 structure for cross-framework comparability whenever possible; propose additions as refinements under existing types before creating a new core type. |

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)
| Dynamic pattern | Description | Diagnostic cues | Analytic use / implications |
|---|---|---|---|
| Amplification | Small initial changes escalate into large effects through reinforcing feedback. | Rapid acceleration; superlinear growth; "takeoff" after a threshold; runaway behaviour | Prioritise early intervention; identify and break reinforcing loops; monitor leading indicators for takeoff. |
| Dampening | Large disturbances are absorbed; stabilising feedback reduces variance and restores a range. | Quick recovery after shocks; bounded oscillations; resistance to change | Identify buffers and control loops; avoid interventions that disable stabilisers; locate conditions where dampening fails. |
| Cascade | A sequence of dependent changes triggers downstream changes (domino effects). | Ordered chain reactions; dependency graphs lighting up; failure spreading along links | Map critical dependencies; insert circuit-breakers; anticipate second/third-order effects. |

| Perturbation | Shocks push the system away from a stable trajectory; responses range from recovery to transformation/collapse. | Spikes, disruptions, discontinuities; temporary vs permanent shifts; stress responses | Use stress testing; distinguish resilience vs fragility; decide when to stabilise, adapt, or allow controlled transformation. |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Hiddens Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Static Bias | Treating a moving system as a snapshot; inferring stable properties from transient states; missing the work of persistence. | Force a trajectory view: "past → present → plausible futures"; identify the active maintenance/regulation processes; add time-series or process-trace evidence. |
| 2 | Category Neglect | Analysing change using only one category (often development) while ignoring termination, transformation, or coupling. | Run a full 6-category scan; ask "What would have to be true for this to be degradation/collapse?" and "What coupling processes are active?" |
| 3 | Timescale Mismatch | Sampling, forecasting, or intervening at the wrong temporal frequency; missing cycles and leading indicators. | Estimate process tempo; match sampling rate to dynamics; look for phase, lag, and delay; instrument leading indicators. |
| 4 | Linear Assumption | Extrapolating linearly through thresholded or feedback-driven systems; missing tipping points and cascades. | Identify feedback loops, thresholds, and resource constraints; stress test with non-linear scenarios; track regime-shift indicators. |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)
| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms in process analysis | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| I. Perceptual | Observations are partial; fast dynamics outpace attention | "We never saw it coming"; missing weak signals; over-reliance on headline metrics | Sampling audit; increase cadence; triangulate sensors/sources; fresh-eyes review |
| II. Systemic | Models simplify; key feedbacks/constraints omitted | Forecast errors; interventions backfire; unexplained oscillations | Alternative model comparison; feedback-loop mapping; sensitivity analysis; delay identification |
| III. Informational | Incentives filter what is reported/recorded | Missing process-trace evidence; asymmetric access; politicised narratives of change | Source-independence check; access audit; incentive review; cross-silo evidence integration |
| IV. Temporal | History embeds constraints and lags; path dependence is hidden | "Sudden" collapse with long precursor; lock-in; irreversibility surprises | Process tracing; timeline reconstruction; leading-indicator search; hysteresis tests |
| V. Relational | Position/role determines visibility; network processes are opaque | Local accounts conflict; spread dynamics misunderstood; coupling ignored | Multi-position inquiry; network/topology mapping; boundary-spanning interviews |
| VI. Ontological | The wrong "thing" is being tracked; categories mis-specified | Mistaking a regime change for noise; conflating entity identity across metamorphosis | Boundary tests; redefine entity/unit of analysis; taxonomy check; run Type 19 (Metamorphosis) and Type 25 (Absorption) sanity checks |

## 8.3 Tier 2 Hiddens Crosswalk (structured deepening)
Use the dedicated Framework of Hiddens to deepen process analysis when:
- evidence is sparse or strategically filtered
- the system is complex/adversarial
- you suspect hidden thresholds, hidden coupling, or hidden maintenance work

**Default mapping heuristics (quick prompts):**
- If "sudden" events appear, assume a **Temporal hidden** and search for long build-up (Types 21–24).
- If interventions backfire, assume a **Systemic hidden** (feedback loops, delays) and re-map dynamics (Section 5).
- If spread is mispredicted, assume **Relational/Informational hiddens** and model topology and incentives (Types 26–30).
- If the entity seems to "change identity," assume **Ontological hidden** and test metamorphosis/absorption boundaries (Types 19, 25).

## 8.4 Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scans (integration with OG §7.5)
When Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate targeted scan from Operating Guide v2.5 §7.5.2:
- **Temporal family** (4 scans): latency, hidden phases, false suddenness, lock-in reversibility
- **Structural/Systemic family** (3 scans): feedback loop opacity, threshold hiding, multi-equilibria
- **Relational family** (3 scans): network-mediated spread, synchronisation, distributed coupling
- **Social/Organisational family** (3 scans): incentive-driven filtering, narrative distortion, scale-crossing blindness

Use the **Triage Matrix** (OG v2.5 §7.5.3) to match symptoms to the most relevant scan.

## 8.5 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) Run the 6-category Hiddens scan and record candidate hidden drivers of change.
2) For the likely hidden items, characterise: mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence, and reversibility.
3) Map hidden–process interactions: which core process types are being hidden, and through which interface type (A/B/C) they act.
4) Re-run the four hiddens source analysis checks and record residual unknowns with monitoring triggers.

## 8.6 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full Hiddens run)
Escalate to a full Hiddens protocol when:
- consequences are high (safety, regulatory, systemic risk)
- the environment is adversarial or secrecy-laden
- stakeholders disagree persistently on "what is changing"
- interventions repeatedly fail, oscillate, or trigger cascades

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms; recommended; mandatory for "Stable" status)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary
- **Process**: A patterned change over time that produces, maintains, transforms, or terminates entities/properties/relations.
- **Regime**: A relatively stable mode of organisation governed by a characteristic set of rules/feedbacks.
- **Trajectory**: The path a process takes through time (often with branches and phase shifts).
- **Tipping point / threshold**: A condition under which small changes produce a regime shift (often via feedback).
- **Feedback loop**: A causal loop where outputs feed back as inputs; can amplify or stabilise.
- **Homeostasis**: Stability via active regulation around setpoints under varying conditions.
- **Path dependence**: Early conditions/choices constrain later possibilities; switching paths becomes costly.
- **Hysteresis**: The system's response depends on history; returning inputs to prior levels does not restore prior states.
- **Interface (process)**: A point/zone where processes meet, transition, synchronise, or compete.
- **Timescale mismatch**: Using an observation or intervention cadence that cannot resolve the process's temporal structure.

# Appendices (optional but recommended)

## Appendix A: Process Diagnostic Prompts (worksheet-ready)
1) **Lifecycle:** What is the focal entity/regime? What counts as "birth," "maturity," and "end" for this thing?
2) **Persistence work:** What must be continuously done for it to keep existing (maintenance, regulation, renewal)?
3) **Branch points:** Where could the trajectory fork? What would make each branch more likely?
4) **Non-linearity:** Where are the thresholds, feedback loops, or resource constraints?
5) **Coupling:** What does this process synchronise with, diffuse through, compete against, or co-evolve with?
6) **Timing:** What is the correct sampling frequency? What delays/lag structures matter?
7) **Failure modes:** What would degradation, dissolution, collapse, or absorption look like in early signals?
8) **Hiddens:** What would you bet is hidden—and how would you detect it?

## Appendix B: Minimal Evidence Requirements (quick checklist)
- Time-stamped observations or trace data spanning at least one characteristic process cycle (or enough duration to falsify cyclicity).
- Clear entity boundary definition and versioning (to avoid ontological drift across metamorphosis/absorption).
- Identification of at least one leading indicator for the dominant dynamic pattern.
- Explicit statement of reversibility assumptions and what would falsify them.
- Baseline or historical reference case for process-type comparison.
- Independent corroboration of timing claims from multiple sources or methods.
