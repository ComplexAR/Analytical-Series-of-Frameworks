# Introduction

## What is Beliefs?

Beliefs are the epistemic structures through which agents — individuals, teams, organisations, and institutions — constitute their working picture of the world. They are not merely information held passively: beliefs determine what is noticed, what counts as evidence, who is trusted as a source, what actions are considered possible, and what is treated as inviolable. A belief is a propositional attitude: an agent's holding-as-true of content — descriptive, causal, normative, predictive, or self-referential — that shapes attention, inference, and action, whether or not the content is accurate or explicitly acknowledged.

In practice, belief failures often masquerade as technical, factual, or motivational failures. An organisation that "knows" a strategy is failing but continues with it; a team that individually doubts a shared assumption but never voices the doubt; a leader who states one belief while consistently acting on another. These are not failures of evidence or information access — they are failures of belief architecture. Understanding what beliefs are operative, how they formed, and what mechanisms protect them from revision is therefore central to diagnosis, intervention, and risk work.

## Why does Beliefs matter for Hiddens work?

This framework surfaces multiple families of hiding mechanisms. **Espoused-operative divergence** (Hidden-26: Operative Belief vs Espoused Belief) occurs when agents' stated beliefs differ from the beliefs actually governing their behaviour, creating a systematic gap between what leadership hears and what agents do. **Identity-protective suppression** (Hidden-11: Identity-Fused Belief) arises when beliefs become fused with personal or group identity, triggering defensive rather than deliberative responses to challenge, and making certain realities literally unthinkable. **Collective false consensus** (Hidden-19, 20: Pluralistic Ignorance and Groupthink) masks individual private doubt behind assumed majority agreement, often sustained by fear of social isolation or retaliation.

**Paradigmatic filtering** (Hidden-25: Paradigmatic Filtering; cross-reference Hiddens type 28 Paradigm Boundary) operates when background theoretical commitments determine what counts as admissible evidence, structuring not just what is believed but what can be believed. **Self-sustaining absence** occurs when beliefs cannot be named or challenged because acknowledging them would breach psychological safety or group loyalty. **Belief perseverance** (Hidden-13: Perseverance Belief) allows entrenched beliefs to survive — and sometimes strengthen — even after their evidential basis is completely discredited.

Without this framework, these Hiddens remain latent: visible only in patterns of inaction, failed interventions, or cascading failures. With the framework, belief architecture becomes an auditable, testable domain of inquiry.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises belief classification through six core elements: (1) a taxonomy of thirty belief types organised into six meta-categories that recur across contexts, allowing rapid classification and comparison; (2) five cross-cutting dimensions (scope, entrenchment, source, confidence, expressibility) that profile any belief and explain why different agents classify the same reality differently; (3) ten dynamics patterns (escalation, crystallisation, pluralisation, drift, transformation, and others) describing how beliefs form, entrench, and shift over time; (4) three interface types (formation, expression, governance) that make visible where and how beliefs interact with decisions, action, and collective sensemaking; (5) Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans that surface the systematic sources of belief-based visibility failure; (6) a six-step application protocol that moves from belief surfacing through typing, profiling, interface mapping, Hiddens checking, and integration handoff.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow. It is designed for repeated use: beliefs are classified at scenario intake, dimensions are profiled during investigation, dynamics are tracked as situations evolve, and re-profiling occurs at scheduled intervals or when interventions fail to produce expected effects.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework early when belief entrenchment, espoused-operative divergence, or collective distortion is suspected; during major decision gates (to validate whether our shared mental models still hold); and when interventions fail to produce expected effects (often a signal of underlying belief architecture problems). The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch belief triage and espoused-operative scan) and Investigative Run Mode (deep dimensional profiling, full belief dynamics mapping, comprehensive Hiddens scanning, and intervention design).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: surface key operative beliefs, profile one or two dimensions, spot obvious espoused-operative gaps, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, identity-protective dynamics are active, belief entrenchment is entrenched, or collective distortion is suspected. The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all belief claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording disagreements, uncertainties, and residual unknowns rather than collapsing them into false consensus.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario and the Operating Guide, producing a disciplined belief profile with explicit Hiddens, F/I/A/U tagging, and bounded closure.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Beliefs are the epistemic structures through which agents — individuals, teams, organisations, and institutions — constitute their working picture of the world. They shape attention, inference, action, and what is treated as inviolable. The Framework of Beliefs provides a repeatable vocabulary for identifying, classifying, and profiling the beliefs operating in any situation. Its primary concern is not what is believed — that is partly the domain of Evidence, Values, and Realities — but how: through what formation mechanisms, with what degree of entrenchment, through what collective dynamics, and with what relationship to the actions agents actually take.

Three root mechanisms deserve particular attention throughout the framework. First, **Cognitive Dissonance** (the internal tension arising when an agent holds contradictory beliefs, or when belief and action conflict) is the primary driver of belief maintenance through rationalisation — it explains why agents who encounter disconfirming evidence often reinforce rather than revise their beliefs. Second, **Identity-Protective Cognition** (the mechanism by which beliefs fused with personal or group identity become immune to evidential challenge, triggering defensive rather than deliberative responses) explains why interventions attacking beliefs directly often backfire, strengthening rather than loosening entrenchment. Third, **Epistemic Trust** (the network of source-credibility relationships through which most beliefs are formed by accepting what trusted sources say, rather than by direct evidence evaluation) explains why evidence presented by a distrusted source rarely updates beliefs, and why discrediting a belief's source can be more effective than discrediting its evidential basis.

The Framework of Beliefs deliberately avoids duplicating what is already owned by adjacent frameworks. The Framework of Failures owns the downstream manifestations of belief failure — Confirmation Bias (type 21), Motivated Reasoning (type 25), Availability Bias (type 23), Hindsight Bias (type 15) — as analytical error modes; the Framework of Beliefs owns the root mechanisms that generate them. The Framework of Culture & Norms owns the social enforcement of belief expression, including Silence Spirals; the Framework of Beliefs cross-references these as collective belief dynamics rather than duplicating them. The Framework of Values owns normative beliefs about what matters, including Sacred Values as a values type; the Framework of Beliefs captures Sacred/Inviolable Belief as a belief-entrenchment mechanism rather than a normative category. These boundaries are explicit and maintained throughout.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Core definitions:**
- **Belief (operational):** a propositional attitude — an agent's holding-as-true of a content (descriptive, causal, normative, predictive, or self-referential) — that shapes attention, inference, and action, whether or not the content is accurate or explicitly acknowledged.
- **Espoused Belief:** a belief an agent states when asked; may or may not correspond to the operative belief governing behaviour.
- **Operative Belief (Theory-in-Use):** the belief that actually governs an agent's behaviour, often tacit and detectable only through behavioural evidence and patterns of action.
- **Cognitive Dissonance:** internal tension arising when an agent holds contradictory beliefs, or when belief and action conflict; primary driver of belief maintenance through rationalisation.
- **Identity-Protective Cognition:** mechanism by which beliefs fused with personal or group identity become immune to evidential challenge, triggering defensive rather than deliberative responses.
- **Epistemic Trust:** the network of source-credibility relationships through which most beliefs are formed by accepting what trusted sources say, rather than by direct evidence evaluation.

**In scope:**
- Six meta-categories and thirty core belief types (formation, content, entrenchment, collective dynamics, evidence interface, belief-action relationship)
- Five dimensions for profiling any belief (scope, entrenchment, source, confidence, expressibility)
- Ten belief dynamics (patterns of formation, resistance, collective distortion, drift, and revision)
- Three interface types (formation, expression, governance)
- Hiddens sources and tiered Hiddens cross-check
- Application protocol and standard deliverables
- Explicit integration with Failures, Culture & Norms, Values, and Hiddens frameworks

**Out of scope (by design):**
- A complete epistemological theory (the framework supports plural epistemologies)
- Full cognitive science or neuroscience of belief (use as inputs where available; do not treat as required)
- Full catalogue of interventions and governance mechanisms (use Interventions, Governance, Responsibility, Learning & Adaptation frameworks)
- Belief content evaluation (whether specific beliefs are true or justified) — that is the domain of Evidence and Diagnosis

**Common confusions:**
- Belief ≠ value (a value is a normative structure of mattering; a belief is a propositional attitude about what is the case — though normative beliefs bridge both)
- Belief ≠ preference (preferences concern ordering of options; beliefs concern what is held as true)
- Espoused belief ≠ operative belief (the framework insists on distinguishing these; treating stated beliefs as operative is the most common analytical error)
- Collective belief ≠ aggregated individual belief (collective belief mechanisms produce outcomes distinct from individual sums)
- Entrenchment ≠ error (entrenched beliefs may be correct; the framework analyses the mechanism, not just the accuracy)

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (recommended)

### I. Belief Formation
Beliefs originate through distinct mechanisms: testimonial acceptance of what trusted sources say; direct perceptual and experiential contact; deliberate reasoning (deduction, induction, abduction); mapping of analogical precedents; and affective states serving as epistemic inputs. Formation mechanisms differ in their acquisition pathways and subsequent susceptibility to revision. Testimonial beliefs depend heavily on epistemic trust; experiential beliefs may show differential clarity and resistance based on embodiment and pattern recognition; inferential beliefs depend on reasoning quality and background assumptions; analogical beliefs depend on appropriate mapping to base case; affect-based beliefs may be driven by emotional states rather than propositional content. Understanding formation mechanisms is critical for belief revision strategies: attacking the same mechanism by which a belief was formed (e.g., targeting the trusted source for testimonial beliefs) may be more effective than evidence provision alone.

### II. Belief Content
Beliefs vary in what they claim: descriptive beliefs assert how the world currently is; causal beliefs assert what causes what and how interventions work; normative beliefs assert what ought to be; predictive beliefs assert future states and probabilities; self-referential beliefs assert claims about oneself (identity, capabilities, role). Different content types trigger different revision constraints: descriptive beliefs can in principle be tested empirically; causal beliefs can be challenged through counterfactual analysis or controlled tests; normative beliefs activate identity and values; predictive beliefs can be evaluated through hindsight; self-referential beliefs activate identity and self-concept. Mixing content types (e.g., smuggling normative claims into descriptive frames, or self-referential threats into ostensibly factual disagreements) is a common source of belief entrenchment.

### III. Belief Entrenchment
Entrenchment captures how resistant a belief is to revision and through what mechanisms. Identity-fused beliefs become merged with personal or group identity, triggering defensive rather than deliberative responses. Dissonance-resolved beliefs are maintained through rationalisation of internal contradiction. Perseverance beliefs persist despite complete evidential discrediting. Pre-theoretical commitments are background paradigmatic assumptions constraining what can be believed prior to evidence evaluation. Sacred or inviolable beliefs are treated as absolutely non-negotiable and exempt from ordinary trade-off reasoning. Understanding entrenchment mechanisms informs intervention strategy: identity-protective beliefs require identity-safe contexts and reframing; perseverance beliefs may require belief exposure or paradigm-shift events; pre-theoretical commitments may need paradigm-challenge sessions or option-expansion work.

### IV. Collective Belief
Individual beliefs aggregate, but collective beliefs operate by distinct mechanisms. Shared mental models are tacit collective beliefs about how the system or world works, held by group members with mutual recognition that others hold them too. Common knowledge structures operate through iterated mutual recognition (everyone knows that everyone knows that...). Institutional beliefs are encoded in procedures, rules, and structures rather than held by individuals. Pluralistic ignorance produces false collective consensus when each member privately rejects a belief while assuming most others accept it. Collective homogenisation and groupthink emerge through conformity pressure and suppressed dissent. These mechanisms differ fundamentally from aggregated individual beliefs and produce visibility failures (Hiddens) that individual-level analysis misses.

### V. Belief-Evidence Interface
This meta-category captures the mechanisms governing how beliefs relate to evidence and new information. Epistemic Trust structures the network of source-credibility relationships determining whose testimony is believed. Confirmatory Processing produces selective evidence uptake supporting existing beliefs (the root mechanism of Confirmation Bias). Motivated Skepticism applies asymmetric evidential standards based on preferred conclusions. Epistemic Vigilance monitors source reliability and claim quality actively. Paradigmatic Filtering uses background theoretical frameworks to determine what counts as admissible evidence. Understanding these mechanisms explains why evidence provision alone rarely updates entrenched beliefs and why source credibility often matters more than evidence content.

### VI. Belief-Action Relationship
This meta-category focuses on the gap between stated and operative beliefs and the mechanisms maintaining both. Operative beliefs (theory-in-use) are those actually governing behaviour, often tacit. Espoused beliefs are those stated on request and may differ from operative beliefs (the espoused-operative divergence). Akratic beliefs are held beliefs consistently contradicted by action. Moral licensing produces cycles where past belief-consistent behaviour licenses subsequent deviation. Self-fulfilling beliefs generate the conditions confirming themselves through motivated action. This meta-category is critical for Hiddens work because espoused-operative divergence is one of the most consequential and persistent visibility failures in institutions.

| 7 | Causal Belief | About what causes what; includes mechanism beliefs and intervention beliefs about how to produce desired effects | Agent asserts causal links ("X causes Y") or intervention claims ("to achieve Z, do W"); strength of belief affects willingness to intervene | Causal beliefs are action-critical; misspecified causal models produce failed interventions; reverse causality and confounding can embed false causal beliefs |
| 8 | Normative Belief | About what ought to be the case; prescriptive and value-laden rather than descriptive | Agent asserts moral, ethical, or prescriptive claims; fused with identity and values; triggers identity-protective response if challenged | Normative beliefs are typically identity-fused; mixing normative claims into ostensibly descriptive debates produces entrenchment; values conflicts often misidentified as factual disagreements |
| 9 | Predictive Belief | About future states, probabilities, and trajectories; confidence in predictions varies with domain and source | Agent asserts forecasts or probabilistic claims; belief affects planning, risk perception, resource allocation; prone to overconfidence or unwarranted pessimism | Predictions can be tested but only with lag; sunk costs in prediction-dependent plans can entrench beliefs despite track record; hindsight bias can distort belief in predictions |
| 10 | Self-Referential Belief | About oneself — capabilities, role, identity, status; strongly identity-fused and resistant to revision | Agent asserts claims about self-competence, role legitimacy, status, or group membership; threat triggers defensive response; affects perception and risk-taking | Self-referential beliefs are highly entrenched; challenge to self-belief is experienced as identity threat; can produce overconfidence or learned helplessness depending on valence |

## 3.3 Types (Category III: Belief Entrenchment)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | Identity-Fused Belief | Belief merged with personal or group identity; challenge triggers defensive rather than deliberative response | Agent experiences challenge to belief as identity threat; defensive response to disconfirming evidence; group loyalty enforces belief expression | Defensive responses can strengthen rather than weaken entrenchment; interventions attacking belief directly trigger backlash; requires identity-safe contexts to enable revision |
| 12 | Dissonance-Resolved Belief | Maintained through rationalisation of internal contradiction; cognitive dissonance is the primary driver of maintenance | Agent holds contradictory beliefs or acts contrary to stated belief but rationalises the contradiction; inconsistency is defended | Rationalisation can be elaborate and subtle; disconfirming evidence triggers rationalisation-strengthening rather than revision; requires safety to acknowledge dissonance |
| 13 | Perseverance Belief | Persists even after its evidential basis is completely discredited; shows remarkable resistance to evidence-based revision | Agent continues to hold belief despite learning that the supporting evidence was false; belief survives evidential elimination | Indicates strong entrenchment through non-evidential mechanisms; requires paradigm-shift events or complete belief replacement rather than evidential challenge |
| 14 | Pre-Theoretical Commitment | Background paradigmatic assumption constraining what can be believed, prior to and independently of evidence evaluation | Agent cannot conceive of alternatives to the commitment; commitment structures interpretation of evidence rather than being challenged by it; frames entire analytical space | Invisible to agent as a belief rather than "how things are"; shifts in pre-theoretical commitments are major cognitive events (paradigm shifts); can require perspective-taking or option-expansion work |
| 15 | Sacred / Inviolable Belief | Treated as absolutely non-negotiable and exempt from ordinary trade-off reasoning and evidential challenge | Agent experiences belief as non-negotiable; attempt to engage in trade-off reasoning or evidential challenge triggers moral outrage or sanctity violation | Sacred beliefs are resistant to rational persuasion; challenge triggers identity defense or moral outrage; requires identity-safe contexts and values-affirmation to enable reconsideration |

## 3.4 Types (Category IV: Collective Belief)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 16 | Shared Mental Model | Collective tacit belief about how the system, organisation, or world works, held with mutual recognition by group members | Group members nod in unison when model is expressed; decisions are made based on model without explicit debate; model is reproduced in team interactions | Invisibility of model until probed; change requires collective work rather than individual conversion; can lock group into outdated interpretation |
| 17 | Common Knowledge Structure | Belief operating through mutual recognition: everyone knows that everyone knows that X; operates at collective level differently than individual aggregation | Agent assumes mutual knowledge ("we all know X"); coordination depends on common knowledge; absent or mis-matched common knowledge produces failures | Detection requires comparing what individuals say they believe privately vs what they assume others believe; common knowledge gaps can produce failure even when individuals hold correct beliefs |
| 18 | Institutional Belief | Belief encoded in procedures, rules, structures, and artefacts rather than held as explicit proposition by individuals | Belief is instantiated in organisational forms, workflows, incentive structures; persists independent of any individual's personal belief | Institutional beliefs are hard to change because they are not held by anyone in particular; require structural change rather than persuasion; can create path dependency and lock-in |
| 19 | Pluralistic Ignorance | Each member of a group privately rejects a belief or norm while assuming that most others privately accept it; produces false collective consensus | Anonymous surveys reveal private rejection; public and private responses diverge; members express fear of being only dissenter | Produces collective inaction despite private agreement that action is needed; can entrench dysfunction because no one wants to be first to dissent; psychological safety is key intervention lever |
| 20 | Collective Belief Homogenisation | Convergence toward false consensus through conformity pressure and suppressed dissent; Groupthink / Abilene Paradox mechanism | Group expresses false unity; dissent is suppressed or self-censored; shared illusion of unanimity despite private doubts | Suppressed dissent can represent critical information loss; group becomes vulnerable to failure modes that individual diversity would catch; requires legitimacy of dissent and psychological safety |

## 3.5 Types (Category V: Belief-Evidence Interface)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 21 | Epistemic Trust Structure | Source-credibility network governing testimony-based belief formation; determines which sources are trusted and why | Agent's belief uptake depends on source credibility more than content; source capture or reputation damage affects belief; distrusted sources struggle to convince even with evidence | Source-based attack on beliefs may be more effective than evidence-based attack; source corruption or loss can collapse entire belief network; requires attention to source credibility maintenance |
| 22 | Confirmatory Processing | Selective evidence uptake supporting existing beliefs; the root mechanism of Confirmation Bias in Failures framework | Agent seeks evidence confirming existing belief; interprets ambiguous evidence as confirmatory; remembers confirmatory evidence more readily | Produces systematic selective attention and memory; evidence gaps masquerade as settled facts; can entrench beliefs despite contrary evidence; requires motivated exposure to disconfirming evidence |
| 23 | Motivated Skepticism | Asymmetric application of evidential standards based on preferred conclusions; root mechanism of Motivated Reasoning in Failures framework | Agent demands more proof for disliked conclusions than liked ones; shifts standards when evidence threatens preferred belief; defends against disconfirming evidence | Standards-shifting can be subtle and defended as rigour; makes dialogue difficult because evidence goalposts move; requires agreement on standards before evidence evaluation |
| 24 | Epistemic Vigilance | Active monitoring of source reliability and claim quality; can be triggered or suppressed by context and identity | Agent scrutinises sources and claims; asks probing questions; remains sceptical of claims that might be self-serving; baseline vigilance varies by person and domain | Can be suppressed by trust, identity alignment, or authority; over-vigilance can produce paranoia or analysis paralysis; requires calibration to domain and source |
| 25 | Paradigmatic Filtering | Background theoretical frameworks determine what counts as admissible evidence; structures not just what is believed but what can be believed | Agent rejects certain evidence as "not real evidence" or "methodologically invalid"; framework determines what questions are even askable; evidence outside framework is invisible | Framework filters become invisible to framework inhabitants; require paradigm exposure or perspective-taking to recognise; shifts in paradigmatic filtering are major cognitive events |

## 3.6 Types (Category VI: Belief-Action Relationship)
| # | Type | Description | Diagnostic cues | Failure / hidden-risk signature |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 26 | Operative Belief (Theory-in-Use) | Belief actually governing behaviour; often tacit and detectable only through behavioural evidence and patterns | Agent's behaviour reveals belief more reliably than self-report; consistent action pattern despite contrary espoused statement indicates operative belief | Espoused-operative divergence is one of the most persistent institutional failures; requires observation and behavioural analysis to surface; can hide intentional deception or unconscious contradiction |
| 27 | Espoused Belief | Belief stated on request; may or may not correspond to operative belief governing behaviour | Agent articulates belief when asked; response can be shaped by social desirability, identity, or desired self-presentation | Unreliable without triangulation to behaviour; can mask operative beliefs through self-deception or conscious concealment; requires careful questioning and anonymous channels to elicit honesty |
| 28 | Akratic Belief | Held belief consistently contradicted by action; agent knows what they believe but repeatedly acts otherwise | Agent expresses intention or belief ("I want to exercise") but behaviour contradicts ("I sedentary"); produces cognitive dissonance and rationalisation | Indicates either weak commitment despite strong expression, or operative belief divergence from espoused; can entrench through rationalisation; may require structural support (e.g., commitment devices) rather than belief change alone |
| 29 | Moral Licensing | Pattern by which past belief-consistent behaviour licenses subsequent deviation; produces cycles of virtue signalling followed by contrary behaviour | Agent engages in belief-consistent action, then uses it to justify contrary behaviour ("I've done enough X, so now I can do contrary Y"); self-aware or unconscious | Can sustain apparent commitment while enabling deviation; cycles can entrench false sense of integrity; requires attention to cumulative pattern rather than individual actions |
| 30 | Self-Fulfilling Belief | Generates the conditions confirming itself through the actions it motivates; belief about future or counterfactual becomes true because of the belief | Agent holds belief about capability or outcome; belief motivates actions that produce the believed outcome; or belief produces inaction that makes negative outcome true | Can entrench negative self-concepts or positive overconfidence depending on valence; can sustain dysfunctional patterns where belief prevents action that would disconfirm it; requires attention to causal loop |

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy (mandatory)
| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| Canonical baseline | 30 core types (6 meta-categories × 5 types) is the baseline. This framework adheres strictly to baseline. If domain-specific extensions are needed, they should be documented as a secondary taxonomy (Appendix D) rather than replacing the core 30. |
| Domain-specific refinement | Domain-specific extensions might break down Formation types into epistemic sources (e.g., distributed epistemic trust networks, institutional mediation of testimony); or Collective Belief types into group-scale variations (e.g., network effects, temporal lags in consensus formation). Document extensions as addenda, not replacements. |
| Preserving mappability | All extensions must map back to one of the six meta-categories and indicate which of the 30 core types they refine or extend. This preserves comparability with other investigations and with other frameworks. |
| Structural invariants | Do not break the 6 meta-category structure. Avoid creating a 7th meta-category unless new domain fundamentally requires it (e.g., discovering a new mechanism of belief formation or entrenchment not covered by the five). If you do extend, increment major version (v2.1 or v3.0 depending on scope). |
| Periodic reassessment | Review the 30 types every 12–18 months across 5–10 diverse investigations. If consistent gaps emerge in a category, consider whether core types need refinement or if domain-specific extension is sufficient. |
| Versioning & governance | Extensions are versioned at vX.Y where X is major (structure) and Y is minor (extensions). Governed by the series maintainer with consultation from domain experts. Changes to core taxonomy require unanimous agreement; extensions require consultation only. |

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Belief

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)
| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Scope | Breadth of domain covered by belief; from narrow (specific case) to wide (universal principle) | Narrow (single case, specific agent) ↔ Wide (universal, applies across agents/domains) | Ask: "Does this belief apply only here, or across many situations?" | Narrow-scope beliefs are easier to isolate and revise; wide-scope beliefs carry higher consequence and stronger entrenchment |
| 2. Entrenchment | Resistance to revision; from fluid (easily updated) to rigid (impervious to evidence/argument) | Fluid (updated when evidence changes) ↔ Rigid (persists despite disconfirming evidence) | Test: Does the agent update belief when evidence changes, or defend? Does identity threat increase defensiveness? | High entrenchment suggests identity fusion or pre-theoretical commitment; requires identity-safe interventions; low entrenchment suggests evidence-receptive agent |
| 3. Source | How the belief was acquired; testimonial, experiential, inferential, analogical, or affect-based | Testimonial ↔ Inferential / Experiential ↔ Analogical ↔ Affect-based | Ask: "How did you come to believe this? Through experience, reasoning, someone told you, emotional sense?" | Different sources suggest different revision strategies; testimonial beliefs vulnerable to source attack; inferential beliefs vulnerable to reasoning/premise challenge |
| 4. Confidence | Agent's self-assessed certainty about the belief; from uncertain to near-certain | Low confidence (uncertain, provisional) ↔ High confidence (certain, felt as settled) | Ask agent directly about confidence on 1–10 scale; observe whether agent expresses doubt or defends | High confidence without high evidence quality indicates entrenchment or affect-based formation; confidence gaps signal opportunities for revision or risk areas |
| 5. Expressibility | Ease of articulating the belief; from explicit and articulate to tacit and hard to articulate | Tacit (hard to articulate, embedded in action) ↔ Explicit (easily stated, verbally fluid) | Ask agent to articulate belief; note ease of expression and coherence of articulation | Tacit beliefs (operative beliefs, shared mental models) are hard to revise because they're not subject to deliberate scrutiny; surfacing tacit beliefs is first step to revision |

# 5. Dynamics (Patterns Over Time)

## 5.1 Dynamic Patterns Table (mandatory)
| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Formation cascades | Belief clusters form in rapid succession as agents adopt similar formation sources (e.g., shared epistemic trust in single source) | Epistemic trust network expansion; mimicry; institutional adoption | Rapid formation can create shared mental models but also shared vulnerabilities to source corruption; clustering suggests tight coupling in belief network | Diversify epistemic sources; rotate institutional carriers; maintain alternative explanations |
| 2 | Entrenchment acceleration | Beliefs become progressively more entrenched as identity fusion, dissonance resolution, and persistence mechanisms accumulate | Identity integration; rationalisation cycles; perseverance under challenge; motivated reasoning under threat | Accelerated entrenchment reduces revision window; entrenched beliefs generate Hiddens (espoused-operative divergence, defensive responses, paradigm boundaries) | Early attention to identity-protective dynamics; safe-to-challenge culture; periodic re-examination of pre-theoretical commitments |
| 3 | Collective false consensus formation | Group moves from private doubt (pluralistic ignorance) to expressed false consensus (groupthink) through conformity pressure and suppressed dissent | Fear of isolation; conformity pressure; leader signals; sanctions on dissent; psychological safety erosion | False consensus produces collective inaction despite individual agreement that action needed; masks critical dissenting information; erodes organisational learning | Legitimacy of dissent; anonymous belief surfacing; psychological safety work; dissent appointment roles |
| 4 | Espoused-operative divergence | Growing gap between stated beliefs and beliefs governing behaviour as agents experience pressure to express organisational line | Conformity pressure; identity threat; retaliation risk; incentive structure; power asymmetry | Divergence enables concealment of operative dissent; produces misalignment between stated strategy and actual implementation; generates Hiddens in form of shadow operations | Safe-to-challenge culture; anonymous feedback; incentive alignment; transparency of operative beliefs and decision logic |
| 5 | Belief drift | Beliefs shift slowly toward new equilibrium without a distinct triggering event; small degradations accumulate; norming of deviance | Accumulating small inconsistencies; incentives normalise deviation; monitoring gamed or ignored; reference point shift | Delayed recognition creates brittle systems; interventions become expensive and disruptive when noticed; can entrench false equilibrium as "new normal" | Leading indicators; periodic re-typing; independent audits; reduction of normalisation of deviance; renewal investments |
| 6 | Paradigm shift | Sudden reorganisation of dominant belief paradigm following accumulation of anomalies that paradigm cannot accommodate | Anomaly accumulation reaches criticality; alternative paradigm becomes available and credible; crisis event; champion emergence | Enables wholesale belief revision that incremental challenge could not achieve; high consequence for decision-making during transition | Paradigm exposure; legitimate alternative scenario exploration; learning from adjacent domains; psychological safety for paradigm questioning |
| 7 | Belief-action feedback | Belief generates action which produces outcome which reinforces belief; self-fulfilling prophecy or virtuous cycle | Self-fulfilling belief mechanism; confirmation bias in outcome interpretation; incentive structure supports interpretation | Can entrench false beliefs (negative self-concept → inaction → failure → reinforced self-concept) or sustain overconfidence (unrealistic positive expectation → overallocation → success despite suboptimal strategy) | Outcome attribution testing; disconfirming scenario planning; sunk cost awareness; periodic strategy reassessment independent of short-term success |
| 8 | Source capture and corruption | Belief network dependent on single epistemic source becomes vulnerable when source is corrupted, loses credibility, or is removed | Concentrated epistemic trust; source moves, dies, loses credibility, or is compromised; alternatives are not equally trusted | Can produce rapid wholesale belief revision when source is revealed as unreliable; or continued adherence despite source corruption if belief is now independent of source | Source diversification; epistemic trust redundancy; periodic source credibility audits; source replacement protocols; documented reasoning reducing testimony dependence |
| 9 | Identity-protective entrenchment escalation | Beliefs fused with identity trigger increasingly defensive responses as identity is threatened; defensive responses can strengthen rather than loosen entrenchment | Identity threat triggers defensive cognition; disconfirming evidence is reinterpreted as attack; defence strengthens belief through rationalisation | Can make belief revision progressively harder as identity stakes increase; interventions attacking belief directly backfire; requires identity-safe approaches | Identity-safe framing; values-affirmation interventions; perspective-taking; belief exposure in safe contexts; reframing of threat |
| 10 | Collective paradigm boundary formation | Group develops shared paradigm boundary where certain hypotheses or evidence types are outside the frame of admissible inquiry | Paradigm formation through shared education, priors, or institutional socialisation; boundary reinforcement through sanctions on boundary violation | Paradigm boundaries enable coherent group action but exclude consideration of alternatives; can be invisible to group members; shifts require perspective-taking | Paradigm exposure through interaction with adjacent paradigms; legitimate alternative scenario exploration; cross-boundary learning |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Espoused-Operative Divergence Blindness | Analysis takes stated beliefs at face value without testing against behavioural evidence; misses the beliefs actually governing action; assumes espoused = operative | Systematically contrast espoused statements with behavioural patterns; use theory-in-use interviews and observation; apply Operative Belief (type 26) typing; run Hiddens Tier 2 scan for espoused-operative gaps |
| 2 | Identity-Protective Entrenchment Misread as Factual Disagreement | Framework assumes disagreement is about facts when it is actually about identity threat; misses defensive cognition and identity-protective mechanisms | Probe identity relevance of beliefs; look for defensive language and affect escalation when belief is challenged; map identity-belief fusion; design identity-safe contexts for belief exploration |
| 3 | Collective Belief Aggregation Error | Assumes collective beliefs are sum of individual beliefs; misses Common Knowledge effects, Pluralistic Ignorance, and Groupthink; treats false consensus as real | Separately map individual private beliefs (anonymous) and expressed beliefs; look for gaps between private and public; apply Pluralistic Ignorance (type 19) and Collective Homogenisation (type 20) typing; run Tier 2 Hidden scan for collective distortion |
| 4 | Paradigm Boundary Invisibility | Fails to recognise background paradigmatic assumptions constraining what can be believed; assumes current frame is "how things are" not a choice | Expose paradigm through perspective-taking with paradigm outsiders; test admissibility rules ("what would count as evidence against this?"); apply Pre-Theoretical Commitment (type 14) typing; design paradigm-challenge sessions |
| 5 | Evidence-Insensitivity Mechanism Masquerading as Factual Error | Attributes belief resistance to ignorance when actual mechanism is Confirmatory Processing, Motivated Skepticism, or Paradigmatic Filtering | Test whether agent updates belief when evidence changes or defends belief despite evidence; map evidence standards and filter rules; apply Belief-Evidence Interface (meta-category V) types; identify selective attention patterns |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)
| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|
| I — Perceptual | Agents lack awareness of operative beliefs; operative beliefs are tacit and non-articulated; espoused-operative divergence is invisible | Stated beliefs differ from behaviour; agents appear unaware of how they actually decide; interviews reveal gap between intended and actual decision logic | Ask agents to articulate operative beliefs explicitly; observe decisions and behavioural patterns; conduct theory-in-use interview; compare stated decision criteria to actual decision outcomes |
| II — Systemic | Institutional structures embody beliefs that are not held by any individual but are instantiated in procedures and incentive structures | Beliefs are "how the system works"; individuals comply without believing but structure persists; changing individual minds doesn't change outcomes | Map institutional beliefs in procedures, incentive structures, role definitions; identify structural carriers of beliefs; note what structures would need to change for belief change to stick |
| III — Informational | Belief formation and confirmation depend on selective evidence uptake; Confirmatory Processing, Motivated Skepticism, or Paradigmatic Filtering actively suppresses disconfirming information | Agents seek confirming evidence; ignore or reinterpret disconfirming evidence; defend beliefs despite contrary information; different agents see "same facts" as supporting opposite beliefs | Test evidence uptake directly; present disconfirming evidence and observe response; map evidence filters and standards; identify selective attention and memory patterns |
| IV — Temporal | Beliefs entrenched through historical accumulation, sunk costs, or temporal lag between belief formation and current reality; new realities not triggering re-belief | Beliefs based on outdated information; temporal lags between reality change and belief update; past events continue to shape current beliefs; beliefs persist despite trajectory change | Compare belief timing to reality timing; look for historical event origins of current beliefs; test whether beliefs track current state or previous state; check for temporal drift without re-typing |
| V — Relational | Beliefs maintained through social enforcement, epistemic trust networks, or power asymmetries; dissent is suppressed through conformity pressure or fear of retaliation | Beliefs appear stable across different contexts but are actually externally maintained; anonymous expression shows different beliefs than public expression; power-laden contexts show more conformity | Compare public and private expressions of beliefs (anonymous surveys); map epistemic trust networks and source credibility; identify conformity pressure and retaliation risks; assess psychological safety |
| VI — Ontological | Paradigm boundaries make certain beliefs unthinkable or certain hypotheses inadmissible; background ontological commitments constrain belief space | Certain questions are not asked because they are considered "off the table"; agents cannot conceive of alternatives to fundamental assumptions; paradigm boundaries are invisible to inhabitants | Expose paradigm through interaction with alternative paradigms; test whether hypotheses are evaluated or rejected a priori; probe admissibility rules; design paradigm-challenge and perspective-taking sessions |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
<!--
Aligned with OG v2.5 §7.2 (sections 7.2.1–7.2.7). Steps 1–4 are the core Micro-Protocol; steps 5–7 extend for full framework execution.
-->
1) **Run Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories** (early pass; assume Hiddens exist). For each meta-category, ask: "Why might this Hiddens source be active here? What symptoms would indicate it?" Record findings in Tier 1 scan table (§8.2).

2) **Shortlist candidate Hiddens**; for each, rate: mechanism (how is it produced/maintained), reducibility (how fixable), detectability (how observable), agency (who maintains it), consequence (what impact). Use Tier 2 crosswalk table (§8.3) to develop working model for each candidate.

3) **Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface** per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains (Type C in Tier 2 table). Detection interface specifies how to surface the Hidden; remediation interface specifies how to address it.

4) **Run the Hiddens source analysis** (§8.1) and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens in Hiddens Register (standard register at §1.6.6). Explicitly mark which Hiddens remain opaque and why.

5) **If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan** (OG v2.5, §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3). Standard Targeted Scans that commonly apply to Framework of Beliefs: (a) **Belief Persistence Scan** (Family A, Scan 4) — when entrenchment, perseverance, or identity-protective dynamics are strong; (b) **Positional Blindness Scan** (Family B, Scan 3) — when belief systems are locked to agent role or power position; (c) **Paradigm Boundary Scan** (Family D, Scan 5, related to Ontological Hiddens VI) — when pre-theoretical commitments or paradigm boundaries are limiting option space.

6) **After executing the framework protocol and any available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1** (pre-closure pass) and note deltas. Have new Hiddens emerged? Have candidate Hiddens been reduced or disconfirmed? Record changes explicitly.

7) **Produce Residual Unknowns** (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and **apply the Escalation Rule** (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or remediation priorities. **Produce closure artefacts per OG v2.5, §7.4:** (1) residual unknowns list, (2) acceptability rationale, (3) monitoring plan, (4) ownership assignment, (5) learning hook.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)
Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- **High consequence** (safety, regulatory, existential, severe harm potential; strategy/transformation at risk; precedent-setting decision)
- **Adversarial context** / strategic concealment likely (active deception; power-driven suppression; retaliation risk for truth-telling)
- **Persistent disagreement** or inconsistent evidence (multiple stakeholders fundamentally disagree on operative beliefs; evidence contradictory)
- **Repeat failures** despite prior interventions (belief-driven failures recurring; prior belief-change attempts unsuccessful)
- **Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement** across hidden types (belief entrenchment reinforced by power, incentives, identity, and culture together; multi-factor lock-in)
- **Decision-critical residual unknowns** (closure would be false; Unknowns could change decisions, accountability, or remediation)

**Tier 3 execution (per OG v2.5, §D.6.10):**
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into full Hiddens taxonomy (all 30+ Hiddens types and mechanisms across G5 Epistemics framework)
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements for claims, more explicit competing hypotheses, more rigorous disconfirming tests
- When adversarial intent is plausible: adds concealment vectors and independent corroboration paths; cross-checks for coordinated deception
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree (OG §4.3, branch A6 high-consequence escalation)
- Produces more detailed Residual Unknowns and stronger escalation recommendation if material Unknowns remain

- Espoused-operative divergence expansion (frequency, scope, consequence)

- **Review cadence:** Re-assess beliefs at scenario transitions (situation type change, major decision point, intervention failure, new evidence); minimum annual re-typing for high-consequence belief domains

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Operative Belief Fidelity | Surface operative beliefs (those governing behaviour) in addition to espoused beliefs (those stated on request). Espoused-operative divergence is a systematic visibility failure; claiming to understand beliefs without this distinction is incomplete. |
| 2. Identity-Protective Dynamics Awareness | Attend to identity-protective cognition and defensive responses when beliefs are challenged. Interventions attacking beliefs directly often backfire; identity-safe contexts enable revision. Distinguish principled disagreement from identity threat responses. |
| 3. Collective Belief Mechanism Distinctness | Treat collective beliefs as operating by mechanisms distinct from aggregated individual beliefs. Pluralistic Ignorance, Groupthink, and Shared Mental Models are not sums of individual beliefs; require separate analysis and intervention. |
| 4. Non-Evaluation of Belief Content | Analyse how beliefs form, entrench, and spread without evaluating whether specific beliefs are true. Truth evaluation is the domain of Evidence and Diagnosis frameworks. This framework analyses mechanism, not content. |
| 5. Hiddens Integration Discipline | Integrate belief analysis with Hiddens work systematically. Beliefs are a primary source of Hiddens (espoused-operative divergence, identity-protective suppression, paradigm boundaries, collective false consensus). Every belief analysis should include Tier 1 Hiddens scanning. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Akratic Belief | A belief held by an agent that is consistently contradicted by the agent's behaviour; produces cognitive dissonance and rationalisation. |
| Affect-Based Belief | A belief formed through emotional states serving as primary epistemic inputs rather than propositional reasoning or evidence. |
| Belief (operational) | A propositional attitude — an agent's holding-as-true of a content (descriptive, causal, normative, predictive, or self-referential) — that shapes attention, inference, and action, whether or not the content is accurate or explicitly acknowledged. |
| Belief Perseverance | A belief that persists even after its evidential basis is completely discredited; shows remarkable resistance to evidence-based revision. |
| Belief-Action Relationship | The gap between stated and operative beliefs and the mechanisms maintaining both; meta-category VI in this framework. |
| Belief-Evidence Interface | Mechanisms governing how beliefs interact with evidence and new information; meta-category V in this framework. |
| Causal Belief | A belief about what causes what; includes mechanism beliefs and intervention beliefs. |
| Cognitive Dissonance | Internal tension arising when an agent holds contradictory beliefs, or when belief and action conflict; primary driver of belief maintenance through rationalisation. |
| Collective Belief | A belief operating at the group level through mechanisms distinct from aggregated individual beliefs; meta-category IV in this framework. |
| Collective Belief Homogenisation | Convergence toward false consensus through conformity pressure and suppressed dissent; Groupthink and Abilene Paradox mechanisms. |
| Confirmatory Processing | Selective evidence uptake supporting existing beliefs; root mechanism of Confirmation Bias in Failures framework. |
| Descriptive Belief | A belief about current states of the world; empirical and in principle testable. |
| Dissonance-Resolved Belief | A belief maintained through rationalisation of internal contradiction; cognitive dissonance is the primary driver of maintenance. |
| Epistemic Trust | The network of source-credibility relationships through which most beliefs are formed by accepting what trusted sources say, rather than by direct evidence evaluation. |
| Epistemic Trust Structure | Source-credibility network governing testimony-based belief formation; determines which sources are trusted and why. |
| Epistemic Vigilance | Active monitoring of source reliability and claim quality; can be triggered or suppressed by context and identity. |
| Espoused Belief | A belief an agent states when asked; may or may not correspond to the operative belief governing behaviour. |
| Espoused-Operative Divergence | Growing gap between stated beliefs and beliefs governing behaviour as agents experience pressure to express organisational line or face identity/retaliation threat. |
| Formation Cascade | Belief cluster formation in rapid succession as agents adopt similar formation sources or through epistemic trust network expansion. |
| Groupthink | Collective belief homogenisation pattern in which group expresses false unity, dissent is suppressed, and shared illusion of unanimity persists despite private doubts. |
| Identity-Fused Belief | Belief merged with personal or group identity; challenge triggers defensive rather than deliberative response. |
| Identity-Protective Cognition | Mechanism by which beliefs fused with personal or group identity become immune to evidential challenge, triggering defensive rather than deliberative responses. |
| Inferential Belief | A belief formed through deliberate deductive, inductive, or abductive reasoning. |
| Institutional Belief | A belief encoded in procedures, rules, structures, and artefacts rather than held as explicit proposition by individuals. |
| Moral Licensing | Pattern by which past belief-consistent behaviour licenses subsequent deviation; produces cycles of virtue signalling followed by contrary behaviour. |
| Motivated Skepticism | Asymmetric application of evidential standards based on preferred conclusions; root mechanism of Motivated Reasoning in Failures framework. |
| Normative Belief | A belief about what ought to be the case; prescriptive and value-laden. |
| Operative Belief (Theory-in-Use) | The belief that actually governs an agent's behaviour, often tacit and detectable only through behavioural evidence and patterns of action. |
| Paradigmatic Filtering | Background theoretical frameworks determining what counts as admissible evidence; structures not just what is believed but what can be believed. |
| Paradigm Shift | Sudden reorganisation of dominant belief paradigm following accumulation of anomalies that paradigm cannot accommodate. |
| Perceptual / Experiential Belief | A belief formed through direct sensory experience or embodied pattern recognition. |
| Perseverance Belief | A belief that persists despite complete evidential discrediting; shows remarkable resistance to evidence-based revision. |
| Pluralistic Ignorance | Each member of a group privately rejects a belief or norm while assuming most others privately accept it; produces false collective consensus. |
| Predictive Belief | A belief about future states, probabilities, and trajectories. |
| Pre-Theoretical Commitment | Background paradigmatic assumption constraining what can be believed, prior to and independently of evidence evaluation. |
| Sacred / Inviolable Belief | A belief treated as absolutely non-negotiable and exempt from ordinary trade-off reasoning and evidential challenge. |
| Self-Fulfilling Belief | A belief that generates the conditions confirming itself through the actions it motivates. |
| Self-Referential Belief | A belief about oneself — capabilities, role, identity, and status. |
| Shared Mental Model | Collective tacit belief about how the system, organisation, or world works, held with mutual recognition by group members. |
| Testimonial Belief | A belief formed by trusting what others say; epistemic trust is the primary mechanism of acquisition. |
| Theory-in-Use | Argyris & Schön's term for operative beliefs governing an agent's behaviour — detectable through behavioural evidence rather than self-report. |

### Appendix C: Worked Example (Micro-Case)
**Scenario:** Manufacturing organisation experiencing chronic quality failures despite repeated interventions. Leadership states belief that "quality is our top priority," but quality incidents recur. Workers report knowing "cutting corners is normal here" and believing "management won't fund proper QA."

**Analysis:**

1. **Espoused-operative divergence (type 27 vs 26):** Leadership's espoused belief ("quality is top priority") diverges from operative beliefs revealed through budget allocation and incident response patterns (speed-to-market and cost control prioritised over quality).

2. **Institutional belief (type 18):** Quality beliefs are encoded in incentive structures (volume bonuses outweigh quality penalties) and procedures (shortcuts are workflow-integrated).

3. **Pluralistic ignorance (type 19):** Workers privately believe quality-cutting is wrong but assume management expects it; management privately wants quality but assumes workers prioritise speed. Neither side expresses true belief.

4. **Causal belief misspecification (type 7):** Leadership's causal model assumes "training = quality improvement" (cognitive skill gap explanation) when actual operative beliefs and incentives generate non-compliance.

5. **Identity-protective entrenchment (type 11):** Challenging quality shortcuts is experienced as disloyalty to team and organisation; workers defend current norms despite knowing they're suboptimal.

6. **Hiddens:** Espoused-operative divergence (leadership and workers both hiding true beliefs); identity-protective suppression of challenge (questioning norms = betrayal); collective false consensus (appearance of agreement masking private doubts).

**Remediation levers:** (a) Surface operative beliefs and incentive misalignment; (b) Safe-to-challenge psychological safety work; (c) Incentive structure redesign; (d) Identity reframing (quality as loyalty, not as constraint); (e) Collective norm exposure (what do we really believe?); (f) Monitoring espoused-operative convergence over time.

### Appendix D: Checklists / Facilitation Prompts

**Belief Surfacing Checklist (Interview / Focus Group):**
- What do you believe is actually happening here? (operative belief probe)
- What would someone say if asked about this publicly? (espoused belief probe; compare)
- What caused this situation? (causal belief)
- What has to change for this to improve? (solution belief)
- What are you not saying here that you think/feel? (identity threat / psychological safety probe)
- How confident are you in this belief? (confidence dimension)
- Where did you come from? (formation mechanism)

**Espoused-Operative Gap Detection Checklist:**
- Do stated decisions match actual decisions? (decision divergence)
- Do espoused values match rewarded behaviours? (incentive misalignment)
- Do public statements match private conversations? (public-private divergence)
- Do written procedures match actual workflows? (procedure-practice divergence)
- Do leaders' public positions match their private doubts? (leadership authenticity)

**Collective Belief Dynamics Checklist:**
- Is there public agreement and private doubt? (Pluralistic Ignorance, type 19)
- Does dissent get suppressed through conformity pressure? (Groupthink, type 20)
- Do people fear being the only dissenter? (Spiral of Silence)
- Is there a shared mental model everyone assumes others hold? (Shared Mental Model, type 16)
- Is the belief encoded in structures/procedures rather than held individually? (Institutional Belief, type 18)
