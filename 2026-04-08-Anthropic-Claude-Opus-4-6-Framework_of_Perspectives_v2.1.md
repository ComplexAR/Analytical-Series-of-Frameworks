# Introduction

## What is Perspectives?

All understanding is perspectival. Analysts, organisations, and institutions see through positions, knowledge bases, temporal orientations, scales, dispositions, and methods. Perspectives are not merely "bias"; they are structured ways of revealing and concealing aspects of reality. A perspective is a vantage point that makes certain aspects salient, certain evidence admissible, certain arguments compelling—and simultaneously renders other aspects invisible, other evidence inadmissible, other arguments incoherent.

This framework operationalises a critical principle: robust analysis requires (i) making perspectives explicit, (ii) profiling their limits and dynamics, and (iii) integrating multiple perspectives through disciplined interfaces—dialogue for mutual learning, translation for meaning transfer, and conflict management for incompatible claims or values. The framework provides a repeatable, testable taxonomy and a set of methods for applying it under resource and time constraints.

## Why does Perspectives matter for Hiddens work?

Perspective-driven invisibility is a core family of Hiddens. When analysis assumes a single "correct" viewpoint or collapses multiple perspectives into false consensus, whole classes of reality-relevant factors remain hidden:

**Positional hiddens** (Hidden-4): Who you are determines what you can see. A frontline operator has access to constraints and surprises that distant leadership cannot perceive. An insider knows how things actually work; an outsider sees patterns and anomalies that have become normalised. A person with stakes in the outcome sees risks and opportunities differently than a disinterested party. These positional differences are not mere "bias"—they are structural boundaries of visibility.

**Perspectival hiddens** (Hidden-5): How you know shapes what you can know. An expert in one domain cannot easily access the knowledge standards and evidence admissibility rules of another discipline. A person trained in theoretical analysis may struggle to recognise practical constraints. A quantitative analyst and a qualitative researcher may hold incompatible views of what counts as evidence. These epistemic differences are not resolvable by more data; they require translation or boundary-spanning.

**Framing capture** (Hidden-8): Power and incentives can suppress certain perspectives or elevate others as though they were neutral. An organisation that privileges quantitative metrics may systematically exclude qualitative understanding. A governance structure that requires senior approval before dissent can surface ensures that inconvenient views remain invisible until crisis.

Without this framework, these Hiddens operate silently. With it, perspective differences become objects of structured analysis, and remediation strategies (dialogue, translation, conflict protocols, governance redesign) become actionable.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises perspective analysis through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 32 perspective types** organised into six meta-categories (Positional, Epistemic, Temporal, Scalar, Orientational, Methodological) that recur across contexts and stakeholders.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (accessibility, stability, partiality, reflexivity, shareability) that profile any perspective and explain which perspectives can be combined and which require translation.

3. **Five perspective operations** (shifting, bracketing, triangulating, embedding, fusing) that describe how to work with perspectives dynamically—breaking lock-in, reducing error, and creating integration.

4. **Three interface types** (dialogue, translation, conflict) that make visible where and how perspectives interact, learn from each other, and collide.

5. **Hiddens source analysis and mandatory tiered Hiddens scans** that surface the systematic sources of perspectival invisibility (lock-in, false neutrality, category neglect, incommensurability assumption, hidden distortion) and detection/remediation strategies.

6. **A 7-step application protocol** that moves from perspective inventory through dimensional profiling, operations selection, interface design, Hiddens checking, and integration handoff.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series workflow. It is designed for use in both Light and Full Depth modes, scaled from quick perspective mapping (15–20 hours) to comprehensive multi-perspectival synthesis (40+ hours).

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework when stakeholder conflict masks perspective differences, when analysis risks being locked into a single disciplinary lens, when evidence is contested across boundaries, or when decision quality depends on integrating diverse viewpoints. The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light perspective mapping) and Investigative Run Mode (deep interviews, dimensional profiling, full Hiddens scanning).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: identify 4–6 key perspectives, profile one or two dimensions, spot obvious dialogue/translation needs, and run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, perspectives are actively conflicting, or you are designing decision processes that require complementary views. The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require F/I/A/U tagging of all perspective-dependent claims and explicit handling of residual disagreements rather than false synthesis.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete specification, standard registers, and run prompts. The framework is directly executable by an LLM given a scenario, stakeholder narratives, and the Operating Guide.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

All understanding is perspectival. Analysts and organisations see through positions, knowledge bases, temporal orientations, scales, dispositions, and methods. Perspectives are structured vantage points that make certain aspects salient and others invisible. They are not merely personal opinions; they are systematic ways of revealing and concealing reality grounded in role, discipline, time, scale, orientation, and method.

This framework provides a standardised approach to:
- **inventory perspectives** across six meta-categories and 32 core types,
- **profile perspectives** along five cross-cutting dimensions to assess accessibility, stability, partiality, reflexivity, and shareability,
- **identify perspective operations** (shifting, bracketing, triangulating, embedding, fusing) that reduce lock-in and improve integration,
- **map interfaces** where perspectives dialogue, require translation, or conflict,
- **scan for Hiddens** characteristic of perspectival analysis (positional/epistemic/framing obscuring, false neutrality, category neglect), and
- **handoff perspective-aware synthesis** to downstream diagnosis, decisions, governance, and learning frameworks.

Robust analysis therefore requires making perspectives explicit, profiling their limits, and integrating multiple perspectives through disciplined methods—not collapsing them into false consensus, but managing their interaction constructively.

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions (narrative)

### I. Positional Perspectives
Positional perspectives arise from where the observer is located relative to the phenomenon. An insider has tacit knowledge and practical constraints that outsiders cannot perceive; an outsider sees patterns and assumptions that have become invisible through familiarity. A participant shapes outcomes and experiences real consequences; an observer can document and measure but may miss crucial tacit dimensions. A first-person perspective captures lived meaning and intent; a third-person perspective enables generalisation but at cost of nuance. These distinctions are not matters of opinion; they are structural asymmetries of access and visibility. A frontline operator and a distant executive literally see different realities because they occupy different positions relative to the system.

### II. Epistemic Perspectives
Epistemic perspectives arise from what and how one knows. An expert in domain X commands depth, fine distinctions, and mechanism-level understanding; a novice brings fresh questions and detects jargon-driven confusion. A disciplinary perspective applies rigorous methods within a field but may commit boundary errors; a transdisciplinary perspective spans domains but risks vagueness. Tacit knowledge enables pattern recognition and situational judgement but is hard to audit or transfer; explicit knowledge can be documented and tested but may miss context-dependent nuance. These are not resolvable by "more data"; they are fundamental differences in evidence standards, admissibility rules, and what counts as valid reasoning.

### III. Temporal Perspectives
Temporal perspectives arise from orientation in time. A present-focused view optimises for current conditions and immediate action but can miss history and downstream consequences. A retrospective view learns from precedent and reconstructs causation but risks hindsight bias and false attribution. A prospective view anticipates futures and plans but can drift into speculation. A developmental view tracks growth and path-dependence but can assume linear progress. A cyclical view recognises repeating patterns and seasonality but can misread structural change. These perspectives often conflict: a leader pursuing "transformation" sees discontinuity; an historian sees recurrence; a strategist sees multiple conditional futures; an operator sees constraints of today.

### IV. Scalar Perspectives
Scalar perspectives arise from level-of-analysis. A local view captures heterogeneity and operational reality but may miss system-wide interactions. A global view sees macro patterns and trade-offs but can flatten local variation. A micro view of mechanisms and individuals captures causal detail but risks composition fallacies. A macro view of aggregate behaviour and institutions captures structural forces but risks division fallacies. A multi-scalar view tracks cross-level interactions but requires explicit boundary discipline. A manager at one level sees local constraints that distant leadership cannot perceive; a chief sees portfolio trade-offs that individual units cannot see.

### V. Orientational Perspectives
Orientational perspectives arise from one's disposition toward the phenomenon. An interested party has motivation and contextual knowledge but risks motivated reasoning. A disinterested party seeks impartial judgement but may underweight practical urgency. A sympathetic stance builds trust and reduces attribution error but can excuse harm. A critical stance detects flaws but can become adversarial. An appreciative stance identifies assets and meaning but can downplay risks. These are not merely emotional stances; they are systematic ways of seeing what matters and what risks, and they interact with evidence and reasoning.

### VI. Methodological Perspectives
Methodological perspectives arise from approach to analysis. A synthetic approach integrates parts into coherent wholes but can conceal uncertainty. An analytic approach decomposes and distinguishes but can miss emergent properties. A theoretical approach explains and generalises but can detach from operational reality. A practical approach focuses on feasibility and action but can prematurely close inquiry. A hermeneutic approach interprets meaning and context but can under-specify causal claims. Different methodologies embody different standards of evidence, different definitions of success, different notions of what needs explanation.

## 3.5 Types (Category V: Orientational Perspectives)
| # | Type | Description | Typical visibility gain | Common failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 23 | Interested | A stance with personal or organisational stakes in outcomes; increases motivation and contextual knowledge | Reveals what is actually at stake; enables prioritisation; grounds analysis in real incentives | Risks motivated reasoning and selective evidence standards; can defend chosen positions beyond evidence; may find what was sought |
| 24 | Disinterested | A stance seeking understanding without personal stakes; supports impartial judgement and fairness | Enables critique without defending vested interests; can see trade-offs clearly; builds credibility | May underweight practical constraints and moral urgency; can appear detached and unhelpful; enables academic distance from consequences |
| 25 | Sympathetic | A stance disposed to interpret charitably and understand reasons from within; builds trust and reduces attribution error | Captures meaning and intent from the inside; enables dialogue; reduces defensive reactions | Can excuse harm or overlook deception; risks co-optation; may lose critical edge |
| 26 | Critical | A stance disposed to scrutiny, falsification, and challenge; detects flaws and weak warrants | Reveals vulnerabilities and assumptions; prevents false consensus; forces precision and evidentiary discipline | Can become adversarial and miss constructive possibilities; may create defensive reactions that suppress information; can focus on problems without feasibility |
| 27 | Appreciative | A stance oriented to value, strengths, and what is worth preserving; identifies assets and meaning | Captures what works and why; builds morale; identifies where to invest; prevents unnecessary destruction | Can downplay risks and structural failure modes; may resist necessary change; risks denying hard truths |

| # | Type | Description | Typical visibility gain | Common failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 28 | Synthetic | An approach that integrates parts into coherent wholes; enables systems-level sensemaking and narrative integration | Creates narrative coherence; enables holistic reasoning; facilitates communication and alignment | Can conceal uncertainty and complexity; blurs important distinctions; can create false precision |
| 29 | Analytic | An approach that decomposes and distinguishes; enables clarity, testability, and diagnosis | Enables precision; creates falsifiable claims; prevents conflation; allows component testing | Can miss emergent properties and cross-cutting interactions; can lose meaning in decomposition; may treat interaction as "noise" |

# 4. Five Dimensions of Any Perspective

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)
| Dimension | Description | Indicative range / values | How to assess | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Accessibility** | How available is this perspective to a given analyst or team (native vs learned vs inaccessible)? | Native – Default, habitual, requires no effort; Acquired – Learned through training/experience; available with activation; Accessible – Can be adopted with effort; requires deliberate perspective-taking; Difficult – Hard to adopt; requires significant work to approximate; Inaccessible – Cannot be genuinely adopted; can only be simulated | Ask: Is this perspective native to our team? If not, what training, immersion, or role access is needed? Can we approximate it ethically? Can we recruit a perspective-holder? | Determines whether to seek direct holders of the perspective (interview them), build capacity to access it (training), or treat it as an epistemic boundary (require translation). |
| **Stability** | How persistent is the perspective once adopted (fixed vs volatile vs fluid)? | Fixed – Structurally determined; cannot be changed; Stable – Tends to persist; requires effort to shift; Flexible – Can shift with moderate ease; Fluid – Naturally shifts; requires effort to maintain; Volatile – Unstable; difficult to hold consistently | Observe whether the team drifts back to default assumptions under pressure; check whether role/incentive structure locks the view; test whether evidence can shift the view | Guides governance: volatile perspectives need scaffolding (rituals, prompts, external accountability); fixed perspectives require compensation via triangulation or mandatory alternative framings. |
| **Partiality** | How much of the relevant reality can this perspective cover (comprehensive vs fragmentary)? | Comprehensive – Aims to see everything relevant; broad scope; Selective – Deliberately focuses on particular aspects; Partial – Necessarily limited; sees some things, not others; Narrow – Highly constrained; tight focus on specific features; Fragmentary – Catches only glimpses; incomplete by nature | List what this perspective systematically omits; compare to the focal decision needs; assess whether omitted factors are high-consequence; test with stakeholders | Prevents overconfidence and false completeness; identifies which complementary perspectives are required; guides interface design (which perspectives must be triangulated?). |
| **Reflexivity** | How explicitly does the perspective recognise itself as a perspective (transparent vs absolutising)? | Transparent – Aware of itself as one perspective among others; explicitly situated; Self-aware – Recognises its own assumptions and limitations; Implicit – Operates without explicit self-reflection; Naïve – Unaware of itself as perspective; takes itself for granted; Absolutising – Treats itself as the only valid/possible perspective | Check whether the perspective is named and owned ("we are seeing this as…") and whether limitations are stated with evidence; ask stakeholders to articulate their own blindspots | Reduces false neutrality and absolutising; enables translation and conflict handling; increases reflexivity in decision-making; prevents governance capture by a single view. |
| **Shareability** | How transmissible is the perspective to others (universal vs ineffable)? | Universal – Can be adopted by anyone; perspective from nowhere; Communicable – Can be explained and taught to others; Translatable – Can be partially conveyed through description or analogy; Personal – Unique to individual; can be pointed at, not fully shared; Ineffable – Cannot be adequately communicated; must be experienced directly | Test whether another role can be taught the view through artefacts, documentation, or brief immersion; or whether it requires lived/role-embedded experience; assess shareability for decision processes | Sets communication strategy: where shareability is low, use intermediaries, shadowing, evidence-rich storytelling, and translated artefacts rather than abstract summaries; where high, build into training and decision protocols. |

# 5. Perspective Operations (Dynamics)

## 5.1 Perspective Operations Table (mandatory)
| # | Operation | Description | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Shifting** | Deliberately move from one perspective to another (role, scale, time, stance, method). | Moving attention and sense-making apparatus from one vantage point to another; perspective-taking or role rotation | Breaks lock-in and exposes what a default lens has normalised or excluded; reveals what is visible only from certain positions | Use explicit prompts (six-category scan); rotate roles in decision reviews; bring in insiders/outsiders; structure perspective-shifting workshops; require alternatives in written analysis |
| 2 | **Bracketing** | Suspend default assumptions long enough to examine alternative interpretations and evidence. | Epoché (phenomenological suspension); temporarily holding assumptions as hypotheses rather than facts | Prevents premature closure and absolutising; increases openness to disconfirming evidence; creates space for dialogue; enables testing without commitment | Adopt 'as-if' exercises; run pre-mortems; use pause-and-check rituals; require alternative hypotheses; implement cooling-off periods before decision commitments |
| 3 | **Triangulating** | Use multiple perspectives in parallel to reduce single-lens error and identify contradictions. | Combining multiple perspectives; methodological pluralism; cross-validation | Improves reliability by cross-checking; reveals translation loss and incommensurabilities; identifies where perspectives genuinely diverge vs where they reflect facts or values | Maintain a perspective register; assign owners; compare artefacts and outputs side-by-side; document contradictions and resolution attempts; use matrix methods to track convergences |
| 4 | **Embedding** | Place a perspective within a wider context (systems, values, history, scale, time) to expose its boundary conditions. | Locating perspective in broader context; adding meta-perspective; applying meta-analysis | Clarifies when a perspective is locally valid but globally misleading; reduces category and boundary errors; enables detection of scope creep and invalid scaling | Add context annotations (systems/time/scale/values/constraints); document boundary conditions; define applicability statements; use multi-scalar analysis; run transition/overlap checks |
| 5 | **Fusing** | Integrate perspectives into a new hybrid lens that is more adequate to the situation than any single view. | Integrating perspectives to create a new synthetic perspective; collaborative sense-making; bridging frameworks | Creates shared language for coordination when conflict persists; supports synthesis and practical decision-making without false consensus; enables new insight | Facilitate synthesis workshops; create shared artefacts (maps, glossaries, shared models); test the fused view against evidence and real constraints; document what is lost in fusion and what is gained |

# 6. Interface Types (Dialogue, Translation, Conflict)

## 6.1 Interface Types Table (mandatory)
| Type | Name | Description | Key questions | Typical examples | Detection signals |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Type A** | Dialogue Interfaces | Perspectives engage in reciprocal exchange to learn and update; focus is mutual intelligibility and calibration. | What do we each see that the other does not? What evidence would change our view? What is being translated or bracketed? Where is trust sufficient for reciprocal learning? | Stakeholder workshops with structured exchange; joint sensemaking forums; incident reviews with multiple roles; facilitated boundary-spanning; paired interviews | Stakeholders ask clarifying questions; views evolve based on hearing others; new questions emerge; common ground grows; documented learning |
| **Type B** | Translation Interfaces | Meaning must be converted between perspectives (e.g., tacit→explicit; local→global; disciplinary jargon→plain language); translation is inherently lossy. | What is lost or distorted in translation? How can lossy translation be acknowledged and compensated for? | Frontline experience → KPI; engineering constraint → executive brief; local incident → enterprise risk rating; qualitative evidence → quantitative metric; practice → policy | Evidence standards differ; same claim means different things; metrics fail to capture lived experience; required bridging documents and mediators; acknowledged asymmetries |

What would count as resolution? Who has legitimacy to decide? Can it be reframed to shift from conflict to complementarity? | Policy vs practice disputes; insider vs outsider disagreement; macro vs micro attribution conflicts; ethics vs feasibility conflicts; risk tolerance differences; resource allocation trade-offs | Stakeholders make incompatible claims; neither party yields to evidence; talks past each other; escalation required; requires arbitration, reframing, or trade-off management; governance needed |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Tiered Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how Hiddens arise in perspective analysis) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Perspectival Lock-In | Analysis becomes trapped in one perspective without recognising partiality; false certainty because alternatives are invisible | Deliberate perspective-shifting; explicit prompts across all six categories; role rotation in decision review; bring in structurally excluded perspectives |
| 2 | False Neutrality | Claiming to occupy no perspective (view from nowhere); presenting one perspective as objective truth | Explicit acknowledgment of analyst position and assumptions; name the perspectives being used; state what is being excluded; transparency about stakes |
| 3 | Category Neglect | Ignoring whole meta-categories of perspectives (e.g., omitting all Temporal perspectives; treating all perspectives as Epistemics) | Systematic scan across all six categories (Positional, Epistemic, Temporal, Scalar, Orientational, Methodological); checklist to ensure coverage; red team to find missing categories |
| 4 | Incommensurability Assumption | Treating perspectives as fundamentally incomparable and therefore dismissing dialogue, translation, or integration | Recognition that dialogue is possible (Type A); translation, though lossy, is achievable (Type B); conflicts can be reframed or arbitrated (Type C); assume compatibility until evidence shows otherwise |
| 5 | Hiddens Omission | Perspective work assumes good-faith visibility and neutral translation; positional exclusion, distortion, and hidden constraints remain untested, creating false sense of pluralism | Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan as a systematic cross-check; shortlist Tier 2 hidden types; attach detection/remediation actions to dialogue/translation/conflict interfaces; escalate to Tier 3 if high-consequence Hiddens are triggered |

## 8.2 Hiddens Six-Category Scan (Tier 1 – invariant)
| Hiddens meta-category (I–VI) | Why it may be active here | Typical symptoms | Default checks to run |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **I – Perceptual** | Perspective-taking is constrained by attention and salience; what is unseen becomes "nonexistent" | Edge cases missing; obvious assumptions unchallenged; weak-signal experiences ignored; "everyone knows" claims with no evidence | Independent observers; anomaly prompts (when has this assumption been wrong?); frontline sampling; explicit "what are we not seeing?" review |
| **II – Systemic** | Incentives and methods systematically privilege some perspectives and suppress others | Ritual neutrality; institutional perspective capture; predictable exclusion of dissent; metrics that favour certain views | Incentive audits; governance review; plural evidence requirements for key claims; red teaming on excluded perspectives; dissent channels |
| **III – Informational** | Translation interfaces rely on information flow; secrecy/fragmentation distort what perspectives can know | Conflicting narratives; missing provenance; undocumented exceptions; late 'discoveries' of hidden factors | Access mapping; provenance checks; triangulation across independent sources; information request lists; protected disclosure channels |
| **IV – Temporal** | Temporal perspectives can hide drift, legacy lock-in, and delayed effects; perspectives lag reality | Stale assumptions; hindsight narratives; underestimation of path dependence; short-termism | Timeline review; lag mapping; re-type cadence; legacy archaeology; scenario backcasting; contrast with real-time data |
| **V – Relational** | Relationship and power asymmetries shape what perspectives are safe to hold and voice | Safe-to-say vs true; performative conformity; unspoken disagreements; hierarchy mutes certain views | Confidential interviews; anonymous surveys; separate spaces for candid conversation; leadership humility; protected escalation channels |
| **VI – Ontological** | Different perspectives may operate from fundamentally different categories or ontologies; treating one ontology as universal | Talking past each other; categories one perspective uses don't exist in another; one group's "obvious facts" are another's "absurd claims" | Articulate ontologies explicitly; name the categories each perspective uses; map translation bridges; acknowledge irreducible plurality; use multi-paradigm approaches where appropriate |

## 8.3 Hiddens Tier 2 Crosswalk (Structured Deepening Layer)

When Tier 1 scan triggers high-consequence Hiddens, map them to the applicable detection and remediation interfaces:

| Hiddens type | Relevance signal | Associated Perspectives issue | Detection interface (Type A–C) | Remediation interface | Escalation rule |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Positional-4** | Insider/outsider, operator/manager asymmetry is material | Positional perspectives are structurally unequal in access | Type A: structured dialogue forcing each to articulate what the other cannot see | Type B: translation protocols; paired analysis; mediation; governance requiring both views | Escalate to Tier 3 if positional gap is blocking key decisions |
| **Perspectival-5** | Different disciplines, methods, or knowledge standards make perspectives incommensurable | Epistemic perspectives and Methodological perspectives diverge on evidence and validity | Type A: dialogue on evidence standards and admissibility; Type B: bridging documents showing translations | Type C: arbitration on which standard applies; create hybrid methods or require plural evidence | Escalate to Tier 3 if incommensurability is being used to dismiss valid perspectives |
| **Framing-8** | Power and governance privilege certain perspective framings as neutral | Orientational and Positional perspectives reveal framing bias | Type A: explicit framing mapping; red team challenge | Type B: translation from privileged frame to alternative frame; Type C: governance redesign requiring multiple framings | Escalate to Tier 3 if framing capture is suppressing dissent or distorting evidence |
| **Informational-11** | Information asymmetries prevent certain perspectives from accessing knowledge | Epistemic perspectives are constrained by information access | Type A: dialogue on what information is available to whom | Type B: information requests; translation from implicit to explicit | Type C: governance around who controls information; transparency and disclosure requirements | Escalate if information is being deliberately withheld or fragmented |
| **Temporal-15** | Perspectives differ in time orientation or lag reality | Temporal perspectives reveal drift and delayed effects | Type A: timeline comparison across perspectives | Type B: translation from short-term to long-term view; backcasting | Type C: arbitration on pace and sequence; governance enforcing re-typing cadence | Escalate if temporal mismatches are causing cascade failures |

- **Scan TH-04 (Framing Capture)**: Direct application. Run when orientational perspectives or governance analysis reveals power shaping framings.

## 8.5 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

When perspective analysis is underway, use these prompts to surface Hiddens actively:

1. **Positional Check**: "Who is not in the room or cannot easily be heard? What would they see differently? What perspectives are structurally excluded by geography, status, or access?"

2. **Epistemic Check**: "What knowledge standards are being used to judge evidence? Whose evidence counts? What evidence standards do other perspectives use, and how are they being treated?"

3. **Framing Check**: "Who named this situation and who controls the frame? What alternative frames are there? Whose frame is being treated as neutral/objective?"

4. **Temporal Check**: "What time horizons is each perspective looking at? Whose urgency is being prioritised? What is being hidden by short-termism or long-termism?"

5. **Informational Check**: "Who has access to what information? What information asymmetries exist? Is information being withheld, fragmented, or distorted?"

6. **Reflexivity Check**: "Which perspective in the room is treating itself as 'neutral' or 'objective'? Which perspectives are explicitly named and owned? What assumptions are being treated as facts?"

## 8.6 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – Full-Spectrum Hiddens Escalation)

Escalate to full Hiddens framework (Operating Guide v2.5 §7.0 complete protocol) if:
- **Consequence is material**: A misclassified perspective or false consensus will result in significant harm, financial loss, or regulatory breach
- **Power or authority disputes** are driving perspective conflicts: genuine disagreement is being masked by positional/framing capture
- **Evidence is contested across perspectives** and no bridge methodology exists: incommensurability cannot be resolved through dialogue or translation alone
- **Perspectives are being actively suppressed**: dissent channels are blocked; safe-to-say differs materially from true; psychological safety is low
- **Multiple Tier 2 Hiddens triggers** activate in the same decision: complexity warrants full-spectrum visibility audit

When Tier 3 is triggered, hand off to the Hiddens framework with:
- Perspective register (which perspectives are hidden/suppressed)
- Interface map (where dialogue/translation/conflict are breaking down)
- Relevant evidence from Tier 1–2 scans
- Stakeholder list and access constraints
- Consequence/urgency assessment

Can it be shared or must it be embodied? | Dimension profiles (5 per perspective); matrix showing compatibility and interface types | 2–4h / 4–6h |
| **4. Interface mapping** | Map where perspectives must engage (Type A: dialogue for mutual learning; Type B: translation for lossy meaning transfer; Type C: conflict for incompatibility/arbitration). Identify risks and remediation strategies. | Where can perspectives learn from each other? Where is translation lossy and what is lost? Where are claims incompatible? What arbitration rules are available? | Interface map (perspective pairs; interface type; risk level; detection strategy; remediation strategy; governance requirement) | 2–3h / 4–6h |
| **5. Hiddens scan (Tier 1+)** | Run mandatory Tier 1 Hiddens scan across six categories. Escalate to Tier 2 if high-consequence Hiddens are triggered. Map detection and remediation to dialogue/translation/conflict interfaces. | Is lock-in present? Is false neutrality claimed? Are categories neglected? Is incommensurability being assumed incorrectly? What is hidden by positional/epistemic/framing position? | Hiddens scan output (Tier 1 checklist + findings); Tier 2 crosswalk (if triggered); Targeted Scans routing (if applicable) | 1–2h / 2–3h |
| **6. Multi-perspectival synthesis** | Integrate perspectives into shared understanding or decision design that acknowledges plurality without false consensus. Document what perspectives agree on; where they diverge and why; how trade-offs will be managed. | What do perspectives agree on? Where is disagreement rooted in facts vs values vs position vs methods? Can perspectives be fused into a shared view or must they be held in tension? What governance is needed to prevent lock-in post-decision? | Synthesis memo (areas of convergence; sources of disagreement; decision design that incorporates complementary perspectives; governance model for multi-perspectival decision-making) | 2–4h / 4–8h |
| **7. Handoff & closure** | Document residual unknowns, perspective gaps, and escalation needs. Prepare handoff artefacts for downstream frameworks (Diagnosis, Decisions, Governance, Learning). Record re-typing triggers. | Which perspectives remain unexamined? Which are structurally excluded? What unknowns remain? What is the escalation pathway for unresolved conflicts? When and how will this analysis be revisited? | Closure note (residual unknowns; perspective gaps; escalation; re-typing cadence/triggers); handoff to Diagnosis/Decisions/Governance with perspective-aware briefs | 1–2h / 2–3h |

**Total time (Light Depth)**: ~13–22 hours | **Total time (Full Depth)**: ~26–41 hours

## 9.2 Standard Deliverables (recommended)

### Core artefacts (all runs)
1. **Perspective Register** – Inventory of identified perspectives, holder roles, dimensions, visibility (what revealed/hidden), and evidence standards per perspective
2. **Dimension Profiles** – Five-dimension profile for each key perspective (accessibility, stability, partiality, reflexivity, shareability)
3. **Interface Map** – Mapping of perspective pairs to interface types (A/B/C); risk assessment; detection and remediation strategies
4. **Hiddens Scan Output** – Tier 1 checklist results; Tier 2 crosswalk (if escalated); relevant Targeted Scans routing
5. **Evidence Ledger** – Key claims mapped to perspectives holding them; evidence standards per perspective; confidence assessments
6. **Synthesis Memo** – Convergences and divergences across perspectives; decision design acknowledging plurality; governance requirements

### Enhanced artefacts (Full Depth only)
7. **Perspective Operations Plan** – Analysis of which operations (shift/bracket/triangulate/embed/fuse) are needed; implementation strategy
8. **Interview Summaries** – Narrative capture of stakeholder perspectives from primary research
9. **Crosswalk Analysis** – How this perspective analysis integrates with other frameworks being applied (Evidence, Hiddens, Diagnosis, Decisions, Governance)
10. **Implementation Risks** – Perspective-driven risks in moving from decision to action; translation and change management implications

# 10. Principles Table

## 10.1 Core Principles (mandatory)

| Principle | Statement | Operational meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Plurality** | Multiple perspectives are not optional extras; they are foundational to robust analysis. Assume genuine plurality; do not collapse into false consensus. | Require at least two contrasting perspectives per major claim. Document where consensus exists and where it does not. Treat disagreement as signal, not noise. When forced to choose, document the trade-off and residual unknowns. |
| **Structured seeing** | Perspectives are not personal opinions or individual biases; they are structured vantage points grounded in position, knowledge, time, scale, orientation, and method. Treat them as analysable objects, not excusable idiosyncracies. | Classify perspectives using six meta-categories and 32 types. Document what each perspective systematically reveals and conceals. Profile along five dimensions. Move beyond "he said / she said" to structured interface management. |
| **No false neutrality** | Claiming to occupy no perspective (view from nowhere) is itself a perspective. Make your position explicit. | Every analysis statement should be tagged: Fact (verifiable), Interpretation (within a perspective), Assumption (untested claim), Unknown (missing information). Document the perspective(s) embedded in the analysis. State what is being excluded and why. |
| **Shareability matters** | Some perspectives can be taught or translated; others are embodied in role and experience and cannot be fully conveyed across boundaries. Understand the difference and adjust your integration strategy. | For shareable perspectives, use training, documentation, and shared artefacts. For ineffable perspectives, use intermediaries, shadowing, storytelling, and embedded immersion. Where perspectives are incommensurable, build bridging artefacts and translation protocols rather than assuming incompatibility. |
| **Hiddens are systematic** | Visibility failures caused by perspective position are not accidental; they are structural. Systematically scan for positional/epistemic/framing obscuring. | Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan as routine. Escalate to Tier 2–3 when stakes are high, conflicts are active, or evidence is contested. Treat Hiddens omission as a defect requiring remediation. |
| **Dialogue over dismissal** | When perspectives differ, default to dialogue, translation, or reframing before assuming incommensurability or conflict. | Presume that Type A (dialogue) and Type B (translation) interfaces are possible until evidence shows otherwise. Only escalate to Type C (conflict arbitration) when dialogue and translation have been exhausted. Document the attempt and the failure mode if conflict escalation is needed. |
| **Integration, not consensus** | Integrate perspectives without requiring agreement. Some disagreements are legitimate and should be preserved, not erased. | Use fusing (operation 5) to create hybrid views where appropriate. Preserve tension and disagreement where values or positions genuinely diverge. Document the source of disagreement and the decision rule for proceeding despite it. |
| **Governance enables perspective diversity** | Perspectives remain diverse only if governance structures require and protect them. Absent intentional governance, default perspectives will dominate and others will be suppressed. | Embed perspective requirements into decision gates. Build plural evidence standards into governance. Create safe channels for dissent. Assign perspective-holders explicit authority. Require periodic perspective-shifting in reviews. Make perspective diversity a governance obligation, not an optional facilitation outcome. |

## 12.2 Integration Notes (optional)

**Backward compatibility**: v2.0 preserves all core content from v1.3 (taxonomy, dimensions, operations, interfaces, protocol). The rewrite is primarily structural (template alignment, clearer signposting, expanded Hiddens/LLM sections) and does not change the substance of the framework. All artefacts and deliverables from v1.x remain valid; mapping to v2.0 is straightforward.

**Forward compatibility**: v2.0 is designed to integrate seamlessly with Operating Guide v2.5 and Master Template v2.3. All cross-references, register schemas, and routing protocols are v2.5-compliant and will remain stable through v2.x minor updates (unless Operating Guide v3.0 is released, at which point a major version rewrite will be required).

**Known limitations**:
- The 32-type baseline assumes six meta-categories; domain-specific extensions may require additional types (see §3.7 extension rules).
- Perspective-sharing at scale (large organisations with 50+ perspectives) requires governance and process scaffolding beyond this framework; consider specialist facilitation.
- Rapid Run Mode timelines (4–8h) assume prior stakeholder mapping and available artefacts; cold-start scenarios typically require Investigative mode (20–40h).

**Recommended next steps** (for maintainers):
- Post-v2.0 release: Field test with 3–5 diverse scenarios; gather feedback on coverage, usability, and time estimates.
- Pre-v2.1: Incorporate domain-specific perspective subtypes (e.g., Perspectives-in-Finance, Perspectives-in-Tech) as optional extensions.
- Medium term: Develop LLM evaluation harness (Appendix E schemas) for automated perspective classification and interface detection.
