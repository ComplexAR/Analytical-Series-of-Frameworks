## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Institutions framework identifies and maps the formal and informal rules, norms, conventions, and enforcement mechanisms that structure behaviour in a scenario; classifies them into six meta-categories and thirty core types; diagnoses gaps between prescribed and actual practice, institutional lock-in, informal capture, and cross-institutional conflicts; and surfaces the visibility failures (invisible informal rules, embedded logics, rule-practice gaps, institutional lock-in, informal capture) that Hiddens analysis must remediate. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Rule-Practice Gap Register, and Institutional Lock-In Inventory with institution-specific blindnesses and governance requirements, enabling downstream frameworks to design institution-aware interventions robust to institutional resistance.

## Section 3: Near-Neighbour Disambiguation Table

| Nearest neighbour | Use Institutions when… | Use neighbour instead when… |

| Governance | Focus is on informal rules, conventions, and how people actually behave regardless of formal authority; rule environment itself is the question. | Focus is on formal decision rights, authority allocation, oversight structures; formal authority architecture is the question. |

| Power | Focus is on rules as stable structures enabling/disabling outcomes independent of immediate power relations; why rules persist. | Focus is on who can shape outcomes; who influences decisions; asymmetric capacity to affect outcomes; immediate power distribution is the question. |

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Drift & Decay | Institutions gradually erode in practice; compliance declines; enforcement weakens; rule environment becomes less structured and ambiguous. | Initial non-compliance is tolerated; enforcement is inconsistent; staff gradually stop following rules; decay normalises. | Ambient uncertainty; creeping violations; coordination failure; systemic non-compliance. |
| Institutionalisation of Workarounds | When formal institutions prevent necessary action, staff create workarounds; over time, workarounds become institutionalised as the real rules; formal rules become performative. | Formal rule creates problem; staff find informal solution; informal solution works and becomes standard; informal solution becomes as binding as formal rule. | True rule environment becomes hidden; governance failure; formal policies are theatre; informal rules are entrenched. |
| Lock-In Deepening | Path dependencies created by initial institutions become tighter and more irreversible; sunk costs increase; complementarities strengthen; alternatives become more costly. | Initial rule creates path; investments depend on path; role structures evolve to depend on path; vested interests develop. | Institutional change becomes impossible; alternatives disappear; actors become locked into suboptimal paths. |
| Norm Cascades | Social norms spread rapidly through networks; what one group does becomes standard practice in next group; norm spread much faster than formal rule change. | Influencer or group adopts norm; observation by others creates imitation; norm spreads through network faster than formal rule. | New norms establish rapidly and with little governance; informal rules become de facto binding. |
| Legitimacy Erosion | Narratives and justifications that made institutions legitimate lose power; people begin to question whether institution is necessary; support weakens. | Institution justified clearly; justification repeated; over time, justification questioned or contradicted by evidence; people stop believing narratives. | Support for institution collapses; change becomes possible; institutions become vulnerable to delegitimisation. |
| Formal Rigidity Driving Informality | When formal institutions are too rigid, create unintended consequences, or conflict with operational reality, staff create informal institutions that parallel or replace them. | Formal rule is perceived as impossible or counterproductive; staff find informal way to accomplish needed result; informal practice becomes standard. | Formal rules are ignored; shadow governance emerges; true operating system is hidden. |
| Informal Capture | Informal institutions are captured by particular interests; informal norms are adjusted to favour particular groups; informal power shapes what rules apply to whom. | Informal norm is established; over time, norm is applied selectively; interpretation drifts to favour powerful interests. | Informal institution becomes mechanism for maintaining power differentials; discrimination becomes normalised. |
| Cross-Institutional Conflict Suppression | When institutions conflict, organisations manage conflicts by suppressing visibility; formal compliance with each institution separately hides contradictions. | Institution A requires outcome X; Institution B requires outcome not-X; different groups follow different institutions; conflicts not surfaced. | Contradictions remain hidden; actors face impossible choices; formal compliance masks systemic dysfunction. |
| Exception Creep | Exceptions to formal rules become more frequent; exceptions gradually undermine the rule; exceptions stop being exceptional. | Rule established with clear criteria for exceptions; exceptions approved in legitimate cases; exception rate increases over time. | Rule is followed in minority of cases; exceptions become standard practice; original rule becomes ineffective. |
| Norm Inversion | When institutions fail or become counterproductive, they may be inverted; what was previously punished becomes rewarded; what was previously rewarded becomes shameful. | Institution established as constraint; institution violated under pressure; violation becomes survival behaviour; inverted norm becomes standard. | Previous institution viewed as foolish; ethical norms reverse; behaviour standards invert. |
| Institutional Forgetting | Rules and institutions are forgotten over time; institutional memory is lost; reasons why rules were established are lost; rules followed without understanding why. | Institution established with purpose; personnel turn over; institutional knowledge not documented; new staff don't understand why. | Rules followed habitually or dropped; original purpose is obscured; institutional knowledge is lost. |
| Innovation Suppression | Institutions can suppress innovation by making it difficult or impossible to try new approaches; alternatives are not permitted or costly to explore. | Institutions establish standard way; rules enforce adherence; innovation violates rules; alternatives are not permitted or are costly. | Change becomes impossible; trapped in suboptimal practices; innovation alternatives foreclosed. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What rules are invisible because they are unwritten, embedded in the environment, or so normalised that they are experienced as facts rather than choices? |
| II (Systemic) | How do institutions couple with other systems, organisations, and processes; what emergent effects arise from interactions between institutional environments; are there systemic contradictions? |
| III (Informational) | What information about institutional non-compliance, rule-practice gaps, and informal institutions is hidden by formal compliance reporting; who knows the true operating system and who does not? |
| IV (Temporal) | What institutional lock-in is increasing over time and becoming less visible; what path dependencies are deepening; what historical contingencies have become impossible to reverse? |
| V (Relational) | Who actually controls institutions, interprets rules, decides exceptions, and shapes informal norms; are power asymmetries masked as technical necessity or rule-of-law; is institutional capture occurring? |
| VI (Ontological) | What categories of people, behaviour, or possibility are forelosed by the institution's logic; who is not represented in the institutional framework; what alternatives are not even conceivable given institutional logics? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Invisible Informal Institutions (rules learned through socialisation, never written, enforced through social pressure)
- Embedded Institutional Logics (narratives about appropriate action so embedded they are experienced as facts)
- Rule-Practice Gaps (systematic divergence between prescribed and actual behaviour, hidden by formal compliance)
- Institutional Lock-In (path dependencies that make change impossible and alternatives invisible)
- Informal Institutional Capture (informal rules shaped by particular interests, hidden by lack of transparency)
- Cross-Institutional Conflicts (contradictions between multiple institutional environments, hidden by formal compliance)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Invisible informal rules guide actual behaviour | Framework of Culture & Norms; Framework of Power | Ethnographic scan + informal norm mapping | Surface informal rules through non-punitive inquiry; document actual practice; make rules explicit and testable |
| Rule-practice gap is material and systematic | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Governance | Compliance audit + practice observation | Document gaps in Institutional Register; assess whether gap is feature or bug; design accountability for actual practice |
| Institutional lock-in prevents necessary change | Framework of Interventions; Framework of Risk | Lock-in inventory + change scenario testing | Design institution-aware interventions that work with lock-in rather than against it; test for second-order resistance |
| Informal capture suspected (rules favour particular interests) | Framework of Power; Framework of Responsibility | Power analysis + selective enforcement audit | Map informal rule application patterns; identify systematic beneficiaries; design transparent exception criteria |
| Cross-institutional conflicts suppress visibility | Framework of Governance; Framework of Decisions | Conflict mapping + institutional logic comparison | Surface contradictions explicitly; redesign institutions to resolve conflicts rather than suppress them; clarify decision authority |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. List focal institutions in the scenario; identify formal rules (laws, policies, procedures, contracts) and informal institutions (norms, logics, conventions).
2. Classify each institution into one or more of the six meta-categories (I–VI) and assign core types (1–30); note uncertainty and non-standard institutions.
3. For each focal institution, assess Scope & Formality (written vs unwritten, formal vs informal), Enforcement Mechanisms (hard vs soft, consistent vs selective), and Rule-Practice Fidelity (formal rules match actual behaviour or diverge).
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan across six Hiddens meta-categories; flag obvious rule-practice gaps, informal rule invisibility, institutional logics that foreclose alternatives, or lock-in effects.
5. Shortlist the 2–3 highest-consequence Hiddens from Tier 1 scan; identify detection/remediation interfaces; flag for escalation if high-consequence and high-uncertainty.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Map focal institution inventory; list all formal rules (policies, procedures, contracts, standards) and informal institutions (norms, conventions, logics); verify scope and coverage.
2. Classify each institution (meta-category + core type 1–30); document non-standard institutions and domain-specific elaborations.
3. Profile each institution across five dimensions: Scope & Formality Gradient (formality spectrum), Enforcement Mechanisms (detection + consequences + escalation + exceptions), Rule-Practice Fidelity (actual compliance vs prescribed), Institutional Logics Visibility (are logics explicit or embedded?), Change & Adaptability (how easily can institution be reformed?).
4. Analyse all twelve dynamics patterns; for each focal institution, assess which patterns are active (nascent, present, high-risk) and why; identify cascades and feedback loops.
5. Map three interface types: Rule Setting & Maintenance (where are rules created and updated?), Rule Practice & Compliance (where is practice monitored?), Detection & Remediation (what happens when gaps are discovered?).
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: for each high-relevance Hidden (e.g., invisible informal rule, rule-practice gap, lock-in, informal capture), identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; assign detection and remediation interfaces.
7. Document unresolved Unknowns; apply Escalation Rule if any Unknown could change decisions or accountability; generate Institutional Map, Rule-Practice Gap Register, Lock-In Inventory, and closure artefacts.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add institution-specific Hiddens shortlist with meta-category tag (I–VI), mechanism, detectability, consequence. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hiddens to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record institution classifications, dimensional profiles, rule-practice gap evidence, enforcement pattern data, informal institution documentation, evidence quality and provenance. | After institutional classification and dimensional profiling; note gaps (e.g., informal rule visibility, lock-in measurement). |
| Rule-Practice Gap Register | Document systematic divergences between formal prescriptions and actual behaviour; record scope, frequency, consequence of gaps; note whether gaps are hidden or visible. | Gap discovery from observation, interviews, compliance audit, or decision trace analysis. |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Propose hypotheses about informal rules, rule-practice causes, lock-in mechanisms, institutional capture; propose tests (ethnographic scan, selective enforcement audit, scenario testing). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decisions. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified focal institutions (formal and informal), classified them into the six meta-categories, assessed critical dimensions (Rule-Practice Fidelity, Enforcement Mechanisms, Institutional Logics Visibility), diagnosed rule-practice gaps, mapped lock-in and informal capture, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-consequence Unknowns to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Governance, Power)?

- Situations Context Classification (what situation type makes which institutions relevant?)
- Boundaries (what governance/jurisdictional constraints affect institutional scope?)
- Power & Incentives (who has power to create/enforce/change institutions; what incentives drive institutional compliance or violation?)
- Evidence (what evidence exists about institutional effectiveness, gaps, and non-compliance?)
- Culture & Norms (what shared values and beliefs reinforce or resist institutions?)
- Agents & Personas (who are the actors subject to institutions; what are their mental models and constraints?)

- Framework of Hiddens (institution-specific Hiddens detection and remediation design)
- Framework of Governance (formal authority structure and decision rights; aligns with institutional framework)

- Framework of Interventions (institution-aware intervention design; working with vs against institutional resistance)
- Framework of Responsibility (accountability mapping for rule compliance and institutional reform)
