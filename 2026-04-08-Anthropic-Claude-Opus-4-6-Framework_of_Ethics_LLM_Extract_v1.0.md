## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Ethics framework systematically surfaces and analyses what normative assumptions, moral claims, and questions of fairness, justice, rights, and duties are embedded in a scenario. It classifies ethical considerations into six meta-categories and thirty core types; maps value-neutral framing that conceals normative choices, distributional invisibility, and ethical blind spots; and surfaces what ought to be valued and whether acceptance is warranted on moral grounds. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Hypothesis/Test Backlog with ethical-blindness analysis, enabling downstream frameworks to design interventions that address substantive justice, not just procedural compliance or efficiency.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ethical Escalation | Moral claim treated as settled or minor becomes pressing; visibility and consequence grow. | Stakeholder persistence; evidence accumulation; moral reframing; moral awakening; social movements. | Some ethical concerns become decision-critical over time; others remain suppressed despite evidence. |
| Ethical Retreat and Normalization | Harm becomes normalized; moral outrage fades; contested issue accepted as "the way things are." | Habituation to injustice; loss of memory; institutional capture; suppression of dissent; reframing as necessary. | Unjust systems become invisible once accepted; moral sensitivity dulls; ethical vigilance erodes. |
| Framework Conflict and Incompatibility | Different ethical frameworks generate incompatible conclusions; conflicts may be irreducible. | Stakeholders operate from different meta-frameworks (consequences vs duties, individual vs communal). | Conflicts may hide genuine moral disagreement; resolution by power rather than argument. |
| Power Capture of Ethical Reasoning | Actors with power redefine ethical terms or selectively invoke frameworks when convenient. | Dominant groups control language; ethical terminology redefined to protect interests; double standards in application. | Ethical reasoning becomes tool of oppression; justice language used to justify injustice. |
| Ethical Trade-off Transformation | Ethical tensions shift over time as understanding deepens and stakeholder perspectives change. | Learning about consequences; changed values; new perspectives; integration of information. | Initial framings prove incomplete; new options emerge; what seemed zero-sum becomes more complex. |
| Responsibility Diffusion and Drift | Accountability for ethical failure drifts; no one is held responsible because responsibility is distributed. | Collective decision-making without individual responsibility; passive systems; distributed chains; structural injustice. | Injustice persists without anyone being responsible; structural injustice feels impersonal. |
| Hidden Justice Cascade | One justice concern surfaces; investigation reveals deeper systemic injustices; surface issue is symptom of larger pattern. | Systemic injustice; multiple overlapping harms; interdependencies among injustices; suppression of whole landscapes. | Fixing surface issue without addressing root injustice; missing opportunity for systemic correction. |
| Ethical Rehabilitation and Learning | Communities, institutions, or individuals develop moral capacities; ethical sensitivity and justice frameworks deepen. | Deliberative engagement with ethical issues; exposure to perspectives of those harmed; moral education; institutional learning. | Ethical development possible through commitment; moral progress possible despite failures. |
| Ethical Regression and Corruption | Moral capacities erode; ethical sensitivity decreases; justice frameworks narrow; regression to simpler, crueller frames. | Habituation to harm; suppression of dissent; lost memory; institutional corruption; dehumanization of outsiders. | Moral capacities not guaranteed; ethical regression can occur; requires active commitment to maintain. |
| Competing Justice Frameworks Emergence | What seemed like one problem contains multiple justice frameworks; stakeholders operate from incommensurable premises. | Multicultural contexts; religious and secular frameworks; individual and communal justice; temporal differences. | Conflicts may be deeper than disagreement about facts; resolution requires bridging or accepting pluralism. |
| Moral Momentum and Moral Exhaustion | Moral energy focused on injustice builds momentum or exhausts stakeholders (fatigue, burnout, withdrawal). | Sustained moral commitment and solidarity vs depletion of activist energy and competing demands. | Moral change requires sustained energy; can be created or dissipated; institutional structures affect possibility. |
| Ethical Accountability Paradox | Those most responsible for ethical harm often have least exposure to its consequences. | Diffusion of power; distance from decision-makers to those harmed; information asymmetries; power to avoid exposure. | Injustice can persist without felt responsibility; moral sensitivity of decision-makers remains low. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What normative choices are being framed as neutral or technical, hiding that someone benefits and someone bears costs? |
| II (Systemic) | How do ethical blindspots in one part of the system create justice concerns in interconnected systems? |
| III (Informational) | What accounts of injustice are not being heard because the voices of those harmed are suppressed or filtered? |
| IV (Temporal) | How is ethical progress or regression unfolding over time—are justice concerns escalating into visibility or being normalized out of view? |
| V (Relational) | How do power asymmetries enable some groups to define justice in their own interest while marginalizing others' justice claims? |
| VI (Ontological) | What categories of being-human are embedded in ethical reasoning—who counts as a bearer of rights, and who is left out? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Value-Neutral Framing (technical language conceals normative choices)
- Distributional Invisibility (aggregated or dispersed harms make who bears costs invisible)
- Ethical Assumptions Made Invisible (background assumptions about fairness treated as settled or inevitable)
- Suppression and Silencing (voices of those harmed not heard in decision-making)
- Ethical Corruption (harm becomes normalized; moral sensitivity erodes)
- Procedural Compliance as Cover (formal rules followed while substantive justice violated)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Value-neutral framing concealing normative choices (technical = ethical) | Framework of Values; Framework of Evidence | Language and framing audit; stakeholder moral claims identification | Expose normative choices; make value commitments explicit and contestable |
| Distributional invisibility hiding who bears costs and harms | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Metrics | Disaggregated outcome analysis; disaggregated harm tracking; affected-population voice | Make distributions visible by group; track whose outcomes improve and whose worsen; amplify affected voices |
| Ethical assumptions built into design treated as background or inevitable | Framework of Technology; Framework of Processes | Design assumption audit; alternative-design exploration; co-design with affected populations | Articulate and question ethical assumptions; explore alternatives; involve affected populations in design |
| Suppression and silencing of justice claims from marginalized groups | Framework of Communications; Framework of Power | Voice and access audit; structural barriers to being heard | Create safe channels for raising justice claims; ensure affected populations have decision authority |
| Ethical framework conflicts not being faced directly | Framework of Values; Framework of Dialogue | Multi-framework articulation; respectful engagement across frameworks; acknowledgment of incommensurability | Map frameworks explicitly; create deliberative spaces; acknowledge tragic choices and reasonable disagreement |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Identify focal ethical questions: What normative issues are decision-critical? Who benefits and who bears costs? What is fair?
2. Scan all six meta-categories lightly; note which categories are most relevant (distributional justice, rights, procedural fairness, relational ethics, framework conflicts, or blind spots?).
3. For each focal ethical question, identify high-consequence ethical types; map which moral frameworks are operative (consequentialist, deontological, virtue, care-based, relational).
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: identify 1–2 major hidden justice concerns (value-neutral framing concealing choices, distributional invisibility, suppressed voices, ethical assumptions made invisible).
5. Shortlist highest-consequence ethical Hiddens; note whether justice claims are visible to all stakeholders or suppressed from some; escalate if high-consequence and asymmetrically visible.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Inventory focal ethical considerations; classify into six meta-categories and core types (I–VI × 5); map ethical landscape comprehensively.
2. For each high-consequence ethical issue, identify operative ethical frameworks (consequentialist, deontological, virtue, care, relational, social contract); note where frameworks align and where they conflict.
3. Assess five cross-cutting dimensions for each ethical issue: Urgency (is justice decision-critical?), Power Asymmetry (who has power to define justice?), Stakeholder Perspective (are affected populations represented?), Framework Conflict (are frameworks incommensurable?), Moral Momentum (is justice concern escalating or normalizing?).
4. Analyse all twelve dynamic patterns; assess which are active (ethical escalation, normalization, framework conflict, power capture, trade-off transformation, responsibility diffusion, justice cascades, moral learning, framework emergence, momentum/exhaustion, accountability paradox).
5. Map suppression mechanisms: which justice claims are not being heard? Who is excluded from ethical reasoning? What ethical considerations are made invisible?
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan (structured deepening): for each high-relevance Hidden (value-neutral framing, distributional invisibility, suppression, ethical assumption invisibility, framework conflict, accountability paradox), identify mechanism, visibility to which stakeholders, consequence; assign remediation interfaces.
7. Document unresolved Unknowns about justice legitimacy, framework compatibility, and stakeholder moral understanding; apply Escalation Rule; generate Residual Unknowns register.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add ethical-blindness Hiddens: value-neutral framing, distributional invisibility, suppression, ethical assumption invisibility, responsibility diffusion; tag by meta-category and affected populations. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate ethical Unknowns and suppressed-voice issues to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record ethical-issue classification, framework analysis, dimension assessments, dynamic pattern presence, evidence about who benefits/bears costs, voice/participation patterns. | After ethical inventory and dimensional profiling; note gaps requiring affected-population engagement and voice-inclusion analysis. |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about justice legitimacy, framework compatibility, stakeholder moral understanding; propose tests (stakeholder interviews, justice-claim analysis, framework-conflict exploration). | Identified Unknowns; residual Hiddens from Tier 1 scan; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request access to affected populations for justice-claim articulation; request evidence about outcome distributions; request information about current decision-making processes. | To reduce Unknowns and validate ethical diagnosis; coordinate with Evidence and Communications frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified focal ethical questions, mapped operative frameworks, assessed who benefits and who bears costs, identified suppressed justice claims, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan across all six meta-categories, and ensured affected populations' voices are included in ethical analysis?

### Full Depth (Investigative Run Mode) — 2–4 hours
**Goal:** Comprehensive ethical mapping; systematic Hiddens work; integration with decision-making

**Steps:**
1. **Situation clarification (15 min):** Stakeholders, decisions, ethical dependencies. Map decision tree.

- **V Relational:** Are care obligations and relational harms invisible?
- **VI Ontological:** Are ethical concepts treated as fixed rather than contested?

- **A Detection/Diagnosis:** How are ethical concerns identified and brought into view?
- **B Judgment/Evaluation:** How are ethical claims evaluated? What frameworks are used?
- **C Action/Remediation:** How do ethics translate to decisions and governance changes?

## Tier 2 Hiddens (Investigative Mode Standard)

## Tier 3 Escalation (Conditional)

**Escalate if:**
- High consequence (safety/existential/severe harm)
- Adversarial context (concealment plausible)
- Persistent disagreement (framework conflicts deep)
- Repeat failures (systemic, not addressed)
- Cascades suspected (one concern reflects deeper systemic injustice)
- Decision-critical residual (ethics unknown could change governance/responsibility)

**When escalating:**
- Expand beyond framework-specific Hiddens into full Hiddens framework taxonomy
- Require stronger evidence, multiple detection paths, explicit disconfirming tests
- Map ethics consequences across all 36 frameworks
- Consider near-group (G4+G5+G6) or full-group (G1–G6) coverage if warranted
- Produce detailed Tier 3 register with mechanisms, persistence drivers, detection/remediation methods

## LLM Execution Discipline (Prompt Rules)

**F/I/A/U tags:** Follow OG §D.10.1 and §11.9
- Use **F** (Fact) for empirical claims about distributions, outcomes, stakeholder statements
- Use **I** (Interpretation) for ethical characterization (e.g., "this is unfair because...")
- Use **A** (Assumption) for normative premises (e.g., "merit-based allocation is fair if...")
- Use **U** (Unknown) for ethical uncertainties (e.g., "unknown whether affected groups were included in design")

**Ethical reasoning discipline:**
- Name frameworks explicitly (utilitarian, deontological, care-based, etc.)
- Map conflicts between frameworks; don't hide disagreement
- Include affected populations' moral perspectives; don't center powerful voices only
- Make normative choices visible; don't hide value claims in technical language
- Use dimension language (universality, urgency, power asymmetry, tractability, perspective) to characterize issues
- Distinguish suppression (active/structural) from invisibility (cognitive difficulty)

**Closure discipline:**
- State what ethical concerns remain unresolved
- Explain why remaining unknowns don't prevent moving forward (if true)
- List who is responsible for ongoing ethical governance
- Specify what monitoring will track ethical performance
- Identify learning hook for future improvement

## Common Use Patterns

**Pattern 1: Ethical tension surfaced, needs clarity**
- Run meta-category scan to identify which categories are in conflict
- Map types involved in each framework
- Use dimensions to characterize positions
- Identify framework conflict explicitly (e.g., "utilitarian efficiency vs. dignity protection")
- Output: framework conflict map + governance implication

**Pattern 2: Decision has hidden normative implications**
- Examine decision through each meta-category
- Identify which ethical considerations are being made (and by whom)
- Check Tier 1 Hiddens: which categories are invisible?
- Surface value-neutral framing (technical language hiding moral choice)
- Output: ethical assumptions register + governance recommendation to include ethics in decision process

**Pattern 3: Vulnerable populations affected; justice concerns raised**
- Prioritize relational ethics (Category IV) and rights (Category II)
- Check stakeholder perspective dimension: are marginalized voices centered?
- Run Tier 1 Hiddens focusing on Suppression (Category VI): what justice claims are silenced?
- Map detection and remediation interfaces: how would affected groups name concern? How would governance respond?
- Output: ethics framework map + governance changes ensuring ongoing voice inclusion

**Pattern 4: Procedural fairness questioned**
- Focus on Category III (Procedural Fairness) plus Category VI (Hiddens: procedural compliance masking injustice)
- Examine: voice, transparency, impartiality, appeals, rule of law
- Check whether procedures respect substantive justice (not just formal compliance)
- Output: procedural fair assessment + recommendations for reform if needed
