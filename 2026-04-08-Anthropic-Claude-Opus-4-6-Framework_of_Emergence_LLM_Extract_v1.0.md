## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Emergence framework identifies what genuinely new, non-reducible, system-level phenomena arise from interactions of components; classifies them into six meta-categories and thirty core types; surfaces the visibility failures (aggregation blindness, temporal mismatch, reductionist model assumptions, category absence, intervention paradox) that prevent early detection and appropriate response; and maps detection, intervention, and adaptation interfaces that account for emergence characteristics. It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, and Risk Register with emergence-specific blindnesses and governance requirements, enabling downstream frameworks to design emergence-aware monitoring and adaptive management robust to non-linear surprise.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bifurcation Cascade | System exhibits multiple bifurcations in sequence; each bifurcation opens new possibilities and closes others; trajectory through bifurcation space is path-dependent. | Parameter change → bifurcation → new baseline → subsequent parameter change → bifurcation in new baseline. | System dynamics shift at each bifurcation; past interventions become ineffective; new baselines require new response strategies. |
| Herding & Information Cascade | Initial uncertain information or early-adopter actions trigger cascade; agents weight observed actions more than independent information; cascade accelerates and creates momentum toward lock-in. | Ambiguity → early actor decision → observation by others → decision to follow observed → cascade → commitment. | Emergent consensus forms rapidly; contrary evidence is discounted; lock-in to early choices even when suboptimal; reversal becomes costly. |
| Contagion & Epidemic Spread | Emergence spreads through contact (infection, information, belief); spread rate depends on contact probability and infectiousness; exponential growth until threshold or saturation. | Infected × contact rate × susceptible population → exponential growth until threshold or saturation point. | System-wide adoption occurs rapidly; speed of spread exceeds governance capacity; containment windows are narrow. |
| Punctuated Equilibrium & Jump Dynamics | System appears stable for extended period then rapidly transitions to new stability region; transition appears sudden but results from slow accumulation of unseen stress. | Slow accumulation (hidden) + trigger event → rapid transition → new equilibrium → slow accumulation in new regime. | Surprises appear from nowhere; recovery is much harder than prevention; early-warning signals are invisible until system has already shifted. |
| Lock-In Amplification | Initial commitment creates path dependence; path dependence increases cost of alternatives; increasing cost reinforces initial choice; lock-in deepens with time. | Initial choice → increasing returns → switching cost rises → alternative options foreclose → lock-in deepens. | Trapped in suboptimal path; escape becomes impossible; alternatives are forgotten; sunk costs prevent learning. |
| Emergence Maturation & Crystallisation | New emergent phenomenon begins as surprise/anomaly; explanatory frameworks develop; frameworks become categories; categories become institutionalised; institutional inertia sets in. | Surprise → ad-hoc explanation → category formation → institutional routine → inertia → resistance to change. | Novel phenomenon becomes locked into institutional framework; learning stops; adaptation becomes rigid; flexibility is lost. |
| Cascading Failure & Systemic Breakdown | System exhibits increasing fragility as components optimise locally; single failure propagates due to tight coupling and lack of buffers; system-wide breakdown becomes likely. | Local optimisation → reduced coupling slackness → increased fragility → single failure → cascade → system breakdown. | Resilience decreases; total failure becomes more probable; recovery from failure is slow or impossible. |
| Self-Organisation & Spontaneous Reorganisation | System exhibits crisis response through spontaneous coordination and reorganisation; new structures emerge without design or planning; emerge initially out of disorder then crystallise. | Disruption → local adaptation → emergence of coordination → spontaneous structure → stabilisation → normalisation. | System survives disruption through adaptive capacity; new structures may be better or worse than original; becomes institutionalised. |
| Dual-Process Competition & Switching | System governed by two qualitatively different processes; processes compete for control; dominance switches based on conditions; oscillation may emerge. | Condition A → Process 1 dominates → Condition B → Process 2 emerges → switching oscillation or stability. | System behaviour becomes inconsistent; decision-makers must understand both processes; interventions must account for both regimes. |
| Adaptive Trap & Moving Target | System adapts in response to intervention; adaptation changes problem landscape; simple moving-target effect means intervention must continually increase to maintain effect. | Intervention → adaptation → reduced effect → increased intervention → faster adaptation → escalation trap. | Interventions become increasingly costly and less effective; adaptive arms race; exhaustion of intervention capacity. |
| Threshold Wandering & Bifurcation Migration | Tipping point or bifurcation threshold drifts or moves due to system evolution, learning, or feedback; prior interventions become less effective; new thresholds emerge. | System learns from near-miss → tolerance increases → threshold shifts → prior interventions less effective. | Early-warning signals become unreliable; interventions must be continuously recalibrated; system exhibits moving targets. |
| Hybrid Emergence & Boundary Dissolution | Genuine hybrids emerge that do not fit existing categories; categorical boundaries become fuzzy; system operates across multiple domains simultaneously; new ontology required. | Categories separate → interaction at boundaries → hybridisation → boundary dissolution → new categories needed. | System cannot be understood through existing frameworks; governance assumes categories that are no longer valid. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What non-linear amplification effects, feedback loops, and state-dependent behaviours are invisible because they are non-obvious or require systems thinking to perceive? |
| II (Systemic) | What emergent phenomena arise from component interactions that are not reducible to component-level analysis; what systemic coupling and emergent effects might arise? |
| III (Informational) | What aggregated data, averaged metrics, or disaggregated measures hide the existence of emergent phenomena or non-linear dynamics operating at system level? |
| IV (Temporal) | What emergence operates on timescales (slow or fast) that do not match observation windows; what early-warning signals are missed; what acceleration or deceleration is invisible? |
| V (Relational) | Who recognises the emergence phenomenon and who is blind to it; does emergence advantage particular interests and harm others; who controls emergence response? |
| VI (Ontological) | What categories and existing frameworks do not accommodate the emergent phenomenon; what naming gaps exist; does ontological novelty render governance categories obsolete? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Aggregation & Averaging Blindness (system-level emergence invisible in component data or averaged metrics)
- Temporal Mismatch & Observability Gap (emergence unfolds on timescale that does not match investigation window)
- Reductionist Model Assumption (linear or component-based models assumed sufficient; feedback and thresholds not modelled)
- Category Absence & Ontological Blindness (emergent phenomenon lacks existing analytical category; forced into ill-fitting frames)
- Intervention Paradox & Unanticipated Feedback (interventions designed to control emergence trigger different emergence)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-linear feedback and threshold behaviour unmodelled | Framework of Systems; Framework of Dynamics | System dynamics audit + feedback mapping | Model feedback loops and thresholds; identify bifurcation points; design monitoring for early-warning signals |
| Aggregation blindness hides system-level emergence | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Metrics | Disaggregation analysis + distribution inspection | Disaggregate data by multiple dimensions; look for bimodal distributions; examine local hotspots; use high-resolution temporal/spatial data |
| Temporal mismatch prevents emergence detection | Framework of Causation; Framework of Risk | Historical data window extension + scenario modelling | Extend observation window; use analogues and comparative cases; model emergence at multiple timescales; schedule re-scans aligned with emergence tempo |
| Ontological novelty not captured in existing categories | Framework of Hiddens; Framework of Categories | Category boundary scan + phenomenon naming exercise | Recognise and name novel phenomena; suspend existing categories; develop hybrid categorisations; track "unclassifiable" frequency |
| Interventions trigger unanticipated emergence | Framework of Interventions; Framework of Risk | Intervention-system feedback modelling + adaptive management | Model intervention-system feedback before acting; design for robustness not optimisation; pilot with monitoring; build adaptive intervention logic |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Specify focal emergent phenomenon(a); identify why reductionist analysis is insufficient; characterise onset, timescale, spatial extent, manifestations.
2. Classify emergent phenomenon into one or more of six meta-categories (I–VI) and assign core types (1–30); note classification uncertainty and cross-category hybrids.
3. Profile phenomenon against five key dimensions: Predictability & Observability (how clearly can it be anticipated?), Reversibility & Robustness (can it be reversed?), Temporal Scope (onset and manifestation timescale), Spatial Extent & Boundary Transcendence (how far does it propagate?), Consequence & Stakeholder Impact (who is affected and how much?).
4. Identify which dynamic patterns (1–12) best describe evolution of phenomenon; assess whether phenomenon is nascent, present, high-risk, or crystallising.
5. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan; identify candidate Hiddens (aggregation blindness, temporal mismatch, reductionist assumptions, category absence, intervention paradox); flag highest-consequence Hiddens for escalation.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Specify focal scenario, emergence phenomenon(a), and analytical goal; clarify scope, time window, consequence level; confirm Run Mode and routing outputs.
2. Collect evidence that focal phenomenon is emergent; identify why component-level analysis is insufficient; characterise emergence signature: onset, timescale, spatial extent, manifestations, stakeholder impacts.
3. Classify emergence phenomenon across six meta-categories; assign core types (1–30); build diagnostic shortlist of most-relevant types and dimensions.
4. Profile phenomenon against five dimensions in detail; identify which dynamic patterns (1–12) describe emergence evolution; build evolution scenarios with timeline and pattern transitions.
5. Identify Detection (early-warning monitoring), Intervention (governance and control levers), and Adaptation (learning and response) interfaces; assess readiness and gaps; design monitoring protocol.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: for each high-consequence Hidden (aggregation blindness, temporal mismatch, reductionist assumptions, ontological novelty, intervention paradox), identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence.
7. Convert emergence analysis into actionable implications for decisions, governance, and risk mitigation; specify contingencies, triggers, and adaptive management; generate closure artefacts and residual unknowns.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add emergence-specific Hiddens shortlist with meta-category tag (I–VI), mechanism (aggregation blindness, temporal mismatch, etc.), detectability, consequence. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hiddens to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record phenomenon classification, dimensional profiles, dynamic pattern assessment, evidence of non-reducibility, evidence quality and gaps. | After emergence classification and dimensional profiling; note gaps (e.g., feedback mapping, threshold testing). |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Propose hypotheses about emergence mechanisms, early-warning indicators, intervention effects, and adaptive responses; propose tests (system dynamics modelling, scenario testing, monitoring protocol). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decisions. |
| Risk Register | Document emergence risks, cascade pathways, tipping point proximity, lock-in mechanisms, reversal costs, governance gaps. | Emergence analysis completion; contingency planning. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified focal emergence phenomenon(a), classified into six meta-categories, assessed critical dimensions (Predictability, Reversibility, Temporal Scope, Spatial Extent, Consequence), identified dynamic patterns, designed detection/intervention/adaptation interfaces, run Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-consequence Unknowns to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Risk, Systems)?
