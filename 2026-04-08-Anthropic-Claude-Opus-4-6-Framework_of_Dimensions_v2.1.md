# Introduction

## What is the Framework of Dimensions?

Dimensions are not merely spatial axes—they represent distinct modes of existence, organisation, and comprehension. A dimension is any axis of variation along which phenomena can be meaningfully distinguished and measured. The same underlying reality exhibits properties at multiple dimensional scales: at the quantum substrate, in geometric space, through energy and entropy, in chemical bonds, through biological life and evolution, in minds and consciousness, across social structures and institutions, within symbol systems and language, through normative obligations and values, embedded in technological systems, operating according to systemic laws, unfolding in time, and extending into modality and reflexivity.

This framework traces the progressive emergence of complexity from the most fundamental substrate through increasingly abstract and reflexive orders. It provides a systematic map for understanding how reality organises itself across 18 distinct tiers of emergence, from quantum mechanics through transcendental philosophy. Without this systematic mapping, analyses typically truncate to whatever tier is convenient (often technical/physical), leading to category errors, missing interventions, and repeated failures.

## Why does the Framework of Dimensions matter for Hiddens work?

Dimensional truncation and translation failure produce four major families of Hiddens:

**Tier truncation (Hidden-12 Reductionism)**: Analysis stops at a convenient tier (often technical or individual) and systematically excludes higher tiers (social, normative, reflexive) or lower-tier constraints. What is hidden is everything that exists above or below the chosen tier. A purely technical analysis of organisational failure misses normative dimensions (legitimacy, trust, values) where the actual root cause resides. A purely psychological explanation of conflict overlooks the institutional structures and incentive systems that drive behaviour.

**Translation failure (Hidden-6 Distortion)**: Phenomena that properly belong to higher tiers are misrepresented in lower-tier terms. Trust is converted to information; meaning is flattened to data; legitimacy is reduced to compliance. What is hidden is the non-substitutable character of higher-tier realities.

**Paradigm boundaries (Hidden-13 Paradigm)**: Dimensions that exist beyond the current analytical paradigm remain categorically invisible. Where paradigm boundaries prevent naming and measuring, phenomena disappear not because they are absent but because the language and models lack categories to capture them.

**Observer blindness (positional and perspectival Hiddens)**: Analysis conducted from a single vantage point systematically omits dimensions that would be salient from other positions. Technical expertise sees technical dimensions; executive roles see organisational dimensions; ground-level workers see implementation dimensions. The same phenomenon appears as entirely different sets of dimensions from different positions.

Without this framework, dimensional incompleteness remains latent—invisible until it generates failure, creates category errors, or blocks understanding. With it, dimensional analysis becomes a repeatable discipline that detects what is missing and why.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises dimensional analysis through six core elements:

1. **An 18-tier taxonomy** (Tier 0–17) containing 110+ dimensions, ranging from quantum substrate through hypothetical and speculative dimensions, providing a comprehensive reference map for any complex phenomenon.

2. **Cross-tier relationship patterns** (emergence, supervenience, downward causation, interpenetration, recursive embedding) that explain how higher-tier phenomena arise from and reshape lower-tier substrates, enabling correct causal reasoning across scales.

3. **Five transversal axes** (complexity, information, value, temporal, scale) that cut across all tiers and provide universal sanity checks on dimensional analysis, preventing errors that would otherwise remain invisible within a single-tier frame.

4. **Theoretical limits analysis** (ineffability, recursion, complexity, observer) that explicitly acknowledges what cannot be resolved through further dimensional analysis and requires robustness and humility rather than certainty.

5. **Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** that surface the characteristic sources of dimensional invisibility (tier truncation, translation failure, paradigm boundaries, observer positioning) and provide systematic detection and remediation moves.

6. **A six-step application protocol** that moves from phenomenon framing through tier identification, dimension specification, cross-tier mapping, Hiddens scanning, and synthesis to actionable implications and monitoring.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow. It is designed for repeated use: initial dimensional scans establish what tiers are relevant; evidence gathering and testing refine dimension profiles; cross-tier relationships are validated or revised; and monitoring tracks whether new dimensions emerge or tier relationships shift.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework early—at intake, when analysts disagree about what "the real problem" is, when solutions keep failing despite evidence of good diagnosis, and when analysis feels complete but interventions backfire.

The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch tier identification and transversal axis spotting) and Investigative Run Mode (deep dimensional profiling and full Hiddens scanning). Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: run a tier relevance scan (Tier 0–17), identify salient dimensions per tier, test transversal axes, and run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high, evidence is contested, multiple framings conflict, or tier interactions are unclear.

The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all tier and dimensional claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording disagreements and residual unknowns rather than collapsing them into false consensus. For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Dimensions are not merely spatial axes but represent distinct modes of existence, organisation, and comprehension. Each dimension introduces qualitatively new possibilities that did not exist at lower levels. This framework traces the progressive emergence of complexity from the most fundamental substrate through increasingly abstract and reflexive orders.

The Framework of Dimensions provides a systematic map for understanding how reality organises itself across multiple levels of abstraction, from quantum substrates through geometric space, biological organisation, cognitive processes, social structures, and ultimately to the transcendental conditions of possibility themselves.

A "dimension" in this framework is any distinct axis of variation that is analytically independent (can vary without necessarily changing other dimensions) and operationally meaningful (can be observed, measured, or reasoned about). Dimensions are organised into 18 tiers, each representing an emergent order where new properties and causal mechanisms come into being.

The framework addresses a foundational problem: without explicit dimensional mapping, analyses tend to:
- truncate to convenient tiers (usually technical),
- collapse multi-tier phenomena into single-tier explanations (category error),
- miss dimensions that don't fit current paradigms,
- treat tier-level interventions as if they operate at different tiers,
- ignore cross-tier coupling and reflexive effects.

This framework forces completeness checks, prevents reductionism, enables correct tier-level intervention design, and makes visible where translation work is required between tiers.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

**Dimension (in this framework):** A distinct mode of existence, organisation, or comprehension; an axis of variation that need not be spatial. Dimensions include physical extension, informational structure, cognition, social organisation, and normative constraints.

**Tier:** An emergent order of dimensions. Higher tiers depend on (but are not reducible to) lower tiers.

**Cross-tier relationship:** A structured link between tiers (e.g., emergence, downward causation) describing how higher-order phenomena arise from and reshape lower-order substrates.

- In scope:
  - The 18-tier hierarchy (Tier 0 through Tier 17) and the dimension catalogues within each tier
  - Cross-tier relationships and transversal axes
  - Theoretical limits and a practical analysis protocol
  - Series-standard hiddens source analysis and Hiddens checks
- Out of scope:
  - Formal proofs of tier completeness
  - Exhaustive domain-specific measurement standards for each dimension
  - Claims that all phenomena can be fully captured dimensionally (limits are explicit)

**Dimensions:** Quantum Substrate (probabilistic, superposed, entangled ground); Field Substrate (continuous fields pervading spacetime); Vacuum/Potential Substrate (ground state and virtual fluctuations).

# 3. Core Dimensions Taxonomy (Detailed Tier-by-Tier Catalogue)

## 3.0 Canonical Rule & Deviations
- Series canonical baseline: 6 meta-categories × 5 types = 30 (used by many series frameworks).
- **Deviation (by design):** This framework is organised as **18 tiers (Tier 0–17)**, containing **110+ dimensions**. The 6×5 baseline does not apply without collapsing tiers and losing meaning.
- Preserving comparability: Use the Tier IDs and dimension codes (e.g., 5.3) as stable anchors; extensions and refinements follow Section 3.19 guidance.

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check (mandatory)

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)
| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Tier truncation | Stops at a convenient tier (often technical/individual) and excludes higher tiers (social, normative) or lower-tier constraints (physical, biological). | Force a complete Tier 0–17 relevance scan; justify exclusions; test whether omitted tiers could dominate outcomes. |
| 2 | Geometric reductionism | Treats all dimensions as spatial/quantitative and misses qualitative, normative, or reflexive dimensions. | Explicitly include Tier 8–9 and Tier 14–15 checks; separate measurement convenience from ontological necessity. |
| 3 | Fungibility illusion | Assumes one dimension can substitute for another (e.g., money for trust; information for legitimacy). | Map conversion paths and frictions; identify non-substitutable constraints; use Resource and Legitimacy frameworks as cross-checks. |
| 4 | Category lock-in | Forces phenomena into existing tiers/dimensions, ignoring emergent or boundary-crossing constructs. | Use Tier 16 and Hidden type 29 (Category Absence) prompts; allow new categories; document uncertainty. |
| 5 | Translation failure | Mis-maps constructs across tiers (e.g., using individual psychology to explain institutional behaviour without mediation). | Require explicit cross-tier mapping; state mediation mechanisms; test for downward causation and supervenience constraints. |
| 6 | Normative smuggling | Introduces value judgments as if they were descriptive facts, or denies normative dimensions that are actually decisive. | Separate descriptive vs normative claims; run Tier 9 scan; make value assumptions explicit and contestable. |
| 7 | Observer invisibility | Assumes a 'view from nowhere' and ignores the analyst's position, incentives, and tooling constraints. | Run positional and perspectival checks; document vantage point and constraints; invite adversarial review. |
| 8 | Reflexivity neglect | Ignores that models and narratives can change the system (feedback between representation and reality). | Apply Tier 14–15 checks; test for self-fulfilling/self-defeating predictions; design monitoring for reflexive loops. |
| 9 | Limit denial | Treats limitations (ineffability, unknowability, recursion) as solvable with more analysis. | Perform explicit limit analysis; set decision thresholds under uncertainty; adopt robustness and safety margins. |
| 10 | Speculative overreach | Confuses Tier 17 hypotheticals with operationally actionable dimensions; overweights speculative narratives. | Tag speculative claims; separate scenario exploration from commitments; demand evidence pathways and falsifiability. |

## 8.1.1 Theoretical Limits (mandatory acknowledgement layer)
The Framework of Dimensions encounters fundamental limits that cannot be overcome through further extension.

| Limit type | Description | Operational implication |
|---|---|---|
| Ineffability Limit | Some aspects of reality may resist dimensional capture entirely — qualia, the present moment, Being itself. The framework can point to this limit but cannot incorporate what lies beyond it. | Maintain humility; consider narrative/phenomenological accounts and robustness; avoid false precision. |
| Recursion Limit | Self-reference generates paradoxes. A dimension that contains itself, or a tier that classifies all tiers including itself, encounters logical difficulties. | Guard against self-reference paradox; use meta-controls and adversarial testing; avoid infinite regress in modelling. |
| Complexity Limit | As dimensions multiply, the combinatorial space of possible dimensional configurations explodes. Practical analysis requires selection and simplification. | Select minimal sufficient dimensions; prioritise by consequence; use staged analysis. |
| Observer Limit | Any dimensional analysis is conducted from somewhere — a cognitive agent in a social context using representational tools. The framework cannot achieve a view from nowhere. | Document vantage point and incentives; triangulate with multiple perspectives and independent evidence. |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) Run Tier relevance scan (Tier 0–17) and record exclusions with justification.
2) Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan across all six meta-categories (assume hiddens exist).
3) Shortlist high-consequence hiddens (Tier 2), and assign at least one detection and remediation move per item.
4) Re-run hiddens sources with an adversarial reviewer, focusing on framing, aggregation/scale, perspectival lock-in, and paradigm boundaries.
5) Record residual unknowns and limits explicitly; design robustness and monitoring.
6) When Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan from OG v2.5 §7.5 (thirteen scans across four families: social/organisational, structural/systemic, temporal, action-induced).

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)
Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- High consequence (safety, regulatory, existential, severe harm potential)
- Adversarial context / strategic concealment likely
- Persistent disagreement or inconsistent evidence
- Repeat failures despite prior interventions
- Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement across hidden types
- Decision-critical residual unknowns about tier completeness or cross-tier relationships

Tier 3 execution (per OG v2.5 §D.6.10):
- Expands beyond Tier 1 categories into the full Hiddens taxonomy (full-spectrum exploration).
- Tightens controls: stronger provenance requirements, more explicit competing hypotheses, more rigorous disconfirming tests.
- When adversarial intent is plausible: adds concealment vectors and independent corroboration paths.
- May justify near-full group coverage (G1–G6) when warranted by routing decision tree branch A6 (OG v2.5 §4.3).

# 10. Framework Principles (mandatory)

## 10.1 Principles Table
| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| Dimensional completeness before optimisation | Do not optimise within a truncated tier set; first verify that the dimension set is sufficient to represent the phenomenon without category error. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms; mandatory for "Stable" status)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Axiological | Pertaining to value, worth, and what matters. From Greek axios (worthy). The axiological dimension concerns what is good, important, or significant. |
| Configuration Space | The abstract mathematical space containing all possible arrangements of a system's components. Each point represents one complete configuration of the system. |
| Counterfactual | What would have been the case if circumstances had been different. Counterfactual reasoning explores alternative possibilities and unrealised histories. |
| Dimension | A distinct mode of existence, organisation, or comprehension along which phenomena can vary independently. Not limited to spatial extension but includes any axis of variation. |
| Downward Causation | Causal influence from higher-level phenomena on lower-level constituents. E.g., mental states affecting brain chemistry, or institutional rules shaping individual behaviour. |
| Emergence | The arising of novel properties at higher levels of organisation that are not predictable from or reducible to lower-level components. The whole exceeds the sum of parts. |
| Epistemic | Pertaining to knowledge, knowing, or the conditions of knowing. From Greek episteme (knowledge). Epistemic dimensions concern what can be known and how. |
| Eschatological | Pertaining to end-states, final purposes, or ultimate outcomes. From Greek eschatos (last). The eschatological dimension concerns where things are ultimately heading. |
| Homeostasis | The maintenance of stable internal conditions despite external fluctuations. A key property of living systems that distinguishes them from equilibrium systems. |
| Ineffable | That which cannot be expressed or captured in language or conceptual frameworks. The ineffable marks the limit of what dimensional analysis can articulate. |
| Intentionality | The directedness or aboutness of mental states. Thoughts, beliefs, and desires are about something — they point beyond themselves to objects or states of affairs. |
| Interpenetration | The simultaneous presence of multiple dimensions from different tiers in any actual phenomenon. Real events exist across many dimensions at once. |
| Liminal | Pertaining to thresholds, transitions, and boundaries. From Latin limen (threshold). Liminal dimensions exist at the edges between other dimensions. |
| Meta-Cognitive | Cognition about cognition; thinking about thinking. Meta-cognitive dimensions involve self-awareness and self-regulation of mental processes. |
| Modal | Pertaining to modes of being: actuality, possibility, necessity, contingency. Modal dimensions concern not just what is but what could be or must be. |
| Morphogenesis | The biological process by which an organism develops its shape and structure. The developmental unfolding from undifferentiated cells to complex form. |
| Normative | Pertaining to norms, standards, and how things ought to be. Normative dimensions involve values, obligations, and evaluative judgments rather than mere description. |
| Omega Point | A hypothetical final state of maximum complexity and consciousness toward which the universe evolves. Associated with Teilhard de Chardin and Frank Tipler. |
| Ontological | Pertaining to being, existence, or what there is. From Greek on (being). Ontological dimensions concern the fundamental nature and categories of reality. |
| Ontogeny | The development of an individual organism from conception to maturity. Contrasts with phylogeny (evolution of species over generations). |
| Phase Space | The mathematical space of all possible states of a system, typically defined by position and momentum coordinates. Each point represents one complete state. |
| Phylogeny | The evolutionary history and development of a species or group of organisms over generations. The tree of evolutionary descent. |
| Prudential | Pertaining to practical wisdom and self-interest. Prudential considerations concern what is good for an individual agent. |
| Qualia | The subjective, qualitative properties of conscious experience. The redness of red, the painfulness of pain — what it feels like from the inside. |
| Recursive | Self-referential; applying to itself. Recursive structures contain themselves as parts, like mirrors facing each other or definitions that refer to themselves. |
| Reflexive | Turning back on itself; self-aware. Reflexive dimensions involve consciousness or analysis directed at its own operations. |
| Semiotic | Pertaining to signs, symbols, and meaning. From Greek semeion (sign). Semiotic dimensions concern how things stand for other things. |
| Strange Loop | A self-referential structure where moving through levels of a hierarchy unexpectedly returns to the starting point. Identified by Douglas Hofstadter as central to consciousness. |
| Substrate | The underlying foundation or base upon which something exists or operates. The substrate dimensions are the preconditions for all other dimensions. |
| Supervenience | A relation of dependence where higher-level properties cannot change without changes in lower-level properties, though the lower does not determine the higher. |
| Tier | A level in the hierarchical organisation of dimensions. Each tier represents a qualitatively distinct level of emergence and organisation. |
| Transcendental | Pertaining to the necessary conditions of possibility for experience or knowledge. What must be the case for anything to appear or be known at all. |
| Transversal | Cutting across multiple tiers rather than belonging to any single tier. Transversal dimensions apply at every level of the framework. |
