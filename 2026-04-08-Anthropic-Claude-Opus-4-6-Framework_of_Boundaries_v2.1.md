# Introduction

## What is Boundaries?

A boundary is not merely a line on a map or a rule on paper—it is a **structured division that separates one regime from another**, with causal, epistemic, and normative consequences. Boundaries define what counts, who belongs, what is knowable, what is permitted, and what persists as an identity. They structure reality: they determine scope, jurisdiction, responsibility, and the feasibility of action. Yet boundaries are often treated as background infrastructure, taken for granted until they fail. When a boundary is unclear, contested, or hidden, problems cascade: mis-scoped problems, accountability gaps, unmanaged edge cases, and surprise failures at interfaces.

This framework makes boundaries **explicit objects of analysis** by providing a comprehensive taxonomy (six meta-categories, thirty types), a dimensional profiling scheme (five dimensions that characterise any boundary), an operational lens (how boundaries are drawn, maintained, crossed, moved, dissolved), and an interface model (transition zones, crossing points, intersections). By surfacing the location, type, properties, and governance of boundaries, the framework enables systematic detection and remediation of boundary-related blindspots.

## Why does Boundaries matter for Hiddens work?

Boundary invisibility is itself a Hidden—a reality-relevant factor that remains invisible to decision-makers, shaping their decisions without being made explicit. This framework surfaces several families of hiding mechanisms:

**Boundary Blindness** (Hidden-3 Observability, Hidden-24 Contextual) arises because boundaries often sit at the edges of attention; they are so fundamental that they become invisible until they fail. A team may not see a jurisdictional boundary until accountability for a failure becomes contested. A system designer may not see a data boundary until information flows across it unexpectedly.

**Boundary Denial and Distortion** (Hidden-6 Distortion, Hidden-8 Framing) occur when incentives, metrics, or narratives suppress recognition of inconvenient boundaries. A cost-saving initiative may drive denial of safety boundaries. A success narrative may obscure the boundary between "rules on paper" and "rules in practice." Strategic actors may conceal exceptions and shadow crossings that violate formal boundaries.

**Positional and Contextual Blindness** (Hidden-21 Positional, Hidden-24 Contextual) arise because boundaries experienced by frontline staff differ from those perceived by distant leadership. A boundary that is "clear" from the center may be "porous" at the edge. The same boundary applies differently across contexts, yet a single definition is imposed universally.

**Legacy Lock-in** (Hidden-17 Ghost/Lock-in) allows obsolete boundaries to persist because changing them requires crossing other boundaries (jurisdictional, political, cultural). A safety boundary that made sense in an old system persists despite enabling new risks. A data classification boundary that no longer reflects current value creates frustration but remains technically enforced.

Without this framework, these Hiddens remain latent: invisible until they generate failure, regret, or cascade effects. With the framework, boundary analysis becomes a repeatable, testable discipline that flags misclassification risks early and enables deliberate governance.

## What does this framework produce?

The framework operationalises boundary analysis through six core elements:

1. **A taxonomy of 30 boundary types** organised into six meta-categories (Physical, Conceptual, Social, Epistemic, Normative, Identity) that recur across contexts, allowing rapid classification and comparison.

2. **Five cross-cutting dimensions** (Sharpness, Permeability, Stability, Visibility, Constructedness) that profile any boundary and explain why different agents classify the same boundary differently.

3. **Dynamics patterns** (Drawing, Maintaining, Crossing, Moving, Dissolving) that describe how boundaries are created and changed, enabling detection of unmanaged operations and sequencing risks.

4. **Three interface types** (Transition Zones, Crossing Points, Intersections) that make visible where and how boundaries interact, clash, or nest—zones of high risk and opportunity.

5. **Hiddens source analysis and tiered Hiddens scans** that surface the four systematic sources of boundary blindness (Boundary Blindness, False Imposition, Category Neglect, Fixity Assumption) and the six categories of visibility failure (Perceptual, Systemic, Informational, Temporal, Relational, Ontological).

6. **A repeatable application protocol** that moves from boundary identification through dimensional profiling, operational analysis, interface mapping, Hiddens checking, and integration handoff to other frameworks.

The framework populates standard registers (Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, Information Requests) that integrate with the broader Analytical Series investigation workflow. It is designed for repeated use: boundaries are classified, decisions are made and implemented, boundary drift is monitored, and re-profiling is triggered by threshold breaches or governance events.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework early when situation boundaries are unclear, when scope or jurisdiction is contested, or when edge-case incidents cluster around interfaces. It is most valuable at scenario intake (to establish what we are including/excluding and at what level), during major decision gates (to validate whether our boundary model still holds), and when interventions fail or boundary disputes emerge (signals of misclassification or governance failure). The framework works in both Rapid Run Mode (light-touch boundary inventory and interface spotting) and Investigative Run Mode (full dimensional profiling, boundary operations analysis, and Hiddens scanning).

Default execution is Light Depth Framework Execution: identify focal boundaries, assign types, profile key dimensions, spot obvious interfaces and crossing risks, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan. Escalate to Full Depth when consequence is high (safety, jurisdictional authority, irreversible harm), evidence is contested, boundary disputes are active, or you are approaching a critical boundary operation (moving, dissolving). The framework's companion prompt discipline rules (OG v2.5, §D.3) require tagging all boundary claims as Fact, Interpretation, Assumption, or Unknown, and explicitly recording disagreements, uncertainties, and residual unknowns rather than collapsing them into false consensus.

For LLM execution, see §1.6 for the complete LLM integration specification, standard registers, and copy-ready run prompts. The framework is designed to be directly executable by an LLM given a scenario and the Operating Guide, producing a disciplined boundary analysis with explicit Hiddens, F/I/A/U tagging, and bounded closure.

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Boundaries structure reality: they define what counts, who belongs, what is knowable, what is permitted, and what persists as an identity. Boundary failures are a common root cause of repeated organisational and analytic errors: mis-scoped problems, contested accountability, unmanaged interfaces, and surprise "edge-case" incidents.

This framework provides:
- a **taxonomy** of boundary kinds (6×5 = 30 types),
- a **profiling scheme** (five dimensions),
- an **operational lens** (how boundaries are drawn, maintained, crossed, moved, dissolved),
- an **interface model** (transition zones, crossing points, intersections),
- a **hiddens source analysis and Hiddens cross-check** layer, and
- a standard **application protocol** with concrete artefacts for downstream work.

# 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions of Any Boundary

## 4.1 Dimensions Table (mandatory)
| Dimension name | Description | Indicative values / range | How to assess quickly | Analytic use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sharpness | How cleanly does the boundary divide inside from outside? | Crisp – Sharp, clear boundary with no ambiguity; clean division between inside and outside; Defined – Clear boundary with minimal ambiguity; edge cases are rare; Fuzzy – Boundary with zone of ambiguity; gradual transition rather than sharp line; Vague – Poorly defined boundary; significant uncertainty about where it lies; Absent – No discernible boundary; continuous gradation without division | Look for edge cases: can reasonable observers disagree on membership? Are there transition zones? | Determines whether to treat the boundary as a line, a band, or a contested zone (impacts measurement, enforcement, and communication). |
| Permeability | How easily can things cross the boundary? | Impermeable – Nothing crosses; absolute barrier between inside and outside; Selective – Some things cross, others don't; boundary filters what passes; Permeable – Most things can cross with some difficulty; boundary slows but doesn't stop; Porous – Easy to cross; boundary barely impedes passage; Open – No impediment to crossing; boundary marks distinction without creating barrier | List what crosses (people, data, material, influence) and how; test friction, gates, and exceptions. | Guides control design: impermeable boundaries need enforcement; porous boundaries need monitoring and buffering. |
| Stability | How much does the boundary move over time or under pressure? | Fixed – Unchanging; boundary remains constant over time and across conditions; Stable – Mostly constant; boundary changes slowly or rarely; Dynamic – Regularly changing; boundary shifts in response to conditions; Fluid – Constantly changing; boundary is in continuous flux; Contested – Actively disputed; multiple parties attempting to define or move the boundary | Assess whether location/definition shifts with context, incentives, or time; identify known drift triggers. | Sets review cadence and governance posture (fixed vs adaptive boundary management). |
| Visibility | How apparent is the boundary to relevant observers? | Explicit – Clearly marked and announced; boundary is obvious and acknowledged; Visible – Detectable with ordinary attention; boundary can be seen if one looks; Implicit – Not marked but recognisable; boundary exists but must be inferred; Hidden – Not apparent without special effort; boundary is concealed or obscure; Invisible – Cannot be detected directly; boundary's existence must be deduced or revealed | Ask who can see it and by what means; check for hidden boundaries (secrecy, opacity, structural invisibility). | Selects assurance methods; low visibility requires instrumentation, access mapping, and independent review. |
| Constructedness | To what extent is the boundary found vs made (natural vs conventional vs imposed vs emergent)? | Natural – Exists independently of human activity; would exist without us; Discovered – Pre-exists but identified by human inquiry; found rather than made; Conventional – Created by agreement or practice; exists because we treat it as existing; Imposed – Deliberately created by specific agents; boundary by design or fiat; Emergent – Arises from interaction without deliberate creation; neither natural nor imposed | Identify the sustaining mechanism: physics, discovery, agreement, fiat, or emergent interaction. | Determines change strategy: natural boundaries require working with constraints; conventional/imposed boundaries can be renegotiated/redesigned. |

# 5. Dynamics (Boundary Operations Over Time)

## 5.1 Boundary Operations Table (mandatory)
| # | Dynamic pattern | Signature | Mechanism | Why it matters | Typical mitigations (optional) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Drawing | New lines are introduced (new rules, fences, definitions, jurisdictions). | Creating a boundary where none existed; establishing new divisions | Creates new exclusion/inclusion effects and new failure modes; often generates contestation. | Make criteria explicit; test edge cases; define owners and review cadence. |
| 2 | Maintaining | Resources are spent to keep the boundary intact (enforcement, policing, rituals, upkeep). | Keeping a boundary in place over time; active preservation | Indicates boundary has stakes; maintenance costs and incentives reveal power and priorities. | Audit maintenance mechanisms and incentives; monitor drift; formalise exception handling. |

| 5 | Dissolving | The distinction collapses or is deliberately removed (integration, deregulation, merger, declassification). | Eliminating a boundary entirely; creating continuity | Alters identity and governance; removes controls but may remove friction and conflict. | Plan transitions; reassess risks and responsibilities; redesign controls for the new continuity. |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)
1) Run Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories (assume hidden boundaries exist).
2) Shortlist relevant hidden types (Tier 2) and rate detectability, agency, and consequence.
3) Assign at least one detection and one remediation move per high-consequence hidden; link actions to interface type(s) (Transition zones / Crossing points / Intersections).
4) Re-run boundary hiddens sources; document residual unknowns and define escalation triggers.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full Hiddens run)
Escalate to a full 30-type Hiddens scan + detection/remediation mapping if any apply:
- High consequence (safety, systemic resilience, regulatory exposure, irreversible harm)
- Strong incentives to conceal or distort boundaries (exceptions, access, jurisdiction, enforcement)
- Persistent disputes over inclusion/exclusion, authority, or definitions
- Repeated "edge-case" incidents at interfaces despite fixes
- Evidence of mirage boundaries (rules-on-paper vs rules-in-practice)

## 12.2 Integration Notes (optional)
Use this framework whenever:
- the situation is mis-scoped or "keeps moving",
- accountability and jurisdiction are contested,
- edge-case incidents occur at interfaces,
- definitions and categories are ambiguous or politicised,
- hidden exceptions or shadow crossings are suspected,
- boundary drift and legacy lock-in constrain feasible change.

### Appendix A — Consolidated 30-type table (single view)
| # | Meta-category | Type | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | I. Physical Boundaries | Spatial | Where one physical region or object ends and another begins (geographic, geometric, and territorial edges). |
| 2 | I. Physical Boundaries | Temporal | Where one period, phase, or event ends and another begins (deadlines, lifecycles, epochs, and windows). |
| 3 | I. Physical Boundaries | Material | Interfaces between substances and media (composition, containment, membranes, and material transitions). |
| 4 | I. Physical Boundaries | Energetic | Thresholds and gradients in energy/force/pressure/temperature that separate regimes (barriers, tipping points). |
| 5 | I. Physical Boundaries | Scalar | Where a phenomenon changes character across levels of scale (micro↔macro), including detectability and aggregation limits. |
| 6 | II. Conceptual Boundaries | Categorical | What counts as an instance of a category and where category membership stops (classification edges). |
| 7 | II. Conceptual Boundaries | Definitional | What a term includes/excludes based on definitions; necessary/sufficient conditions and scope constraints. |
| 8 | II. Conceptual Boundaries | Semantic | Where meanings shift; zones of ambiguity, polysemy, and pragmatic drift in language and interpretation. |
| 9 | II. Conceptual Boundaries | Logical | What is consistent/possible vs inconsistent/impossible within a given logical space or constraint set. |
| 10 | II. Conceptual Boundaries | Disciplinary | Where one field's methods/assumptions apply and another's begin; frame conflicts and handoff boundaries. |
| 11 | III. Social Boundaries | Group | Who is inside/outside a group; membership, belonging, and exclusion mechanisms. |
| 12 | III. Social Boundaries | Institutional | Where an organisation's authority, responsibility, and identity begin/end (roles, remit, accountability). |
| 13 | III. Social Boundaries | Jurisdictional | Where one authority's legitimate power ends and another begins (governance and enforcement edges). |
| 14 | III. Social Boundaries | Class | Divisions of status, wealth, privilege, and stratification that shape access and outcomes. |
| 15 | III. Social Boundaries | Cultural | Where one cultural system of meaning and practice ends and another begins; norms, rituals, and identity markers. |
| 16 | IV. Epistemic Boundaries | Knowledge | Edges of established knowledge; what is known vs unknown, and where uncertainty is irreducible or unresolved. |
| 17 | IV. Epistemic Boundaries | Knowability | Boundaries set by the limits of what can be known (in principle, not just in practice); principled unknowability. |
| 18 | IV. Epistemic Boundaries | Methodological | Limits imposed by methods; what a tool/approach can and cannot reveal (measurement and model horizons). |
| 19 | IV. Epistemic Boundaries | Evidential | Edges of what evidence can credibly support; inference limits and evidentiary standards. |
| 20 | IV. Epistemic Boundaries | Perspective | What is visible/knowable from a given standpoint; epistemic constraints of position and role. |
| 21 | V. Normative Boundaries | Legal | What is lawful/unlawful and the jurisdictional reach of legal systems. |
| 22 | V. Normative Boundaries | Moral | What is ethically permissible/impermissible; boundaries of acceptable harm, duty, and rights. |
| 23 | V. Normative Boundaries | Professional | What is appropriate within a profession; role obligations, standards, and conduct limits. |
| 24 | V. Normative Boundaries | Conventional | What is socially normal/abnormal and proper/improper within a community or context. |
| 25 | V. Normative Boundaries | Sacred | What is inviolable vs profane; taboo, reverence, and boundary-protection through sanction. |
| 26 | VI. Identity Boundaries | Personal | What makes an individual the 'same person' over time; self-boundaries and continuity criteria. |
| 27 | VI. Identity Boundaries | Organisational | What makes an organisation itself (mission, brand, remit); limits of institutional selfhood. |
| 28 | VI. Identity Boundaries | Species | Membership boundaries of biological kinds; classification and reproductive/ecological criteria. |
| 29 | VI. Identity Boundaries | Essential | What must be present for something to be what it is; necessary features and identity conditions. |
| 30 | VI. Identity Boundaries | Developmental | How identity persists through change; thresholds where development becomes transformation into 'something else'. |

### Appendix C — Crossing control checklist (Type B)
| Control question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| What is the authorised crossing point? | Reduces shadow crossings and ambiguous handoffs. |
| Who is authorised to cross (and by what proof)? | Defines accountability and limits abuse. |
| What is logged and how is it reviewed? | Supports detectability and post-incident reconstruction. |
| What are the exceptions and how are they approved? | Prevents silent boundary erosion and secret pathways. |
| What happens when crossing fails? | Defines containment, rollback, and escalation at boundaries. |
| When is the boundary re-reviewed or redefined? | Manages drift and contestedness proactively. |
