## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Legitimacy framework maps recognised rightfulness of power, action, and authority across six meta-categories (Legal, Moral, Cultural, Procedural, Performance, Relational) with thirty core types, enabling practitioners to diagnose which legitimacy bases are relied upon, which are contested, and where portfolio vulnerabilities create crisis risk. It assesses legitimacy through dimensional profiling (source basis mix, audience alignment, contestability, internalisation depth, temporal character), identifies dynamic patterns of erosion and substitution, and surfaces legitimacy-specific Hiddens (narrative distortion, framing closure, audience fragmentation, shadow bargaining, positional exclusion, category confusion). It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, Hypothesis/Test Backlog, and Intervention Portfolio with audience-specific legitimacy risks, remediation designs, and monitoring dashboards.

## Section 4: Condensed Core Taxonomy

| Meta-category | Core types (names only) | Diagnostic question (1 sentence) |
|---|---|---|
| I. Legal–Formal Legitimacy | Constitutional, Rule-of-Law, Mandate & Office-Holding, Jurisdictional, Procedural-Compliance | Is authority lawfully granted, within delegated scope, applied consistently, and supported by fair procedures and independent remedies? |
| II. Moral–Substantive Legitimacy | Justice-Based, Rights-Respecting, Welfare & Harm-Reduction, Integrity & Non-Corruption, Proportionality & Restraint | Is action substantively right by moral standards (justice, rights, harm reduction, integrity, proportionate means)? |
| III. Traditional & Cultural Legitimacy | Customary & Precedent-Based, Sacred & Religious-Authority, Identity & Narrative-Fit, Heritage & Continuity, Local-Embeddedness | Does action fit recognised traditions, identities, symbols, and cultural continuity within community memory and values? |
| IV. Procedural & Participatory Legitimacy | Electoral & Consent-Based, Deliberative & Reason-Giving, Transparency & Accountability, Representation & Inclusiveness, Due-Process & Remedy | Was decision made through fair processes with meaningful voice, reasons, transparency, representation, and accessible remedies? |
| V. Performance & Output Legitimacy | Effectiveness & Problem-Solving, Security & Protection, Reliability & Predictability, Competence & Expertise, Responsiveness & Learning | Does authority deliver valued outcomes, solve problems reliably, provide security, maintain competence, and respond to feedback over time? |
| VI. Relational & Contested Legitimacy | Social-Recognition & Social-Licence, Peer & International, Relational-Justice, Counter-Legitimacy & Alternative-Claims | Who recognises authority and on what terms; how do competing claims, audiences, and relational experiences shape what counts as legitimate? |

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accumulation, Erosion & Path-Dependence | Legitimacy builds slowly through repeated confirmation; erodes through violations or betrayal; recovery is slow and constrained by path dependence. | Trust reserves accumulate via consistent behaviour; violations create memory and constrain future claims; early breaches establish low-trust baseline. | Identical actions accepted in one context rejected in another. Sudden failure after long success. Explains recovery horizons and temporal legitimacy debt. |
| Basis Substitution & Compensatory Logics | Actors attempt to substitute one basis for another (e.g., performance compensating for procedural deficits; legal authority substituted for moral legitimacy). | Justifications shift as vulnerabilities are exposed; some bases partially compensate while others are non-substitutable constraints. Substitution attempts signal weakness. | Misdiagnosis of substitutability leads to failed interventions (PR instead of rights, performance instead of transparency). Portfolio imbalance. |
| Cross-Audience Divergence & Polarisation | Different audiences recognise different bases; legitimacy becomes segmented or polarised as audiences separate, rivals emerge, and echo chambers harden. | Distinct value standards, information environments, and identities produce divergent recognition; rivals exploit splits; isolation grows. | Governance deadlock and contested authority. Interventions for one audience delegitimise in another. Institutional paralysis. |
| Crisis, Exception & Re-Negotiation | Crisis increases tolerance for exceptional measures, then triggers re-evaluation as fear subsides and exceptional costs accumulate; exceptionalism persists into legitimacy cliff. | Emergency legitimacy is lent under necessity; if safeguards, sunsets, and proportionality are weak, legitimacy depletes post-crisis when publics expect return to normal. | Explains post-crisis backlash and windows of opportunity for reform. Anticipates legitimacy cliffs and why emergency powers are dangerous if unbounded. |
| Delegation, Proxy & Layered Legitimacy | Legitimacy flows through delegated chains; agents can spend or squander delegator legitimacy; proxy behaviour feeds back to delegator credibility and creates accountability gaps. | Delegators lend legitimacy to agents; agents build or deplete it; divergence from values damages delegator credibility. Accountability layers create diffusion. | Bad frontline behaviour delegitimises entire institutions. Explains where accountability gaps hide. Key for outsourcing and contractor behaviour. |
| Narrative Capture & Coordinated Messaging | Single narrative or messaging discipline displaces competing accounts; institutions converge on one story; alternative framings suppressed; appears as consensus. | Shared briefing, institutional pressure, reputational incentives, or deliberate coordination produce message discipline; alternatives ridiculed. Enforced conformity. | Creates illusion of legitimacy while masking contestation and diversity. Brittle: narrative breaks suddenly. Prevents adaptation and course correction. |
| Institutional Encrustation & Legitimacy Rituals | Legitimacy practices become decoupled from substance; compliance theatre, performative consultation, and symbolic remedies multiply without addressing core contestation. | Organisations institutionalise safe practices without riskier substance; effort and budget flow to performance rather than remediation; innovation is risky. | Legitimacy appears stable while real acceptance erodes silently. Crisis is sudden when ritual adequacy fails. Learning and adaptation stall. |
| Information Fragmentation & Competing Legitimacy Narratives | Different stakeholders see different information and justifications; no integrated legitimacy picture exists; information asymmetry and misinformation thrive. | Access control, selective disclosure, siloed institutions, and communication fragmentation create narrative gaps; audiences lack shared ground. | Enables polarisation and prevents shared legitimacy diagnosis. Different stakeholders believe incompatible facts. Unification becomes harder over time. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What legitimacy signals (trust, grievances, sentiment, cooperation) are subtle or early-warning and may be missed until surprise backlash or defection occurs? |
| II (Systemic) | How do organisational power, reputation incentives, and fear of dissent filter reporting about legitimacy risks and suppress contestation signals? |
| III (Informational) | What does the information environment hide or reveal about legitimacy; is access symmetric; are audiences fragmented; are competing legitimacy claims visible? |
| IV (Temporal) | How do historical legitimacy breaches, institutional memory loss, and accumulated grievance shape present recognition and create brittleness under stress? |
| V (Relational) | Who is heard and who is systematically unheard in legitimacy claims; which groups are structurally excluded from voice; what alternative claims exist? |
| VI (Ontological) | What legitimacy standards and categories are assumed to be universal when they are audience-specific; where do groups mean different things by legitimacy itself? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Narrative Distortion & Spin (legitimacy rationales sanitised; true decision logic hidden; trade-offs and motives obscured)
- Legitimacy Mirage & Performance (apparent legitimacy created by coherence, repetition, staged consent, institutional authority rather than durable recognition; brittle)
- Framing Closure (invisible boundaries shape what is discussable: who counts, what harms count, what standards apply; legitimate alternatives excluded from consideration)
- Secrecy & Opacity (key facts, rationales withheld; legitimacy judgment impossible without information; audiences fill gaps with suspicion)
- Echo & False Consensus (same legitimacy claim repeated across institutions from shared sources; false consensus masks deep contestation)
- Audience Fragmentation & Information Asymmetry (different audiences receive different justifications; no integrated picture; incoherent narrative; polarisation)
- Shadow Bargaining & Backchannels (real legitimacy decisions occur informally while formal processes remain performative; true negotiations hidden)
- Institutional Amnesia (past legitimacy breaches, promises, grievances forgotten; system repeats legitimacy-depleting patterns)
- Positional Blindness & Exclusion (voice and authority asymmetric; powerful see consensus, affected see exclusion; marginal voices unheard)
- Category Confusion & Perspective Bias (disputes are category disputes, not factual; dominant lens excludes alternative standards; inresolvable "talking past each other")

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Narrative distortion and hidden decision logic | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Hiddens (Tier 2) | Traceability & rationale audit (Type A/C) | Publish decision logs, trade-offs, actual criteria. Require transparency and independent verification. Separate remediation from PR. |
| Legitimacy theatre and performative processes | Framework of Processes; Framework of Governance | Lived-experience testing and stakeholder probes (Type B/C) | Test genuine participation vs scripted consent. Measure acceptance across audiences. Strengthen binding decision routes. |
| Audience fragmentation and polarisation | Framework of Power & Influence; Framework of Agents & Personas | Cross-audience forums and translation sessions (Type B/C) | Map audiences and standards explicitly. Run deliberation across segments. Create boundary objects. Build unified decision narrative. |
| Positional exclusion and marginal voice silencing | Framework of Processes; Framework of Personas | Positional representation audit and voice inclusion (Type B) | Shift decision rights toward affected parties. Protect dissent. Strengthen remedy accessibility. Elevate marginal positions in governance. |
| Shadow bargaining and backchannels | Framework of Governance; Framework of Institutions | Formal channel strengthening and transparency (Type A/B) | Reduce friction and fear in formal processes. Formalise key decisions. Strengthen due process. Protect whistleblowing. Surface informal bargains and make accountable. |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. List focal authority claims or contested rightfulness incidents in the scenario; identify who claims legitimacy, on what bases, and who contests or accepts it.
2. Classify each legitimacy claim across the six meta-categories (I–VI) and assign primary types; note which bases are actively invoked and which are absent or weak.
3. For each focal claim, assess the five dimensions (source basis mix, audience scope and alignment, contestability and resilience, internalisation depth, temporal character); identify portfolio vulnerabilities and audience divergence.
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan (light): touch all six Hiddens meta-categories (Perceptual, Systemic, Informational, Temporal, Relational, Ontological); flag obvious hiding mechanisms (narrative gaps, fragmentation, exclusion, shadow decision-making).
5. Shortlist 3–5 highest-consequence legitimacy risks from Tier 1 scan; assign to one of three interface types (Type A: Authority/Mandate, Type B: Stakeholder/Participation, Type C: Narrative/Recognition); note detection/remediation actions; flag for escalation if high-consequence and high-uncertainty.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Map legitimacy landscape: list all authority claims, claimants, audiences, and contestation; create legitimacy inventory with basis mixes, audience segments, and signal summaries.
2. Classify all legitimacy claims (meta-categories I–VI, core types 1–30); document basis mixes, audience-specific standards, and detection signals for each claim.
3. Assess all five dimensions for each focal claim: basis mix and substitutability, audience scope/alignment/divergence, contestability/resilience/brittleness, internalisation depth (ritual vs genuine), temporal character (accumulation/debt, path dependence, crisis window).
4. Analyse all eight dynamic patterns; for each focal claim, assess which patterns are nascent/present/high-risk; identify cascades, feedback loops, and equivalence relations.
5. Map three interface types and remediation routes: Type A (Authority/Mandate governance, formal legitimacy), Type B (Stakeholder recognition/participation, relational legitimacy), Type C (Narrative/Recognition, external legitimacy); assign decision levers for each.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan (structured deepening): assess ~15 candidate Hiddens from §8.3 (Distortion, Mirage, Framing, Secret, Echo, Fragmentation, Shadow, Amnesia, Anachronism, Positional, Perspectival, Category Absence, Counter-Claims). For high-relevance Hiddens, identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; assign detection and remediation interfaces.
7. Generate closure artefacts: document legitimacy map with hotspots, residual Unknowns/Hiddens (mechanism + impact + next probe), monitoring dashboard (signal indicators, escalation triggers), intervention portfolio (portfolio by basis, by audience, by interface), and apply Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any Unknown could change decisions or accountability.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add shortlist of legitimacy-specific Hiddens (Distortion, Mirage, Framing, Shadow, Fragmentation, Positional, Echo, Amnesia, Category Absence, etc.) with mechanism, detectability, consequence, and detection/remediation interface. | Tier 1 scan completion; escalate any high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hidden to Tier 2 and apply Escalation Rule. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record legitimacy claim classifications, dimensional profiles, audience-specific standards, dynamic pattern presence/absence, signal interpretation, evidence quality, provenance. | After classification and dimensional profiling; note gaps (audience perception data, historical legitimacy incident records, alternative claim documentation, signal anomalies). |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about legitimacy trajectory (erosion/resilience), substitutability constraints, audience divergence, hidden contestation; propose tests (stakeholder perception probes, lived-experience sampling, narrative analysis, historical case review, shadow-bargaining discovery). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decisions. Prioritise tests that reduce high-consequence Unknowns. |
| Information Requests | Request legitimacy incident history, audience perception data (trust, grievances, exit signals), past legitimacy assessments, decision rationale documentation, participation records, narrative mapping, alternative claim evidence, remediation commitment tracking. | To reduce Unknowns and validate Hiddens detection; coordinate with Evidence, Governance, and Power & Influence frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we mapped all major legitimacy claims across six meta-categories, assessed audience-specific standards and divergence, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories, identified 3+ highest-consequence Hiddens with detection/remediation interfaces, and either resolved or escalated all high-stakes Unknowns to appropriate frameworks (Hiddens, Evidence, Governance, Power, Institutions)?
