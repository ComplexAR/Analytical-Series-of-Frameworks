## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Space and Place framework maps how geographic location, spatial relationships, infrastructure connectivity, place-meaning, and territorial governance shape what is materially possible, who has access to opportunities and resources, what visibility gaps hide, and how costs and benefits are distributed across locations. It classifies spatial phenomena into six meta-categories and thirty core types; maps five cross-cutting dimensions (scale, connectivity, visibility, place-meaning intensity, territorial contestedness); identifies twelve dynamic patterns that surface how spatial configurations change over time; and surfaces Hiddens families (geographic privilege blindness, spatial cost displacement, place-invisibility, territorial framing effects, infrastructure vulnerability, place-meaning erasure). It populates the Spatial Inventory, Access & Privilege Register, Infrastructure & Connectivity Register, Territorial Governance Register, and Hiddens Register, enabling downstream frameworks to account for location-dependent factors and detect spatial injustice.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Infrastructure lock-in and path dependence | Past infrastructure constrains future development; new development follows established corridors; shifting systems is very expensive. | Sunk costs; switching costs increase; users locate based on existing infrastructure; economic activity clusters. | Creates cumulative advantage for locations with good infrastructure; makes transitions expensive; constrains innovation. |
| Urban agglomeration and cumulative advantage | Urban centers grow; talent, investment, services concentrate; peripheral regions lose population and economic activity. | Agglomeration benefits attract more activity; cycle of concentration; talented migration to urban centers. | Creates winner cities and loser regions; geographic inequality increases; concentrates power and resources. |
| Connectivity cascade and systemic disruption | Improved connectivity opens new possibilities but creates new vulnerability; disruption cascades system-wide. | Improved infrastructure enables flows; dependency on connectivity increases; disruption anywhere cascades. | Increases vulnerability to location-specific disruptions that cascade; loss of local self-sufficiency; systemic fragility. |
| Spatial displacement of environmental and social cost | Economic activity concentrates; environmental and social costs displace to other locations; costs borne by powerless communities. | Profit centers are in powerful locations; costs displaced to peripheral locations; decision-makers invisible to consequences. | Creates environmental injustice; geographic inequality maintained through displacement; costs invisible to beneficiaries. |
| Place-meaning contestation and narrative conflict | Place-meanings are contested; new meanings imposed; old meanings erased; contestation shapes behavior and governance. | Change in place (urbanization, immigration, economic transformation) threatens existing meaning; new groups impose meanings; old residents resist. | Creates conflict and resistance; those losing meaning experience alienation; contestation prevents coordination. |
| Resource depletion and economic collapse | Resource-dependent communities face collapse if resources deplete; depletion invisible until imminent. | Specialized dependence on resources; depletion curves; information suppressed; collapse rapid when obvious. | Dependent communities face sudden unemployment; migration; ghost towns; communities unprepared for transition. |
| Territorial reorganization and governance disruption | Changes to territorial boundaries create winners and losers; disruption during transition; displaced experience injustice. | Reorganization redefines authority and property rights; those benefiting resist change; those harmed experience disruption. | Disruption during transition; uncertainty about new rules; reorganization often imposed on margins; lacks legitimacy. |
| Infrastructure failure cascades | Failure at critical nodes cascades through connected systems; cascade effects often unexpected. | High connectivity creates dependency on continuous infrastructure operation; failure unexpected; those at critical nodes have power. | Creates hidden vulnerability; those at critical nodes may not recognize responsibility; failure is catastrophic. |
| Spatial inequality persistence and lock-in | Geographic inequality tends to persist and increase; mechanisms of persistence invisible. | Regions starting advantaged attract more investment; disadvantaged regions lose talent and investment; cumulative advantage increases. | Inequality treated as inevitable; disadvantaged regions blamed; mobility increases inequality; power concentrated. |
| Access and opportunity barrier formation | Barriers to access become entrenched; those unable to access opportunities fall further behind. | Poor infrastructure prevents access; those unable to move remain in disadvantaged locations; investment concentrates elsewhere. | Creates locked-in disadvantage; lack of access perpetuates poverty; brain drain from inaccessible locations. |
| Governance authority creep and encroachment | Over time, boundaries shift; larger authority scope expands at expense of smaller; encroachment invisible until challenged. | Administrative changes and reorganization shift boundaries; larger jurisdictions expand scope; historical territories absorbed. | Those losing autonomy may not notice until critical decision; autonomy erosion gradual; small territories lack power to resist. |
| Place-based stigma as self-fulfilling prophecy | Places carry stigma; stigma affects investment and migration; lack of investment makes places actually poor; stigma becomes true. | Place labeled as bad; investors avoid; talent out-migrates; services decline; deprivation deepens; stigma becomes factual. | Stigmatized places remain trapped in disadvantage; stigma prevents investment and talent that could change conditions. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What geographic and climatic characteristics are being treated as irrelevant when they actually shape material possibility and constraint? |
| II (Systemic) | What infrastructure dependencies and bottlenecks are invisible to those who do not work through them; what cascades are created by high connectivity? |
| III (Informational) | What information about distant locations or consequences reaches decision-makers; what place-based narratives are shaping treatment of locations? |
| IV (Temporal) | What geographic constraints are treated as temporary when they are actually permanent; what lock-in effects are being ignored? |
| V (Relational) | What power is distributed according to geographic location; who benefits from geographic arrangement; whose interests are marginalised? |
| VI (Ontological) | What place-meanings are being erased by imposed external classifications; whose ontologies of place are being overridden? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Geographic privilege blindness (those in advantaged locations unaware of advantages)
- Infrastructure vulnerability invisibility (dependencies not obvious to users)
- Spatial cost displacement concealment (costs borne by distant, powerless communities)
- Place-invisibility (distant locations and their costs not salient to decision-makers)
- Territorial framing effects (decisions frame territory in ways serving some interests)
- Access barrier invisibility (mobility constraints of some populations not recognized)
- Urban agglomeration benefits extraction (advantages concentrated, disadvantages dispersed)
- Governance authority creep (boundaries erode invisibly over time)
- Place-meaning erasure (indigenous and local meanings overridden without visibility)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Geographic privilege and advantage invisibility | Framework of Access & Equity; Framework of Systems | Privilege audit; access equity mapping | Make geographic advantage explicit; audit how privilege is distributed by location; remedy concentrated advantage. |
| Infrastructure vulnerability and cascade risk | Framework of Risk; Framework of Systems | Infrastructure dependency mapping; vulnerability analysis | Map critical nodes and cascade paths; design redundancy; distribute critical functions geographically. |
| Spatial displacement of environmental/social costs | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Governance | Multi-location impact assessment; cost attribution analysis | Trace costs to origin; make displacement visible; internalise costs; remediate communities bearing costs. |
| Place-invisibility to distant decision-makers | Framework of Evidence; Framework of Communication | Information flow amplification; visibility design | Amplify signals from distant locations; design accountability to place-affected communities; include place voice in decisions. |
| Territorial governance authority gaps | Framework of Governance; Framework of Coordination | Jurisdictional mapping; authority clarification; cross-boundary mechanism design | Clarify authority scope; design cross-boundary governance; address ungoverned spaces; ensure accountability. |
| Place-meaning erasure and contestation | Framework of Hiddens; Framework of Power | Place-meaning documentation; narrative preservation; contested authority | Document multiple place-meanings; honor local meanings alongside development; include place-attached communities in decisions. |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Identify relevant geographic locations and places in the scenario; confirm spatial scale (local/regional/national/transnational).
2. Map critical infrastructure: what connects locations; what are bottlenecks and dependencies; what cascades are plausible.
3. Quick place-meaning audit: what do key locations mean locally; where are place-meanings contested or erased.
4. Access and privilege quick check: where is opportunity concentrated; are any populations isolated or have restricted mobility.
5. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six meta-categories; flag obvious geographic privilege, infrastructure vulnerability, or place-invisibility.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Create detailed geographic map: locations, places, spatial relationships; document physical and natural characteristics.
2. Inventory place-meanings: interviews and documentation of what each location means locally; identify contested meanings.
3. Audit infrastructure comprehensively: map all critical systems; identify bottlenecks, isolated locations, dependencies, and cascade paths.
4. Analyze access and opportunity distribution: population distribution, mobility barriers, accessibility variations, resource distribution across space.
5. Map territorial governance: jurisdictions, authority scope, boundaries, authority gaps, cross-boundary coordination mechanisms.
6. Assess all five dimensions: Spatial scale; Connectivity integration; Visibility to decision-makers; Place-meaning intensity; Territorial contestedness.
7. Analyse dynamic patterns: identify which patterns (agglomeration, displacement, lock-in, contestation, etc.) are active or emerging.
8. Map three interfaces: Detection (how spatial phenomena become visible), Remediation (what can be changed about spatial arrangement), Interaction (governance coordination across space).
9. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: for each high-relevance Hidden, identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; assign interfaces; document residual Unknowns.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Spatial Inventory | Document locations, places, spatial relationships, place-meanings, geographic characteristics, infrastructure, connectivity. | After mapping completion. |
| Access & Privilege Register | Geographic distribution of population, opportunities, resources; access barriers; privilege and disadvantage mapping. | After access analysis. |
| Infrastructure & Connectivity Register | Critical infrastructure systems; bottlenecks; isolated locations; dependencies; cascade vulnerabilities. | After infrastructure audit. |
| Territorial Governance Register | Jurisdictions, authority scope, boundaries, gaps, contested territories, cross-boundary coordination mechanisms. | After governance mapping. |
| Spatial Dynamics Register | Infrastructure lock-in effects; urban agglomeration; resource depletion; territorial shifts; place-meaning evolution. | After dynamics analysis. |
| Hiddens Register | Space and place-specific Hiddens with mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; privilege blindness; displacement invisibility; place-erasure. | After Tier 1 scan; escalate high-consequence to Tier 2. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified relevant locations and places, mapped critical infrastructure and connectivity, audited place-meanings and contested narratives, analysed access and geographic privilege distribution, assessed territorial governance, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and escalated any high-consequence spatial Hiddens to appropriate frameworks?
