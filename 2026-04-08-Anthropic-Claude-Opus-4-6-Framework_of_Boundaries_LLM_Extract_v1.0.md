## Section 2: Operational Definition

The Boundaries framework identifies and classifies all salient boundaries across six domains (physical, conceptual, social, epistemic, normative, identity); profiles each boundary along five cross-cutting dimensions (sharpness, permeability, stability, visibility, constructedness); maps boundary operations (drawing, maintaining, crossing, moving, dissolving) and interface types (transition zones, crossing points, intersections); and surfaces boundary-related Hiddens (boundary blindness, false imposition, category neglect, fixity assumption). It populates the Hiddens Register, Evidence Ledger, and Governance recommendations, enabling early detection of misscoping, jurisdiction disputes, and unmanaged edge cases.

## Section 5: Condensed Dynamics

| Dynamic pattern | Signature (what you observe) | Mechanism (why it happens) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boundary Blindness | Boundaries invisible until they fail; salient boundaries crowd out subtle ones; position-dependent visibility of boundaries. | Boundaries treated as background infrastructure; tacit assumptions go unchallenged; distance and specialization create local invisibility. | Incidents cluster at boundary interfaces; accountability gaps emerge; misscoping occurs repeatedly. |
| Boundary Drift | Boundary location, permeability, or enforcement shifts incrementally without formal review or decision; gradual misalignment with intent. | Operational pressures drive de facto exceptions; monitoring is intermittent; formal review cadence is absent or ignored. | Formal rules diverge from practice; accumulating exceptions create shadow systems; rapid failure when drift is discovered. |
| Contested Boundaries | Multiple legitimate authorities, disciplines, or stakeholders disagree on boundary location, type, or operation; disputes are active or latent. | Power asymmetries; different authorities have different stakes; disciplines have different assumptions about what is natural/obvious. | Responsibility gaps; conflicting enforcement; unmanaged escalations; deadlock or coalition collapse. |
| Boundary Interaction & Cascade | Two or more boundaries interact (nesting, overlapping, conflicting); crossing one boundary unexpectedly triggers others; edge cases multiply. | Boundaries are interdependent; systems design treats boundaries as isolated; interaction effects unmapped and unmanaged. | Crossing failures; unexpected constraints; brittle systems where single failure cascades across multiple boundaries. |
| Grey Zones & Permeability | Boundary sharpness decreases; zone of ambiguity grows; stakeholders disagree on membership; exceptions accumulate in transition zones. | Boundary designed as crisp but applied to messy reality; incentives reward exceptions; authority reluctant to enforce hard boundaries. | Decision paralysis; accountability evasion; inconsistent treatment of similar cases; legitimacy deficits. |
| Legacy Lock-in | Obsolete boundaries persist because changing them requires crossing other high-cost boundaries (jurisdictional, cultural, political); boundary debt. | Sunk costs; path dependency; institutional inertia; boundaries protect invested interests; renewal cycles are long. | Misalignment with current strategy; friction in transformation; risk accumulation. |
| False Imposition | Boundaries applied universally despite contextual variation; one-size-fits-all boundary classification overrides legitimate local variation. | Centralized authority imposes boundary; local context ignored; standardization valued over adaptation; monitoring is coarse. | Local workarounds; shadow systems; compliance theater; frustration and turnover. |
| Category Neglect | Important boundary types missing from analysis or governance; blind categories; boundaries taken for granted. | Analyst or authority focuses on salient boundary types; invisible boundaries assumed not to matter; knowledge of category variety is poor. | Surprises at undiscussed boundaries; unmapped interfaces; ungovernedcrossings. |

## Section 6: Compact Hiddens Cross-Check Prompts

### 6a. Scan Questions by Meta-Category (I–VI)

| Hiddens meta-category | Scan question (1 sentence max) |
|---|---|
| I (Perceptual) | What boundary locations, types, and transitions are invisible from common positions or roles; what salient boundaries crowd out subtle ones? |
| II (Systemic) | How do boundaries couple with institutions, processes, and norms; what emergent boundary interactions and cascade risks exist? |
| III (Informational) | What boundary classifications are contested or unavailable; what evidence gaps exist about boundary enforcement, drift, or crossing patterns? |
| IV (Temporal) | What boundaries have drifted or become obsolete; what legacy lock-in persists; what boundary operations are planned but unmanaged? |
| V (Relational) | Who controls boundary definition and enforcement; who is excluded from boundary governance; what power asymmetries are hidden by technical boundary definitions? |
| VI (Ontological) | What boundary types are not even conceivable in current classification; what entities and categories are foreclosed by current boundary schema? |

### 6b. Primary Hiding Mechanisms This Framework Detects

- Boundary Invisibility (boundaries sit at attention edges; ubiquity makes them unnoticed until failure)
- Boundary Denial & Distortion (incentives suppress recognition of inconvenient boundaries; denial distorts boundary perception)
- Positional Blindness (boundaries experienced differently across roles/positions; what is 'clear' centrally is 'porous' at edges)
- Legacy Lock-in (obsolete boundaries persist; changing them requires crossing other costly boundaries)
- False Universality (boundaries applied uniformly despite contextual variation; local adaptation ignored)
- Fixity Assumption (boundaries treated as immutable when they are dynamic; drift goes undetected)
- Category Neglect (important boundary types absent from analysis or governance; invisible until critical failure)

### 6c. Detection/Remediation Interface Pointers

| When this type of Hidden emerges | Invoke this framework | Interface type | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boundary invisibility or blindness | Framework of Hiddens; Framework of Realities | Position-dependent visibility audit; stakeholder perspective mapping | Conduct boundary visibility scan from multiple positions; create shared boundary inventory. |
| Contested boundary location or type | Framework of Governance; Framework of Power | Legitimacy and authority analysis; stakeholder negotiation; dispute resolution | Map authority distribution; establish boundary definition process; conduct mediation. |
| Boundary drift undetected | Framework of Time; Framework of Evidence | Monitoring and forensic audit; historical comparison | Establish boundary monitoring cadence; conduct drift analysis; refresh boundary definitions. |
| Legacy boundary lock-in | Framework of Interventions; Framework of Learning | Change sequencing; boundary dissolution roadmap | Design boundary migration path; manage transition costs; establish sunset conditions. |
| Boundary cascade or interaction failure | Framework of Systems; Framework of Risk | Multi-boundary dependency map; interaction analysis | Map boundary interactions; identify high-risk intersections; design crossing controls. |

## Section 7: LLM Execution Protocol

### 7a. Light Depth Execution (Scan-level)

1. Identify focal boundaries from scenario; list by name and tentative type (e.g., Institutional, Temporal, Epistemic).
2. For each boundary, assign to one or more meta-category (I–VI) and note the core type from taxonomy (1–30).
3. Assess two key dimensions quickly: Sharpness (crisp, fuzzy, or vague?) and Visibility (obvious, hidden, contested, or position-dependent?).
4. Run Tier 1 Hiddens scan: touch all six meta-categories; flag obvious blindspots (invisible boundaries, positional variance, contested definitions).
5. Shortlist 2–3 highest-risk boundaries; note if Unknowns are high; flag for escalation if critical boundary governance is missing.

### 7b. Full Depth Execution (Complete framework run)

1. Create boundary inventory: list all focal boundaries with formal definition, location, authority, enforcement mechanism, stakeholder map.
2. Assign each boundary: meta-category (I–VI) and core type (1–30); note taxonomy deviations or domain-specific subtypes.
3. Profile all five dimensions per boundary: Sharpness (crisp/defined/fuzzy/vague/absent), Permeability (sealed/controlled/permeable/undefined), Stability (fixed/maintained/drifting/dissolving), Visibility (obvious/hidden/contested/position-dependent), Constructedness (natural/constructed/negotiated).
4. Analyze boundary operations: map how each boundary is drawn, maintained, crossed, moved, dissolved; identify sequencing risks and control gaps.
5. Map interfaces: identify transition zones (grey zones with ambiguity), crossing points (controlled transitions), intersections (where multiple boundaries interact); flag high-risk intersections.
6. Run Tier 1 + Tier 2 Hiddens scan: for each boundary with high Unknown or high-consequence Hidden, identify mechanism, detectability, agency, consequence; assign detection/remediation interfaces.
7. Document governance requirements: monitoring cadence, exception handling, dispute resolution process, authority clarity, boundary change process; generate recommendations.

### 7c. Register Updates and Gate Question

| Register | Update type | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Hiddens Register | Add boundary-specific Hiddens with meta-category tag (Perceptual, Systemic, Informational, Temporal, Relational, Ontological); mechanism; consequence. | After Tier 1 scan; escalate high-consequence/high-uncertainty Hiddens to Tier 2. |
| Evidence Ledger | Record boundary type classifications, dimension profiles, operation analyses, governance status; note evidence quality and gaps (policy-in-practice divergence). | After boundary profiling and operation analysis; note missing definitions, enforcement data, dispute documentation. |
| Hypothesis/Test Backlog | Add hypotheses about boundary perception, drift, contestation, operation sequencing; propose tests (stakeholder interviews, policy audit, practice observation). | Identified Unknowns and residual Hiddens; escalation decisions. |
| Information Requests | Request boundary definitions (formal and practical), enforcement data, exception logs, stakeholder perspectives, governance process documentation. | To reduce Unknowns and validate Hidden detection; coordinate with Governance and Power frameworks. |

**Gate Question (pre-closure check):** Have we identified all focal boundaries, classified them (meta-category + type), profiled key dimensions, mapped operations and interfaces, run a Tier 1 Hiddens scan, and identified governance requirements for high-risk boundaries?
