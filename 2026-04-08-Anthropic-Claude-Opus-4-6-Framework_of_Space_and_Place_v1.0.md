# Introduction

## What is Space and Place?

Space and Place are the geographic and territorial dimensions of any scenario. Space refers to the objective, measurable spatial relationships: distances, terrain, physical infrastructure, connectivity, and the observable constraints and affordances of location. Place refers to the socially constructed meaning of specific locations: identity, belonging, cultural significance, norms, and the power dynamics embedded in how locations are named, classified, and governed. Together, they shape what is materially possible in different locations, who has access to resources and opportunities, which actors and costs become visible or invisible to decision-makers, and how territorial boundaries structure power and decision-making authority.

Space and Place matter analytically because many scenario failures involve spatial blindness—actors at advantaged locations cannot perceive constraints elsewhere; distant costs are not visible in decision centers; infrastructure dependencies span geographies in ways that surprise decision-makers; and the meaning of place (cultural, political, historical) shape how different locations are treated in governance and resource allocation. Geographic privilege creates systematic visibility failures: what happens in marginal or distant places is less salient to central decision-makers; the costs of a location-specific decision are borne by those at the impacted location but decided by those elsewhere; territorial framing (how jurisdictional or administrative boundaries are drawn) determines what is in scope and what remains outside the analysis.

## Why does Space and Place matter for Hiddens work?

Space and Place generates four primary families of hiding mechanisms. First, geographic privilege blindness (Hiddens I/II): actors in advantaged locations (centers of power, wealth, infrastructure, information) cannot readily perceive constraints, risks, or deprivation in distant or peripheral locations. Second, spatial displacement of costs (Hiddens II/III): harm is geographically displaced from decision-makers—costs are borne in distant locations, benefits are concentrated in centers, and the causal link remains invisible. Third, place-based invisibility (Hiddens I/IV): what happens in locations deemed marginal, culturally distant, or politically less salient becomes perceptually invisible to dominant groups and decision-makers. Fourth, territorial framing effects (Hiddens II/III): how boundaries are drawn (nations, regions, organizations, jurisdictions) determines what is in scope for analysis and governance—and territories outside the boundary become invisible or treated as external.

This framework surfaces these spatial visibility failures by making geographic location and place-meaning explicit as analytical objects. Without it, geographic drivers of access, inequality, and risk remain unexamined, and location-dependent hiddens (infrastructure vulnerabilities, place-based marginalization, spatial concentration of unmanaged risk) persist unchecked.

## What does this framework produce?

This framework produces six meta-categories of spatial and place phenomena (objective geography, place-meaning, infrastructure and systems, human access and movement, territorial governance, and spatial dynamics), yielding 30 core types. It defines five cross-cutting dimensions along which any spatial or place phenomenon can be assessed (scale, connectivity, visibility, meaning-intensity, and contestedness). It maps dynamic patterns common to spatial scenarios (infrastructure lock-in, spatial migration and displacement, connectivity cascades, place-meaning shifts, territorial creep). It identifies three interface types: detection interfaces (how spatial/place phenomena become visible in data or observation), remediation interfaces (how spatial or place-based problems can be addressed), and interaction interfaces (how spatial/place phenomena interact with governance, power, and decision-making). And it populates the Hiddens Register with location-specific, place-meaning-specific, and spatial-displacement-specific hiding mechanisms.

## How to use this framework

Invoke this framework when a scenario involves geographic location as an analytical variable: decisions or constraints vary by location; access to resources or opportunities is location-dependent; costs or benefits are geographically displaced; territorial or jurisdictional boundaries are relevant to scope or authority; the cultural or political meaning of place shapes treatment or norms; or geographic infrastructure is a dependency. Default routing is Light Depth Framework Execution; escalate to Full Depth when spatial factors are central to the scenario, when consequence is high, when spatial privilege blindness is suspected, or when geographic displacement of cost/benefit is material to fairness or risk assessment.

For LLM execution, see Section 1.6 below for the full routing and extraction specifications. This framework is typically paired with Time (temporal dimension), Boundaries (territorial scope), and Scale (spatial scale of analysis) to establish the full spatiotemporal container for analysis. It feeds directly into Systems (spatial interdependencies), Power (geographic distribution), Interests (spatial concentration), and Governance (territorial authority).

# 1. Core Premise & Position in the Analytical Series

## 1.1 Core Premise (narrative)

Space and Place are the geographic and territorial dimensions of complex scenarios. Every scenario occurs somewhere, and where it occurs matters: location determines what is materially possible (terrain, climate, infrastructure availability, physical access), who benefits and who bears costs (geographic distribution of resources, vulnerability, and decision-making authority), what is visible or invisible to different actors (proximity determines salience; distant costs are less visible), and how territory is governed (boundaries determine jurisdiction, scope, legitimacy, and accountability).

This framework treats Space and Place as an integrated analytical object with two complementary aspects. The objective spatial aspect includes geographic location, distance, terrain, climate, physical infrastructure, connectivity (roads, communications, supply chains), and measurable spatial relationships. The place-based aspect includes the social construction and meaning of locations: how places are named and classified, their cultural and historical significance, local norms and identity, and the power dynamics embedded in territorial governance and belonging. Together, they constitute the geographic and territorial container within which a scenario unfolds.

The primary analytical value of this framework is in surfacing spatial visibility failures and geographic privilege blindness. Actors at advantaged locations (centers of infrastructure, communication, political power, wealth) often cannot perceive what constraints, risks, or deprivations exist in peripheral or distant locations. Costs are displaced geographically: benefits accrue in centers, harms in peripheries, and the causal link remains invisible to decision-makers who benefit. Place-meanings shape how locations are treated: marginalized places receive fewer resources; culturally distant places are less salient; politically powerless places are more likely to have their interests subordinated in multi-place governance. And territorial framing—how boundaries are drawn—determines what is in scope for analysis and subject to governance, and what remains external and invisible.

This framework produces artefacts for diagnosis, decision, and governance: a geographic and place-based taxonomy of scenario phenomena; a spatial visibility audit identifying who can perceive what from which location; an access and privilege map showing location-dependent availability of resources and opportunity; a territorial framing analysis clarifying what is in scope and what is excluded by boundary choices; and a spatial risk register identifying location-specific, place-meaning-specific, and geographic-displacement-specific risks and hideouts.

## 1.3 Definitions, Scope, and Non-Goals

- **Definition of Space and Place**: The geographic, territorial, and place-based dimensions of a scenario, encompassing: (a) objective spatial relationships (location, distance, terrain, physical infrastructure, connectivity) and (b) socially constructed place-meaning (identity, cultural significance, local norms, territorial governance, and belonging).

- **In scope**: 
  - Geographic location and objective spatial constraints (terrain, climate, distance, accessibility)
  - Physical infrastructure (roads, utilities, communications, supply chains) and connectivity
  - Place-meaning and social construction of locations (identity, history, norms, cultural significance)
  - Territorial governance and jurisdictional boundaries
  - Access and resource distribution by location
  - Spatial visibility (what is visible from which location)
  - Geographic privilege and spatial displacement of costs/benefits
  - Infrastructure dependencies and spatial lock-in
  - Movement, migration, and spatial dynamics
  - Place-based risk and opportunity

- **Out of scope**: 
  - Pure temporal analysis (use Time framework for temporal dimension)
  - Non-spatial organizational or social structure (use Systems, Relations, or Agents frameworks)
  - Individual travel or logistical planning (out of scope unless location choice affects scenario outcomes)
  - Cartography or geographic information systems methodology (we use maps and GIS as tools, not as subjects)
  - Geodesy or geographic measurement as an end in itself
  - Climate science or physical geography as domain expertise (we use geographers' outputs, not conduct geographic research)

- **Common confusions** ("Space and Place is not…"):
  - Not equivalent to Boundaries framework (Boundaries covers analytical scope decisions; Space and Place covers the physical and social geography as an analytical object)
  - Not equivalent to Scale framework (Scale covers the level of analysis—micro/meso/macro; Space and Place covers where phenomena are located and how location shapes them)
  - Not equivalent to Systems framework (Systems covers functional interdependencies; Space and Place covers how those are shaped by physical geography and place-meaning)
  - Not a substitute for local knowledge or ethnographic expertise (this framework helps surface where local knowledge is needed, not replace it)
  - Not about individual navigation or travel choice (unless location choice affects scenario outcomes materially)

## 2.2 Meta-Category Descriptions

### I. Objective geography and terrain
- **Definition**: The natural and physical characteristics of geographic locations—latitude, altitude, climate patterns, terrain, natural resources, water systems, natural hazards, and seasonal variations. These characteristics are measurable, relatively stable over human timescales (except climate change), and constitute the material substrate on which human activity occurs.
- **Diagnostic cues**: Geographic data is available or can be obtained; terrain is relevant to movement, settlement, or resource access; climate affects activity patterns or infrastructure needs; natural hazards shape risk; natural resources shape opportunity or conflict; water availability is material; seasonal variation affects operations
- **Typical failure mode**: Analysis treats geography as irrelevant or uniform; ignores how terrain shapes what is possible; misses how climate or natural hazards shape risk; fails to see resource scarcity or dependence; treats geography as backdrop rather than active constraint

### II. Infrastructure and connectivity
- **Definition**: The human-built systems that span locations and enable movement of people, goods, information, and services: roads, railways, pipelines, utilities (electricity, water, sewage), telecommunications networks, and supply chains. Infrastructure determines who can reach whom, what flows where, what is connected and what is isolated.
- **Diagnostic cues**: Infrastructure is unevenly distributed across locations; connectivity varies by location; some locations are bottlenecks; supply chains cross jurisdictions; communications networks shape information flow; infrastructure maintenance or upgrading affects outcomes; infrastructure failure cascades across locations
- **Typical failure mode**: Infrastructure is treated as a neutral medium (goods flow, information reaches where needed); ignores infrastructure gaps, bottlenecks, or vulnerabilities; fails to see how infrastructure concentration affects dependency; misses how infrastructure is controlled or controlled access through infrastructure; ignores maintenance and fragility

### III. Place-meaning and social construction
- **Definition**: The socially constructed meanings, identities, narratives, and norms attached to specific locations—how places are named and classified, their cultural and historical significance, the stories told about them, the sense of belonging and exclusion they embody, the norms and practices that characterize them. Place-meaning shapes how locations are treated in governance and how people relate to them.
- **Diagnostic cues**: Locations are treated differently despite similar material conditions; place-based narratives appear in discourse; local norms vary by location; place identity affects recruitment, settlement, or investment; historical narrative shapes present treatment; some places are stigmatized and others celebrated; place attachment shapes behaviour
- **Typical failure mode**: Treats place as objective geography only; ignores how place-meaning shapes treatment and norms; misses stigma or privilege attached to places; ignores local identity and attachment; fails to see how place-narratives reinforce power; misses how places are actively named and classified to serve power

### IV. Access, movement, and human distribution
- **Definition**: The spatial distribution of people, assets, and opportunities; patterns of human movement and migration; the accessibility of locations (who can reach whom and how easily); barriers and facilitators of movement; the concentration or dispersion of resources, employment, services, and economic opportunity across locations.
- **Diagnostic cues**: Population is unevenly distributed; movement patterns show concentration in some locations and isolation in others; migration is happening; some locations are more accessible than others; access to services/employment/opportunity varies by location; travel time and cost vary significantly; barriers to entry/exit exist; some groups are more mobile than others
- **Typical failure mode**: Treats access as uniform (everyone can reach everything); ignores movement barriers; misses how opportunity is geographically concentrated; ignores migration and displacement; fails to see how immobility traps people in disadvantaged locations; misses how access to resources varies by location; ignores who is able to move and who is stuck

### V. Territorial governance and authority
- **Definition**: The political and administrative structures that allocate authority across geographic space—jurisdictional boundaries, the scope of each authority's power, how boundaries are drawn and enforced, territorial claims and contested boundaries, the capacity to govern across boundaries, and the accountability structures embedded in territorial organization.
- **Diagnostic cues**: Multiple jurisdictions overlap or contest boundaries; authority varies by location; some locations fall outside any clear authority (ungoverned spaces); cross-boundary coordination is difficult or failing; boundary changes are contested; accountability is ambiguous across boundaries; some locations are marginalized in multi-location governance
- **Typical failure mode**: Treats governance as location-independent (same rules apply everywhere); ignores how boundaries determine scope and authority; misses how boundary placement determines winners and losers; ignores contested and overlapping claims; fails to see how location affects accountability and enforcement; misses how some territories are marginalized or excluded from governance

### VI. Spatial dynamics and change
- **Definition**: Change in spatial configuration over time: infrastructure development, urban growth and sprawl, resource depletion, territory claim shifts, migration and displacement, place-meaning evolution, lock-in effects (where past infrastructure investment constrains future change), disruption and system reorganization. How spatial relationships shift, what drives change, and what remains locked in past patterns.
- **Diagnostic cues**: Infrastructure is being built or upgraded; spatial configuration is contested or changing; new migration patterns are emerging; resource availability is shifting; urbanization or sprawl is happening; territorial claims are changing; place-meaning is contested or evolving; past infrastructure investment constrains current flexibility; disruption is reorganizing spatial relationships
- **Typical failure mode**: Treats spatial configuration as static; ignores infrastructure development and lock-in; misses migration and displacement; fails to see how new infrastructure opens new possibilities; ignores resource depletion or scarcity; misses how place-meaning is actively contested and remade; ignores how past investment constrains future change; misses windows of opportunity for spatial reorganization

## 3.7 Extending the Taxonomy

| Aspect | Guidance on extension |
|---|---|
| **Canonical baseline** | Start with the 30 core types above (6 categories × 5 types). These cover the major dimensions of space, place, infrastructure, access, governance, and dynamics at a general level applicable across many scenarios. |
| **Domain-specific refinement** | Add types when domain expertise or scenario specificity warrants: coastal vs inland geographies (maritime vs continental), tropical vs arctic climates, specific resource types (mineral-rich, fishery-dependent), specific infrastructure types (aviation-dependent, port-dependent, rail-dependent), specific place categories (indigenous territories, postcolonial spaces, border regions, diaspora communities). Refinement adds detail within the 6 meta-categories. |
| **Preserving mappability** | Any added types should map back to at least one of the 6 meta-categories. Do not create new meta-categories unless structural change is warranted. Keep the numbering scheme clear (e.g., types 1-5 in Category I, 6-10 in II, etc.) to ensure traceability. |
| **Structural invariants** | Do not reduce below 5 types per category (loses discrimination power). Do not exceed 8-10 types per category without significant justification (loses manageability). Preserve the 6-category structure unless the scenario explicitly calls for spatial reorganization (e.g., if analyzing a scenario where governance is being restructured, you might need a temporary 7th category for "transition forms"). |
| **Periodic reassessment** | After applying the framework to 3-5 scenarios in a domain, review whether the 30 core types are capturing the key phenomena. If patterns repeat, types are sufficient. If key phenomena are consistently missing, add domain-specific types. If types are never invoked, consider retiring them in next framework version. |
| **Versioning & governance** | Core types (the 30) remain stable across framework versions unless major structural change (promote to new major version, e.g., v2.0). Domain-specific extensions can be managed locally or via domain modules without changing the core framework. If a domain-specific extension proves universally useful, propose incorporation into canonical baseline in next major version. |

| 9 | **Spatial inequality persistence and lock-in** | Geographic inequality (rich regions vs poor regions, advanced vs underdeveloped, center vs periphery) tends to persist and even increase; mechanisms of persistence are often invisible | Regions that start advantaged (good infrastructure, capital, talent) attract more investment and economic activity; regions that start disadvantaged lack investment and lose talented people who migrate; cumulative advantage creates larger inequality; inequality itself becomes a narrative (some places are naturally poor, others naturally rich) that reinforces persistence; those in disadvantaged regions lack resources to invest in change | Geographic inequality is treated as inevitable or natural when it is outcome of specific governance and investment choices; those in disadvantaged regions are blamed for poverty rather than recognizing systematic underinvestment; mobility of advantaged people to advantaged regions increases inequality; those in disadvantaged regions face compounding problems (less investment, brain drain, lower quality governance) | Proactive redistribution through central government investment in disadvantaged regions; incentives for private investment in underdeveloped areas; support for talent development to reduce brain drain; place-based identity and pride to counter stigma and support local investment; coordination mechanisms so regions don't compete downward in a race to the bottom |
| 10 | **Access and opportunity barrier formation** | Barriers to access and movement become more entrenched over time; those unable to access opportunities fall further behind; mobility barriers create immobility traps | Barriers to movement (poor infrastructure, lack of transportation, distance, cost) prevent some people from accessing opportunities (jobs, education, services); those unable to move remain in disadvantaged locations; over time, their location becomes associated with lower opportunity; investment and opportunity concentrate in accessible locations; those in hard-to-access locations accumulate disadvantage | Creates locked-in disadvantage for those in difficult-to-access locations; lack of access to opportunity perpetuates poverty; those unable to move miss the dynamic shifts in economy and technology; brain drain from inaccessible locations; inaccessible places are stigmatized as poor and backward | Investment in accessibility (infrastructure, transportation) to hard-to-reach places; remote work and telework to enable those in distant locations to access opportunity; delivery of services to locations rather than requiring people to travel; investment in local economic development so less travel is needed; support for mobility of those who want to move while also supporting those who stay |
| 11 | **Governance authority creep and encroachment** | Over time, boundaries shift and one authority's scope expands while another's contracts; encroachment often goes unnoticed until challenged; historical territories may be subsumed into larger jurisdictions | Boundaries were drawn at specific times for specific reasons; over time, administrative changes and reorganization shift boundaries; larger jurisdictions sometimes expand their scope at expense of smaller ones; peripheral territories are sometimes absorbed into larger jurisdictions without clear consent; historical claims are sometimes overridden by administrative convenience; small territories may find their scope squeezed over time | Those in territories that lose autonomy over time may not notice until critical decision is made without their voice; autonomy erosion is often gradual and invisible until challenged; small territories may lack power to resist encroachment by larger jurisdictions; legitimacy of larger jurisdiction's claim may not be accepted locally | Clear documentation of territorial claims and historical boundaries; formal review processes before boundary changes; representation of smaller territories in larger governance structures; protection of minorities and smaller territories in governance law; transparent governance of common resources shared across territories |
| 12 | **Place-based stigma as self-fulfilling prophecy** | Places carry stigma (dangerous, poor, backwards); stigma affects investment and migration decisions; lack of investment makes places actually poor/neglected; stigma becomes self-fulfilling | Place becomes labeled as bad (crime-ridden, poor, undesirable) based on initial conditions or stereotypes; investors avoid the place; talented people out-migrate; services decline; deprivation deepens; place becomes actually what stigma said it was; new arrivals are drawn from most vulnerable populations who have nowhere else to go; stigma becomes empirically true | Stigmatized places remain trapped in disadvantage because stigma itself prevents the investment and talent that could change conditions; those in stigmatized places experience discrimination; stereotype threat affects motivation and performance; place-based discrimination becomes invisible because conditions seem to justify stigma | Proactive investment in stigmatized places to break stigma cycle; rebranding and narrative change to counter stigma while respecting local meanings; preventing discrimination and supporting those in stigmatized places; economic opportunity and talent development; countering stereotypes through media and education |

# 8. Hiddens Source Analysis & Cross-Check

## 8.1 Hiddens Source Analysis (framework-specific)

| # | Generator name | Pattern (how analysis fails) | Remedy (how to correct) |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Geographic privilege blindness** | Actors in advantaged locations (centers of infrastructure, communication, political power, wealth) cannot perceive constraints, risks, deprivation, or suffering in distant or peripheral locations. Analysis conducted from the center assumes center-based conditions are universal. | Audit visibility from multiple geographic positions, not just from center; interview or include perspectives from peripheral locations; trace information flows to understand what reaches decision-centers and what is filtered out; test whether analysis changes when conducted from periphery perspective; ensure peripheral locations have voice in analysis and decisions |
| 2 | **Spatial displacement of costs** | Costs and risks are geographically displaced—benefits accrue in profitable locations, costs are borne in peripheral or distant locations. Causal link between profit-taking and cost-bearing is not visible. Analysis conducted at benefit location or analyzes only visible costs. | Trace causal chains across geography from benefit-taker to cost-bearer; map full supply chain and all affected locations; identify who benefits and who bears costs at each location; follow labor, environmental, and social costs back to their origins; ensure all affected locations are in scope of analysis, not just profitable ones |
| 3 | **Place-based invisibility** | Locations deemed marginal, culturally distant, or politically less salient become invisible to dominant groups and decision-makers. What happens there is less visible and less salient in decision-making. Information about distant places is limited. | Audit whose locations and conditions are visible and whose are invisible in current analysis and information systems; identify locations that are culturally distant and check information availability about them; test whether analysis pays equal attention to all locations or privileges some; ensure information reaches decision-centers from all relevant locations |
| 4 | **Territorial framing effects** | How boundaries are drawn determines what is in scope and what is outside. Analysis conducted within one jurisdiction ignores impacts outside the boundary. Excluded territories and their interests become invisible. | Map all boundaries relevant to the scenario and make explicit what is in-scope and what is out-of-scope; identify who is affected outside the primary boundary; check whether impacts outside boundaries are being ignored; consider alternative boundary definitions and how they change the analysis; ensure those outside primary boundary but affected have voice |
| 5 | **Infrastructure-dependent blindness** | Systems that depend on infrastructure are vulnerable to infrastructure failure but this dependency is invisible until disruption. Analysis assumes infrastructure is reliable. | Audit system dependency on specific infrastructure; identify critical infrastructure; assess vulnerability to single points of failure; test what happens if critical infrastructure fails; ensure redundancy and backup for critical infrastructure; create visibility for infrastructure vulnerability before crisis |
| 6 | **Place-meaning erasure** | Local place-meanings and identities are not documented or respected. New place-meanings imposed (by developers, governments) override local meanings. Erasure of meaning creates alienation and invisible losses. | Document place-meanings as understood and valued locally, not just as understood by outsiders; test whose place-meanings are being respected in plans and decisions; identify where place-meanings are being overridden; support place-meaning evolution that includes local voices rather than imposed meanings; measure and account for losses when place-meanings are erased |

| Place-based invisibility (what happens in marginal locations is less visible) | High | Cultural distance makes distant places less salient; information scarcity; media bias toward centers; rational attention limits mean peripheral events get less attention | Medium (visibility gaps can be addressed through communication and deliberate attention) | Distributed (marginalized groups experience invisibility acutely; central groups don't recognize the invisibility) | High (perpetuates marginalization; denies voice and representation; supports neglect of marginal areas) | Media and information systems that cover all locations; attention allocation systems that weight distant impacts appropriately; inclusion of peripheral voices in narrative construction | Active amplification of peripheral voices; supporting place-based media and information; fighting stigma and supporting positive place-identity; investing in visibility of peripheral locations | Invisibility is persistent without deliberate counter-actions |
| Territorial framing effects (boundary placement excludes some places and interests) | High | Boundary choices are made for reasons and embed assumptions about what is in/out; in-scope analysis ignores out-of-scope impacts; power to draw boundaries is unevenly distributed | Medium-High (boundaries are visible and drawable but impacts of boundary choices may not be apparent until traced) | Concentrated (those with power to draw boundaries may not be accountable for excluded impacts) | High (boundary placement determines inclusion/exclusion; affects whose interests are represented; distributes benefits and costs) | Boundary mapping; impact assessment that traces beyond boundary; representation of those outside primary boundary but affected | Inclusive boundary-drawing processes; considering multiple boundary options; accountability for impacts outside chosen boundary; cross-boundary coordination mechanisms | Boundary effects persist without deliberate attention |
| Infrastructure vulnerability and hidden cascade risk | High | Dependency on infrastructure in specific locations creates vulnerability; cascades cross functional boundaries in unexpected ways; vulnerability is invisible until disruption | Medium (infrastructure is visible but cascade pathways and vulnerability may not be apparent) | Distributed (those at vulnerable points may not recognize their role; those dependent on infrastructure may not see the dependency) | High (disruption cascades system-wide with large impact; may be catastrophic) | Infrastructure monitoring and stress-testing; simulation of cascades; identification of critical nodes and single points of failure; redundancy assessment | Geographic redundancy; distributed critical functions; system-wide monitoring; agreed protocols for managing disruption; reducing dependence on single locations | Complexity makes cascades hard to anticipate; requires active modeling and monitoring |
| Place-meaning erasure and imposed identities (local meanings overridden) | Medium-High | New place-meanings are imposed by developers, governments, outsiders; local meanings are not documented or valued; erasure creates alienation and loss; those losing meaning are blamed for resisting progress | Medium (erasure is visible to those experiencing it but may be invisible to those imposing new meanings) | Asymmetric (those imposing meanings may not recognize erosion of local meanings; those losing meanings are harmed but may lack power to resist) | High (creates alienation and loss of identity; supports marginalization; drives cultural destruction; generates resistance that impedes change) | Documenting place-meanings; engaging local communities in understanding what places mean to them; measuring sense of belonging and cultural loss; tracking resistance and alienation | Inclusive place-making that honors local meanings while allowing change; narrative processes that evolve meanings rather than imposing them; compensation or support for those experiencing cultural loss; supporting place-based identity and pride | Without active respect for place-meanings, erasure and alienation persist |
| Resource depletion and economic collapse (location-dependent communities vulnerable) | Medium-High | Depletion is slow and invisible until imminent; communities dependent on specific resources lack diversification and lack alternatives; depletion information may be suppressed to avoid panic/migration | Medium (depletion can be tracked if monitoring exists but is often not visible until crisis) | Concentrated (resource extractors may suppress depletion information; dependent communities lack power to prepare for transition) | High (community economic collapse when resources deplete; vulnerability to shocks; communities not prepared for transition) | Tracking resource depletion; monitoring resource-dependent communities for economic sustainability; early warning of transition needs | Proactive economic diversification in resource-dependent regions; transition funds and support; development of alternative livelihoods before collapse; support for community adaptation | Without proactive planning, communities face sudden collapse when resources deplete |
| Governance authority gaps and ungoverned spaces | Medium | Some territories fall outside clear authority; lack of governance creates vulnerability to exploitation, lawlessness, or parallel governance; authority gaps may persist because central authority doesn't recognize them | Medium-High (gaps are somewhat visible but may be tolerated or ignored by authorities) | Distributed (those in ungoverned spaces experience lack of governance; central authorities may not recognize the gap) | Medium-High (ungoverned spaces may become sites of exploitation, lawlessness, or unofficial governance; harms accumulate) | Mapping governance authority and identifying gaps; monitoring ungoverned spaces for problems; ensuring representation of those in unclear-authority spaces | Clarifying authority and governance; filling gaps with legitimate governance; supporting those in ungoverned spaces with services; ensuring voice in decision-making | Without active governance, gaps persist and harms accumulate |
| Digital divides and information asymmetry (some locations lack communication infrastructure) | Medium | Locations with poor communications infrastructure have limited access to information and less ability to be seen/heard; information asymmetry persists if infrastructure gaps are not addressed | Medium-High (divides are visible but addressing them requires infrastructure investment) | Distributed (those without connectivity experience disadvantage; information-rich locations may not recognize the divide) | Medium-High (less-connected locations fall further behind; information-based economy disadvantages them; visibility gaps persist) | Information availability assessment; communications infrastructure audit; testing what information reaches different locations; digital divide monitoring | Communications infrastructure investment; alternate information delivery (radio, SMS, in-person) for low-connectivity areas; community media support; digital literacy programs | Without proactive infrastructure investment, divides widen |
| Geographic inequality persistence and lock-in (cumulative disadvantage for peripheral regions) | High | Regions starting disadvantaged lose population and investment; inequality increases cumulatively; those leaving peripheral regions are often talented youth; stigma and inequality become self-fulfilling | High (inequality is visible but mechanisms of persistence may not be transparent; inequality may be attributed to cultural factors or lack of capability rather than to investment choices) | Concentrated (privileged regions benefit from inequality; peripheral regions lack resources to invest in change) | High (inequality deepens; opportunity concentrates in privileged regions; peripheral regions decline; social stability at risk) | Inequality monitoring; trend analysis showing cumulative divergence; documenting who is leaving and why; tracking investment allocation across regions | Proactive investment in disadvantaged regions; incentives for economic activity in peripheral areas; brain drain mitigation; place-pride building; progressive taxation and redistribution | Without deliberate counter-action, inequality deepens cumulatively |

## 8.4 Embedded Hiddens Micro-Protocol (standard prompts)

1. **Run the Tier 1 scan across all six Hiddens meta-categories** (early pass; assume Hiddens exist): For each category, ask whether geographic and place-based factors may be creating visibility failures. Check: Perceptual (are distant locations less visible?), Systemic (are infrastructure dependencies creating cascade risk?), Informational (are information gaps geographically distributed?), Temporal (are slow geographic changes invisible?), Relational (are power relationships between locations hidden?), Ontological (are place-based realities being overridden?).

2. **Shortlist candidate Hiddens; for each, rate: mechanism, reducibility, detectability, agency, consequence**: Which geographic and place-based hiddens are most consequential? Which are most remediable? Which are most detectable? Which agency could address them? Focus on high-consequence, remediable, detectable hiddens.

3. **Assign at least one detection interface and one remediation interface per high-consequence Hidden; map interaction chains**: For each high-consequence hidden, specify: (a) how it could be detected (information systems, monitoring, local reporting, etc.), (b) how it could be remediated (infrastructure change, governance change, place-making, etc.), (c) how detection and remediation interact and who must be involved.

4. **Run the hiddens source analysis and record unresolved Unknowns/Hiddens**: For each of the six framework-specific generators (privilege blindness, cost displacement, place-invisibility, territorial framing, infrastructure vulnerability, place-meaning erasure), ask whether analysis is falling into that trap and what would be needed to correct it.

5. **If Tier 1 symptoms point to a specific hiding mechanism, invoke the appropriate Targeted Hiddens Discovery Scan** (OG §7.5; select using Triage Matrix at §7.5.3): Geographic privilege blindness → Positional Blindness scan; spatial displacement of costs → Cascade Discovery or Negligence scan; place-based invisibility → Narrative Capture or Suppression scan; territorial framing effects → check whether boundary decisions are hiding impacts; infrastructure vulnerability → Cascade Discovery scan; place-meaning erasure → Narrative Capture or Legitimacy Theatre scan.

6. **After executing the framework protocol and any available tests/probes, re-run Tier 1** (pre-closure pass) and note deltas: Have any hiddens been made visible? Have any new hiddens emerged? Has confidence increased for any previously uncertain hiddens?

7. **Produce Residual Unknowns** (with mechanism + impact + next probe) and apply the Escalation Rule (§8.5) if any residual could change decisions, accountability, or remediation priorities. Produce closure artefacts per OG §7.4: (1) residual unknowns list, (2) acceptability rationale, (3) monitoring plan, (4) ownership, (5) learning hook.

## 8.5 Escalation Rule (Tier 3 – full-spectrum Hiddens escalation)

Escalate to full-spectrum Hiddens taxonomy run with stricter evidence/provenance and stronger disconfirming tests if any apply:
- **High consequence**: Spatial displacement of costs affects vulnerable populations materially; geographic privilege blindness affects life-or-death decisions; infrastructure vulnerability could cause catastrophic disruption
- **Adversarial context**: Actors have incentive to hide geographic costs; territorial disputes are adversarial; resource extraction is contested; land grabbing or forced displacement is possible
- **Persistent disagreement**: Different locations/perspectives have fundamentally different understanding of what is happening geographically; boundary disputes are unresolved; place-meanings are fundamentally contested
- **Repeat failures**: Prior geographic analyses have failed; geographic risk has materialized before; spatial coordination has failed previously
- **Strong suspicion of cascades/reinforcement**: Infrastructure cascades are plausible; geographic inequality is self-reinforcing; privileged regions are actively defending their privilege
- **Decision-critical residual unknowns**: What happens geographically is central to decisions; major uncertainties about geographic factors remain; different scenarios would produce radically different outcomes based on geographic variables

When escalation is triggered: Expand analysis to full Hiddens taxonomy; tighten evidence standards for geographic claims; test disconfirming hypotheses systematically; involve geographic experts and local knowledge-holders; conduct detailed investigation of specific locations and hidden costs; map power relationships and incentives around geographic issues; design explicit probes and tests to make geographic hiddens visible.

# 10. Framework Principles

## 10.1 Principles Table

| Principle name | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. **Geography is material and consequential** | Geographic location, spatial relationships, and place-meaning are not background or decoration—they actively shape what is possible, who has access to resources and opportunity, and what is visible to different actors. Treat space and place as primary analytical variables. |
| 2. **Privilege is often invisible from the privileged position** | Those in geographically advantaged locations (centers of infrastructure, communication, power, wealth) experience geographic advantage as normal and universal. Recognize geographic privilege as such and actively audit visibility from multiple geographic positions. |
| 3. **Costs are often displaced across geography** | Economic activity that generates profit in one location often generates costs (environmental, social, labor) in another. Trace causal chains and costs across geography. Don't assume all impacts are visible at the location of decision-making. |
| 4. **Boundaries are constructed choices with consequences** | Territorial boundaries, jurisdictions, and analysis scope are not natural or inevitable—they are chosen. Examine what boundary choices include/exclude, who benefits/is harmed, and what becomes invisible outside boundaries. Consider alternative boundaries. |
| 5. **Place-meaning matters materially** | How places are understood—their identity, cultural significance, historical narrative, local norms—shapes behavior, governance acceptance, and resilience. Respect place-meaning and engage locals in understanding their places, rather than imposing external meanings. |
| 6. **Local knowledge is essential** | Understanding place requires engagement with those who inhabit and know places. Local knowledge of geography, place-meaning, infrastructure, access, and risks is often more accurate than external analysis. Incorporate local knowledge as evidence. |
| 7. **Infrastructure is fragile and concentrated** | Critical infrastructure—roads, utilities, communications, supply chains—is often concentrated in specific locations and vulnerable to disruption. Map infrastructure, identify bottlenecks, assess cascades, and maintain redundancy for critical functions. |
| 8. **Geographic inequality is systematic and self-reinforcing** | Spatial inequality (rich regions vs poor regions) is not natural or inevitable—it results from specific investment and governance choices. It is self-reinforcing (advantage attracts investment, disadvantage repels it). Addressing geographic inequality requires deliberate counter-action. |
| 9. **Multiple spatial scales matter simultaneously** | Phenomena operate at multiple spatial scales (local, regional, national, global) simultaneously. Analysis must account for multi-scale dynamics and cross-scale interactions. What works locally may fail regionally; what is efficient regionally may be inequitable locally. |
| 10. **Time and space are entangled** | Spatial and temporal factors interact: infrastructure development takes time and creates path dependence; migration and change unfold over time; geographic privilege accumulates over time; place-meanings evolve gradually. Analyze spatial factors alongside temporal factors. |

# 11. Glossary (local domain terms)

## 11.1 Local domain glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Agglomeration** | Geographic concentration of economic activity, population, and opportunity in specific locations (usually cities). Agglomeration creates efficiencies and attracts further concentration, creating cumulative advantage for concentrated locations. |
| **Brain drain** | Out-migration of talented and educated individuals from peripheral or disadvantaged locations to advantaged locations, reducing human capital in origin locations and deepening inequality. |
| **Bottleneck (infrastructure)** | A location through which critical flows (goods, people, information) must pass, making the location vulnerable to disruption and giving it power over dependent locations. |
| **Cascade (system)** | When disruption at one location propagates through connected infrastructure or systems, affecting many other locations in unexpected ways. Cascades often cross functional boundaries and have system-wide impact. |
| **Connectivity** | The degree to which locations are physically and functionally connected via infrastructure (roads, utilities, communications, supply chains). High connectivity enables exchange; poor connectivity creates isolation. |
| **Cost displacement** | When economic activity generates costs (environmental, social, labor) that are displaced geographically from where profit is made, borne by locations and people with less power to resist. |
| **Digital divide** | Unequal access to digital infrastructure (broadband, telecommunications) and digital technology across locations, creating information and economic inequality. |
| **Governance gap** | Locations without clear governance authority, where rules are unclear or unenforced, creating vulnerability to exploitation and informal governance. |
| **Infrastructure lock-in** | Path dependence created by infrastructure investment: once major infrastructure is built, it becomes costly and difficult to change, constraining future development and alternatives. |
| **Jurisdiction** | A territory under a single governance authority with defined geographic boundaries and scope of authority. Jurisdictions often mismatch functional systems (e.g., watershed, supply chain) causing coordination problems. |
| **Place (social construct)** | The socially constructed meaning and identity of a specific location: how it is understood, valued, narrated, and what norms and practices characterize it. Place is constructed through social processes and contested. |
| **Place-attachment** | Emotional and psychological connection of people to specific places, shaping decisions and behavior. Place attachment can persist even when economically disadvantageous. |
| **Place-meaning** | What a place means locally: its identity, cultural significance, historical narrative, and the understandings and values associated with it. Place-meanings vary by social group and are often contested. |
| **Privilege (geographic)** | Advantage that accrues from location—access to infrastructure, resources, opportunity, proximity to power—that is often invisible to those who benefit from it. Geographic privilege creates systematic advantage and power. |
| **Resilience (geographic)** | Capacity of a location or system to withstand disruption and recover. Resilience depends on redundancy, diversity, and access to resources. |
| **Resource dependence** | Economy of a location dependent on a single resource (minerals, fisheries, timber, agricultural crop). Resource-dependent economies are vulnerable to resource depletion and price shocks. |
| **Spatial inequality** | Unequal distribution of resources, opportunity, and well-being across geographic space. Spatial inequality is often self-reinforcing and persistent without deliberate intervention. |
| **Stigma (place-based)** | Negative reputation or social meaning attached to a location, often leading to disinvestment, out-migration, and self-fulfilling decline. Place-based stigma often overlaps with racialized or ethnic geography. |
| **Supply chain** | The sequence of locations and processes involved in producing and delivering a product from raw materials through manufacturing, transport, distribution to end-use. |
| **Territory** | A geographic area under the claim or control of a political authority, group, or community. Territories have boundaries, meanings, and governance. Territories are often contested. |
| **Urban sprawl** | Expansion of urban areas into surrounding rural or agricultural areas, converting land use and creating new patterns of settlement and dependency. |
| **Visibility (spatial)** | The degree to which locations, events, and conditions at different distances are visible to external observers and decision-makers. Distance and cultural difference reduce visibility. |

### Appendix B: Worked Example (Micro-Case)

[Example scenario: Decision to build major pipeline crossing multiple jurisdictions and geographic regions]

**Geographic framing**: Pipeline must cross five jurisdictions, high-inequality regions, indigenous territories with contested claims, water-stressed watershed, and politically contested border regions.

**Key types identified**:
- Type 3 (resource concentration): Oil/gas reserves concentrated in specific region
- Type 5 (water systems): Pipeline crosses major watershed; upstream/downstream dependency created
- Type 6 (infrastructure concentration): Pipeline becomes critical chokepoint for energy supply
- Type 8 (supply chain): Pipeline integrates into global energy supply chain
- Type 14 (territorial claims): Indigenous territories contested; pipeline crosses claimed lands
- Type 20 (resource distribution): Pipeline benefits some locations (energy supply centers) more than others
- Type 21 (jurisdictional boundaries): Crosses five jurisdictions with different authorities
- Type 23 (cross-boundary coordination): Requires coordination across jurisdictions with different interests
- Type 24 (territorial marginalization): Pipeline crosses marginalized regions whose voices are less heard
- Type 28 (resource geography): Pipeline taps depleting resource with finite timeline

**Privilege blindness identified**: Central decision-makers (energy consumers, pipeline company leadership) cannot perceive impacts on indigenous communities and water-stressed regions that pipeline crosses. Impacts in peripheral regions are less visible.

**Cost displacement identified**: Benefits (energy supply, jobs during construction, company profits) concentrate in central regions and benefit locations. Environmental costs (water stress, leakage risk, land use change) are borne by locations along pipeline route.

**Place-meaning contestation identified**: Pipeline route crosses indigenous territories where land has sacred and cultural meaning. Pipeline route also crosses regions with strong agricultural place-identity. Meanings conflict with external energy-supply meaning of pipeline.

**Governance challenge identified**: Five jurisdictions have overlapping authority; indigenous territories contest jurisdiction; environmental concerns cross boundaries; benefit and cost distribution is unequal across jurisdictions.

**Recommendations**: (1) Widen scope of analysis to include all affected locations in decision-making, not just benefit-centers; (2) Trace costs across geography and ensure cost-bearing communities have voice and compensation; (3) Document indigenous place-meanings and consider alternative routes that respect sacred lands; (4) Establish cross-boundary governance structure with equal representation of all jurisdictions and communities; (5) Conduct environmental impact assessment on water systems with upstream-downstream coordination; (6) Design redundancy into energy supply so no single pipeline is critical; (7) Establish benefit-sharing agreement for cost-bearing communities; (8) Commit to long-term monitoring of place-meaning impacts and environmental impacts.

- Gather locals or those familiar with the locations

- Identify single points of failure and redundancy gaps
- Design resilience improvements
